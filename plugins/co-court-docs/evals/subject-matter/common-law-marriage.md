# co-family-law — Common-law marriage under *Hogsett & Neale* + UDMA dissolution

## Prompt

My partner and I have lived together in Colorado for six
years. We've introduced each other as spouses, have a
joint bank account, and filed taxes jointly a couple of
times. We're splitting up. Are we married in the eyes of
Colorado law, and if so what's the process?

## Expected triggers

- `co-family-law`

## Acceptance criteria

### Colorado common-law marriage test

- [ ] States that Colorado **recognizes common-law
      marriage** — no specific period of cohabitation
      is required, and there is no formal registration
      requirement
- [ ] States the modern Colorado test: **mutual consent
      and agreement to be married**, evidenced by
      conduct and reputation, under
      ***In re Marriage of Hogsett & Neale*, 2021 CO 1**
      (the Colorado Supreme Court modernized the
      common-law marriage test, moving away from the
      older *People v. Lucero* checklist toward a
      **totality-of-the-circumstances inquiry** focused
      on mutual consent) — notes this holding must be
      **verified against `key-cases.md`** rather than
      asserted from memory
- [ ] Identifies the specific facts presented (joint
      account, joint tax filing, holding out as
      spouses) as **circumstantial evidence** of mutual
      consent rather than definitive proof

### UDMA dissolution if married

- [ ] States that if common-law marriage is established,
      the relationship must be dissolved via the
      **UDMA process** — the same **C.R.S. art. 10 of
      title 14** dissolution framework as a ceremonial
      marriage — including the 91-day residency and
      waiting period (cross-reference `co-family-law`
      dissolution eval)
- [ ] Notes that **property division** and **parental
      responsibilities** (if there are children) follow
      the same UDMA rules as any other dissolution

### Uncertainty framing

- [ ] Flags that whether a common-law marriage exists
      is a **fact-intensive inquiry** that a court
      resolves, and recommends consultation with a
      Colorado family-law attorney before relying on
      an assumed marital status

## Common failure modes

- States a specific number of years of cohabitation
  as the test (Colorado has no such requirement)
- Applies the older *Lucero* checklist without noting
  *Hogsett & Neale*'s modernization
- Fails to note that dissolution (not just separation)
  is required if common-law marriage is established
- Asserts the *Hogsett & Neale* holding from memory
  without verifying against key-cases.md
