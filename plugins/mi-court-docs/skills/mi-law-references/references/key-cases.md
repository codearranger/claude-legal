## Summary disposition — MCR 2.116 standards

- **Maiden v Rozwood, 461 Mich 109; 597 NW2d 817 (1999)** — the
  canonical statement of the **summary-disposition standards**.
  Distinguishes the grounds: a **(C)(8)** motion (failure to state a
  claim) is tested on the **pleadings alone**, accepting all
  well-pleaded factual allegations as true and granting only where the
  claim is so clearly unenforceable that no factual development could
  justify recovery; a **(C)(10)** motion tests **factual sufficiency**
  on the documentary evidence, with the court viewing the affidavits,
  depositions, admissions, and other evidence in the light most
  favorable to the non-moving party. The non-movant may not rest on
  mere allegations or denials but must set forth specific admissible
  facts showing a genuine issue for trial. Routinely cited as the
  lead authority on MCR 2.116.

- **Quinto v Cross & Peters Co, 451 Mich 358; 547 NW2d 314 (1996)** —
  the **burden-shifting mechanics on a (C)(10) motion**. Once the
  moving party properly supports its motion identifying the absence of
  a genuine factual issue, the burden shifts to the opposing party to
  come forward with **specific facts** (by affidavit or other
  documentary evidence that would be admissible) establishing a genuine
  issue for trial; a party may not survive on the pleadings or on
  speculation. Read together with *Maiden* for the (C)(10) framework.

## Comparative fault — tort damages

- **Modified comparative fault (statutory).** Michigan's
  comparative-fault regime is principally **statutory**: **MCL
  600.2959** reduces a plaintiff's damages by the plaintiff's
  percentage of comparative fault, and **bars recovery of noneconomic
  damages where the plaintiff's fault is greater than the aggregate
  fault of all others** (the so-called 51% bar for noneconomic
  damages). Economic damages remain recoverable, reduced by the
  plaintiff's fault percentage, even above that threshold (as applied
  through MCL 600.6304 / 600.6306). The fault-allocation framework also
  appears at **MCL 600.6304** (allocation of fault among all persons).
  Start the analysis with the statutes in `mi-statutes-debt/`; confirm
  current text and the controlling appellate gloss before relying on a
  specific percentage rule.

## Consumer protection — Michigan Consumer Protection Act

- **Smith v Globe Life Ins Co, 460 Mich 446; 597 NW2d 28 (1999)** —
  the leading (and much-criticized) construction of the **Michigan
  Consumer Protection Act (MCPA), MCL 445.901 et seq.** Reads the
  MCPA's "**specifically authorized**" **exemption** (MCL 445.904)
  broadly: where the **general transaction** is one specifically
  authorized by law and regulated, the MCPA does not apply — the
  inquiry is not whether the specific misconduct alleged was
  authorized. The decision substantially narrowed MCPA coverage of
  regulated industries; account for it (and any subsequent legislative
  or judicial developments) before pleading an MCPA claim. Pair with
  the FDCPA / state debt-collection analysis in `mi-consumer-debt`.

## Family law — property division and custody

- **Sparks v Sparks, 440 Mich 141; 485 NW2d 893 (1992)** — the
  controlling authority on **equitable division of marital property**.
  Michigan divides marital property **equitably**, not necessarily
  equally; the goal is an equitable distribution in light of all the
  circumstances. *Sparks* sets out the relevant **factors** a trial
  court must weigh (duration of the marriage; each party's contribution
  to the marital estate; the parties' ages, health, and life status;
  necessities and circumstances; earning abilities; the parties' past
  relations and conduct, i.e., fault; and general principles of
  equity), and holds that **fault is only one factor** and may not be
  given disproportionate weight. The appellate review standard:
  underlying findings of fact reviewed for clear error; the ultimate
  dispositional ruling affirmed unless the reviewing court is left with
  a firm conviction that the division was inequitable.

- **Berger v Berger, 277 Mich App 700; 747 NW2d 336 (2008)** — a widely
  cited **child-custody** decision under the **Child Custody Act (MCL
  722.21 et seq.)**. Discusses the **established custodial environment**
  inquiry (an ECE exists where, over an appreciable time, the child
  naturally looks to the custodian for guidance, discipline, the
  necessities of life, and parental comfort), the **best-interest
  factors (MCL 722.23)**, and the **standard of review (MCL 722.28)** —
  findings of fact reviewed against the great weight of the evidence,
  discretionary rulings for an abuse of discretion, and questions of
  law for clear legal error.

## How to use this file

2. Pull the full opinion (courts.michigan.gov / CourtListener) and

   `citation-format.md`): lowercased, non-italicized party names; `;`
   between parallel cites.
