# mi-personal-injury — MCL 500.3135 serious-impairment threshold (McCormick v Carrier) in an auto case

## Prompt

I was hurt in a car crash in Michigan — the other driver ran a
red light. I have a herniated disc, missed two months of work,
and still can't lift my kids. Someone told me Michigan's
no-fault system makes it hard to sue the other driver for pain
and suffering. Can I still sue, and what do I have to prove?

## Expected triggers

- `mi-personal-injury`

## Acceptance criteria

### The no-fault threshold (MCL 500.3135)

- [ ] Explains that under Michigan **no-fault**, recovery of
      **noneconomic damages (pain and suffering)** against the
      at-fault driver requires crossing the **MCL 500.3135**
      tort threshold — **death, permanent serious disfigurement,
      or serious impairment of body function** — citing
      MCL 500.3135 and reading the current threshold categories
      from the corpus rather than asserting them from memory
- [ ] Distinguishes the **threshold tort claim** (noneconomic
      damages, only if the threshold is met) from **PIP /
      no-fault benefits** (medical and wage-loss benefits payable
      regardless of fault) and from **excess economic loss**

### The McCormick framework

- [ ] Applies the ***McCormick v Carrier*** standard for
      "serious impairment of body function" — an **objectively
      manifested impairment** of an **important body function**
      that **affects the person's general ability to lead his or
      her normal life** — and notes the analysis is fact-specific
      and not reducible to a rigid numeric test; verifies the
      *McCormick* cite via the references corpus (`key-cases.md`)
      / CourtListener rather than asserting it from memory
- [ ] Applies the litigant's facts (herniated disc, work loss,
      lifting limitation) to the framework as illustrative, not
      as a guaranteed outcome

### Procedural notes

- [ ] Notes the relevant **statute of limitations** for the auto
      tort claim and reads it from the corpus rather than

- [ ] Notes comparative-fault implications under Michigan law

## Common failure modes

- Says the litigant cannot sue at all because of no-fault
  (ignoring the threshold exception)
- Conflates PIP benefits with the threshold tort claim
- Recites or invents the *McCormick* standard / cite without
  verifying it against the corpus
- Asserts the SOL or threshold categories from memory
