# mi-draft-declaration — Notarized affidavit under MCR 2.119(B)

## Prompt

I'm self-represented in a Michigan civil case and I need to
swear out a written statement of facts to attach to my motion —
things only I personally know, like when I got served and what
the other side told me on the phone. How do I draft a proper
affidavit for a Michigan court, and do I really need a notary?

## Expected triggers

- `mi-draft-declaration`
- `mi-statewide-format`
- `mi-pro-se`

## Acceptance criteria

### Form of the affidavit (MCR 2.119(B))

- [ ] States that an affidavit supporting or opposing a motion
      must comply with **MCR 2.119(B)** — made on personal
      knowledge, stating admissible facts, and showing the
      affiant is competent to testify to the matters stated
- [ ] Structures the affidavit with a caption per **MCR 1.109 /
      MCR 2.113**, a jurat, numbered factual paragraphs, and the
      affiant's signature
- [ ] Confines content to **personal knowledge** facts (not
      argument, legal conclusions, or hearsay) and flags that
      facts not within personal knowledge should be removed

### Notarization vs. the verification alternative

- [ ] Explains that a traditional affidavit is **sworn before a
      notary** (notary jurat with commission expiration)
- [ ] Offers the **MCR 1.109(D)(3)** alternative: a document may
      be **verified** by a signed declaration under penalty of
      perjury (the unsworn-declaration / verified-statement
      option) **in lieu of** notarization — read the current
      attestation language from the references corpus rather than
      quoting a fixed sentence from memory
- [ ] Notes when the notarized form is preferable (e.g., where a
      rule or the court specifically requires an affidavit) vs.
      when the MCR 1.109(D)(3) verification suffices

### Composition

- [ ] Self-represented signature block (no P-number)
- [ ] Caption / formatting consistent with `mi-statewide-format`

## Common failure modes

- Produces a "declaration" with no notary jurat and no
  MCR 1.109(D)(3) verification language at all
- Lets the affidavit drift into legal argument instead of
  personal-knowledge facts
- Quotes a hard-coded penalty-of-perjury sentence instead of
  pointing to the current MCR 1.109(D)(3) language in the corpus
- Cites a federal 28 U.S.C. § 1746 declaration instead of the
  Michigan rule
