# The Pro-Se Drafting Framework — Adapted for Oregon

The "pro-se drafting framework" is a drafting discipline developed by
  for pro se litigants. It is rule-of-
thumb best practice and unrelated to any specific court rule, but
its conventions match what Oregon civil judges expect when
reading pro se motions.

## The four principles

### 1. Front-load the strongest facts

The judge reads the first paragraph of every motion. Make it
count.

**Bad opening (pro se debt-defense motion):**

> "This matter comes before the Court on Defendant's Motion to
> Compel. Plaintiff filed its Complaint on January 14, 2025,
> alleging breach of contract. Defendant answered on February
> 11, 2025. On March 3, 2025, Defendant served Requests for
> Production..."

**Good opening (same case):**

> "Plaintiff is a debt buyer that purchased Defendant's alleged
> account through an undated bulk assignment with no
> identifying information. When Defendant requested the chain
> of title under ORCP 43, Plaintiff produced a single sheet of
> paper styled 'Bill of Sale' that does not name Defendant, does
> not reference the account number, and is signed by no one.
> This Motion seeks an order compelling Plaintiff to produce
> the documents that, if they exist, prove its standing to
> sue."

The good version puts the strongest fact (Plaintiff cannot prove
chain of title) in front of the judge in one paragraph. The
procedural history can come later in Section II.

### 2. Keep motions concise

Target 4–6 pages for the memorandum, even on complex motions.
Length is correlated with the strength of the underlying case
only loosely; a tight motion signals confidence.

Techniques:

- **Bold lead-ins** on each paragraph of argument: "**The
  documents are within the ORCP 36 B(1) scope.**"
- **One fact per sentence** in the statement of facts
- **Move narrative to the declaration** — the motion cites
  paragraphs in the declaration ("Decl. ¶ 4")
- **Cut adverbs**: "clearly", "obviously", "plainly" don't
  persuade the judge; they signal weakness

### 3. Write to the judge, not to opposing counsel

The judge will read both sides' filings. Opposing counsel knows
the case. Your audience is the judge, who is reading the file
for the first or second time.

Avoid:

- Ad hominem ("Plaintiff's counsel has repeatedly misrepresented
  the facts")
- Sarcasm ("Plaintiff seems to think...")
- Argument by adjective ("Plaintiff's frivolous objections")

State facts instead. The court will draw the characterizations
on its own.

**Bad**: "Plaintiff's objections are baseless and frivolous."

**Good**: "Plaintiff objected on grounds of relevance, but
the request specifically targets the documents Plaintiff is
required to authenticate under OEC 901 to admit its primary
exhibits. (Decl. ¶ 6; Ex. 2 at p. 3.)"

### 4. Let the record speak

Attach the documents that prove the point. Cite them by exhibit
number and page. A well-indexed record persuades more than
rhetoric.

- "Exhibit 1 at p. 2" not "as the Court can see from Plaintiff's
  conduct"
- "Decl. ¶ 4–7" not "as the timeline shows"
- "ORCP 36 B(1)" not "the discovery rules require"

## Oregon-specific Parker tweaks

### Cite the rule first

Oregon judges expect the procedural authority on the first line
of the motion. "Pursuant to ORCP 46 A, Defendant moves..." not
"This Court has the power to compel..."

### Use the exact ORCP subsection

ORCP subsections use upper-case letters separated by spaces:
**ORCP 21 A(8)**, not "Rule 21(A)(8)" or "21(a)(8)". When citing
ORCP in a motion, follow the Oregon Appellate Courts Style
Manual.

### Don't say "12(b)(6)"

That's Federal. Oregon's analog is **ORCP 21 A(8)** — failure
to state ultimate facts constituting a claim.

### Don't say "interrogatory"

Oregon does not have written interrogatories under ORCP as a
matter of right (see ORCP 39 limits depositions; no analog to
FRCP 33). If the user's prior pleadings reference
"interrogatories", flag the issue — either the case is in
arbitration where the arbitrator's rules allow them, or the
parties have stipulated, or it's a labeling error.

### Exhibits are numbered

Exhibit 1, Exhibit 2, Exhibit 3. Lettered exhibits (A, B, C) are
the Washington convention and signal a Washington template
ported to Oregon without adaptation.

### "v." not "vs."

Oregon convention.

## Example motion structure — pro se Oregon

```
            IN THE CIRCUIT COURT OF THE STATE OF OREGON
                  FOR THE COUNTY OF MULTNOMAH

VELOCITY INVESTMENTS, LLC,      │  Case No. 25CV12345
                                │
     Plaintiff,                 │  DEFENDANT'S MOTION TO
                                │  COMPEL PRODUCTION OF
     v.                         │  DOCUMENTS UNDER ORCP 46 A
                                │  (ORAL ARGUMENT REQUESTED)
JOHN DOE,                       │
                                │
     Defendant.                 │
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

                              MOTION

Pursuant to ORCP 46 A, Defendant John Doe, pro se,
respectfully moves this Court for an order compelling
Plaintiff to produce the documents responsive to Defendant's
First Requests for Production, served on April 1, 2025. This
Motion is supported by the Memorandum below, the Declaration
of John Doe filed herewith, and the records of this action.

                          MEMORANDUM

I.  RELIEF REQUESTED

    Defendant asks the Court to enter an order:
    (a) compelling Plaintiff to produce documents responsive
        to Requests Nos. 1–6 by [date certain];
    (b) overruling Plaintiff's objections; and
    (c) awarding Defendant reasonable expenses incurred in
        making this Motion, per ORCP 46 A(4)(a).

II. STATEMENT OF FACTS

    1.  Plaintiff is a debt buyer that filed this action on
        January 14, 2025. (Compl. ¶ 1.) Plaintiff's Complaint
        alleges that Plaintiff purchased an account
        originally owned by Bank of America. (Compl. ¶ 4.)

    2.  Defendant denies the existence and ownership of the
        account. (Answer ¶ 4.)

    3.  On April 1, 2025, Defendant served First Requests for
        Production Nos. 1–6. (Decl. ¶ 2; Ex. 1.) The Requests
        target the chain of title from Bank of America to
        Plaintiff, including:
          - The cardholder agreement (RFP 1)
          - Monthly statements (RFP 2)
          - The bill of sale (RFP 3)
          - The assignment schedule (RFP 4)
          - The pool sale agreement (RFP 5)
          - The current account history (RFP 6)

    4.  On May 1, 2025, Plaintiff served Responses
        objecting on grounds of relevance, burden, and
        possession. (Decl. ¶ 3; Ex. 2.) Plaintiff produced no
        documents.

    5.  On May 10, 2025, Defendant met and conferred via
        email and phone. (Decl. ¶ 4; Ex. 3.) Plaintiff
        declined to supplement.

III. STATEMENT OF ISSUES

     1. Whether documents proving the chain of title from the
        original creditor to a debt-buyer plaintiff are
        within ORCP 36 B(1) scope when the defendant denies
        the existence of the account.
     2. Whether Plaintiff's "possession" objection is valid
        when Plaintiff alleges it owns the account.
     3. Whether fee-shifting under ORCP 46 A(4)(a) is
        mandatory given Plaintiff's failure to produce was
        "without substantial justification".

IV. EVIDENCE RELIED UPON

    - Declaration of John Doe, filed herewith
    - Exhibit 1: First Requests for Production
    - Exhibit 2: Plaintiff's Responses and Objections
    - Exhibit 3: May 10, 2025 meet-and-confer correspondence

V.  AUTHORITIES

    - ORCP 36 B(1) (scope of discovery)
    - ORCP 43 (requests for production)
    - ORCP 46 A (motion to compel)
    - ORCP 46 A(4)(a) (mandatory fee-shifting on motion to
      compel)
    - Multnomah SLR 5.045 (meet-and-confer)

VI. ARGUMENT

    A.  The documents are within ORCP 36 B(1) scope.

        ORCP 36 B(1) defines the scope of discovery as any
        non-privileged matter relevant to a claim or defense
        and proportional to the needs of the case. ...

    B.  The "possession" objection contradicts Plaintiff's
        Complaint.

        Plaintiff's Complaint alleges it owns the account.
        (Compl. ¶ 4.) Plaintiff cannot now claim it does not
        possess the documents proving ownership. ...

    C.  Fee-shifting is mandatory under ORCP 46 A(4)(a).

        ORCP 46 A(4)(a) requires the court to award
        reasonable expenses including attorney fees on a
        successful motion to compel "unless the court finds
        that the opposing party's failure was substantially
        justified." Plaintiff's failure cannot be
        substantially justified ...

VII. CONCLUSION

     For the foregoing reasons, Defendant respectfully
     requests entry of the Proposed Order filed herewith.

DATED this ____ day of __________, 20__.

______________________________________
JOHN DOE
Defendant, pro se
[Street Address]
[City, OR ZIP]
[Phone]
[Email]


                  CERTIFICATE OF SERVICE
[Per UTCR 1.090]
```

This is 4 pages of memorandum (or close to it) with structured
sections that a judge can navigate in under 5 minutes. The same
factual material would balloon to 12+ pages in a freeform pro se
motion.

## What Parker doesn't tell you (Oregon flavor)

- **Working copies** are not in Parker but are critical in
  Multnomah / Washington Co for any motion over the local
  threshold. Always check the SLR.
- **OSB#** is required for licensed counsel but **omitted** for
  pro se. Don't include a fake number — that risks UPL
  sanctions under ORS 9.160.
- **Mandatory arbitration** under ORS 36.400 changes the venue
  for motions in $10K–$50K cases. Read the case file before
  drafting.
- **The Oregon Style Manual** uses different citation
  conventions than the BlueBook (no periods after Or, P3d, USC,
  CFR; spaces between ORCP subsection letters). Match the
  manual, not BlueBook.
