# Service Protocol — Oregon Pro Se

## Service of initial process (ORCP 7)

When you (as plaintiff) file a new case, the **summons and
complaint** must be served on the defendant per ORCP 7. eFiling
does NOT serve initial process.

### Methods (ORCP 7 D(2))

| Method | When usable |
|--------|-------------|
| **Personal service** | Always; preferred for definitiveness |
| **Substituted service** | Adult resident at the dwelling, plus mailing |
| **Office service** | For businesses, served on registered agent or specified person |
| **Service by mail** | Only with prior written consent of defendant, OR after another method has been attempted unsuccessfully |
| **Service by publication** | Last resort; requires court order showing diligent inquiry |

### Who can serve

- **A sheriff** (typically $30–$80 per attempt depending on
  county)
- **A private process server** (typically $50–$150 per
  successful service)
- **Any adult non-party** (ORCP 7 E) — a friend or family
  member, but NOT the plaintiff personally

You cannot serve your own process. A non-party adult must do it.

### Return of Service

After service, the server signs a **Return of Service** form
under penalty of perjury, stating the date, time, place, and
manner of service. File this with the court within 63 days of
service per ORCP 7 D(3) — late filing risks ORCP 54 dismissal.

OJD forms page has Return-of-Service templates:
https://www.courts.oregon.gov/forms

## Service of subsequent papers (ORCP 9)

After the initial complaint is served, all later filings are
served under ORCP 9. Pro se filers have these options:

### Through File and Serve (eService)

If both you and the opposing party are registered for eService
on File and Serve, eService is the default. The portal sends an
email notification and a download link to the receiving party
at the moment you file.

Pro se filers should **register for eService** on File and Serve
even if they file paper — it dramatically simplifies receiving
documents from opposing counsel (no more chasing mail).

### Email

Acceptable if:

- The receiving party has consented in writing (e.g., a stipulation
  about service), OR
- The receiving party has registered for eService on File and
  Serve (UTCR 21.100 deems this consent to email service)

### Mail (USPS first-class)

Universally acceptable. **Three days are added** to the response
deadline when service is by mail (ORCP 10 C) — important for
deadline computation.

### Hand delivery

Universally acceptable. Date the recipient's signed
acknowledgment; if no acknowledgment, photograph delivery.

### Recommended belt-and-suspenders for pro se

When opposing counsel has a documented history of disputed
service, serve by **two** methods:

- Email (immediate; preserves a timestamp)
- USPS Certified Mail with Return Receipt (slow but
  irrefutable; ~$5)

Document both on the Certificate of Service.

## Certificate of Service (UTCR 1.090)

Every filing served on another party must have a Certificate of
Service stating:

- Date of service
- Method (mail, eService, email, hand)
- Name and address (or email) of each party served
- Signature of the server

### UTCR 1.090 form

```
                  CERTIFICATE OF SERVICE

I hereby certify that on the ____ day of __________, 20__, I
served a true and correct copy of the foregoing [Document
Title] on the following parties in the manner indicated:

Veronica Velocity                    eService via File and Serve
Velocity Investments, LLC            (also via email to
[Address]                            veronica@velocity.com)
[Email]

                            _______________________________
                            John Doe
                            Defendant, pro se
                            [Address]
                            [Phone]
                            [Email]
```

Append the Certificate as the last page of the served document,
or attach as a separate page in the eFile packet.

## Practical tips for pro se service

### 1. Photograph postmarked envelopes

Before opening any incoming mail from the court or opposing
counsel, photograph the envelope showing the postmark date. The
postmark sometimes matters for deadline computation (e.g., when
opposing counsel claims they mailed on a date earlier than the
postmark shows).

### 2. Confirm receipt in writing

After you serve a filing, send a short email to opposing
counsel: "I've served [document] today by [method]. Please
confirm receipt." A confirming email creates a contemporaneous
record.

### 3. Memorialize phone calls

After any phone call with opposing counsel, immediately send a
short email: "To confirm our call today at 2:15 PM: [summary of
what was said]." If opposing counsel later disputes the call,
the email is contemporaneous evidence.

### 4. Keep a service log

A simple spreadsheet:

| Date | Filing | Method | Recipient | Postmark / Tracking | Notes |
|------|--------|--------|-----------|---------------------|-------|
| 04/15/25 | Motion to Compel | eService + Certified Mail | Velocity / counsel | EJ123456789US | |

Update after every service. At hearing, this log can become an
exhibit if service becomes contested.

### 5. When in doubt, over-serve

Three methods (eService + email + Certified Mail) on a
disputed party costs nothing extra. Failing to serve is a
ground for the court to disregard your filing. Over-serving
costs nothing and proves the record.

## Common pro se service mistakes

| Mistake | Consequence |
|---------|-------------|
| Serving your own initial complaint | Service invalid (ORCP 7 E); case dismissable |
| Forgetting the Certificate of Service | UTCR 1.090 violation; clerk may reject the filing |
| Mailing without tracking | If opposing claims non-receipt, no proof |
| Email without the receiving party's eService registration | May not be valid under UTCR 21.100 |
| Not adding 3 days for mail (ORCP 10 C) | Miscomputed response deadlines |
| Hand delivery without acknowledgment | If recipient claims non-receipt, no proof |
