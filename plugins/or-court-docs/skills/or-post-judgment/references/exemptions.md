# Oregon Exemptions from Execution — Annotated Catalog

Oregon's exemption statutes are in **ORS 18.345–18.385** (state
exemptions). Federal exemptions are also available in some
cases (the debtor chooses between state and federal in
bankruptcy under 11 USC § 522; in non-bankruptcy state
collection, state exemptions apply).

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Amounts and provisions update; verify
> against current ORS before relying.

## Real property

### Homestead (ORS 18.395)

| Item | Limit |
|------|-------|
| Single debtor | $40,000 of equity in principal residence |
| Joint owners (both debtors) | $50,000 of equity |
| Mobile home as principal residence | Same |
| Floating home as principal residence | Same |

The homestead must be the **principal residence** — vacation
homes, rental property, and second homes are not protected.

### Vacant lot intended as homestead

Not protected under ORS 18.395. The structure must be the
actual residence.

## Personal property (ORS 18.345)

| Item | Limit |
|------|-------|
| Motor vehicle | $3,000 equity in one vehicle |
| Tools, equipment, books, instruments used in trade | $5,000 |
| Books, family pictures, musical instruments | $600 each |
| Household goods, furniture, appliances | $3,000 aggregate |
| Wearing apparel, jewelry, personal items | $1,800 |
| Watch | $600 |
| Health aids (wheelchair, hearing aid, glasses) | Unlimited |
| Burial plot | Unlimited |
| Domestic animals | $1,000 aggregate; for working animals, may be included under tools of trade |
| Health-insurance proceeds for medical expenses | Unlimited within reason |

## Earnings (ORS 18.385)

### Disposable earnings exemption

Wages from current or recently terminated employment:

- **75% of disposable earnings** is exempt
- **Or**: the amount by which disposable exceeds 40× federal
  minimum hourly wage per week — whichever yields more
  exemption
- At $7.25 federal minimum: ~$290/week is the floor; below
  that, no garnishment

**"Disposable earnings"**: gross wages – mandatory deductions
(taxes, FICA, mandatory retirement, court-ordered child
support).

### Self-employment income

Treated as earnings if it constitutes a regular paycheck.
Independent-contractor income may be subject to account-
receivable garnishment rather than wage garnishment — different
rules.

### Hardship deviation

ORS 18.385 allows the court to reduce the 25% rate on a
showing of hardship. Practical: dependent obligations,
medical, eviction risk.

## Retirement and benefit accounts

### Pensions, retirement, IRAs (ORS 18.358)

- ERISA-qualified plans (401(k), 403(b)): exempt
- Traditional IRA, Roth IRA: exempt up to amount needed for
  retirement
- Public employee retirement (PERS): exempt
- Inherited IRAs: depending on the source, may or may not be
  exempt — fact-specific

### Federal benefits (federal preemption)

These are protected under federal law, in any state:

- Social Security retirement and disability (42 USC § 407)
- Supplemental Security Income (SSI)
- VA benefits (38 USC § 5301)
- Federal retirement (CSRS, FERS, military)
- Railroad retirement
- Federal student aid (FSA)

Federal benefits retain their exempt character when **directly
deposited** into a bank account, under 31 CFR 212. Banks must
apply a safe-harbor protection equal to the sum of federal-
benefit deposits in the 2 months before the levy.

### State benefits (ORS 18.345)

- Unemployment compensation
- Workers' compensation
- Public assistance (TANF, OHP, SNAP)
- State tax refunds (including Oregon EITC)
- Crime-victim compensation
- Adoption-assistance payments

## Insurance proceeds

| Type | Exemption |
|------|-----------|
| Life insurance proceeds payable to a dependent | Unlimited |
| Health-insurance proceeds for medical expenses | Unlimited within reason |
| Disability income insurance | Unlimited |
| Cash-surrender value of life insurance | $9,000 limit (verify current) |
| Annuity (pre-existing) | Up to $9,000/year |
| Other insurance proceeds | Limited; consult ORS 18.345 |

## Wildcard

Oregon does NOT have a general "wildcard" exemption in
non-bankruptcy contexts. (Bankruptcy debtors using the federal
exemption schedule under 11 USC § 522 have a wildcard of
~$1,400; verify current.)

## What is NOT exempt

- Investment accounts (brokerage, securities) — non-retirement
- Real property other than principal residence
- Boats, RVs, second vehicles (beyond the $3,000 motor-vehicle
  exemption for one)
- Collectibles, artwork (some can fit under household goods if
  used)
- Cryptocurrency
- Cash beyond what fits in another category

## How to claim an exemption

Exemptions are **not self-executing**. The debtor must
affirmatively claim them by:

### For wage garnishment:

- The employer typically applies the 75% exemption
  automatically when the writ is served
- For additional protection (federal benefits, head of
  household), file a Challenge to Writ under ORS 18.700+
  within 30 days

### For bank levy:

- File a Challenge to Writ under ORS 18.700+ within 30 days
- Identify the specific funds claimed exempt and the
  statutory ground
- Attach bank statements showing the deposits

### For supplemental proceedings (debtor exam):

- Claim the exemption on the record when asked about the
  property
- Be specific: "That account contains my Social Security
  direct deposits, exempt under 42 USC § 407"

### For property seizure (rare in civil cases):

- File a Claim of Exemption before the seizure or within 5
  days after (ORS 18.652)

See `references/garnishment-response.md` for the full
procedure.

## Pre-judgment exemption planning

Once a creditor files suit, planning is constrained — moving
non-exempt assets into exempt categories within the lookback
periods can be unwound as fraudulent transfer (ORS 95.230 et
seq.). Legitimate exemption planning happens **before**
litigation arises:

- Set up direct-deposit of federal benefits to a clearly
  identified account
- Maintain retirement contributions
- Keep good records of source-of-funds for each asset

For post-judgment exemption claims, the source-of-funds record
is what proves the exempt character.

## Federal exemption choice in bankruptcy

If the debtor files bankruptcy in Oregon, they can choose
between Oregon state exemptions (ORS 18.345+) and federal
exemptions (11 USC § 522). The federal schedule is generally
more generous in:

- Wildcard (~$1,400)
- Vehicle exemption ($4,450)
- Retirement (no cap on ERISA-qualified)
- Household goods ($725 per item, $14,875 aggregate)

The Oregon schedule may be more generous in:

- Homestead ($40,000 / $50,000 joint)
- Tools of trade ($5,000)
- Tenancy by the entirety (joint marital property; not
  available in federal schedule)

For non-bankruptcy state collection, only state exemptions
apply.

## Common exemption-claim pitfalls

| Pitfall | Consequence |
|---------|-------------|
| Missing the 30-day Challenge window | Garnishment proceeds; property lost |
| Failing to attach proof (bank statements, benefit letters) | Court denies for lack of evidence |
| Claiming general "hardship" without statute | Most exemptions are statutory; "hardship" alone isn't a ground |
| Commingling exempt and non-exempt funds | Loses the exempt character; harder to trace |
| Forgetting to claim at supplemental proceedings | Risks contempt; assets discovered |
| Asserting exemptions that don't apply (e.g., "wildcard" in non-bankruptcy) | Credibility damaged |
