# Satisfaction of Judgment — Oregon

## When a judgment is paid in full

The judgment creditor must file a **Satisfaction of Judgment**
under **ORS 18.235** within **60 days** of full payment.

The Satisfaction:

- Identifies the case and the judgment
- States the amount paid
- States that the judgment is satisfied in full (or partially,
  if a partial payment)
- Is signed by the creditor or counsel

Once filed, the judgment is no longer collectible. Renewal,
further writs, and credit-reporting collection activity must
stop.

## Why this matters

A judgment remains a public record for 10 years (renewable
once). Failure to file a Satisfaction:

- Allows the creditor to attempt further collection
- Keeps the judgment on the debtor's credit report
- Allows the judgment to be sold to a third party (who can
  then attempt collection)

Filing the Satisfaction is the creditor's obligation, but the
debtor needs to follow up if the creditor doesn't.

## Procedure when the creditor refuses to file

If the creditor fails to file a Satisfaction within 60 days
of full payment:

### Step 1: Written demand

Send a written demand letter to the creditor (USPS Certified
Mail with Return Receipt):

```
[Date]

[Creditor / counsel address]

Re:  Satisfaction of Judgment, Case No. 25CV12345

Dear [name]:

I have paid the judgment in this matter in full. The total
amount paid is $[amount], satisfied on [date]. Receipts
attached.

Under ORS 18.235, the judgment creditor must file a
Satisfaction of Judgment with the Court within 60 days of
full payment. As of today's date, [date], I have not seen a
Satisfaction filed in the court's record.

Please file the Satisfaction with the Court within 14 days
of this letter. If you do not, I will file a motion to
compel and seek the fees and costs of that motion under
ORS 20.105 and applicable rule.

[Signature]
[Name][, pro se]
```

### Step 2: Motion to compel filing of Satisfaction

If 14 days pass without compliance, file a Motion under ORS
18.235 to compel the creditor to file a Satisfaction (or for
the court to enter an order declaring the judgment satisfied).

The motion shows:

- The judgment
- The proof of payment (canceled checks, wire receipts, money
  orders, bank records)
- The demand letter
- The creditor's failure to comply

### Step 3: Fee award

ORS 20.105 (objectively unreasonable position) may support a
fee award against a creditor who refuses to acknowledge a
satisfied judgment. The standard is *Mattiza v. Foster* — the
creditor has no objectively reasonable basis to deny
satisfaction when payment in full is documented.

## Partial Satisfaction

If only part of the judgment is paid, the creditor files a
**Partial Satisfaction** identifying:

- The amount paid
- The remaining balance

The remaining balance continues to be collectible. The
debtor's payment is credited and reduces the judgment.

## Settlement scenarios

Many judgments are satisfied through **settlement**, not full
payment of the original amount. When a creditor settles for
less than the full amount, the Satisfaction:

- States the amount actually paid
- States that the judgment is "satisfied" — meaning the
  creditor has no further claim, even if the paid amount is
  less than the original judgment

The debtor's negotiating leverage: the creditor accepts the
settled amount in exchange for a Satisfaction. The
settlement letter should:

- Confirm the amount being paid
- Confirm the creditor will file a Satisfaction within 14
  days of receipt
- Acknowledge that the creditor takes the payment in full
  satisfaction of the judgment

Without this language, the creditor may take the payment but
claim a continuing balance.

## Credit reporting after Satisfaction

Even after a Satisfaction is filed, the judgment may continue
to appear on the debtor's credit report as "paid" or
"satisfied" for up to 7 years (Fair Credit Reporting Act,
15 USC § 1681c). The Satisfaction record does NOT remove the
judgment from the credit report — it just changes the status
to satisfied.

To dispute inaccurate credit-report entries:

- File a dispute with each credit bureau (Equifax, Experian,
  TransUnion) under FCRA
- If the bureau verifies the entry and the entry is still
  inaccurate (e.g., shows "unpaid" after Satisfaction filed),
  FCRA private action available under 15 USC § 1681n / 1681o
