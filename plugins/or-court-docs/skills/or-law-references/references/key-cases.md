# Oregon Key Cases — General Civil

A short catalog of the Oregon decisions this plugin relies on
across general civil practice. Subject-matter-specific key cases
(FDCPA, UTPA, business-records foundation in debt cases, chain
of title) live in `or-consumer-debt/references/key-cases.md`.

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Verify every case is still good law
> before citing. Use CourtListener or KeyCite-equivalent service
> to confirm.

## Summary judgment (ORCP 47)

### Two Two v. Fujitec America, Inc., 355 Or 319, 325 P3d 707 (2014)

The leading recent SJ-burden decision. Holds:

- The moving party has the initial burden of demonstrating there
  is no genuine issue of material fact and that it is entitled
  to judgment as a matter of law.
- Once the moving party makes a prima facie showing, the burden
  shifts to the non-movant to designate specific facts showing
  a genuine issue for trial.
- Inferences from the underlying facts are drawn in the light
  most favorable to the non-movant.
- Conclusory denials, allegations, or speculation are
  insufficient to defeat SJ.

### Jones v. General Motors Corp., 325 Or 404, 939 P2d 608 (1997)

The classic SJ-inference standard:

- The court draws all reasonable inferences from the underlying
  facts in favor of the non-moving party.
- This is a question of law for the court; whether a reasonable
  jury could find for the non-movant is the inquiry.

### Robinson v. Lamb's Wilsonville Thriftway, 332 Or 453, 31 P3d 421 (2001)

ORCP 47 C affidavits / declarations must:

- Be made on personal knowledge
- Set forth facts that would be admissible at trial
- Show that the affiant is competent to testify

A declaration that fails any of these is properly disregarded
on SJ.

## Default judgment vacation (ORCP 71)

### Wagar v. Prudential Ins. Co. of America, 276 Or 827, 556 P2d 658 (1976)

The leading older case on ORCP 71 B(1) excusable neglect. Sets
out the three-factor analysis still cited:

1. The reason for the failure (was it a mistake, surprise, or
   excusable neglect?)
2. Whether the moving party has a meritorious defense
3. Whether the opposing party will be prejudiced by vacating

### Recent ORCP 71 decisions

Oregon Court of Appeals has produced several recent ORCP 71
opinions refining the standard for "reasonable time" under
ORCP 71 B and the showings required for grounds (4) (void
judgment) and (6) (any other reason). Always shepardize *Wagar*
before relying on its formulation.

## Attorney fees and ORS 20.105

### Mattiza v. Foster, 311 Or 1, 803 P2d 723 (1990)

The leading case on ORS 20.105 "objectively unreasonable":

- The test is **objective**, not subjective — the question is
  whether a reasonable attorney would have advanced the position
- Pro se status is not a shield
- The court should consider both the factual record and the
  applicable law at the time the position was taken
- Frivolous appeals can support an ORS 20.105 award

### Strawn v. Farmers Ins. Co. of Oregon, 353 Or 210, 297 P3d 439 (2013)

The leading recent decision on **lodestar methodology** and the
ORS 20.075 factors:

- Lodestar = hours reasonably expended × reasonable hourly rate
- The court must consider the ORS 20.075 factors in determining
  the reasonableness of both hours and rates
- Block-billing without task detail supports reduction
- Findings on each factor are required, but need not be lengthy

### Lemargie v. Johnson, 212 Or App 451, 157 P3d 1284 (2007)

ORS 20.096 reciprocal fee-shifting:

- A one-sided contract fee clause is read reciprocally by
  operation of ORS 20.096
- Prevailing party — on either side — recovers under the clause
- The clause's literal scope (e.g., "in any action to enforce")
  is enforced; ambiguous clauses are construed against the
  drafter

### Domingo v. Anderson, 325 Or 385, 938 P2d 206 (1997)

The American Rule baseline:

- Each party bears its own attorney fees unless authorized by
  statute, contract, or rule
- No common-law fee-shifting in Oregon
- Statutes authorizing fees are strictly construed

## Discovery and ORCP 46

### Phillips v. Beaverton Aluminum Co., 314 Or App 257, 498 P3d 814 (2021)

Recent ORCP 46 A(4)(a) fee-shifting decision:

- The presumption is fees follow a granted motion to compel
- "Substantially justified" requires more than non-frivolousness
- A genuine ambiguity in the rule may support substantial
  justification; routine objections without basis do not

(Note: this is illustrative; verify the exact citation and
holding before relying. Recent ORCP 46 decisions are scattered.)

## Pleading sufficiency and ORCP 21

### Granewich v. Harding, 329 Or 47, 985 P2d 788 (1999)

ORCP 21 A(8) "failure to state ultimate facts":

- The complaint must allege the **ultimate facts** that constitute
  each element of the claim
- "Notice pleading" is rejected — Oregon uses **fact pleading**
- Conclusory allegations of legal elements are insufficient
- Plaintiff is entitled to all favorable inferences from
  well-pleaded facts

This distinguishes Oregon pleading from federal "notice pleading"
under FRCP 8(a) and the *Twombly/Iqbal* plausibility standard.
**Oregon requires more specificity in the complaint** than the
federal courts do.

### Slogowski v. Lyness, 324 Or 436, 927 P2d 587 (1996)

Motion to strike under ORCP 21 E:

- The court may strike "redundant, irrelevant, immaterial,
  impertinent or scandalous" matter
- The standard is restrictive; doubts resolved against striking
- Motions to strike are disfavored

## Service of process (ORCP 7)

### Baker v. Foy, 310 Or 221, 797 P2d 349 (1990)

Substituted service under ORCP 7 D(2)(b):

- Service must be at the defendant's **dwelling or usual place
  of abode**, not merely a residence the defendant visits
- The recipient must be a "**competent adult member of the
  household**"
- Failure to satisfy these elements renders service insufficient
  and dismisses for lack of personal jurisdiction (ORCP 21
  A(2))

## Pleading amendments (ORCP 23)

### Eagle Industries, Inc. v. Thompson, 321 Or 398, 900 P2d 475 (1995)

ORCP 23 A "freely given":

- Leave to amend is the strong default
- Refused only for clear prejudice, undue delay, bad faith, or
  futility of amendment
- The court considers the stage of the litigation; pre-trial
  amendments are more freely allowed than post-trial

## Voluntary dismissal (ORCP 54)

### Tikka v. Martin, 271 Or App 588, 352 P3d 60 (2015)

ORCP 54 A voluntary dismissal:

- A plaintiff may dismiss without leave before answer or motion
  for SJ is filed
- Once the answer or SJ motion is on file, dismissal requires
  court order
- The court has discretion to impose conditions (e.g., that
  defendant's costs be paid)

## Mandatory arbitration (ORS 36.400)

### Powell v. Sundling, 285 Or App 76, 396 P3d 332 (2017)

Trial de novo under ORS 36.425:

- A party may request trial de novo within 20 days of the
  arbitration award
- If the requesting party does not improve on the award by
  more than 10%, the court must award the responding party's
  attorney fees and costs
- The fee-shifting is meant to discourage frivolous trials
  de novo

## Recurring use patterns

When a motion needs SJ standard:
- Cite *Two Two* for burden
- Cite *Jones* for inferences

When a motion needs ORCP 71 vacation:
- Cite *Wagar* for the three-factor analysis

When seeking fees:
- Cite the underlying statute/contract/rule
- Cite *Strawn* for lodestar
- Cite *Lemargie* for ORS 20.096 reciprocity

When defending ORS 20.105 fees:
- Cite *Mattiza* — objectively reasonable means non-frivolous

When pleading sufficiency is challenged:
- Cite *Granewich* — Oregon is fact pleading, not notice
  pleading
