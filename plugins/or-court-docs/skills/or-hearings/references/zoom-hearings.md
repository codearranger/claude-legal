# Remote Hearings — WebEx / Zoom in Oregon Circuit Courts

The OJD adopted **Cisco WebEx** as the official remote-hearing
platform statewide during the COVID era and has retained it for
routine civil matters. Many practitioners still colloquially
call them "Zoom hearings" — they're WebEx in Oregon.

## When remote is used

- **WebEx is default** for routine civil motion hearings in
  most counties (Multnomah, Washington, Lane, Marion)
- **In-person is default** for evidentiary hearings, summary
  judgment, trials, and complex matters
- **The assigned judge's standing order** controls — some
  judges keep WebEx for everything, others prefer in-person
  for any contested matter

The hearing mode is communicated:

- Multnomah: on the JA's confirmation email when the date is
  set
- Washington Co: on the Civil Division's confirmation
- Other counties: on the date-confirmation communication
- Always reflected on the Notice of Hearing filed in the case

## Connection logistics

### WebEx connection methods

- **Video via WebEx app or browser**: best — full audio, video,
  and screen-share capability
- **Audio-only via WebEx call-in number**: backup if video
  fails; the WebEx invitation provides a phone call-in number
  and meeting ID
- **Standard telephone dial-in**: each circuit publishes a
  standard call-in line for the courtroom (look up on the
  court's standing-order page)

### Pre-hearing setup checklist

- [ ] **48 hours before**: install WebEx app or test browser
      version; verify the invitation link is reachable
- [ ] **24 hours before**: test camera, microphone, and
      speakers using a test call
- [ ] **Day of, 30 minutes before**: log in and join the
      waiting room
- [ ] **Quiet location**: no roommates, kids, pets, or
      background noise
- [ ] **Plug into power**: don't run on battery; hearings can
      run long
- [ ] **Ethernet preferred over Wi-Fi**: more stable
      connection
- [ ] **Headphones recommended**: prevents echo and improves
      audio quality
- [ ] **Phone backup**: have the call-in number and meeting
      ID handy in case video fails
- [ ] **Materials at hand**: motion, exhibits, opposing
      response, your outline

## Etiquette on WebEx

### Identification

- **Display your full name** in WebEx, not "iPad" or
  "[brand of phone]" — the clerk needs to identify
  participants
- **Identify yourself when you join**: "Good morning, this is
  [Name], pro se defendant in [Case Title], Case No.
  25CV12345"

### Camera

- **Camera on** is the default for the speaker. Some judges
  allow camera off for non-speaking participants.
- **Look at the camera** when speaking, not the screen. This
  is the eye-contact equivalent on video.
- **Camera at eye level** — prop laptop on books if needed.
  Looking up your nostrils is a bad look.
- **Plain background** — neutral wall or blurred background.
  Avoid bright windows behind you (silhouettes the speaker).
- **Adequate lighting** in front of you, not behind. Window
  light from the side, or a desk lamp aimed at your face.

### Microphone

- **Mute when not speaking** to suppress background noise.
  Unmute when about to speak.
- **Don't multi-task** — typing, eating, drinking are all
  audible.
- **Don't have a TV or radio on** in the same room.
- **Pets**: mute the door bell. If a dog barks, apologize
  briefly and continue.

### Speaking

- **Pause before speaking** — there's a 1-second latency on
  WebEx; let the previous speaker finish.
- **Speak slightly slower** than you would in person — the
  audio compression eats syllables.
- **Identify yourself when speaking**: "Your Honor, John Doe
  for Defendant — my response is..."

## Sharing exhibits on WebEx

Oregon judges generally **do not encourage** screen-sharing of
exhibits during routine motion hearings. The briefs and
working copies should cover it. If you need to show something:

- **Refer the court to the exhibit** by number and page:
  "Your Honor, please refer to Defendant's Exhibit 2 at page
  3 of the bill of sale."
- **The judge has the working copies** in front of them
  (chambers or on the bench's monitor) and can find the
  passage
- **For evidentiary hearings**, screen-sharing of exhibits is
  proper; the procedure is to share with the host (clerk) and
  reference the exhibit as you do in person

## Technical failures

### Audio fails

- **Stay calm** — text the JA via the meeting chat or send a
  brief WebEx message
- **Dial in by phone** as backup; the call-in number is in the
  WebEx invitation
- **Mute the WebEx video** when dialed in to prevent feedback;
  keep the video for visual presence

### Video fails

- **Keep audio open** — the hearing can proceed audio-only
- **Reconnect** if possible; the judge will pause
- **Apologize briefly** when reconnected: "I apologize, Your
  Honor — my video dropped"

### Whole connection drops

- **Try to rejoin** immediately
- **Call the JA's phone number** (look it up before the
  hearing)
- **If you cannot rejoin within a few minutes**, the court will
  decide whether to proceed or continue

The court understands tech issues; a recurring problem can be
grounds for continuance, especially for a pro se filer.

## Confidentiality and recording

- **Do not record the hearing yourself**. The court's record
  is what counts. Unauthorized recording can be a misdemeanor.
- **No screen-sharing of confidential materials** unless the
  hearing is sealed
- **Be aware who else is in the room with you** — opposing
  counsel can hear them in some configurations

## Differences between WebEx and Zoom (since people use both
words)

| Feature | WebEx (Oregon courts) | Zoom (common alternative) |
|---------|----------------------|----------------------------|
| Waiting room | Yes — clerk admits participants | Yes |
| Chat | Yes; visible to all unless private | Yes |
| Screen-share | Yes; host-controlled | Yes |
| Recording | Court-controlled only | Court-controlled only |
| Phone dial-in | Yes — standard call-in number | Yes |
| Background | Blur or virtual; check stability | Blur or virtual; more stable |

Substantively similar. The interface differs, but the etiquette
is identical.

## County-specific remote-hearing notes

### Multnomah

- WebEx is default for civil motions
- Connection info on the JA's confirmation email
- Each judge has a permanent WebEx room; the meeting ID is
  consistent
- Some judges use the courtroom's WebEx terminal rather than
  the meeting room; effectively the same

### Washington County (OR)

- WebEx or in-person — judge-dependent
- Civil Division's confirmation specifies the mode
- Connection info varies by judge

### Lane County

- Strong individual-judge variation
- Court Administrator's office can provide WebEx info for
  judges who do not have a JA-administered room

### Other counties

- Generally WebEx for routine motions
- Smaller counties may use Zoom or other platforms; check the
  specific court's standing order

## Pro se WebEx tips

- **Use a laptop or desktop**, not a phone, for video. Camera
  positioning and screen real-estate matter.
- **Practice with a friend** before the hearing — test audio,
  video, and screen-share if you plan to use it
- **Have a glass of water** at hand — your throat will dry up
  from nerves
- **Wear pants** even though you're on camera waist-up — some
  Oregon judges have a habit of asking participants to stand
  briefly (which is when the pajamas problem surfaces)

## After the hearing

- **Stay on the line until the judge dismisses everyone**
- **Mute and leave the WebEx room when the judge has
  closed the matter**
- **No post-hearing chat in the WebEx room** — take any
  conversation off-platform

## Quick reference

| Issue | Fix |
|-------|-----|
| Can't connect | Dial the call-in number; text/call the JA |
| Audio echoes | Use headphones; mute when not speaking |
| Background noise | Move to a quieter room; close windows |
| Camera too low | Prop laptop higher with books |
| Lighting too dark | Move a lamp in front of you; don't sit with window behind |
| Connection drops mid-argument | Reconnect immediately; apologize briefly |
| Don't have video capable device | Telephone-only is accepted by most Oregon judges |
