# Oral Argument — Oregon Civil Motion Practice

## The 10–20 minute reality

Most Oregon civil motion hearings allocate **10–20 minutes per
side**. Some judges set a strict time limit; others are
informal. The practical effect: you get to make 2–3 strong
points well, not 8 points lightly.

## Structure of effective argument

### 1. Opening (15–30 seconds)

Identify yourself, the case, the relief sought, and (optionally)
the single sentence summary of why the court should grant it.

```
Good morning, Your Honor. John Doe, pro se defendant, in Case
No. 25CV12345. The Court has Defendant's Motion to Compel
before it. We respectfully ask the Court to grant the motion
and order Plaintiff to produce responsive documents within 14
days, because Plaintiff alleges it owns this debt but has
refused to produce the documents proving that ownership.
```

That opening tells the judge: who you are, what case you're in,
what you want, and the one sentence the judge needs to remember.

### 2. The strongest point (2–4 minutes)

Your single best argument, made well. Cite the rule, cite the
record, and explain the application.

```
Plaintiff's Complaint, at paragraph 4, alleges that Plaintiff
"acquired" the account through "an assignment from Bank of
America." That allegation is what entitles Plaintiff to sue.
But when we asked for the documents proving that allegation —
the bill of sale, the assignment schedule, the cardholder
agreement that was assigned — Plaintiff objected on grounds of
"not in possession, custody, or control." ORCP 36 B(1) defines
"control" as the legal right to obtain. Plaintiff cannot
simultaneously allege it owns the account and claim it lacks
control over the documents proving ownership.
```

Notice the structure: claim → rule → application → conclusion.
Don't bury the conclusion at the end.

### 3. Anticipate the counter-argument (1–2 minutes)

What is the other side going to say, and why is it wrong?

```
Plaintiff will likely argue that the documents are held by
Bank of America, not Plaintiff. But Plaintiff's relationship
with Bank of America is contractual — the bulk sale agreement
gives Plaintiff the right to obtain account documentation, and
"control" under ORCP 36 includes contractual right of access.
The Court should not accept "not in possession" as an excuse
when Plaintiff voluntarily entered into a contract that gives
it access.
```

### 4. Field judge questions (varies)

The judge will have read the briefs. They will have questions.
You don't know in advance what they'll be, but common
categories:

- **Clarification**: "Just to be clear, are you asking for
  X or Y?"
- **Application**: "How does your argument apply if the bill
  of sale is from after the suit was filed?"
- **Pushback**: "Plaintiff cites *Smith v. Jones* for the
  contrary position — what's your response?"
- **Practical**: "If I grant this, how soon can production
  be done?"

For each, answer the question first, then explain.

### 5. Closing (15–30 seconds)

Restate the relief. Don't summarize the argument; just restate
what you want.

```
For these reasons, Your Honor, we respectfully ask the Court
to grant Defendant's Motion to Compel, order production within
14 days, and award reasonable expenses under ORCP 46 A(4)(a).
The Proposed Order is in the file. Thank you, Your Honor.
```

## Pacing

- **Speak slowly**. Pro se litigants tend to rush from nerves.
  Slow down — the court reporter (or the recording) needs to
  capture every word.
- **Pause between sentences** at transition points. Let the
  judge absorb each point.
- **Don't speed through citations**. Read the case name and
  paragraph numbers slowly so the court can take notes.

## What to bring to the lectern

- **A printed copy of your motion and any exhibits** the judge
  might want to discuss
- **Your outline** (one page, key points only)
- **A pen and paper** to take notes during opposing argument
- **A glass of water** (if the lectern has one) — pause to
  drink if your throat goes dry

## What NOT to do

- **Read your brief aloud** — wastes the only chance to speak
  to the judge directly
- **Argue with opposing counsel** — speak to the judge, not
  across the room
- **Speak over the judge** — never; the judge always wins
- **Get emotional or angry** — even when opposing counsel
  misrepresents the record, point it out calmly: "Counsel just
  characterized the bill of sale as proving ownership; the
  bill of sale is in the record as Exhibit 2, and the Court
  can see it does not name Defendant."
- **Make new arguments not in the briefs** — the rule of
  thumb is the brief is the argument; the hearing clarifies
  the brief
- **Make personal attacks** — "Plaintiff's counsel is lying"
  is almost always counter-productive

## Handling missteps

If you mis-state a citation or get a fact wrong:

- **Correct it immediately**: "I'm sorry, Your Honor —
  Exhibit 3 is the bill of sale, not Exhibit 2."
- **Don't fabricate**: never make up a fact or rule citation
  in real-time. If you don't know, say so.
- **Stay calm**: pro se litigants stumble all the time, and
  Oregon judges are accustomed to it. A graceful correction is
  better than pretending nothing happened.

## Hearing the bench's ruling

If the judge rules from the bench:

- **Listen carefully** — write down the specific words. The
  ruling becomes the order.
- **Note any modifications** to your proposed order — these
  go into the final order you submit
- **Ask clarifying questions if you don't understand** — but
  don't argue. "Your Honor, just to confirm: production within
  14 days?" is appropriate; "But Your Honor, that doesn't
  give me time to..." is arguing.

If the judge takes the matter under advisement:

- **Thank the court**
- **Ask when to expect a ruling** — "When might we expect the
  Court's ruling?"
- **Note any actions you need to take in the interim**

## Common pro se hearing dynamics

### When opposing counsel is hostile or interrupts

- Address the judge: "Your Honor, may I finish?" The judge will
  manage opposing counsel.
- Never engage in back-and-forth with opposing counsel directly.
  Always speak to the bench.

### When opposing counsel mis-states the record

- Point it out calmly with a record citation: "Counsel's
  characterization of paragraph 4 of the Declaration differs
  from what the Declaration actually says. The text is at page
  3 of Defendant's Declaration."

### When the judge is impatient or hostile

- Stay calm. Answer questions directly. Stick to your two or
  three points.
- Don't capitulate on substance, but acknowledge tone: "I
  understand the Court has concerns; let me address that
  point directly."

### When the judge seems sympathetic

- Don't over-press. If the judge is leaning your way, conclude
  cleanly and let the briefs do the rest.

## Final thoughts

A pro se litigant who appears prepared, professional, and
direct typically gets a fair hearing on the merits. Oregon
judges are generally patient with pro se filers who are
trying to follow the rules. The hearing is a chance to show
the court that you are.
