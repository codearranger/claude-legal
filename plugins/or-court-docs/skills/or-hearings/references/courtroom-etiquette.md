# Courtroom Etiquette — Oregon Civil Practice

## General formality level

Oregon civil courts are **moderately formal**. Less ceremonial
than some Eastern states; more formal than the most casual
California practice. The Multnomah Central Courthouse, post-
2020, has a less stuffy feel than older courthouses but the
substantive rules are unchanged.

## Standing and seating

- **Stand when the judge enters** the courtroom. Remain
  standing until the judge takes the bench and says "Be
  seated" or otherwise indicates.
- **Stand when speaking** to the court. The exception: at
  counsel table during housekeeping matters where everyone is
  seated; stand when arguing or introducing exhibits.
- **Sit when opposing counsel is arguing** so the judge can
  see them clearly. Take notes.
- **Stand when the judge leaves** the courtroom at the end of
  the hearing.

## Forms of address

| Person | Address |
|--------|---------|
| Circuit Court Judge | "Your Honor" |
| Circuit Court Pro Tem Judge | "Your Honor" |
| Magistrate (if present) | "Your Honor" or "Magistrate" |
| Court Clerk | "Madam Clerk" / "Mr. Clerk" / first name with permission |
| Opposing counsel | "Counsel" or "Mr./Ms. [Last Name]" |
| Opposing party (pro se) | "Mr./Ms. [Last Name]" or "Plaintiff" / "Defendant" |
| Witness | "Sir" / "Ma'am" or "Mr./Ms. [Last Name]" |
| Court Reporter | "Madam Reporter" / "Mr. Reporter" (rarely addressed directly) |
| Court Security / Bailiff | "Officer" or "Deputy" |

## Speaking conventions

- **One at a time**. Never interrupt the judge. Never speak
  while opposing counsel is speaking unless making a formal
  objection.
- **"Your Honor" at the start of any statement to the court**,
  especially when asking a question or making a request.
- **Refer to your client as "Defendant" or by last name**.
  Don't say "my client" repeatedly — say "Mr. Smith" or
  "Defendant Smith".
- **Refer to opposing party as "Plaintiff" or by last name**,
  not "the other side".

## Approach to the bench / lectern

- **Wait for the judge to call your matter** by case name or
  number. Do not approach the lectern before being recognized.
- **Approach the lectern, not the bench**. The bench is the
  judge's space; the lectern is yours. Stay at the lectern
  unless the judge invites you to approach.
- **Stand at the lectern** when speaking. If there is no
  lectern, stand at counsel table.

## Documents and exhibits

- **Have your packet ready** before your matter is called.
  Tabbed. Indexed if multi-page.
- **Hand documents to the clerk**, who hands them to the
  judge. Never hand documents directly to the judge.
- **Don't approach the bench** to show the judge a document
  unless invited.

## Cell phones, recording, and decorum

- **Cell phones on silent** — not vibrate, silent. Better,
  off.
- **No recording** without prior court permission. Many Oregon
  courts record proceedings; the official record is what
  counts.
- **No food or drink** in the courtroom (water at the lectern
  is sometimes OK; ask the clerk).
- **No hats** indoors except religious head coverings.
- **No reading newspapers, books, or other non-case
  materials** while waiting for your matter to be called.
- **Conversations** with anyone other than the court should be
  in a whisper or outside the courtroom.

## Dress code by setting

### Multnomah Central Courthouse — civil motion

Business or business-casual. The new building, post-2020, has
a slightly more casual feel than the old courthouse but the
expectation is still professional dress. A pro se litigant in
a button-up shirt and slacks (or business-equivalent attire
for any gender) is appropriate.

### Smaller / rural counties

Similar standard. Court dress matters less in a rural
courthouse than in Portland, but no jeans, no t-shirts.

### Evidentiary hearings, trials

Step up to suit-level dress. Trials are more formal than
motion hearings; juries and the record both benefit from a
litigant who looks the part.

### Remote (WebEx) hearings

Same standard, top half only (camera height). A collared
shirt is the minimum. Avoid bright patterns that cause moiré
on camera. Plain background or a conservative blurred
background.

## Specific etiquette rules by county

### Multnomah Circuit Court

- WebEx hearings are routine; no in-person formality reduction
  expected
- Working copies delivered to chambers (not handed to judge in
  court)
- Standing orders posted on each judge's page; review before
  hearing
- Each civil judge's individual style may vary — some are very
  formal, others more conversational

### Washington County Circuit Court (OR)

- Similar formality level to Multnomah
- Civil Division clerk's counter handles working-copy delivery
- Civil courtrooms are in the main courthouse building, with
  separate access for in-custody criminal matters

### Other counties

- Generally similar formality; rural counties may be slightly
  more conversational but always professional
- Pro tem judges in smaller counties may have a less formal
  manner; still address them as "Your Honor"

## When things go wrong

### You are late

- **Notify the court** immediately if possible (call the JA or
  the courtroom)
- **Apologize on arrival**: "Your Honor, I apologize for being
  late. [Brief explanation if relevant — traffic, technical
  issue.]"
- **Don't make excuses** beyond a single sentence

### You mis-state a fact or rule

- **Correct it immediately**: "Your Honor, I misspoke — the
  rule is ORCP 21 A(8), not ORCP 21 B."
- **Don't try to cover** — the correction is more credible
  than the cover-up

### Opposing counsel is rude or hostile

- **Address the judge, not opposing counsel**: "Your Honor,
  Counsel's comment is not responsive to my motion."
- **Don't escalate** — Oregon judges expect civility and will
  manage opposing counsel; reciprocating gets both sides
  warned
- **Document any pattern** for use later (sanctions motion if
  appropriate)

### You don't understand the judge's question

- **Ask for clarification**: "Your Honor, I want to be sure I
  understand the question. Are you asking whether [X] or
  whether [Y]?"
- **Don't guess** — answering the wrong question is worse
  than asking

### You're called by the wrong name or title

- **Don't make a big deal** of it. Quietly correct in passing:
  "Your Honor, just for the record, I am Defendant Doe — Mr.
  Smith is opposing counsel."

## Pro se litigants and the judge

Oregon judges are generally **patient with pro se filers** who
are trying to follow the rules. Pro se litigants who:

- Dress appropriately
- Are on time
- Address the court correctly
- Have an organized packet
- Answer questions directly
- Concede appropriate points
- Are concise

…get a fair hearing on the merits. Pro se litigants who do the
opposite (casually dressed, rude, late, scattered, evasive)
forfeit credibility on the substance.

The single biggest tell of a prepared pro se litigant is the
**organized packet**: tabbed exhibits, an outline, a printed
copy of the order they want. The judge sees this immediately
and adjusts expectations accordingly.

## Closing the hearing

- **Thank the court** at the end of your argument: "Thank you,
  Your Honor."
- **Stand when the judge leaves the bench** at the end of the
  hearing.
- **Collect your materials** quietly. Don't engage opposing
  counsel in the courtroom — take any post-hearing
  conversation outside.

## Common etiquette failures (Oregon flavor)

| Failure | Why it matters |
|---------|----------------|
| Sitting while addressing the court | Disrespect; some judges direct litigant to stand |
| Calling the judge "Judge" alone | Informal; mark of inexperience |
| Speaking over the judge | Always loses; signals disrespect |
| Arguing with opposing counsel directly | Distracts; judge will redirect |
| Texting during hearing | Visible; insulting to the court |
| Filming or recording without permission | Misdemeanor in some circumstances |
| Walking in late without acknowledgment | First impression; suggests case is not prioritized |
| Failing to stand when the judge enters | First-impression failure |
