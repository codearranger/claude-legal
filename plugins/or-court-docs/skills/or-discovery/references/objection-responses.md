# Discovery Response and Objection Drafting — Oregon

This reference covers drafting responses to discovery — both
when you serve responses (you're the responding party) and when
you need to evaluate the opposing party's responses (you're the
requesting party).

## Response format (ORCP 43 B, 45 B)

Each response should:

1. **Restate the request verbatim** (so the response is self-
   contained)
2. **State the objections** (if any), with specificity
3. **State the response** (after objections):
   - **Produce** — "Documents responsive to this request are
     produced as Bates Nos. ORLLC0001–0042"
   - **State no responsive documents** — "After reasonable
     search, [Party] has no documents responsive to this
     request in its possession, custody, or control"
   - **Identify the documents** — for RFPs, identify by Bates
     range, document description, and date; for RFAs, admit or
     deny

### Sample RFP response

```
RESPONSE TO REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION NO. 3:

Request: All documents constituting, referencing, or
summarizing the original cardholder agreement between
Defendant John Doe and Bank of America for account number
ending in XXXX, including signature pages, incorporated terms
and conditions, and any electronic acceptance records.

OBJECTIONS: Plaintiff objects on the following grounds:

(a) Burdensome — the Request requires Plaintiff to search the
    records of an entity (Bank of America) over which Plaintiff
    has no control. Documents not in Plaintiff's possession,
    custody, or control are beyond ORCP 43 A.

(b) Vague — "incorporated terms and conditions" and
    "electronic acceptance records" are not defined and
    encompass an unknown range of materials.

Subject to and without waiving these objections, Plaintiff
responds as follows:

Plaintiff has reasonably searched its records and produces
herewith a Bill of Sale dated March 14, 2024 (Bates No.
ORLLC0001) and an Assignment Schedule dated March 14, 2024
(Bates No. ORLLC0002). Plaintiff has no other documents
responsive to this Request in its possession, custody, or
control.

Plaintiff reserves the right to supplement upon further
investigation.
```

This response is well-formed because:

- It restates the request
- States objections with **specific grounds**
- Notes the objections without refusing to respond
- Identifies what was searched
- Identifies what was produced and what was not
- Reserves supplementation

A bare "Objection — burden. None produced." would be
insufficient under ORCP 43 / 46 — it invites a motion to compel.

## Objection library — when to use and when to avoid

### Relevance (ORCP 36 B(1))

**Use when**: the request seeks documents that have no
connection to any claim or defense in the case.

**Avoid when**: the documents have any plausible connection,
even tangential. Relevance under ORCP 36 B(1) is broad — "any
matter relevant to a claim or defense" — and courts construe it
liberally.

### Burden / overbroad

**Use when**: production would require disproportionate effort.
Must be **quantified**: number of documents, hours of review,
cost of vendor.

**Avoid when**: the burden is normal. "Burden" is
proportionality-based; a debt-buyer responding to RFPs in its
own case cannot complain about burden when responding to its
own allegations.

### Privilege (attorney-client; work product)

**Use when**: the document is protected. Provide a **privilege
log** identifying:

- Document author and recipient
- Date
- General subject matter
- Basis for the privilege

**Avoid when**: the document isn't actually privileged.
"Privilege" as a blanket objection without a log is
insufficient.

### Trade secret / confidential

**Use when**: production would disclose competitively sensitive
information. Couple with a request for a protective order under
ORCP 36 C.

**Avoid when**: the "trade secret" is generic business records.
Courts look skeptically at trade-secret claims over routine
documents.

### Not in possession, custody, or control

**Use when**: the documents are genuinely outside the
responding party's reach.

**Critical**: "control" means the legal right to obtain — even
if the documents are held by a third party (subsidiary, agent,
contractor, former employee). A debt buyer cannot claim it
"doesn't have" the original creditor's records if it has a
contractual right to obtain them.

### Vague / ambiguous

**Use when**: the request is genuinely unclear about scope.

**Avoid when**: the request uses defined terms or industry-
standard language. "Vague" is often a stalling objection — many
judges discount it.

### Premature

**Use when**: the case is not yet at the discovery stage (e.g.,
before responsive pleading is filed).

**Avoid when**: discovery has begun.

## When you're the requesting party — overcoming objections

When the opposing party serves the response above (or one with
similar pattern objections), the move-to-compel workflow is:

1. **Identify the specific objection** for each disputed
   request
2. **Meet and confer** — explain why the objection is invalid
   or how it can be addressed (narrower scope, protective order,
   etc.)
3. **Force them to commit** — if they're standing on the
   objection, document it; if they're willing to supplement,
   set a deadline
4. **Motion to compel** with mandatory fee-shifting under ORCP
   46 A(4)(a) if the meet-and-confer fails

### Substantial justification standard

The fees are "mandatory" except when the opposing party's
position was "substantially justified" — meaning more than
non-frivolous, but less than ultimately right. *See Phillips v.
Beaverton Aluminum Co.*, 314 Or App 257 (2021).

For an obvious-overreach objection (e.g., "relevance" objection
to a document that goes to the plaintiff's own allegations),
substantial justification is hard to find. Press for the fees.

## Supplemental responses

ORCP 43 B (and similar rules) require a party to **supplement**
a discovery response if:

- The party learns the response was incorrect or incomplete
  when made, or
- The party learns the response is now incorrect or incomplete

Failure to supplement supports an ORCP 46 motion and ORCP 17
sanctions if the failure was knowing.

When supplementing, follow the same format as the original
response, identifying it as a "Supplemental Response" and
identifying the new documents or new admissions.

## Request for admission responses

RFA responses are simpler — admit, deny, or state inability:

```
RESPONSE TO REQUEST FOR ADMISSION NO. 5:

Request: Admit that Plaintiff is a Delaware limited liability
company.

Response: Admitted.
```

Or:

```
RESPONSE TO REQUEST FOR ADMISSION NO. 7:

Request: Admit that the account ending in XXXX was opened by
John Doe on or about August 1, 2018.

Response: Denied. Plaintiff lacks information sufficient to
admit or deny because the account was originally with Bank of
America, and Plaintiff's records show only the post-charge-off
balance.
```

The "lacks information" answer is a permissible response under
ORCP 45 only if the party has made a reasonable inquiry and
the information cannot be obtained.

### RFA deemed admissions

If no response within 30 days (or 45 if served with summons),
the RFA is **deemed admitted** under ORCP 45 B. Calendar this
deadline immediately upon service.

To withdraw an admission, motion under ORCP 45 C — high bar.

## Privilege log format

When privilege is asserted, the log identifies (typically as a
table):

| # | Date | Author | Recipient(s) | Subject | Privilege |
|---|------|--------|--------------|---------|-----------|
| 1 | 4/1/24 | Atty Smith | Co. CEO | Litigation strategy | Atty-client |
| 2 | 4/2/24 | Co. CEO | Atty Smith | Response | Atty-client |

The log goes in a separate document filed with the response (or
served with the response). The point is to enable the
requesting party to evaluate the privilege claim — vague
"litigation matters" descriptions don't satisfy.

## Pro se response practice

Pro se respondents should:

- Not over-object — every unfounded objection invites a motion
  to compel
- Always produce what they have — the "limited to what's
  available" approach
- Be precise about scope of the search — "I searched my
  personal email archive for 2017–2024"
- Keep records of what was searched and what wasn't (for
  later defense against motion to compel)

Pro se requesters should:

- Use precise, bounded requests (date ranges, party-specific)
- Request a privilege log if "privilege" is asserted in the
  abstract
- Meet and confer in writing
- Move to compel when the opposing party stonewalls, citing
  ORCP 46 A(4)(a) for fees

## Common pitfalls

| Pitfall | Consequence |
|---------|-------------|
| Boilerplate objections without grounds | Court may overrule on motion |
| "Privilege" without a log | Log required; bare assertion insufficient |
| Failure to identify what was produced | Court may compel re-production with index |
| Not supplementing on new information | ORCP 46 motion + possible ORCP 17 sanctions |
| Missing RFA response deadline | Deemed admitted; ORCP 45 C withdrawal is hard |
| Compound RFA | Confusing response; harder to use at trial |
