# Idaho Rules of Evidence (I.R.E.) — map

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Navigational map only. Read the verbatim rule (the
> canonical isc.idaho.gov page, or `court-rules/` once populated) and confirm
> it is current before relying on it.

The Idaho Rules of Evidence are modeled on the Federal Rules of Evidence and
share the FRE's three-digit numbering. They apply in civil and criminal
proceedings in Idaho courts (with the usual exceptions for certain
preliminary and limited-jurisdiction proceedings — confirm).

## Relevance — Rules 401-403

- **Rule 401** — definition of relevant evidence (any tendency to make a
  material fact more or less probable).
- **Rule 402** — relevant evidence generally admissible; irrelevant evidence
  inadmissible.
- **Rule 403** — exclusion of relevant evidence when its **probative value
  is substantially outweighed** by unfair prejudice, confusion of the
  issues, misleading the jury, undue delay, waste of time, or needless
  presentation of cumulative evidence.

## Hearsay — Rules 801-807

- **Rule 801** — definitions; what is and is not hearsay (including
  party-opponent statements treated as non-hearsay).
- **Rule 802** — the hearsay rule (hearsay inadmissible unless an exception
  applies).
- **Rule 803 — exceptions where the availability of the declarant is
  immaterial.** The load-bearing exception for documentary civil practice is:
  - **Rule 803(6) — records of a regularly conducted activity (the
    "business records" exception).** A record is admissible if (a) it was
    made at or near the time by — or from information transmitted by —
    someone with knowledge; (b) it was kept in the course of a regularly
    conducted activity of a business or organization; (c) making the record
    was a regular practice of that activity; (d) these conditions are shown
    by the testimony of a **custodian or other qualified witness** *or* by a
    **certification** that complies with **Rule 902(11)**; and (e) the
    opponent does not show that the source of information or the
    circumstances indicate a **lack of trustworthiness**. This is the central
    admissibility hook in debt-buyer and account-records disputes.
  - Other commonly used 803 exceptions: present sense impression, excited
    utterance, then-existing state of mind, absence of a business record, and
    public records.
- **Rule 804** — exceptions that apply only when the declarant is unavailable
  (former testimony, statement against interest, etc.).
- **Rule 805** — hearsay within hearsay.
- **Rule 807** — the residual exception.

## Authentication and identification — Rules 901-902

- **Rule 901** — requirement of authentication: evidence sufficient to
  support a finding that the item is what the proponent claims, including
  testimony of a witness with knowledge, distinctive characteristics, and the
  like.
- **Rule 902 — self-authenticating evidence** (no extrinsic foundation
  needed). For civil document practice the key provision is:
  - **Rule 902(11) — certified domestic records of a regularly conducted
    activity.** A **custodian's (or other qualified person's) written
    certification** can satisfy the Rule 803(6) foundation **without a live
    foundation witness**, provided the proponent gives the adverse party
    **advance written notice** of the intent to offer the record and **makes
    the record and certification available for inspection** far enough in
    advance for the opponent to challenge them. Pair 902(11) with 803(6) when
    laying a paper foundation for account or transaction records.

## Laying a foundation — practical note

The 803(6) / 902(11) pairing is the standard path for admitting business and
account records without a live custodian. The certification must track the
803(6) elements (made at/near the time, by someone with knowledge, kept in
the regular course, regular practice), and the proponent must satisfy the
902(11) **pre-trial notice and inspection** condition. A defect in either the
certification content or the notice procedure is the usual ground for a
foundation objection.
