# Idaho Default and Set-Aside (I.R.C.P. 55, 60(b))

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Setting aside a default is a demanding,
> fact-specific remedy; verify the standards against current Idaho law
> before relying on them.

When a defendant fails to plead or otherwise defend within the **21-day**
answer window (I.R.C.P. 12(a)(1)(A)), the plaintiff can take a default and
then a default judgment. This file covers entry of default, default
judgment, and the two paths back. Pull verbatim text from
`../../id-law-references/references/court-rules/IRCP-civil-procedure.md`.

---

## Entry of default — I.R.C.P. 55(a)

When a party against whom affirmative relief is sought **fails to plead or
otherwise defend**, the plaintiff may apply for an **entry of default**.
Entry of default is a clerical/court step that precedes any default
judgment.

> ⚠ **An application for default is a hard signal to act now.** If you
> have not yet answered, **file the answer immediately** — a defendant who
> appears or files before default is entered is in a far stronger
> position than one moving to set aside afterward.

---

## Default judgment — I.R.C.P. 55(b)

After default is entered, the plaintiff may seek a **default judgment**.
Depending on the claim, the court may require proof of damages (e.g., a
hearing or affidavit/declaration establishing the amount). A default
judgment on an unverified or unproven debt amount is vulnerable on the
damages showing — see `id-consumer-debt`.

---

## Setting aside an *entry of default* — Rule 55(c): "good cause"

An **entry of default** (no judgment yet) is set aside on a showing of
**good cause** under **I.R.C.P. 55(c)** — a more forgiving standard than
vacating a judgment. Move promptly and explain why the default occurred.

---

## Setting aside a *default judgment* — Rule 60(b)

Once a **default judgment** has been entered, relief runs through
**I.R.C.P. 60(b)**, a demanding standard:

- **(b)(1)** mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or **excusable neglect** —
  the usual ground; a movant generally must also show a **meritorious
  defense** (a defense that, if proven, would change the outcome —
  e.g., statute of limitations, lack of standing / chain of title).
- **(b)(4) void judgment** — e.g., **defective service** depriving the
  court of jurisdiction. A void-judgment attack does **not** require the
  meritorious-defense showing and is not bound by the same fixed time
  window.
- Other grounds: **(b)(2)** newly discovered evidence; **(b)(3)** fraud /
  misrepresentation / misconduct; **(b)(5)** satisfied or no longer
  equitable; **(b)(6)** any other reason justifying relief.

> **Timing.** A Rule 60(b) motion must be brought within a **reasonable
> time**, and for grounds **(1)–(3)** no later than the **outer limit**
> the rule fixes (a set number of months after entry — confirm the
> current figure under **I.R.C.P. 60(c)** in the corpus). The void-
> judgment ground (b)(4) is not bound by that fixed window.

---

## Practical sequence for a pro se defendant

1. **Confirm posture** — entry of default only (Rule 55(c) good cause) vs.
   default *judgment* entered (Rule 60(b)).
2. **Move promptly** — delay erodes both standards.
3. **Attach a proposed answer** showing a meritorious defense, supported
   by an **affidavit or declaration** of the facts (e.g., why service
   failed, why the debt is barred). Draft with `id-draft-motion` and
   `id-draft-declaration`.
4. **Check service** — a void-for-defective-service attack (Rule 60(b)(4))
   may avoid the meritorious-defense burden.
5. **Calendar everything** with `id-deadlines`.

Full post-judgment vacatur and collection mechanics are in
`id-post-judgment`.
