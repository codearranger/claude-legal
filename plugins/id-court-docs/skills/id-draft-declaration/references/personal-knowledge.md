# Personal Knowledge and Admissibility — Idaho Affidavits and Declarations

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** This is drafting guidance. Confirm the current
> I.R.C.P. 56(c) text and the Idaho Rules of Evidence before relying on a
> sworn statement.

A sworn statement that supports or opposes a motion — and especially a
motion for summary judgment under **I.R.C.P. 56** — must satisfy the
content requirements of **I.R.C.P. 56(c)**, whether it takes the form of a
notarized Affidavit or an Idaho Code § 9-1406 Declaration. Verbatim rule
text: `../../id-law-references/references/court-rules/IRCP-civil-procedure.md`.

## The three I.R.C.P. 56(c) requirements

An Affidavit or Declaration used to support or oppose a motion must:

1. be **made on personal knowledge**;
2. **set out facts that would be admissible in evidence**; and
3. **show that the affiant or declarant is competent to testify** on the
   matters stated.

Keep each averment to facts the affiant/declarant could competently testify
to at trial. A sworn statement that buries hearsay, argument, or legal
conclusions inside a "personal knowledge" averment invites a motion to
strike.

## Laying the personal-knowledge foundation

Open the statement by establishing how the affiant knows the facts:

- "I am the [role] in this matter and personally [participated in /
  witnessed / received] the events described below."
- "In the regular course of my [business / personal recordkeeping], I
  maintain and am familiar with the records described herein."

## Information and belief

When a fact rests on **information and belief** rather than direct
knowledge, say so and give the basis:

> "Upon information and belief, [fact]. The basis for this belief is my
> review of [the account statement] attached as Exhibit B."

An information-and-belief averment may be insufficient to carry a fact at
summary judgment, where I.R.C.P. 56(c) demands personal knowledge.

## Sworn vs. argued — the cardinal rule

The sworn statement's job is to **state facts**; the motion and its
Memorandum argue the law (see `id-draft-motion`). Resist arguing in a sworn
statement ("Plaintiff's claim is barred by the statute of limitations") —
that conclusion belongs in the Memorandum, not the Affidavit/Declaration.

## Numbered paragraphs and exhibits

State each averment in a **separately numbered paragraph**, limited so far
as practicable to a single set of circumstances, so the Memorandum can cite
a precise paragraph as record support — e.g., ([Name] Aff. ¶ 3). When the
statement relies on a written instrument, **attach a copy as a labeled
exhibit** and refer to it by letter. Run `id-fact-check` to audit
sworn-vs-argued alignment before filing.
