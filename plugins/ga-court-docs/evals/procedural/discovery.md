# ga-discovery — O.C.G.A. § 9-11-26–37 + 50-interrogatory cap + USCR 5 period

## Prompt

I'm in a Georgia civil case and I want to send discovery to the debt
buyer suing me. How many interrogatories can I send, what other tools do
I have, how long do I have, and what happens if they don't respond?

## Expected triggers

- `ga-discovery`
- `ga-draft-motion`

## Acceptance criteria

### Interrogatory cap and toolkit

- [ ] States the **O.C.G.A. § 9-11-33** interrogatory limit (counting
      subparts) and reads the current cap from the references corpus
      rather than asserting the number from memory
- [ ] Identifies the full Georgia toolkit and governing sections:
      interrogatories (§ 9-11-33), requests for production (§ 9-11-34),
      requests for admission (§ 9-11-36 — **deemed admitted** if no timely
      response), depositions (§ 9-11-30), and the § 9-11-26(b) scope —
      reads each from the corpus

### Response time and discovery period

- [ ] States the **30-day response window** for written discovery (and
      the longer window when discovery is served with the summons) — reads
      the current counts from the corpus rather than asserting them
- [ ] Notes the **USCR 5 six-month discovery period** running from the
      filing of the answer — reads the period from the corpus

### Failure to respond — motion to compel

- [ ] Routes a non-response into a **motion to compel under
      O.C.G.A. § 9-11-37**, with a hearing set by **Rule Nisi** (USCR
      6.3/6.4), and notes the court may award expenses
- [ ] Notes RFAs **deemed admitted** can be withdrawn only under
      § 9-11-36(b)

## Common failure modes

- Asserts the interrogatory cap from memory (e.g., the federal 25)
  instead of reading the § 9-11-33 number from the corpus
- Misses that unanswered RFAs are deemed admitted under § 9-11-36
- Omits the USCR 5 six-month discovery-period clock
- Cites FRCP rules instead of the O.C.G.A. § 9-11 counterparts
