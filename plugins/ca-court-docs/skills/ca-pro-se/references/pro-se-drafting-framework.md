# The Pro-Se Drafting Framework — Adapted for California

The "pro-se drafting framework" is a drafting discipline for pro se litigants
focused on persuading a busy judge efficiently. It is not a court
rule — it is best practice that aligns with what California civil
judges actually respond to when reading pro se motions.

## The four principles

### 1. Front-load the strongest facts

The judge reads the first paragraph of every motion. Make it count.

**Bad opening (pro se debt-defense motion):**

> "This matter comes before the Court on Defendant's Motion to
> Compel. Plaintiff filed its Complaint on January 14, 2025,
> alleging breach of contract. Defendant answered on February 11,
> 2025. On March 3, 2025, Defendant served Requests for Production..."

**Good opening (same case):**

> "Plaintiff is a debt buyer that purchased Defendant's alleged
> account through an undated bulk assignment with no account-specific
> identifying information. When Defendant requested the chain of title
> under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.010, Plaintiff produced
> a single-page document titled 'Bill of Sale' that does not name
> Defendant, does not reference the account number, and bears no
> authenticated signature. This Motion seeks an order compelling
> Plaintiff to produce the documents that, if they exist, prove its
> standing to sue."

The good version puts the strongest fact (Plaintiff cannot prove
chain of title) in front of the judge in one paragraph. The
procedural history comes later in the Statement of Facts.

### 2. Keep motions concise

California Rules of Court, rule 3.1113(d) caps memoranda at **15
pages** for most motions (20 pages for summary judgment; 25 for an
opposition). Treat these as ceilings, not targets.

Techniques for staying concise:

- **Bold lead-ins** on each paragraph of argument: "**The documents
  are within the Code of Civil Procedure section 2017.010 scope.**"
- **One fact per sentence** in the statement of facts
- **Move narrative to the declaration** — the motion cites paragraphs
  in the declaration ("Doe Decl., ¶ 4")
- **Cut adverbs**: "clearly", "obviously", "plainly" do not persuade;
  they signal weakness
- **Table format** for routine facts (dates of service, response
  dates) — faster to read than prose

### 3. Write to the judge, not to opposing counsel

The judge will read both sides' filings. Opposing counsel knows the
case. Your audience is the judge, who is reading the file for the
first or second time.

Avoid:

- Ad hominem ("Plaintiff's counsel has repeatedly lied about the
  facts")
- Sarcasm ("Plaintiff apparently believes...")
- Argument by adjective ("Plaintiff's frivolous, baseless, bad-faith
  objections")

State facts instead. The court will draw the characterizations on
its own.

**Bad:** "Plaintiff's objections are baseless and frivolous."

**Good:** "Plaintiff objected on grounds of relevance, but the
request specifically targets documents Plaintiff is required to
authenticate under Evidence Code section 1400 to admit its primary
exhibits. (Doe Decl., ¶ 6; Ex. A, p. 3.)"

### 4. Let the record speak

Attach the documents that prove the point. Cite them by exhibit
letter and page. A well-indexed record persuades more than rhetoric.

- "Exhibit A at page 2" not "as the Court can see from Plaintiff's
  conduct"
- "Doe Decl., ¶ 4–7" not "as the timeline shows"
- "Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310" not "the discovery rules require"

## California-specific Parker tweaks

### Cite the code section on the first line

California judges expect the procedural authority on the first line
of the motion. "Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310,
subdivision (a), Defendant moves..." not "This Court has the power to
compel..."

### Use the California Style Manual citation format

| Code | Abbreviated form | Example |
|------|-----------------|---------|
| Code of Civil Procedure | Code Civ. Proc. | Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300 |
| California Rules of Court | CRC | CRC 3.1113(d) |
| Evidence Code | Evid. Code | Evid. Code, § 1400 |
| Government Code | Gov. Code | Gov. Code, § 68630 |
| Business and Professions Code | Bus. & Prof. Code | Bus. & Prof. Code, § 6125 |
| California Code of Regulations | Cal. Code Regs. | Cal. Code Regs., tit. 2, § 11024 |

Use the comma before the section symbol: "Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300"
not "Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.300".

BlueBook citations are appropriate in federal court, not California
Superior Court. Do not use "CCP § 2030.300" in headings or text
unless you are using the running-text abbreviated form.

### Don't say "12(b)(6)"

That's federal (FRCP 12(b)(6)). California's analog is **demurrer**
for failure to state a cause of action under **Code Civ. Proc.,
§ 430.10(e)**.

| Federal | California |
|---------|------------|
| Motion to dismiss (FRCP 12(b)(6)) | Demurrer (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(e)) |
| Summary judgment (FRCP 56) | Summary judgment (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c) |
| Motion to compel (FRCP 37) | Motion to compel further responses (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300, 2031.310, 2033.290) |
| Interrogatory limit (25) | Interrogatory limit (35 — Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.030) |
| 3 days for mail service | **5 days** for mail service (Code Civ. Proc., § 1013) |
| 30 days to respond to RFPs | **30 days** (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.260) |

### Points and authorities are required

Unlike some states where a brief can accompany a motion informally,
California Rules of Court, rule 3.1113(a) requires a memorandum of
points and authorities with every motion. A motion without P&A may be
denied on that basis alone. Keep it targeted — make the strongest 2-3
arguments in detail rather than 8 arguments superficially.

### Separate statement — when required

California requires a **Separate Statement** for two common motions:

1. **Summary judgment / summary adjudication** (CRC 3.1350): must
   contain each material fact claimed to be undisputed or disputed,
   with supporting evidence citation.
2. **Motion to compel further discovery responses** (CRC 3.1345):
   must contain each discovery request at issue, the response, the
   factual and legal reasons for compelling further response.

Omitting the required separate statement is a ground for denial.

### Tentative-ruling regime

California's tentative-ruling system is described in CRC 3.1308. The
judge posts a preliminary ruling by the day before the hearing (most
courts by 3:00 p.m.):

- If no party requests oral argument by 4:30 p.m. the day before,
  the tentative becomes the final order without a hearing.
- If you receive an adverse tentative, **you must request oral
  argument** — silence is acquiescence.

Check the court's website (or phone the clerk's line) for the
tentative. If you want oral argument regardless of the tentative,
indicate that in your papers ("Oral Argument Requested").

See `ca-hearings` and `ca-hearings/references/tentative-rulings.md`
for the full regime.

## Example motion structure — pro se California

```
SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA
COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES
CENTRAL DISTRICT

VELOCITY INVESTMENTS, LLC,           Case No. 25STCV12345

     Plaintiff,                      Dept. 34

     vs.                             DEFENDANT'S MOTION TO
                                     COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES
JOHN DOE,                            TO REQUESTS FOR
                                     PRODUCTION (CCP § 2031.310)
     Defendant.
                                     Hearing Date: June 10, 2025
                                     Time:         8:30 a.m.
                                     Dept.:        34

                                     [ORAL ARGUMENT REQUESTED]


                                  NOTICE

TO PLAINTIFF AND ITS ATTORNEYS OF RECORD:

PLEASE TAKE NOTICE that on June 10, 2025, at 8:30 a.m. in
Department 34 of the above-entitled court, located at 111 N.
Hill Street, Los Angeles, California 90012, Defendant John
Doe, In Pro Per, will and hereby does move this Court for an
order compelling Plaintiff to provide further responses to
Requests for Production, Set One, Nos. 1-6.

This Motion is made pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure
section 2031.310, on the grounds that Plaintiff's responses
consisted entirely of boilerplate objections without
substantive production or verified statements of inability
to comply. This Motion is based on the Memorandum of Points
and Authorities below, the Separate Statement filed herewith,
the Declaration of John Doe filed herewith, and the records
of this action.

              MEMORANDUM OF POINTS AND AUTHORITIES

I.  INTRODUCTION

    Plaintiff is a debt buyer that has filed suit claiming
    Defendant owes a credit card balance originally owed to
    Bank of America. Defendant disputes the debt. Defendant
    served Requests for Production targeting the chain of
    title from Bank of America to Plaintiff — the documents
    that, if they exist, prove Plaintiff's standing to sue.
    Plaintiff responded with form objections and produced
    nothing. This Motion asks the Court to order Plaintiff to
    either produce the documents or verify it cannot.

II. STATEMENT OF FACTS

    1.  Plaintiff filed this action on January 14, 2025,
        alleging Defendant owes $4,872.00 on a credit card
        account originally issued by Bank of America.
        (Compl., ¶ 1.)

    2.  Defendant answered on February 11, 2025, denying
        the existence of the debt and the chain of title.
        (Answer, ¶¶ 3-5.)

    3.  On April 1, 2025, Defendant served Requests for
        Production, Set One, Nos. 1-6. (Doe Decl., ¶ 2;
        Ex. A.)

    4.  On May 1, 2025, Plaintiff served responses
        objecting to all six requests. Plaintiff produced
        no documents. (Doe Decl., ¶ 3; Ex. B.)

    5.  On May 10, 2025, Defendant sent a meet-and-confer
        letter. (Doe Decl., ¶ 4; Ex. C.) Plaintiff did
        not supplement.

III. LEGAL STANDARD

     Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310,
     subdivision (a), a propounding party may move for
     an order compelling further responses if the
     responding party produces an incomplete response,
     an objection to the inspection demand, or a
     representation of inability to comply...

IV.  ARGUMENT

     A.  The documents are within the Code of Civil
         Procedure section 2017.010 discovery scope.

         [Argument here — 2-3 paragraphs, tight, record-
          cited]

     B.  Plaintiff's boilerplate objections are
         insufficient.

         [Argument here]

     C.  Monetary sanctions are mandatory under Code of
         Civil Procedure section 2023.030, subdivision (a).

         [Argument here — track time; request specific
          hourly rate and hours]

V.   CONCLUSION

     For the foregoing reasons, Defendant respectfully
     requests:

     1.  An order compelling Plaintiff to serve further
         responses to Requests for Production Nos. 1-6
         within 14 days;
     2.  Monetary sanctions against Plaintiff in the
         amount of $[X], representing [hours] hours at
         $[rate]/hour for preparation of this Motion.

DATED: May 27, 2025      Respectfully submitted,

                         ________________________________
                         JOHN DOE
                         In Pro Per
                         [Address]
                         [City, CA ZIP]
                         Tel: [Phone]
                         Email: [Email]
```

This is approximately 5-6 pages of memorandum with the separate
statement as a separate document. A judge can navigate the motion
in under 5 minutes. The same material in a freeform narrative
blooms to 12+ pages.

## What Parker doesn't tell you (California flavor)

- **Tentative rulings** are not in Parker but are the single most
  important procedural fact in California motion practice. Always
  check. Contest an adverse tentative or it becomes the order.
- **Separate statements** are required for SJ and discovery motions —
  not optional, not substitutable with a table in the motion.
- **Mandatory sanctions** under Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030 shift
  the economics of discovery disputes. Track time and fees from the
  first meet-and-confer letter; a fee petition may follow.
- **State Bar number** is required for licensed counsel but **omitted
  entirely** for In Pro Per litigants. A fake SBN number is a UPL
  issue under Bus. & Prof. Code, § 6125.
- **Unlimited vs. limited civil jurisdiction** changes which rules
  apply: unlimited civil (over $35,000, formerly $25,000) follows the
  full CCP discovery regime; limited civil ($35,000 or less) has
  procedural shortcuts (Code Civ. Proc., § 92 et seq.).
