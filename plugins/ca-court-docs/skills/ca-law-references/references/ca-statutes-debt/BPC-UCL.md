## Overview

The Unfair Competition Law (UCL), codified at Cal. Bus. & Prof.
Code §§ 17200-17210, is California's broadest consumer-
protection statute. Its core provision — § 17200 — prohibits
**any unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent business act or
practice** and any unfair, deceptive, untrue, or misleading
advertising.

The UCL is the workhorse of California consumer litigation
because:

1. The "unlawful" prong borrows almost any other statutory or
   common-law violation as a UCL predicate (FDCPA, Rosenthal,
   FDBPA, CDCLA, federal banking laws, common-law fraud, etc.).
2. The 4-year SOL (§ 17208) is significantly longer than the
   1-year SOL on most underlying federal/state consumer-
   protection claims.
3. The unfairness prong reaches conduct not specifically
   prohibited by other statutes.
4. The fraudulent prong applies an "objective" deception
   standard (likely to deceive a reasonable consumer) — easier
   than common-law fraud.

## § 17200 — UNFAIR COMPETITION DEFINED

**As used in this chapter, unfair competition shall mean and
include any unlawful, unfair or fraudulent business act or
practice and unfair, deceptive, untrue or misleading
advertising and any act prohibited by Chapter 1 (commencing
with Section 17500) of Part 3 of Division 7 of the Business
and Professions Code.**

### The three prongs

#### "Unlawful" prong

Borrows any other statutory or common-law violation. If the
defendant violated another statute (federal or state), that
violation is automatically a UCL violation.

**Examples of predicate violations**:
- Federal FDCPA violations
- Rosenthal Act violations
- FDBPA violations
- CDCLA non-licensure
- TCPA violations
- TILA violations
- Common-law fraud
- Breach of fiduciary duty
- Federal banking-law violations

#### "Unfair" prong

The standard is somewhat fluid. Two prevailing tests:

1. **"Tethered" test** (for competitors): Conduct is unfair if
   it threatens an incipient violation of antitrust law,
   violates the policy or spirit of antitrust law, or otherwise
   significantly threatens competition. *Cel-Tech Comm., Inc.
   v. Los Angeles Cellular Tel. Co.* (1999) 20 Cal.4th 163.

2. **"Balance" test** (for consumers): Conduct is unfair if the
   harm to the consumer outweighs the utility of the practice.
   *S. Bay Chevrolet v. General Motors Acceptance Corp.* (1999)
   72 Cal.App.4th 861.

The "unfair" prong reaches conduct that is not specifically
"unlawful" but is nevertheless oppressive or harmful.

#### "Fraudulent" prong

Conduct is fraudulent under the UCL if it is likely to deceive
the reasonable consumer. This is an OBJECTIVE standard, **NOT**
the higher common-law fraud standard requiring intent,
reliance, etc.

*In re Tobacco II Cases* (2009) 46 Cal.4th 298.

## § 17201 — Person defined

Any person, corporation, firm, partnership, joint stock
company, or any other association or organization.

## § 17203 — REMEDIES

**Any person who engages, has engaged, or proposes to engage
in unfair competition may be enjoined in any court of competent
jurisdiction. The court may make such orders or judgments,
including the appointment of a receiver, as may be necessary
to prevent the use or employment by any person of any practice
which constitutes unfair competition, as defined in this
chapter, or as may be necessary to restore to any person in
interest any money or property, real or personal, which may
have been acquired by means of such unfair competition.**

### Key remedy: restitution

UCL relief is **EQUITABLE**, not legal. Available remedies:

1. **Injunction** — prohibiting future violations
2. **Restitution** — return of money or property taken from
   the plaintiff (or class) via the unlawful practice

UCL does NOT provide:
- Compensatory damages (e.g., for emotional distress, lost
  wages)
- Punitive damages
- Attorney's fees (absent § 1021.5 or similar)

**Practical implication**: a UCL claim alone provides limited
monetary recovery. The typical strategy is to pair UCL with
another claim (FDCPA, Rosenthal, CLRA) that provides statutory
damages, and use UCL's longer SOL and broader scope to capture
additional categories of misconduct.

## § 17204 — STANDING

**Actions for any relief pursuant to this chapter shall be
prosecuted exclusively in a court of competent jurisdiction
by the Attorney General or a district attorney or by a county
counsel authorized by agreement with the district attorney in
actions involving violation of a county ordinance, or by a city
attorney of a city, or city and county, having a population in
excess of 750,000, or by a city prosecutor in any city having
a full-time city prosecutor with the consent of the district
attorney, or by a city attorney in any city and county or, with
the consent of the district attorney, by a city prosecutor in
any city having a full-time city prosecutor in the name of the
people of the State of California upon their own complaint or
upon the complaint of a board, officer, person, corporation,
or association, or by a person who has suffered injury in fact
and has lost money or property as a result of the unfair
competition.**

### Private standing requires injury + lost money/property

**Prop 64 (2004) restricted UCL standing**. To bring a private
UCL action, the plaintiff must allege and prove:

1. **Injury in fact** (Article III-style harm)
2. **Lost money or property** as a result of the unfair
   competition

*Kwikset Corp. v. Superior Court* (2011) 51 Cal.4th 310 —
leading case on Prop 64 standing requirements.

For consumer-debt defendants: paying any portion of an unlawful
debt (or even spending money on legal fees to defend) typically
satisfies the "lost money or property" requirement.

## § 17205 — Cumulative remedies

Unless otherwise expressly provided, the remedies or penalties
provided by this chapter are cumulative to each other and to
the remedies or penalties available under all other laws of
this state.

## § 17206 — Civil penalties (Attorney General / public prosecutor)

Up to **$2,500 per violation** in actions brought by the
Attorney General or local public prosecutors. **NOT available
in private actions.**

## § 17208 — STATUTE OF LIMITATIONS

**Any action to enforce any cause of action pursuant to this
chapter shall be commenced within four years after the cause
of action accrued. No cause of action barred under existing
law on the effective date of this section shall be revived by
its enactment.**

The 4-year SOL is one of the most powerful features of the
UCL. It allows plaintiffs to reach further back in time than
the 1-year SOL on FDCPA / Rosenthal / FDBPA, capturing a
larger universe of violations.

### Continuing-violation doctrine

For ongoing patterns of conduct (e.g., systematic FDCPA
violations), the SOL may run from the last violation rather
than the first. *Aryeh v. Canon Business Solutions, Inc.*
(2013) 55 Cal.4th 1185.

## False-Advertising Law (§ 17500 et seq.)

Cross-referenced because the UCL incorporates violations of
the False Advertising Law (FAL):

**§ 17500 — Prohibition**: It is unlawful for any person, firm,
corporation or association, or any employee thereof with intent
directly or indirectly to dispose of real or personal property
or to perform services, professional or otherwise, or anything
of any nature whatsoever or to induce the public to enter into
any obligation relating thereto, to make or disseminate or
cause to be made or disseminated before the public in this
state, or to make or disseminate or cause to be made or
disseminated from this state before the public in any state,
in any newspaper or other publication, or any advertising
device, or by public outcry or proclamation, or in any other
manner or means whatever, including over the Internet, any
statement, concerning that real or personal property or those
services, professional or otherwise, or concerning any
circumstance or matter of fact connected with the proposed
performance or disposition thereof, which is untrue or
misleading, and which is known, or which by the exercise of
reasonable care should be known, to be untrue or misleading,
or for any such person, firm, or corporation to so make or
disseminate or cause to be so made or disseminated any such
statement as part of a plan or scheme with the intent not to
sell that personal property or those services, professional or
otherwise, so advertised at the price stated therein, or as
so advertised.

A False Advertising Law violation is automatically a UCL
violation (via the "unlawful" prong AND via the UCL's express
incorporation in § 17200).

## Application to consumer-debt cases

### As a vehicle for cumulative recovery

A typical consumer-debt cross-complaint pleads UCL alongside
Rosenthal and FDCPA:

1. **Count 1 — Rosenthal Act** (statutory damages $100-$1,000;
   actual damages; attorney's fees)
2. **Count 2 — FDCPA** (statutory damages up to $1,000;
   actual damages; attorney's fees)
3. **Count 3 — UCL** (restitution; injunctive relief;
   longer SOL captures additional violations)

### As a vehicle for fee recovery via § 1021.5

UCL alone provides no fees, but **CCP § 1021.5** (private
attorney general doctrine) may award fees when:

- A significant benefit was conferred on the general public
  or a large class
- The necessity and financial burden of private enforcement
  outweigh the plaintiff's personal stake
- Fees should not be paid out of the recovery

UCL class actions often qualify for § 1021.5 fees.

### Restitution as the primary monetary remedy

In a UCL claim, monetary recovery is limited to RESTITUTION —
the return of money or property the defendant took from the
plaintiff. Calculations:

- **For unlawful payments**: the consumer's payments to the
  collector (potentially the entire balance paid)
- **For unlawful late fees / interest**: the unlawful portion
  only

## Quick-reference

| Feature | UCL |
|---|---|
| Prohibited conduct | "Unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent" business practices (§ 17200) |
| Remedies | Restitution + injunction (no damages; § 17203) |
| Civil penalties (AG only) | Up to $2,500/violation (§ 17206) |
| Standing | Injury + lost money/property (Prop 64; § 17204) |
| Attorney's fees | None (absent CCP § 1021.5) |
| SOL | 4 years (§ 17208) |
| Class action | Yes |
| Tagging with other claims | Yes — typically paired with Rosenthal, FDCPA, FDBPA, CLRA |
