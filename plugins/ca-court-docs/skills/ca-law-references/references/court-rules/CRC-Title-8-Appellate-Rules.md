## Overview

CRC Title 8 governs appeals in the California courts of appeal and the California Supreme Court. For civil practitioners, the most critical provisions are: the 60-day appeal clock (Rule 8.104), the prohibition on citing unpublished opinions (Rule 8.1115), the format requirements for appellate briefs (Rules 8.200–8.224), and the record-preparation rules (Rules 8.120–8.130). Title 8 also governs writs of mandate, prohibition, and review (Rules 8.480–8.498).

---

## Division 1 — General Rules

### Rule 8.10 — Application

**Summary.** Title 8 governs practice in the Supreme Court and courts of appeal. Cases from the superior court proceed directly to the court of appeal of the district in which the superior court is located; the Supreme Court has discretionary jurisdiction over court of appeal decisions.

---

## Division 2 — Taking the Appeal

### Rule 8.100 — Filing the Notice of Appeal

**Summary.** An appeal is commenced by filing a notice of appeal in the superior court within the time prescribed by Rule 8.104 (or 8.108, if extended). The notice must identify the judgment or order appealed from and the names of all appellants.

```
(a) Where filed.
    The notice of appeal must be filed in the superior court, not
    in the court of appeal.

(b) Contents.
    The notice of appeal must:
    (1) Identify the judgment or order being appealed;
    (2) State the name of each appellant;
    (3) State the name of the trial court and the case number.

(c) Premature notice.
    A notice of appeal filed before the judgment is entered is
    treated as filed immediately after entry of the judgment.
    (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 8.104(e).)
```

---

### Rule 8.104 — Time to Appeal (the 60-Day Clock)

**Summary.** The single most important appellate rule for civil practitioners. A notice of appeal must be filed within 60 days after service of a file-stamped copy of the judgment or order, or 60 days after a party serves a Notice of Entry of Judgment, or 180 days after entry of judgment — whichever is earliest. The deadline is jurisdictional; a late notice of appeal is dismissed.

```
(a) Normal deadline.
    Unless a statute or rule 8.108 provides otherwise, a notice of
    appeal must be filed on or before the earliest of:
    (1) 60 days after the superior court clerk serves on the party
    filing the notice of appeal a document entitled "Notice of
    Entry" of judgment or a file-stamped copy of the judgment;
    (2) 60 days after the party filing the notice of appeal serves
    or is served by a party with a document entitled "Notice of
    Entry" of judgment or a file-stamped copy of the judgment; or
    (3) 180 days after entry of judgment.

(b) "Entry" of judgment.
    Judgment is entered when the clerk enters it in the register of
    actions. (CCP § 664.5.)

(c) File-stamped copy.
    Service of a file-stamped copy of the judgment triggers the
    60-day period even without a formal "Notice of Entry."

(d) Jurisdictional.
    The deadline is jurisdictional. A court of appeal cannot extend
    it except under rule 8.108. A late notice of appeal must be
    dismissed.

(e) Mailing by clerk.
    If the clerk mails the file-stamped judgment, the 60-day period
    begins from the date of mailing, not receipt.
```

**Practice note.** Do not rely on the 180-day backstop. If opposing counsel serves a "Notice of Entry," the 60-day clock runs from that service. Mistake is the leading cause of missed appeal deadlines.

---

### Rule 8.108 — Extensions of Time to Appeal

**Summary.** The 60-day appeal deadline can be extended by timely filing of: (1) a motion for new trial, (2) a motion to vacate the judgment, (3) a motion for judgment notwithstanding the verdict (JNOV), or (4) a motion for reconsideration. When such a motion is timely filed, the time to appeal from the original judgment (or from the order on the post-trial motion) is extended until 30 days after the clerk serves notice of entry of the order on the post-trial motion.

```
(a) Extensions.
    If any party serves and files a valid notice of intention to move
    for a new trial, or a valid motion to vacate the judgment or to
    reconsider an appealed order, the time to appeal from the original
    judgment is extended as follows:

    (1) If the motion is denied (or deemed denied by inaction), the
    time to appeal the original judgment extends to the later of:
    (a) the original 60-day deadline (rule 8.104), or
    (b) 30 days after the clerk serves notice of entry of the
    order denying the motion (or the date the motion is deemed denied);

    (2) If the motion is granted and a new judgment or order is entered,
    the time to appeal from the new judgment runs from service of notice
    of entry of that new judgment.

(b) Motions for new trial.
    CCP § 660 provides that a motion for new trial is deemed denied
    by operation of law if not ruled upon within 60 days of notice of
    entry of judgment (or within 60 days of the filing of the first
    notice of intention to move for new trial, whichever is later).

(c) JNOV.
    A timely motion for judgment notwithstanding the verdict (JNOV)
    under CCP § 629 extends the time to appeal in the same manner
    as a motion for new trial.
```

## Division 3 — The Record on Appeal

### Rule 8.120 — Normal Record — Clerk's Transcript

**Summary.** The record on appeal normally includes a clerk's transcript (the papers and orders filed in the superior court) and a reporter's transcript (the oral proceedings). The appellant designates the record and bears the cost of preparation.

```
(a) Clerk's transcript — contents.
    The normal clerk's transcript includes the pleadings and the
    judgment or order appealed from. Additional documents must be
    designated by the appellant.

(b) Designation.
    Within 10 days after filing the notice of appeal, the appellant
    must serve and file a designation of the record.
```

---

### Rule 8.124 — Clerk's Transcript — Designation and Preparation

**Summary.** The appellant must designate the documents to be included in the clerk's transcript by filing a designation with the superior court clerk. The designation must list every document by name and date of filing.

```
(a) Contents of designation.
    The appellant must designate:
    (1) Every pleading to be included (complaint, answer, etc.);
    (2) Every motion and opposition relevant to the appeal;
    (3) The judgment or order appealed;
    (4) Any jury instructions given or refused (if relevant).

(b) Certification.
    After preparation, the clerk certifies the transcript and sends
    it to the court of appeal.

(c) Cost.
    The appellant pays the cost of the clerk's transcript unless the
    appellant is entitled to proceed in forma pauperis.
```

---

### Rule 8.130 — Reporter's Transcript — Designation and Preparation

**Summary.** When oral proceedings are at issue on appeal, the appellant must request a reporter's transcript and pay the reporter's fee. A reporter's transcript is required for claims of insufficiency of the evidence, erroneous jury instructions, or any issue turning on what was said in court.

```
(a) Designation.
    An appellant who intends to raise any issue that requires
    consideration of oral proceedings must request that the court
    reporter prepare a transcript of those proceedings.

(b) Deposit.
    The appellant must pay a deposit to the court reporter. The
    reporter may decline to prepare the transcript until the deposit
    is paid.

(c) Stipulated transcript.
    Parties may stipulate to an agreed statement or settled statement
    in lieu of a reporter's transcript (rule 8.134, 8.137).

(d) Consequence of failure.
    Failure to designate necessary portions of the reporter's
    transcript may result in the court presuming the omitted evidence
    supports the judgment.
```

---

### Rule 8.134 — Agreed Statement

**Summary.** The parties may prepare an agreed statement of the case — a summary of the material facts and proceedings in lieu of a full reporter's transcript — and submit it to the superior court for certification.

---

### Rule 8.137 — Settled Statement

**Summary.** If no reporter's transcript is available (e.g., the proceedings were not reported), the appellant may prepare a settled statement and submit it to the superior court for approval.

## Division 4 — Briefs

### Rule 8.200 — Briefs — Filing

**Summary.** In a civil appeal, the appellant files the opening brief, the respondent files the respondent's brief, and the appellant may file a reply brief. Optional petitions for rehearing and answers follow a different schedule.

```
(a) Sequence.
    (1) Appellant's opening brief: filed within 70 days after the
    record is filed in the court of appeal.
    (2) Respondent's brief: filed within 30 days after the
    appellant's opening brief is filed.
    (3) Appellant's reply brief: filed within 20 days after the
    respondent's brief is filed.
    (4) No further briefs without leave of court.
```

---

### Rule 8.204 — Briefs — Format

**Summary.** Appellate briefs must comply with specific format requirements: typeface, type size, margins, line spacing, and word count limits. Rule 8.204 is the appellate analog to CRC 2.100–2.119 for trial court documents.

```
(a) Format requirements.
    Briefs must comply with the following:
    (1) Font: proportional serif font (e.g., Times New Roman)
    or nonproportional (Courier); 13-point minimum for body text.
    (2) Line spacing: 1.5 or double.
    (3) Margins: 1.5 inches on left; 1 inch on right, top, and bottom.
    (4) No line numbering required.
    (5) Page numbers at the bottom of each page.

(b) Length limits.
    (1) Opening and respondent's briefs: no more than 14,000 words
    if produced on a computer, or 50 pages if typewritten.
    (2) Reply brief: no more than 7,000 words or 25 pages.

(c) Tables.
    Briefs must contain:
    (1) A table of contents;
    (2) A table of authorities;
    (3) A "Statement of Appealability" (if the appellant is raising
    a jurisdictional issue).

(d) Certificate of word count.
    If a word-count limit applies, the brief must include a certificate
    of word count, signed by the attorney, at the end of the brief.

(e) Covers.
    Briefs must have color-coded covers:
    (1) Appellant's opening brief: blue cover.
    (2) Respondent's brief: yellow cover.
    (3) Appellant's reply brief: tan (buff) cover.
    (4) Amicus curiae briefs: green cover.
    (5) All other briefs: white cover.
```

## Division 9 — Writs

### Rule 8.485 — Petition for Writ of Mandate or Prohibition

**Summary.** A party may seek a writ of mandate (CCP § 1085) or prohibition from the court of appeal, typically to challenge a non-appealable trial court order, to seek an immediate ruling on a pure question of law, or to challenge a discovery order. Petitions are filed directly in the court of appeal.

### Rule 8.498 — Writ — Expedited Briefing

**Summary.** Upon issuing an alternative writ or order to show cause, the court of appeal may set an expedited briefing schedule (often 5–15 days for each brief). Writs bypass the full appellate timeline.

## Division 11 — Unpublished Opinions

### Rule 8.1105 — Publication of Opinions

**Summary.** An opinion of the court of appeal is published (and thus citable as precedent) only if the court of appeal certifies it for publication, or if the Supreme Court orders it published. Most court of appeal opinions are unpublished.

---

### Rule 8.1115 — Citation of Unpublished Opinions — CRITICAL RULE

**Summary.** An unpublished court of appeal opinion may not be cited or relied upon in any California court proceeding, except in limited circumstances. This rule has no federal analog (Ninth Circuit rules differ). Citing an unpublished California opinion as precedent is an ethical violation and may result in sanctions.

```
(a) Prohibition.
    An opinion of a California Court of Appeal or superior court
    appellate division that is not certified for publication or
    ordered published must not be cited or relied on by a court or
    a party in any other action.

(b) Exceptions.
    An unpublished opinion may be cited or relied on when the opinion
    is relevant under the doctrines of law of the case, res judicata,
    or collateral estoppel.

(c) Citation of out-of-state or federal unpublished opinions.
    Rule 8.1115 applies only to California unpublished opinions.
    Unpublished opinions of federal courts and out-of-state courts
    may be cited subject to the rules of those courts.

(d) Citation of opinions not yet final.
    An opinion of a California court of appeal may be cited even if
    it has not yet become final, but its precedential value is
    subject to any subsequent modification or review.
```

**Practice note.** Before citing any California appellate decision, verify its publication status at Google Scholar, Westlaw, Lexis, or the California Courts website. A slip opinion or PDF found online may be unpublished. The prohibition is strict.

---

### Rule 8.1120 — Depublication

**Summary.** Any party or amicus may petition the Supreme Court to order a court of appeal opinion depublished (removed from the official reports). A depublished opinion may not be cited as authority.

```

  Normal deadline:     60 days from service of file-stamped judgment
                       OR Notice of Entry of Judgment

  Effect:              Jurisdictional — cannot be extended except
                       by Rule 8.108 post-trial motions

EXTENSIONS (Rule 8.108):
  Post-trial motion:   new trial / JNOV / vacate / reconsider
  Extended deadline:   30 days after notice of ruling on post-trial motion

BRIEFING (Rule 8.200):
  Opening brief:       70 days after record filed
  Respondent's brief:  30 days after opening brief
  Reply brief:         20 days after respondent's brief

UNPUBLISHED OPINIONS (Rule 8.1115):
  Rule:                California unpublished opinions may NOT be cited
                       Exceptions: law of case, res judicata, collateral estoppel only
```
