## Overview

CRC Title 3 is the primary source of procedural rules for California civil practice at the trial court level. It covers case management (Division 7), law and motion (Divisions 10–11), discovery motions (Division 19), ADR (Divisions 8–9), complex litigation (Division 3, part 4), and coordination (Division 4). For civil practitioners, Title 3 is consulted at every stage of litigation.

## Division 3 — Specific Proceedings

### Part 4 — Complex Cases (Rules 3.400–3.403)

#### Rule 3.400 — Complex Case Designation

**Summary.** A "complex case" is one that requires exceptional judicial management to avoid placing unnecessary burdens on the court or the litigants and requires more than one year to complete. Civil cases that are "complex" are assigned to a dedicated complex litigation program (if available in the court).

```
(a) Definition.
    A "complex case" is an action that requires exceptional judicial
    management to avoid placing unnecessary burdens on the court or the
    litigants and to expedite the case, keep costs reasonable, and
    promote effective decision making by the court, the parties, and
    counsel.

(b) Factors.
    In deciding whether an action is a complex case under (a), the
    court must consider whether the action is likely to involve:
    (1) Numerous pretrial motions raising difficult or novel legal
    issues that will be time-consuming to resolve;
    (2) Management of a large number of witnesses or a substantial
    amount of documentary evidence;
    (3) Management of a large number of separately represented parties;
    (4) Coordination with related actions pending in one or more courts
    in other counties, states, or countries, or in a federal court; or
    (5) Substantial postjudgment judicial supervision.

(c) Provisionally complex cases.
    The following categories of cases are presumptively complex unless
    the court finds they are not:
    (1) Antitrust or trade regulation claims;
    (2) Construction defect claims involving many parties or structures;
    (3) Securities claims or investment losses involving many parties;
    (4) Environmental or toxic tort claims involving many parties;
    (5) Claims involving mass torts;
    (6) Claims involving class actions.
```

#### Rule 3.401 — Assignment to Complex Litigation Program

**Summary.** When a case is designated as complex, it is assigned to the complex litigation program (if the court has one) or to a single judge for all pretrial and trial purposes. The assigned judge manages the case from designation through judgment.

#### Rule 3.402 — Procedure on Designation

**Summary.** The plaintiff must complete the civil case cover sheet (Form CM-010) and check the "complex" box if the case is provisionally complex. The defendant may counter-designate within 15 days of service of the complaint. The court then makes a determination.

```
(a) Plaintiff's designation.
    The plaintiff or cross-complainant must complete the civil case
    cover sheet (Judicial Council form CM-010) and indicate whether
    the case is a complex case.

(b) Defendant's counter-designation.
    Within 15 calendar days after service of the complaint, the
    defendant may file a counter-designation indicating whether the
    case is or is not complex. The defendant must use the civil case
    cover sheet.

(c) Court's determination.
    The court must determine whether the case is complex and notify
    the parties.
```

#### Rule 3.403 — Case Management in Complex Cases

**Summary.** In a complex case, the assigned judge typically holds an early case management conference and issues a case management order covering the schedule for discovery, law and motion, class certification (if applicable), and trial.

## Division 6 — Pretrial and Trial Management (Selected Rules)

### Rule 3.300 — Case Consolidation

**Summary.** A court may consolidate cases for trial or for all purposes when the cases involve common questions of law or fact. A motion to consolidate must be made in the first-filed case.

```
(a) Grounds.
    When actions involving a common question of law or fact are pending
    before the court, the court may:
    (1) Order a joint hearing or trial on any or all matters in issue
    in the actions;
    (2) Order the actions consolidated; or
    (3) Make such other orders concerning proceedings as may tend to
    avoid unnecessary costs or delay.
```

### Rule 3.350 — Amendment of Pleadings

**Summary.** Amendments to pleadings after the case management conference require leave of court on a noticed motion, except that parties may stipulate to amendments before trial.

### Rule 3.400 — Complex Case Designation

See Part 4 above.

## Division 7 — Case Management (Rules 3.700–3.740)

### Rule 3.700 — Purpose

**Summary.** The purpose of case management is to manage civil cases from filing to disposition, minimize delay, ensure proper allocation of court resources, and assist parties in resolving disputes efficiently.

### Rule 3.710 — Application

**Summary.** Case management rules apply to all general civil cases (unlimited and limited jurisdiction) unless a specific rule or local rule provides otherwise. Small claims and unlawful detainer cases follow different timelines.

### Rule 3.714 — Civil Case Cover Sheet

**Summary.** The plaintiff must file a civil case cover sheet (Form CM-010) at the time of filing the complaint. The cover sheet identifies the case type, complex designation, and cause of action.

```
(a) Requirement.
    At the time of filing the initial complaint or petition, the
    plaintiff or petitioner must file a civil case cover sheet on
    Judicial Council form CM-010.

(b) Contents.
    The cover sheet must indicate:
    (1) The case type (e.g., auto tort, PI/PD/WD, employment, contract);
    (2) Whether the case is complex;
    (3) The cause(s) of action.

(c) Cross-complaint.
    A party filing a cross-complaint that adds new parties to the
    action must file a civil case cover sheet addendum (Form CM-010A).
```

### Rule 3.720 — Case Management Conference — Setting

**Summary.** The court must set a case management conference (CMC) within 180 days after the filing of the complaint. The court clerk sends notice of the CMC to all parties.

```
(a) Setting.
    The court must set a case management conference not more than
    180 days from the filing of the complaint.

(b) Notice.
    The court clerk must mail or electronically serve notice of the
    case management conference on all parties at least 45 days
    before the conference date.
```

### Rule 3.722 — Case Management Statement

**Summary.** Each party must file and serve a case management statement (Form CM-110) at least 15 calendar days before the CMC. The statement addresses: service of process, discovery plan, ADR, trial date estimate, and any unusual procedural issues.

```
(a) Filing deadline.
    Each party must file a case management statement (Judicial
    Council form CM-110) no later than 15 calendar days before
    the date set for the case management conference.

(b) Content.
    The case management statement must address:
    (1) The status of service on all named parties;
    (2) The status of any cross-complaints;
    (3) The parties' views on alternative dispute resolution;
    (4) A discovery plan;
    (5) An estimate of the length of trial;
    (6) Whether the case is suitable for an early settlement conference;
    (7) Any pending or anticipated motions;
    (8) Any issues of complex litigation.

(c) Joint statement.
    Parties may file a joint case management statement.
```

### Rule 3.724 — Case Management Conference — Proceedings

**Summary.** At the CMC, the court reviews the case management statements, addresses any case management issues, sets a trial date, and may set a schedule for discovery and motions.

### Rule 3.727 — Trial Setting Order

**Summary.** After the CMC, the court issues a trial setting order (also called a "case management order") specifying the trial date, trial readiness conference date (if any), and key pretrial deadlines.

### Rule 3.728 — Continuance of CMC

**Summary.** A CMC may be continued for good cause; continuances are disfavored and will not be granted solely for convenience.

### Rule 3.730 — Subsequent Case Management Conferences

**Summary.** The court may schedule subsequent CMCs or status conferences as needed to monitor the case's progress toward trial. Parties must update their case management statements at each subsequent CMC.

### Rule 3.740 — Mandatory Settlement Conference

**Summary.** Courts may require parties to attend a mandatory settlement conference before trial. Settlement conferences are generally conducted by a judge other than the trial judge. Rule 3.740 and Local Rules govern the timing and content of pre-trial settlement conferences.

## Division 8 — ADR (Alternative Dispute Resolution)

### Rule 3.850 — Definitions

**Summary.** Defines terms for court-connected ADR: "mediation," "neutral evaluation," "arbitration," and "settlement conference." Court-connected ADR is voluntary unless ordered or required by local rule or statute.

### Rule 3.851 — General Requirements

**Summary.** Courts must make information about court-connected ADR available to all civil litigants and must include ADR information with the first notice sent to parties.

### Rule 3.869 — Mediation Confidentiality

**Summary.** All communications in a mediation are confidential under Cal. Evid. Code §§ 1115–1128 (the mediation confidentiality statute). This rule incorporates that statutory scheme.

**Cross-reference:** Cal. Evid. Code § 1119 (written and oral communications in mediation are not discoverable or admissible); § 1121 (mediator not compellable to testify).

### Rule 3.891 — Judicial Arbitration

**Summary.** Judicial arbitration (non-binding, unless both parties accept the award) applies in unlimited civil cases with amounts in dispute of $50,000 or less per plaintiff, unless a party requests trial de novo within 30 days of the award.

```
(a) Scope.
    Judicial arbitration applies to civil cases in the unlimited
    jurisdiction category if the amount in controversy is $50,000
    or less per plaintiff and the case is not exempt under statute.

(b) Award.
    The arbitration award is not binding unless:
    (1) Both parties accept the award in writing; or
    (2) Neither party requests a trial de novo within 30 days
    after service of the arbitration award.

(c) Trial de novo.
    A party may request a trial de novo within 30 days after
    service of the arbitration award by filing a request with
    the court. The requesting party pays the arbitration fee
    unless the trial verdict exceeds the arbitration award by
    more than 10 percent.
```

## Division 10 — Discovery (Rules 3.1000–3.1020)

### Rule 3.1000 — Discovery Motions — Meet and Confer Requirement

**Summary.** Before filing any motion to compel, a party must meet and confer with opposing counsel in good faith and attempt to resolve the dispute informally. The moving party's declaration must describe the meet-and-confer efforts.

```
(a) Requirement.
    A motion to compel must be accompanied by a declaration showing
    the moving party made a reasonable and good-faith attempt at
    informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.

(b) Informal Discovery Conference.
    Some courts (e.g., OCSC, LASC in certain departments) require an
    Informal Discovery Conference (IDC) before a motion to compel
    may be filed. Check local rules.
```

### Rule 3.1010 — Depositions by Remote Means

**Summary.** A deponent may be deposed by telephone or other remote electronic means if all parties consent or if the court so orders on noticed motion. The officer administering the oath must be physically present with the deponent or located in a state that allows remote administration of oaths.

### Rule 3.1020 — Discovery Motions — Format

**Summary.** Motions related to discovery must comply with the motion format requirements of Division 11, including the separate statement requirement (Rule 3.1345) for motions to compel further responses. The separate statement is mandatory; failure to file one is grounds for denial.

## Division 11 — Law and Motion (Rules 3.1100–3.1390)

### Rule 3.1100 — Application

**Summary.** Division 11 applies to all written motions in civil cases filed in the superior court. "Motion" means a request for a court order; it includes demurrers, motions to strike, motions for summary judgment/adjudication, and other dispositive or nondispositive motions.

---

### Rule 3.1110 — Motions — Required Components

**Summary.** A motion in California superior court consists of (1) a notice of motion identifying the relief sought, (2) a memorandum of points and authorities (required unless the motion is too simple to require one), (3) supporting declarations, and (4) a proposed order.

```
(a) Required papers.
    Except as otherwise provided, a motion must consist of:
    (1) A notice of motion;
    (2) A memorandum of points and authorities;
    (3) Supporting evidence (declarations, exhibits) as appropriate;
    (4) A [Proposed] Order.

(b) Notice of motion.
    The notice of motion must state:
    (1) The name of the party making the motion;
    (2) The grounds on which the motion is made;
    (3) The type of relief sought;
    (4) The date, time, and place of the hearing.

(c) Proposed order.
    A proposed order is required for:
    (1) Motions that seek a court order (as opposed to merely seeking
    a ruling on an issue of law);
    (2) Demurrers with leave to amend;
    (3) Motions to compel.
```

---

### Rule 3.1112 — Motion Papers — Format

**Summary.** Motion papers (the notice of motion, memorandum, declarations, and exhibits) must comply with CRC 2.100–2.119 (format). The memorandum may not exceed the page limits set in Rule 3.1113.

---

### Rule 3.1113 — Memorandum of Points and Authorities — Page Limits

**Summary.** A memorandum of points and authorities in support of or in opposition to a motion may not exceed 15 pages, unless a different limit is specified for a particular type of motion or the court grants permission to exceed the limit. This is one of the most-cited motion rules.

```
(a) Page limit — supporting memorandum.
    Except as otherwise provided in this rule, a memorandum in
    support of or in opposition to a motion must not exceed 15 pages.

(b) Reply memorandum.
    A reply memorandum must not exceed 10 pages.

(c) Summary judgment / adjudication.
    A memorandum in support of or in opposition to a motion for
    summary judgment or summary adjudication must not exceed 20 pages.

(d) Demurrer.
    A memorandum in support of or in opposition to a demurrer must
    not exceed 15 pages.

(e) Complex litigation.
    In complex cases designated under rule 3.400, the court may
    set different page limits by court order.

(f) Application to exceed.
    A party that wants to file a memorandum exceeding the page limit
    must file an application for permission to file an oversized
    memorandum. The application must set forth the reasons why
    additional pages are necessary. The court may grant the
    application before the hearing on the motion.

(g) Attachments.
    Exhibits attached to a declaration are not counted toward the
    page limit.
```

**Practice note.** The 15-page limit is frequently forgotten. Courts may strike an over-length memorandum on motion or sua sponte.

---

### Rule 3.1115 — Motion Papers — Continuance

**Summary.** A party may seek a continuance of a motion hearing by noticed motion or ex parte application. Good cause must be shown. Continuances of law and motion matters are not favored.

---

### Rule 3.1300 — Filing Deadline for Motion Papers

**Summary.** Motion papers must be filed and served at least 16 court days before the hearing date (the "16-court-day rule"). Opposition must be filed 9 court days before; reply must be filed 5 court days before. When service is by mail, the notice period is extended by 5 calendar days.

```
(a) Notice period.
    Unless otherwise ordered or specifically provided by law, all
    moving papers in a noticed motion must be served and filed
    at least 16 court days before the hearing.

(b) Opposition.
    All papers opposing a motion must be served and filed at
    least 9 court days before the hearing.

(c) Reply.
    Any reply to the opposition must be served and filed at
    least 5 court days before the hearing.

(d) Extension for mail service.
    If the moving papers are served by mail, the 16-court-day
    period is extended by 5 calendar days. If served by fax or
    overnight delivery, the period is extended by 2 court days.
    (CCP § 1005(b).)

(e) Extension for electronic service.
    If the moving papers are served electronically, the period
    is extended by 2 court days. (CCP § 1010.6(a)(3)(B).)
```

**Practice note.** Count backward from the hearing date: day of hearing is day 0; reply due day 5 (not counting the hearing day); opposition due day 9; motion filed and served day 16. Count court days (weekdays excluding judicial holidays). Mail service adds 5 calendar days to each period.

### Rule 3.1302 — Ex Parte Applications

**Summary.** A party seeking an emergency order (ex parte) must give notice to all parties at least one court day before the ex parte hearing, except in rare circumstances permitting no notice. Notice must be given by 10:00 a.m. the court day before the hearing in most courts. The application must show immediate, irreparable harm.

```
(a) Notice.
    An applicant for an ex parte order must notify all parties no
    later than 10:00 a.m. the court day before the ex parte hearing,
    unless the court for good cause dispenses with the notice.

(b) Content.
    The ex parte application must include:
    (1) A declaration showing the nature of the emergency, the
    harm that would result if the order is not issued ex parte,
    and why a noticed motion is inadequate to address the harm;
    (2) A declaration showing notice was given or, if not given,
    the reasons for non-notice;
    (3) The proposed order.
```

---

### Rule 3.1306 — Oral Argument

**Summary.** On written notice to the court and opposing parties before the hearing, a party may request oral argument. In courts using a tentative ruling system, if no party requests oral argument by the deadline set by the court, the tentative ruling becomes the order of the court.

---

### Rule 3.1308 — Tentative Rulings

**Summary.** Courts using a tentative ruling system post their tentative decisions on each law and motion matter before the hearing. Any party may appear and argue; if no party requests argument by the specified deadline, the tentative ruling becomes the final order without a hearing. This is one of the most important procedural rules for California civil practitioners.

```
(a) Posting.
    A court using a tentative ruling system must post its tentative
    rulings on the internet or in the courthouse by 3:00 p.m. the
    court day before the hearing.

(b) Requesting oral argument.
    A party that wants oral argument must notify the court and all
    other parties no later than 4:00 p.m. the court day before the
    hearing (or by such other time as specified by local rule).

(c) No request — ruling becomes final.
    If no party timely requests oral argument, the tentative ruling
    becomes the final order of the court. The prevailing party must
    prepare a formal written order if one is required.

(d) Oral argument requested.
    If oral argument is requested, the matter is argued at the
    scheduled hearing time, and the court issues its ruling from the
    bench or takes the matter under submission.
```

**Practice note.** Most large California superior courts (LASC, SFSC, OCSC, SDSC, Santa Clara) have tentative ruling systems. Check the court's local rules and the department's posted procedures for the specific deadline for requesting argument (varies by court — 4:00 p.m. is common but not universal) and the URL for posting tentative rulings.

### Rule 3.1320 — Demurrers

**Summary.** A demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of a pleading. CCP § 430.10 lists the grounds; the procedure is governed by CRC 3.1320 and CCP §§ 430.10–430.80.

```
(a) Grounds.
    A demurrer may be filed on any of the grounds in CCP § 430.10,
    including:
    (1) The court lacks subject matter jurisdiction;
    (2) The party lacks capacity;
    (3) The pleading fails to state a cause of action;
    (4) The claim is uncertain, ambiguous, or unintelligible;
    (5) The action is barred by the statute of limitations.

(b) Time to demur.
    A defendant must demur or answer within 30 days after service
    of the complaint, unless a different period is set by stipulation
    or court order. (CCP § 412.20.)

(c) Meet and confer required.
    Before filing a demurrer, the demurring party must meet and
    confer with the party who filed the pleading. (CCP § 430.41.)

(d) Meet-and-confer declaration.
    The demurrer must be accompanied by a declaration showing
    the meet-and-confer effort. If the parties were unable to
    meet and confer, the court may order further meet-and-confer.

(e) Ruling.
    If a demurrer is sustained, the court must allow leave to amend
    unless the pleading cannot be cured. If overruled, the defendant
    must answer within 10 days.
```

**Cross-reference:** CCP §§ 430.10–430.80; CRC 3.1320(b) (specific timing rules).

---

### Rule 3.1322 — Motions to Strike

**Summary.** A motion to strike targets specific language in a pleading that is irrelevant, false, or improper. The procedure follows CCP §§ 435–437, with the motion filed within the same time as a demurrer.

```
(a) Grounds.
    A motion to strike may be filed under CCP § 435 to strike:
    (1) Irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in a pleading;
    (2) A pleading not drawn in conformity with the laws of this
    state, a court rule, or an order of the court.

(b) Timing.
    A motion to strike must be filed within the same period as a
    demurrer to the pleading. (CCP § 435.)
```

---

### Rule 3.1323 — Motions to Strike — Anti-SLAPP

**Summary.** An anti-SLAPP motion to strike under CCP § 425.16 must be filed within 60 days of service of the complaint. Discovery is stayed once an anti-SLAPP motion is filed, until the court rules on the motion.

**Cross-reference:** CCP § 425.16 (the anti-SLAPP statute — see CCP-Procedure-Survey.md for detail).

---

### Rule 3.1324 — Amendment Before Trial

**Summary.** Amendments to pleadings after the case management conference require a noticed motion for leave to amend, accompanied by a copy of the proposed amendment. The court considers prejudice to the opposing party, the moving party's diligence, and whether the amendment is futile.

```
(a) Motion required.
    A party must obtain the court's permission, by noticed motion,
    before amending a pleading after the case management conference.

(b) Declaration.
    The motion must be accompanied by a declaration:
    (1) Identifying the proposed amendment;
    (2) Specifying when the facts giving rise to the amendment
    were discovered;
    (3) Explaining the delay (if any) in seeking amendment.

(c) Copy of amendment.
    The motion must include a copy of the proposed amended pleading
    as an exhibit.
```

---

### Rule 3.1325 — Motions — Continuance

**Summary.** See Rule 3.1115. Motions for continuance of trial must be filed separately from motions relating to law and motion matters and must comply with CRC 3.1332.

---

### Rule 3.1332 — Trial Continuances

**Summary.** Continuances of trial dates are disfavored and will be granted only on a showing of good cause. The court considers: proximity to trial, hardship to the parties, prior continuances, and court calendar congestion.

```
(a) Policy.
    Trial dates are firm. Continuances are disfavored unless there
    is an affirmative showing of good cause.

(b) Good cause factors.
    The court may consider, among other factors:
    (1) Proximity to trial date;
    (2) Unavailability of trial counsel due to illness or emergency;
    (3) Unavailability of a party or essential witness;
    (4) Substitution of counsel shortly before trial date;
    (5) Significant unresolved discovery.

(c) Stipulations.
    A stipulation of counsel is not sufficient by itself to establish
    good cause for a trial continuance.
```

### Rule 3.1345 — Separate Statement — Generally

**Summary.** A separate statement is required for motions to compel further discovery responses. It is a standalone document that, for each discovery request at issue, sets out verbatim: (1) the request, (2) the response, and (3) the reasons why the response is deficient. Without a separate statement, a motion to compel further responses will be denied.

```
(a) Requirement.
    Any motion involving the content of a discovery request or the
    responses to such a request must be accompanied by a separate
    statement.

(b) Format.
    The separate statement must provide:
    (1) The text of each request, interrogatory, question, or
    inspection demand at issue, verbatim;
    (2) The text of each response, answer, or objection to that
    request, verbatim;
    (3) A statement of the factual and legal reasons why the
    court should compel a further response.

(c) Applicable motions.
    The separate statement requirement applies to motions to:
    (1) Compel further responses to interrogatories (CCP § 2030.300);
    (2) Compel further responses to requests for production
    (CCP § 2031.310);
    (3) Compel further responses to requests for admission
    (CCP § 2033.290);
    (4) Compel further responses to deposition questions or
    deposition notice demands;
    (5) Compel or quash a subpoena.

(d) Exception.
    A separate statement is not required for a motion to compel
    initial responses (not "further" responses).
```

**Practice note.** The separate statement is frequently the longest document in a discovery motion. Budget time accordingly; a complex RFP motion may require a 30–100 page separate statement.

### Rule 3.1350 — Motion for Summary Judgment or Summary Adjudication — Separate Statement

**Summary.** A motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication must include a separate statement of undisputed material facts. Each fact must be separately stated and supported by evidence in the record. The opposing party must respond to each fact with a reference to admissible evidence showing a dispute. This is the mandatory separate statement for summary judgment.

```
(a) Supporting separate statement.
    A motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication must include
    a separate statement setting forth plainly and concisely all
    material facts that the moving party contends are undisputed.
    Each fact must be followed immediately by a reference to the
    supporting evidence.

(b) Opposing separate statement.
    The opposing party must file a responding separate statement that:
    (1) Responds to each fact in the moving party's statement; and
    (2) Identifies any additional material facts that the opposing
    party contends are undisputed, each followed by a reference
    to supporting evidence.

(c) Format of fact statements.
    Each material fact must be stated in a single sentence or short
    paragraph. The supporting evidence must be identified precisely
    (e.g., "Declaration of Jane Smith, ¶ 5"; "Exhibit A, page 3,
    lines 10–15").

(d) Consequence of failure.
    Failure to file the required separate statements may result in
    denial of the motion or, for the opposing party, in the facts
    of the moving party's statement being deemed undisputed.
```

**Cross-reference:** CCP § 437c; CRC 3.1350(b) (opposing statement); CRC 3.1354 (objections to evidence).

---

### Rule 3.1351 — Summary Judgment — Notice Period

**Summary.** A motion for summary judgment must be filed and served at least 75 days before the hearing date (by CCP § 437c(a)). If served by mail, add 5 calendar days; by fax or overnight delivery, add 2 court days; by electronic means, add 2 court days. This 75-day notice period applies only to summary judgment, not ordinary motions.

**Cross-reference:** CCP § 437c(a) (the statutory 75-day notice rule).

---

### Rule 3.1354 — Objections to Evidence — Summary Judgment

**Summary.** Evidentiary objections to declarations and exhibits submitted in support of or opposition to a summary judgment motion must be filed in writing, in a specific format. Objections must be filed with the opposition or reply; belated objections may be disregarded.

```
(a) Written objections.
    A party may not orally object to evidence submitted in connection
    with a summary judgment motion. All objections must be in writing.

(b) Format.
    Written objections must:
    (1) Identify each item of evidence to which an objection is made;
    (2) State the specific ground(s) for each objection;
    (3) Be filed and served along with the opposition papers
    (for opposition evidence: filed with the reply).

(c) Ruling.
    The court is not required to rule expressly on each objection;
    unruled-upon objections are preserved for appeal. Failure to
    object in writing waives the objection.
```

---

### Rule 3.1362 — Motions to Be Relieved as Counsel

**Summary.** An attorney seeking to withdraw from representation must file: (1) a motion to be relieved as counsel (MC-051), (2) a declaration (MC-052), and (3) a proposed order (MC-053). Service must be made on the client and all other parties.

---

### Rule 3.1370 — Motions to Seal Records

**Summary.** A party seeking to file a document under seal must file a motion or application, supported by a specific factual showing that sealing is necessary to protect a specific overriding interest (First Amendment balancing applies under California Rules of Court Rules 2.550–2.551).

**Cross-reference:** CRC Rules 2.550–2.551 (the substantive sealing standards).

---

### Rule 3.1382 — Motions in Limine

**Summary.** Motions in limine are typically filed immediately before trial and seek advance rulings on the admissibility of evidence. CRC does not set specific page limits for motions in limine; local rules or the trial judge's procedures govern.

---

### Rule 3.1390 — Trial Preparation

**Summary.** Before trial, parties must comply with the court's trial setting order and any standing orders regarding exchange of witness lists, exhibit lists, jury instructions, and trial briefs.

NOTICE PERIODS (Rule 3.1300):
  Motion filed/served:  16 court days before hearing
  Opposition due:        9 court days before hearing
  Reply due:             5 court days before hearing
  Mail service adds:     5 calendar days to each period
  Electronic service:    2 court days to each period

SUMMARY JUDGMENT (CCP § 437c; CRC 3.1350):
  Notice period:         75 days before hearing
  Separate statement:    required (supporting + opposing)
  Memo page limit:       20 pages (CRC 3.1113(c))

TENTATIVE RULINGS (Rule 3.1308):
  Posted by:             3:00 p.m. court day before hearing
  Argument request:      by 4:00 p.m. court day before (varies by court)
  No request:            tentative becomes final order

EX PARTE APPLICATIONS (Rule 3.1302):
  Notice to parties:     by 10:00 a.m. court day before
  Declaration:           showing emergency and inadequacy of noticed motion
