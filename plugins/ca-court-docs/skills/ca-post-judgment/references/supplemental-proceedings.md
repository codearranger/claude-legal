# California Supplemental Proceedings
## Judgment Debtor Examination and Related Enforcement Tools

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Verify against current rules and case
> law before filing.

---

## Overview

After a money judgment is entered, the judgment creditor has
several tools to discover the debtor's assets and compel
payment. This reference covers the main supplemental proceedings
available in California.

---

## Judgment Debtor Examination — Code Civ. Proc., § 708.110

### What it is

A court-ordered oral examination of the judgment debtor, under
oath, about assets, income, liabilities, and ability to satisfy
the judgment. It is essentially a deposition with contempt
sanctions for non-appearance.

### How the creditor obtains the order

1. Creditor files an **application** (usually ex parte) to the
   court, with a declaration showing the judgment has not been
   satisfied.
2. The court issues an **Order to Appear for Examination**
   (also called an "OAE" or Form SC-134 in small claims / or a
   court-drafted order in unlimited civil).
3. The order must be served on the debtor **personally** (not by
   mail) (Code Civ. Proc., § 708.110(d)).
4. Service must be at least **10 days before the examination
   date**.

### What the debtor must do

- Appear at the time and place specified in the order.
- Bring any documents subpoenaed under Code Civ. Proc.,
  § 708.130 (bank statements, tax returns, pay stubs, etc.).
- Answer truthfully — the examination is under oath.

### Failure to appear

Failure to appear without prior excuse or postponement exposes
the debtor to:
- **Bench warrant** (arrest warrant) for contempt of court.
- Civil contempt sanctions, including fines and imprisonment.

**If you cannot appear**: contact the creditor's attorney or the
court in advance. Request a continuance in writing. The creditor
may agree, or you may seek a court order to continue. Never
simply fail to appear.

### Scope of examination

The creditor may ask about:
- All assets (real and personal property)
- Sources and amounts of income
- Bank accounts, investments, retirement accounts
- Debts owed to the debtor by third parties
- Vehicles, jewelry, valuable personal property
- Transfers of property within the past 4 years (relevant to
  fraudulent transfer analysis)

The debtor **may** claim exemptions during the exam. Name the
specific property and the statutory basis (e.g., "that bank
account is my wages, exempt under Code Civ. Proc., § 704.080").
Silent refusal to answer is not a valid exemption claim.

### Documents commonly subpoenaed

Under Code Civ. Proc., § 708.130, the creditor may require
production of:
- Past 12 months of bank statements (all accounts)
- Past 3 years of federal income tax returns
- Pay stubs (last 3–6 months)
- Vehicle titles
- Brokerage / retirement account statements
- Documents showing assets transferred to third parties

## Third-Party Examination — Code Civ. Proc., § 708.120

A third party (e.g., the debtor's bank, employer, business
partner) whom the creditor reasonably believes has property
belonging to the debtor can be ordered to appear and answer
questions, and to produce documents.

The same personal service and 10-day notice rules apply.

---

## Assignment Order — Code Civ. Proc., § 708.510

### What it is

The court may order the debtor to **assign to the judgment
creditor** the debtor's rights to receive money from a third
party. This is a particularly powerful tool for recurring income
streams:
- Contract payments (e.g., the debtor has an ongoing contract
  to provide services and is paid monthly)
- Rental income
- Royalties
- Payments under a settlement agreement

### How it works

1. Creditor files an application or motion showing the debtor
   has the right to receive payments from a third party.
2. Court enters an order requiring the debtor to assign those
   payment rights to the creditor.
3. The order is served on the third-party payor.
4. The payor makes future payments to the judgment creditor
   rather than the debtor.

The assignment order continues until the judgment is satisfied
or the court terminates it.

### Earnings exception

Earnings (wages from employment) cannot be reached by an
assignment order; the creditor must use the earnings withholding
order procedure instead (Code Civ. Proc., § 708.550).

---

## Turnover Order — Code Civ. Proc., § 708.205

The court may order the debtor or a third party to transfer
**specific personal property** to the levying officer. Applies
where the property is identifiable (e.g., specific business
equipment, checks owed to the debtor, securities).

---

## Fraudulent Transfer — Code Civ. Proc., § 708.230 et seq. (UFTA)

If the debtor has transferred assets to a third party to avoid
the judgment, the creditor may pursue a **fraudulent transfer
action** under the Uniform Voidable Transactions Act (Civ. Code,
§ 3439 et seq.). Evidence of recent asset transfers is a key
purpose of the debtor exam.

---

## Debtor Exam Preparation — For the Debtor

If served with an Order to Appear for Examination:

1. **Read the order carefully** — note the date, time,
   location, and any documents required.
2. **Calendar the exam date** — and a preparation date at least
   3–5 days before.
3. **Gather the documents listed in any subpoena** (or in the
   order itself).
4. **Identify exempt assets** (see `references/exemptions.md`)
   so you can claim them on the record.
5. **Prepare honest answers** — perjury is a felony.
6. **Do NOT transfer or hide assets** after service — this is
   fraudulent transfer and contempt.
7. **Bring identification and the order itself**.
8. If you have defenses (e.g., the judgment has been paid, the
   judgment is void), raise them by motion before the exam; do
   not simply refuse to appear.
