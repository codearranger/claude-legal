# Key Cases — California Consumer-Debt Defense

A catalog of California appellate and federal decisions most
relevant to California consumer-debt defense.

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Always verify a case is still good
> law before citing. Use Westlaw, Lexis, or CourtListener.

## California — Rosenthal Act

### Komarova v. National Credit Acceptance, Inc.
(2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 324

**Significance**: Broadest reading of Rosenthal Act coverage.
The Court of Appeal held:

- The Rosenthal Act covers debt collectors even when they are
  collecting debts that they own (first-party collection)
- The "debt collector" definition in Civ. Code, § 1788.2(c)
  is broad — any person who regularly engages in debt
  collection in the ordinary course of business
- A company that purchases charged-off debts and collects
  them is a "debt collector" under the Rosenthal Act
- Knowledge that a practice violates the Act is NOT required
  for liability under § 1788.30(a)

**Use**: To establish Rosenthal Act coverage against a debt
buyer arguing it is exempt from California consumer-
protection statutes because it "owns" the debt.

### Davidson v. Seterus, Inc.
(2018) 21 Cal.App.5th 283

**Significance**: Rosenthal Act applies to mortgage servicers
acting as first-party collectors.

- Mortgage servicer engaged in collection practices on behalf
  of the loan's beneficial owner
- Court held that servicer was a "debt collector" within
  the Rosenthal Act's definition
- Rosenthal Act incorporates FDCPA standards via § 1788.17;
  servicer's conduct was measured against incorporated FDCPA
  provisions

**Use**: When the servicer (not the original lender) is the
collection actor; servicer cannot escape Rosenthal liability
by arguing it is not technically a "debt collector" in the
classic sense.

### Best Service Co., Inc. v. Pickett
(2013) 217 Cal.App.4th 1209

**Significance**: Rosenthal Act standing and statutory damages.

- Plaintiff sued under Rosenthal Act for harassment in
  connection with debt collection
- Court clarified the standard for statutory damages of $100
  to $1,000 per violation
- Discussion of the factors a court considers in awarding
  damages within the range

**Use**: In briefing statutory damages requests under
Civ. Code, § 1788.30(a)(2).

## California — UCL (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 17200)

### Kwikset Corp. v. Superior Court
(2011) 51 Cal.4th 310

**Significance**: UCL standing requires "injury in fact and
lost money or property."

- Post-Prop. 64 (2004), plaintiff must show actual economic
  injury
- "Lost money or property" is broadly construed — includes
  money paid in response to a UCL violation, loss of value
  of property, and other economic injuries
- "Injury in fact" is the constitutional minimum

**Use**: When crafting the UCL cause of action — consumer
who paid on an invalid debt, or who incurred costs defending
an unlicensed collector's suit, has standing.

### Cel-Tech Communications, Inc. v. Los Angeles Cellular
Telephone Co.
(1999) 20 Cal.4th 163

**Significance**: Three-prong UCL standard; "unfair" prong
analysis.

- Unlawful prong: borrows violation of any law
- Unfair prong: conduct that threatens harm to consumers
  substantially outweighing its benefits; courts weigh the
  harm against any utility of the conduct
- Fraudulent prong: conduct likely to deceive a reasonable
  consumer

**Use**: When framing the UCL "unfair" prong in cases where
the specific statutory violation may not be clear, but the
debt collector's conduct is patently unfair.

### Aryeh v. Canon Business Solutions, Inc.
(2013) 55 Cal.4th 1185

**Significance**: UCL 4-year SOL; discovery rule.

- UCL's 4-year SOL in § 17208 is subject to the discovery
  rule
- The 4 years begins when the plaintiff discovers, or through
  reasonable diligence should have discovered, the facts
  supporting the claim
- Continuous accrual doctrine: each successive violation may
  trigger a new limitations period

**Use**: When the underlying violation occurred more than
4 years ago but the consumer did not discover it until
later; also relevant for continuing violations.

### Zhang v. Superior Court (Cal. Security Alarm Co.)
(2013) 57 Cal.4th 364

**Significance**: UCL-insurance context, but broadly applicable.

- Clarifies that the UCL's "unlawful" prong is not limited to
  practices that are independently actionable — any violation
  of any law (even without a private right of action) serves
  as the predicate
- CDCLA violations, FDCPA violations, and Rosenthal Act
  violations all qualify as "unlawful" prong predicates

**Use**: When arguing that a CDCLA licensing violation or
FDBPA non-compliance supports a UCL claim.

## California — FDBPA and Chain of Title

### Courts applying FDBPA § 1788.58

No single defining California appellate decision had yet
established a landmark FDBPA ruling as of the author's
knowledge cutoff. The statute has been applied at the
trial court and limited-jurisdiction court level.

**Key FDBPA § 1788.58 analysis points from trial-court
practice**:

- A complaint lacking the § 1788.58 required elements fails
  to state a cause of action; demurrer is the correct remedy
- A debt buyer cannot evade § 1788.58 by claiming the
  account records are unavailable — if unavailable, the debt
  buyer lacks the foundation to sue
- Some trial courts have granted summary judgment for the
  debtor when the debt buyer could not produce documentation
  of the chain of title required by § 1788.52

**Practice**: Always search recent California appellate
decisions (CourtListener, Google Scholar, Lexis) for
current FDBPA precedents before filing any brief.

## California — Civil Procedure / Fees

### Riverisland Cold Storage, Inc. v. Fresno-Madera PCA
(2013) 55 Cal.4th 1169

**Significance**: Parol evidence rule in California.

- California parol evidence rule (Civ. Code, § 1625; CCP
  § 1856) bars extrinsic evidence to contradict a fully
  integrated written contract
- But fraud, mistake, and similar equitable exceptions allow
  extrinsic evidence to show the contract itself was
  procured by fraud
- The Supreme Court held that the prior *Pendergrass* rule
  restricting fraud exception was overruled

**Use**: In debt cases when debtor claims fraudulent
representations led to the account; also relevant to parole
evidence challenges to cardholder agreement terms.

### Lippitt v. Raymond James Financial Services, Inc.
(2003) 340 F.3d 1033 (9th Cir.)

**Significance**: California's UCL and CLRA interact with
FDCPA; both may be pleaded.

**Use**: In cases involving multiple California consumer-
protection statutes.

## 9th Circuit / Federal — FDCPA

### Henson v. Santander Consumer USA, Inc.
582 U.S. 79 (2017)

**Significance**: "Debt collector" definition — debt buyers.

- A debt buyer that collects debts it purchased for its own
  account is generally NOT a "debt collector" under FDCPA
  § 1692a(6)'s primary definition (one whose "principal
  business purpose" is debt collection)
- BUT a debt buyer may be a "debt collector" if it
  "regularly collects or attempts to collect … debts owed
  or due or asserted to be owed or due another"
- The distinction: if the debt buyer collects through its
  own in-house operations, FDCPA coverage is uncertain; if
  it hired a servicer who is the third-party collector, the
  servicer is clearly covered

**California note**: Even if a California debt buyer avoids
FDCPA "debt collector" status under *Henson*, it is still
covered by the Rosenthal Act (see *Komarova*).

### Heintz v. Jenkins
514 U.S. 291 (1995)

**Significance**: Attorneys are debt collectors.

- An attorney whose practice is significantly the collection
  of consumer debts is a "debt collector" under FDCPA
  § 1692a(6)
- Filing a collection lawsuit is "collecting a debt"
- Attorney-debt-collector plaintiffs face FDCPA counterclaims
  just as non-attorney collectors do

**Use**: When the plaintiff's attorney is a known volume
debt-collection firm; the attorney-firm itself may be a
debt collector.

### Rotkiske v. Klemm
589 U.S. 8 (2019)

**Significance**: FDCPA 1-year SOL accrues at violation.

- The 1-year SOL in 15 U.S.C. § 1692k(d) begins on the
  date of the violation, not on the date of discovery
- The discovery rule generally does not apply to FDCPA
  unless equitable tolling is justified
- For California practice: file FDCPA counterclaims within
  1 year; for older violations, plead UCL

### Crawford v. LVNV Funding, LLC
758 F.3d 1254 (11th Cir. 2014)

**Significance**: Filing a proof of claim on a time-barred
debt in bankruptcy violates FDCPA.

- Foundational case for the proposition that using legal
  process on a time-barred debt is a per se FDCPA violation
- Codified in Reg F § 1006.26

**Note**: Midland Funding, LLC v. Johnson, 581 U.S. 224
(2017), limited Crawford in the specific context of Chapter
13 bankruptcy proofs of claim. The general principle that
suing on a time-barred debt can violate the FDCPA remains
good law in state-court debt-defense contexts.

## Business records authentication — federal

### Federal cases on "remote custodian"

California courts follow the same general principle as
federal courts: a debt buyer's custodian cannot authenticate
the original creditor's records. Federal district court
decisions from California, the 9th Circuit, and other circuits
all support the defense position.

Key search terms for current cases: "business records"
"debt buyer" "remote custodian" "Evid. Code 1271" or
"FRE 803(6)"; also "foundation" "original creditor records"
"debt buyer custodian."
