# Rosenthal Fair Debt Collection Practices Act
## Cal. Civ. Code, §§ 1788–1788.33

California's primary consumer-debt collection statute. It is
the state-law analog to the federal FDCPA — but with critical
California-specific expansions.

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Verify current text at leginfo.
> legislature.ca.gov.

## The critical California distinction: applies to original creditors

The FDCPA covers only **third-party debt collectors** —
entities collecting on behalf of someone else. It does NOT
apply to original creditors collecting their own debts.

The Rosenthal Act **covers original creditors** (first-party
collectors). Cal. Civ. Code, § 1788.2(c) defines "debt
collector" to include:

> "any person who, in the ordinary course of business,
> regularly, on behalf of himself or herself or others, engages
> in debt collection."

This is broader than the FDCPA definition and encompasses
first-party collectors. *Komarova v. National Credit
Acceptance, Inc.* (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 324 broadly
construes Rosenthal Act coverage.

**Practical implication**: When the original creditor
(Bank of America, Capital One, Synchrony Bank) is the
plaintiff — not a debt buyer — the FDCPA may not apply, but
the Rosenthal Act does. Always plead the Rosenthal Act.

## FDCPA incorporation — § 1788.17

Cal. Civ. Code, § 1788.17 provides:

> "Notwithstanding any other provision of this title, every
> debt collector collecting or attempting to collect a consumer
> debt shall comply with the provisions of Sections 1692b to
> 1692j, inclusive, of, and shall be subject to the remedies
> in Section 1692k of, Title 15 of the United States Code."

Effect: Every FDCPA § 1692b–1692j prohibition is incorporated
directly into the Rosenthal Act. Every FDCPA violation in
California is simultaneously a Rosenthal Act violation —
with Rosenthal's remedies (§ 1788.30) applying on top of
FDCPA § 1692k.

## Definitions — § 1788.2

**"Debt collector"** (§ 1788.2(c)):
- Any person who regularly engages in debt collection in the
  ordinary course of business
- On behalf of themselves or others
- Includes original creditors, debt buyers, collection agencies,
  and attorneys who regularly collect debts

**"Debt"** or **"consumer debt"** (§ 1788.2(f)):
- Money, property, or their equivalent due or owing by a
  natural person from a consumer credit transaction
- Includes credit-card debt, personal loans, utility bills,
  medical bills, and similar personal obligations

**"Consumer credit transaction"** (§ 1788.2(e)):
- Transaction by a natural person primarily for personal,
  family, or household purposes

**"Debtor"** (§ 1788.2(h)):
- A natural person who is alleged to owe a consumer debt

## Prohibited practices — §§ 1788.10–1788.16

### § 1788.10 — Prohibited collection practices

No debt collector shall:

- (a): Communicate with the debtor by use of threats of
  violence or other criminal means to harm the person,
  reputation, or property of any person
- (b): Use obscene or profane language in communicating
  with any person
- (c): Make a false accusation of fraud or other criminal
  conduct, or threaten to so accuse
- (d): Communicate with a debtor in an attempt to collect
  without disclosing that the communication is from a debt
  collector
- (e): Cause a telephone to ring repeatedly or continuously
  so as to annoy the debtor

### § 1788.11 — Telephone practices

No debt collector shall:

- (a): Place a telephone call to the debtor after 9 PM or
  before 8 AM local time at the debtor's location without
  prior consent
- (b): Telephone the debtor more than 2 times in a 7-day
  period, exclusive of debt collection calls during which
  actual contact is made (under the 2023 amendment by SB 616
  — verify current text; federal Reg F's 7-in-7 rule applies
  via § 1788.17)
- (c): Place a telephone call to the debtor without disclosing
  the collector's name and that the call is from a debt
  collector
- (d): Place a telephone call to the debtor's employer except
  to verify employment

### § 1788.12 — Third-party communication restrictions

No debt collector shall communicate with a person other than
the debtor regarding a consumer debt unless:

- (a): The person is the debtor's attorney
- (b): The communication is for the purpose of locating the
  debtor and the collector does not reveal the purpose or
  the existence of the debt

### § 1788.13 — Prohibited false/misleading representations

No debt collector shall:

- (a): Make false representation that the debt collector is
  vouched for, bonded, affiliated with, or is an instrument
  of a government
- (b): Make false representation of the character, extent,
  or amount of the debt
- (c): Make false representation that the debt collector is
  an attorney at law
- (d): Make false representation that the non-payment of a
  debt will result in arrest or imprisonment
- (e): Communicate false credit information
- (f): Represent a legal proceeding has been or is about to
  be commenced when that is false
- (g): Falsely represent the character, extent, or amount
  of consumer credit charges or fees

### § 1788.14 — Prohibited debt-collection conduct

No debt collector shall:

- (a): Collect any amount (including interest, fees, charges,
  or expenses incidental to the principal obligation) unless
  authorized by the agreement creating the obligation or
  permitted by law
- (b): Use a business, company, or organization name other
  than the true name of the debt collector's business when
  collecting or attempting to collect a consumer debt
- (c): Collect a fee from the debtor for services rendered
  on behalf of the creditor

### § 1788.15 — Harassment and unlawful conduct in collection

No debt collector shall:

- (a): Use any form of communication that simulates legal
  process or that is designed to make it appear that it is
  from a court or government agency
- (b): Threaten to communicate to any person, except through
  proper legal proceedings, the fact that the debtor does
  not pay debts

### § 1788.16 — Communication to the public

No debt collector shall:

- (a): Send communications that are designed to embarrass the
  debtor or that communicate the existence of the debt to the
  public
- (b): Use any form of communication that simulates a telegram
  or emergency message

## The FDCPA incorporation layer — § 1788.17

As noted above, § 1788.17 imports 15 U.S.C. §§ 1692b–1692j
directly. For California practice, this means:

- Every § 1692c communication violation is a Rosenthal violation
- Every § 1692d harassment violation is a Rosenthal violation
- Every § 1692e false-representation violation is a Rosenthal
  violation
- Every § 1692f unfair-practice violation is a Rosenthal
  violation
- Every § 1692g validation failure is a Rosenthal violation
- Reg F violations (12 CFR pt 1006) are incorporated too —
  because § 1788.17 imports § 1692k's application to "any
  regulation prescribed" under Title 15

## Civil liability — § 1788.30

**§ 1788.30(a)**: Any debt collector who violates any provision
of the Rosenthal Act is liable to the debtor for:

- (1): **Actual damages** sustained as a result of the
  violation, including damages for embarrassment, humiliation,
  mental anguish, and emotional distress (subject to reasonable
  proof)
- (2): **Statutory damages** of at least $100 and up to $1,000
  per violation (not per action — unlike FDCPA's per-action
  $1,000 cap)

**§ 1788.30(b)**: In a class action, statutory damages up to
$500,000 or 1% of the debt collector's net worth, whichever
is less.

**§ 1788.30(c)**: Any debt collector who violates the Rosenthal
Act shall be liable to the debtor for **reasonable attorney's
fees** and costs as determined by the court.

**California advantage over FDCPA**: The Rosenthal Act's
statutory damages are **per violation**, not per action.
Multiple violations by the same collector (e.g., 10 abusive
phone calls) = $1,000 × 10 = up to $10,000 in statutory
damages, compared to FDCPA's single $1,000 cap.

### § 1788.30(d) — Bona fide error defense

A debt collector is not liable for a violation that is:

- Not intentional; AND
- Resulted from a bona fide error; AND
- The collector maintained procedures reasonably adapted to
  avoid the specific error

## Statute of limitations — § 1788.30(f)

**1 year** from the date of the last violation.

**California UCL extension**: Bus. & Prof. Code, § 17208
provides a 4-year SOL for UCL claims. Every Rosenthal Act
violation is also an "unlawful" prong predicate. For
violations between 1–4 years old, plead UCL.

## Sample Rosenthal Act counterclaim

```
THIRD CAUSE OF ACTION — California Rosenthal Fair Debt
Collection Practices Act
(Cal. Civ. Code, §§ 1788–1788.33)

[N]. Plaintiff/Counter-Defendant is a "debt collector"
within the meaning of Cal. Civ. Code, § 1788.2(c) in that
it regularly engages in debt collection in the ordinary
course of its business.

[N+1]. The alleged debt is a "consumer debt" within the
meaning of Cal. Civ. Code, § 1788.2(f) — an obligation
arising from a personal credit-card account incurred
primarily for personal, family, or household purposes.

[N+2]. Plaintiff/Counter-Defendant violated the Rosenthal
Act in the following ways:

  (a) By falsely representing the character, extent, and
      amount of the alleged debt, in violation of Cal. Civ.
      Code, § 1788.13(b) and § 1788.17 (incorporating
      15 U.S.C. § 1692e(2));

  (b) By bringing a legal proceeding after the statute of
      limitations under CCP § 337 had expired — falsely
      representing that a legal proceeding was about to be
      legitimately commenced — in violation of Cal. Civ.
      Code, § 1788.13(f) and § 1788.17 (incorporating
      15 U.S.C. § 1692e(5));

  (c) By attempting to collect amounts not authorized by
      the agreement creating the debt, in violation of
      Cal. Civ. Code, § 1788.14(a) and § 1788.17
      (incorporating 15 U.S.C. § 1692f(1));

  (d) By failing to provide a validation notice complying
      with 15 U.S.C. § 1692g(a), as required by Cal. Civ.
      Code, § 1788.17.

[N+3]. As a direct and proximate result, Counter-Claimant
suffered actual damages including [describe]. Counter-
Claimant is entitled to statutory damages of $100 to $1,000
per violation under Cal. Civ. Code, § 1788.30(a)(2), and
reasonable attorney's fees and costs under Cal. Civ. Code,
§ 1788.30(c).
```

## Comparison: Rosenthal Act vs. FDCPA

| Feature | FDCPA | Rosenthal Act |
|---------|-------|---------------|
| Covers original creditors | No | Yes |
| Statutory damages | $1,000 per action | $100–$1,000 per violation |
| Attorney's fees (consumer wins) | Yes | Yes |
| SOL | 1 year | 1 year (+ UCL 4 years) |
| UCL "unlawful" predicate | Yes (via § 17200) | Yes |
| Federal vs. state | Federal | California only |
| Bona fide error defense | Yes | Yes (§ 1788.30(d)) |
