# Recent Decisions — California Consumer-Debt Defense
## 2020–2025

This file summarizes California appellate and 9th Circuit
decisions from approximately 2020–2025 that are relevant to
consumer-debt defense. **These decisions change frequently.**
Always verify currency before citing — use Westlaw, Lexis,
CourtListener, or Google Scholar.

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Verify all citations before use.
> This file requires periodic refresh.

## Note on currency

The decisions below reflect the author's knowledge as of early
2026. California consumer-debt law is actively evolving:

- FDBPA enforcement is generating increasing trial-court and
  appellate precedent
- CDCLA licensing requirements (effective 2022) are beginning
  to produce enforcement actions and collateral litigation
- CFPB Reg F (effective Nov. 2021) is generating new 9th
  Circuit and district court decisions
- The DFPI is actively enforcing CDCLA against unlicensed
  debt buyers and collectors

Run searches before relying on any case in this file.

## Rosenthal Act — Recent Decisions

### Davidson v. Seterus, Inc.
(2018) 21 Cal.App.5th 283 [note: just before 2020 cutoff]

Remains the leading case on Rosenthal Act coverage of
mortgage servicers and first-party collectors acting through
servicers. Still frequently cited.

### Recent Rosenthal Act developments (search for):

- Cases applying the "debt collector" definition under
  § 1788.2(c) to new business models (fintech lenders,
  student loan servicers, medical billing companies)
- Cases addressing Rosenthal Act damages in cases with
  multiple violations by the same collector
- Cases addressing the interaction between Rosenthal Act
  bona fide error defense and systemic practices

**Search terms**: "Rosenthal" "Cal. Civ. Code 1788" "debt
collector" site:courtlistener.com or scholar.google.com
(filter: California courts)

## FDBPA — Recent Decisions

### General note

FDBPA litigation is concentrated in California limited civil
and unlimited civil courts (superior court). Appellate
opinions specifically addressing FDBPA § 1788.58 are still
relatively limited as of early 2026, but are growing.

### Key FDBPA issues generating recent litigation

1. **What constitutes a "debt buyer"** under § 1788.50:
   - Does a company that purchases one pool of debts and
     uses a servicer for collection qualify?
   - Does a bank subsidiary that acquires charged-off
     consumer accounts qualify?

2. **The § 1788.58 complaint requirements**:
   - Courts are generally enforcing § 1788.58 strictly
   - Demurrers are regularly sustained when the complaint
     omits the charge-off date, balance, or chain of title
   - Some courts have granted SJAs when the debt buyer
     could not document the chain

3. **The § 1788.60 pre-suit notice requirement**:
   - Courts are split on whether pre-suit notice failure
     is a complete defense or merely an affirmative defense
     that must be raised or waived

**Search terms**: "FDBPA" OR "1788.58" OR "Fair Debt Buying
Practices Act" (filter: California courts)

## UCL and CDCLA — Recent Developments

### DFPI enforcement actions (2022–2025)

Following the CDCLA's effective date of January 1, 2022,
the DFPI has issued:

- Consent orders against unlicensed debt collectors and
  buyers that continued operating in California post-2022
  without obtaining a DFPI license
- Cease and desist orders to out-of-state debt buyers
  with California portfolios who did not seek licensing

**Practice**: Check the DFPI enforcement actions page for
the collector/buyer you are defending against. Enforcement
action = strong UCL predicate.

**DFPI enforcement actions**: https://dfpi.ca.gov/
enforcement-actions/

### UCL class actions based on CDCLA non-licensure

Post-CDCLA class actions have been filed in California
federal and state courts alleging UCL "unlawful" prong
violations based on CDCLA non-licensure. These cases are
working their way through the courts.

**Search terms**: "CDCLA" OR "Cal. Fin. Code 100000" OR
"DFPI" "debt collector" "UCL" (filter: recent filings)

## FDCPA / Reg F — 9th Circuit Recent Decisions

### Time-barred debt and Reg F § 1006.26

Multiple 9th Circuit and California district court decisions
since November 2021 have applied Reg F § 1006.26 to debt-
buyer suits on time-barred debts. The general consensus:

- Filing suit on a debt the collector knows or should know
  is time-barred violates § 1006.26(b)
- "Should know" is an objective standard; the date of last
  payment is typically known from the collector's own records
- California-specific: with the 4-year SOL under CCP § 337,
  any suit filed more than 4 years after the last payment
  is vulnerable

**Search terms**: "Reg F" "1006.26" "time-barred" "9th
Circuit" OR "California" (CourtListener, filter ca9 or cand/
cacd/caed/casd)

### Validation notice under Reg F § 1006.34

Decisions analyzing whether a collector's initial
communication satisfies the itemization requirements of
§ 1006.34. The CFPB's model validation notice provides a
safe harbor; departures from the model are generating
litigation.

**Key issue**: The § 1006.34(c)(2)(viii) itemization-date
requirement. Collectors who send summary letters without
an itemization-date reference are generating challenges.

## 9th Circuit — "Debt Collector" Definition Post-Henson

*Henson v. Santander Consumer USA, Inc.*, 582 U.S. 79 (2017)
held that a debt buyer collecting for its own account is not
automatically a "debt collector." Post-Henson decisions have
addressed:

- Whether a debt buyer that uses a third-party servicer is
  a "debt collector" — generally yes, the servicer is
  covered and the buyer may be too
- Whether a debt buyer that uses its own employees to
  collect is a "debt collector" — depends on whether
  collection is a "principal business purpose" or a regular
  activity

**California note**: This distinction matters less in
California because the Rosenthal Act covers all debt
collectors including original creditors and debt buyers
regardless of the FDCPA "regularly collects" analysis.

## Attorney Fees — Recent Decisions

### 9th Circuit on FDCPA fee awards

The 9th Circuit has continued to apply a lodestar analysis
for FDCPA attorney's fees under § 1692k(a)(3). Recent
decisions have addressed:

- Block billing — courts are inconsistent; some reduce,
  some accept with explanation
- Lodestar multipliers for consumer-protection cases —
  rare; lodestar is the ceiling in most 9th Circuit cases
- Fees for time spent on fee petition — generally
  recoverable but subject to reasonableness review

### California Civ. Code § 1788.30(c) — attorney's fees

California appellate decisions in the 2020–2025 period have
affirmed the one-way fee-shifting provision of § 1788.30(c):

- Consumer prevailing on a Rosenthal Act claim is entitled
  to attorney's fees as a matter of right
- The amount is determined by the lodestar method
- Defendant-debt-collector cannot offset fees by pointing
  to small recovery; the statute is one-way
