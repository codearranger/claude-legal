# Attorney's Fees and Costs — California Consumer-Debt Cases

Fee recovery is often the dominant strategic consideration in
California debt-defense cases — both for defendant pursuing
prevailing-party fees and for resisting plaintiff's fee claims.

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Verify current statutes.

## Defendant's prevailing-party fee paths

When defendant prevails (dismissal, judgment for defendant,
or favorable settlement), fees may be available from multiple
sources. Plead all of them to preserve maximum recovery.

### Path 1: FDCPA § 1692k(a)(3) — one-way, mandatory

**Mandatory** when consumer prevails on an FDCPA counterclaim:

- "Reasonable attorney fees" and costs
- Available only to the consumer/debtor — not to the debt
  collector (one-way fee-shifting)
- "Prevailing party" under FDCPA requires the consumer to
  have obtained some relief on the FDCPA counterclaim

**Procedurally**: File a post-judgment motion for attorney's
fees under CCP § 1033.5 and California Rules of Court,
rule 3.1702 (motion for fees after judgment must be filed
within the time limits set by the court or by statute).

### Path 2: Rosenthal Act — Civ. Code, § 1788.30(c)

**Mandatory** when consumer prevails against a debt collector
under the Rosenthal Act:

- Civ. Code, § 1788.30(c): "In the case of any successful
  action to enforce the liability under subdivision (a) or
  (b), the court shall award to the prevailing debtor,
  reasonable attorney fees and costs"
- "Shall award" = mandatory; the court has no discretion
  to deny fees to a prevailing consumer
- Amount: lodestar analysis (reasonable hours × reasonable
  rate); California courts apply *Ketchum v. Moses* (2001)
  24 Cal.4th 1122 lodestar methodology

**Important**: § 1788.30(c) is one-way — the successful debt
collector does NOT get fees against the consumer unless the
consumer's Rosenthal Act claim was brought in bad faith
(§ 1788.30(e)).

### Path 3: Civ. Code, § 1717 — contract reciprocity

If the original cardholder agreement contains an attorney's
fees clause (typical: "You agree to pay our attorney's fees
and costs if we must collect a debt you owe us"), § 1717
makes it **reciprocal**:

- The prevailing party (regardless of which party) on a
  contract claim recovers attorney's fees
- If defendant prevails on plaintiff's breach-of-contract
  claim, defendant recovers under the fee clause by
  operation of § 1717
- Applies even if the fee clause only runs one way (from
  debtor to creditor)

**Limitation**: Plaintiff must be able to prove the cardholder
agreement exists and contains a fee clause. If the debt buyer
cannot produce the agreement, they cannot invoke § 1717 —
and neither can the defendant on that path.

**However**: If the agreement is authenticated and contains
a fee clause, and defendant wins, § 1717 applies.

### Path 4: FDBPA — Civ. Code, § 1788.62

When a debt buyer fails to comply with FDBPA requirements
and the consumer prevails:

- Civ. Code, § 1788.62 provides that the debt buyer is
  liable to the debtor for "costs and attorney fees"
- This is in addition to (not in lieu of) Rosenthal Act and
  FDCPA fee recovery

### Path 5: CCP § 1021.5 — private attorney general

When the consumer's action enforces an important right
affecting the public interest:

Elements:
1. The action enforces an important public right or confers
   a significant benefit on the general public
2. Private enforcement is necessary (public enforcement is
   inadequate)
3. Financial burden on the consumer is disproportionate to
   their individual stake

**Consumer-debt applicability**: Courts have awarded § 1021.5
fees in consumer-protection cases involving:

- Systematic FDCPA or Rosenthal Act violations by a debt
  collector
- Widespread FDBPA non-compliance
- CDCLA licensing violations affecting a class of consumers

**Procedure**: Move for § 1021.5 fees by post-judgment motion.
The motion must show all three elements.

### Path 6: Statutory prevailing-party costs — CCP § 1032

Every prevailing party (on any civil claim) is entitled to
costs as a matter of right under CCP § 1032(b):

- Filing fees
- Service fees
- Reporter fees for depositions used at trial
- Expert witness fees (if properly noticed under § 2034)
- Copying costs for exhibits at trial
- Cost bill must be filed within 15 days of judgment

**Note**: CCP § 1032 covers costs, not attorney's fees.
Costs and fees are different.

### Path 7: CCP § 998 — settlement offer mechanics

CCP § 998 provides consequences for refusing a reasonable
settlement offer:

- **Defendant makes a § 998 offer**: if defendant offers
  to settle for an amount, and plaintiff fails to accept,
  and plaintiff does not obtain a more favorable judgment,
  plaintiff cannot recover costs (and defendant recovers
  costs after the offer)
- **In the reverse direction** (less common in debt-defense):
  plaintiff's § 998 offer that defendant rejects, with
  defendant ultimately recovering less, shifts costs to
  defendant

**Strategy in debt-defense**: Once defenses are established,
consider making a § 998 offer (e.g., offer to dismiss for
no money exchanged). If plaintiff rejects and then loses,
defendant can recover costs from the date of the offer.

## Plaintiff's fee paths (and how to defend against them)

### Original cardholder agreement fee clause

Most cardholder agreements contain a fee clause. Plaintiff
will argue for fees under the contract if it prevails.

**Defense**: Plaintiff must prove the agreement, its terms,
and that the fee clause was part of the contract. If plaintiff
cannot authenticate the cardholder agreement (and this is
common in debt-buyer cases), plaintiff cannot claim
contract-based fees under § 1717. Without the contract,
there is no fee clause to enforce.

**Also**: § 1717 makes the clause reciprocal — if plaintiff
invokes it and loses, defendant recovers under § 1717.

### FDCPA and Rosenthal Act — one-way fee-shifting

FDCPA § 1692k and Rosenthal Act § 1788.30(c) are explicitly
one-way: fees are available only to the prevailing consumer.
A debt collector that prevails against a consumer does NOT
recover fees under these statutes.

**Rare exception**: Rosenthal Act § 1788.30(e) — if the
court finds that the consumer's action was brought in "bad
faith," the court may award reasonable attorney's fees to
the debt collector. This is rarely invoked successfully
against pro se defendants.

## Lodestar calculation

When fees are awarded, California courts use the lodestar
method (*Ketchum v. Moses* (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122):

**Lodestar = Hours reasonably expended × Reasonable hourly rate**

Then adjusted by a multiplier (up or down) based on:

- Novelty and difficulty of the issues
- Skill required
- Results obtained vs. amount sought
- Contingency risk
- Preclusion of other employment

**Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1702**: the motion for fees
must be filed within the time limits stated by the court
or by the applicable fee statute. Check the judgment for
any deadline set by the court. Default: notice of motion
must be served and filed within 60 days after service of
notice of entry of judgment (Cal. Rules of Court, rule
3.1702(b)(1)).

**Supporting documentation**:

- Time records (itemized by date, task, attorney, time)
- Declarations from counsel re: reasonable rates in the
  local market
- Comparator rates from similar matters

## Defending against plaintiff's fee claim

If plaintiff seeks fees:

1. Object within the applicable deadline
2. Challenge hours (excessive; block-billed; clerical tasks)
3. Challenge rates (use declarations showing lower market
   rates; argue defendant is less experienced)
4. Challenge the fee basis (plaintiff has not established it
   prevailed on the fee-bearing claim; the fee clause in the
   contract requires production of the contract)
5. If plaintiff cannot produce the cardholder agreement,
   plaintiff cannot establish a contract-based fee clause
   (§ 1717 requires proving the agreement and the fee
   provision)

## Pro se fee limitations

A pro se defendant **cannot recover attorney fees** for their
own time in representing themselves. However:

- **Costs** under CCP § 1032 are recoverable
- **Statutory damages** under Rosenthal Act § 1788.30(a)(2)
  ($100–$1,000 per violation) are recoverable without
  an attorney
- **FDBPA statutory damages** under Civ. Code, § 1788.62 may
  be recoverable
- **UCL restitution** (money paid in response to illegal
  collection) is recoverable

A pro se defendant who prevails may recover:
- CCP § 1032 costs (filing fees, service fees, etc.)
- Rosenthal Act statutory damages ($100–$1,000 per violation)
- UCL restitution if any money was collected
- Total range: potentially $500–$5,000+ in statutory damages
  plus costs

## Sample fee claim section in a motion

```
VIII. FEES AND COSTS

Defendant respectfully requests that the Court award
reasonable attorney's fees and costs under:

  (a) 15 U.S.C. § 1692k(a)(3) (FDCPA) — mandatory one-way
      fee-shifting when consumer prevails on FDCPA claim;

  (b) Cal. Civ. Code, § 1788.30(c) (Rosenthal Act) —
      mandatory fee-shifting when consumer prevails on
      Rosenthal Act claim;

  (c) Cal. Civ. Code, § 1717 (contract reciprocity) —
      the cardholder agreement, Exhibit [N] hereto, contains
      a fee clause; § 1717 makes it reciprocal; Defendant
      is the prevailing party on the contract claim;

  (d) Cal. Civ. Code, § 1788.62 (FDBPA) — Plaintiff failed
      to comply with FDBPA requirements; Defendant prevails;

  (e) CCP § 1021.5 — this action vindicates public rights
      by enforcing FDCPA, Rosenthal Act, FDBPA, and CDCLA
      against a systematic violator.

Defendant will submit supporting declarations and time records
pursuant to Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1702.
```
