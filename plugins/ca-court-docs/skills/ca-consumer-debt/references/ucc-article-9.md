# UCC Article 9 (Cal. Comm. Code, §§ 9101–9809)
## Secured Transactions and Debt-Buyer Chain of Title

California's enactment of Revised UCC Article 9 is at
**Cal. Comm. Code, §§ 9101–9809**. It governs **secured
transactions** — debts secured by collateral. Most credit-
card debt is **unsecured**, so Article 9 doesn't apply
directly. But it applies to:

- Auto loans
- Equipment finance
- Secured personal loans
- Certain commercial-line transactions

And the Article 9 assignment and notification concepts
inform debt-buyer analysis even for unsecured debt.

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Verify current Cal. Comm. Code.

## When Article 9 applies in California debt-defense

### Secured-debt cases

If the alleged debt is secured (auto loan, secured personal
loan, equipment finance), Article 9 governs:

- Perfection of the security interest (Cal. Comm. Code,
  § 9203)
- Priority among secured creditors (§ 9322)
- Default and repossession (§ 9601 et seq.)
- Disposition of collateral after default (§ 9610)

**Deficiency after repossession**: If a creditor repossesses
and sells collateral, and then sues for the deficiency, the
deficiency action is subject to Article 9 compliance:
- Notice of sale required under § 9614 (consumer goods)
- Commercially reasonable sale required under § 9627
- Failure to comply may bar or reduce the deficiency

### Unsecured-debt cases and Article 9 concepts

For credit-card debt and other unsecured obligations, Article 9
doesn't apply directly — but its assignment and notification
concepts are frequently invoked by debt buyers. Key provisions:

- **§ 9102(a)(2) — "account"**: a right to payment of a
  monetary obligation, whether or not earned by performance,
  arising out of a contract for services or goods. Note that
  credit-card accounts may qualify as "accounts" in some
  analyses.
- **§ 9102(a)(61) — "payment intangible"**: a general
  intangible under which the account debtor's principal
  obligation is a monetary obligation; covers some credit-
  card debt and most unsecured loans.
- **§ 9309(3) — automatic perfection for payment intangibles**:
  assignment of a payment intangible is perfected
  automatically at attachment — no filing required.
- **§ 9406 — notification of assignment**: After the account
  debtor (consumer) is notified of the assignment, the
  consumer must pay the assignee; payment to the original
  creditor after notification does not discharge the debt.

## Key Cal. Comm. Code, § 9000s sections for debt analysis

### § 9102 — Definitions

**"Account"** (§ 9102(a)(2)):
- A right to payment of a monetary obligation, whether or
  not earned by performance
- Arising out of: (i) goods sold or leased; (ii) services
  rendered; (iii) policies of insurance; (iv) use of a credit
  card; or similar
- But not evidenced by chattel paper or instruments

**"Account debtor"** (§ 9102(a)(3)):
- Person obligated on an account
- In debt-buyer cases, the consumer is the "account debtor"

**"Payment intangible"** (§ 9102(a)(61)):
- General intangible under which the account debtor's
  principal obligation is a monetary obligation
- Most credit-card debts and personal loans fall here once
  an account is charged off

**"General intangible"** (§ 9102(a)(42)):
- Any personal property other than accounts, commercial
  tort claims, deposit accounts, documents, goods,
  instruments, investment property, letter-of-credit rights,
  letters of credit, money, or oil, gas, or other minerals
- Covers most intangible payment obligations

### § 9203 — Attachment and enforceability

For a security interest to attach and be enforceable:

1. Value must have been given
2. The debtor must have rights in the collateral
3. One of:
   - Debtor authenticated a security agreement describing
     the collateral
   - Collateral is in possession of secured party
   - Collateral is controlled by secured party

**Relevance to debt defense**: If the creditor's claim rests
on a security agreement and the creditor cannot produce the
authenticated security agreement, the security interest
may never have attached — meaning the creditor cannot
repossess or claim a security interest in the collateral.

### § 9309 — Security interests perfected upon attachment

**§ 9309(3)**: A security interest in assignment of accounts
and payment intangibles that does not secure an obligation
is perfected automatically when it attaches.

**Relevance**: When a debt buyer acquires a portfolio of
charged-off credit-card accounts or payment intangibles,
the transfer may be automatically perfected under § 9309(3)
without filing a UCC-1 — meaning the debt buyer doesn't need
to file a UCC financing statement to "own" the debt.

However, automatic perfection does not solve the
authentication problem: the debt buyer must still prove
through admissible evidence that the specific account was
transferred as part of the assignment.

### § 9322 — Priority among competing security interests

Determines which creditor has priority when multiple parties
claim security interests in the same collateral.

For debt-defense: if multiple debt buyers claim to own the
same charged-off account (a real but rare occurrence when
the same pool was sold to multiple purchasers due to a data
error), § 9322 priority rules apply.

### § 9406 — Discharge of account debtor; notification of
assignment

**§ 9406(a)**: An account debtor may discharge its
obligation by paying the assignor until:
- The account debtor receives notification that the
  amount is due or is to become due to the assignee, AND
- The notification reasonably identifies the rights assigned

**§ 9406(b)**: If requested by the account debtor, the
assignee must seasonably furnish reasonable proof that
the assignment has been made; if the assignee fails, the
account debtor may discharge by paying the assignor.

**Defense use of § 9406**:

1. If the debt buyer cannot prove it notified the consumer
   of the assignment (and many cannot, for pooled purchases),
   the consumer's payments to the original creditor discharge
   the debt

2. The § 9406(b) right to demand proof of assignment is a
   useful discovery hook — the debt buyer must produce
   reasonable proof of the assignment; if it cannot, the
   consumer may discharge by paying the original creditor

**California consumer-debt practice**: most debt buyers send
a "notice of assignment" letter when they acquire the
account. If the consumer received no such notice, § 9406(a)
provides that the consumer could have validly paid the
original creditor. If the consumer did pay the original
creditor after charge-off (unusual but possible), that
payment may discharge the debt vis-à-vis the debt buyer.

### § 9601–9628 — Default and enforcement

For secured-debt cases:

- **§ 9601**: After default, secured party may reduce claim
  to judgment or pursue UCC remedies
- **§ 9609**: May take possession of collateral (repo)
- **§ 9610**: May dispose of collateral after giving notice
- **§ 9614**: Notice requirements for consumer-goods
  collateral (detailed content requirements)
- **§ 9626**: If secured party fails to comply with Article
  9 procedures, secured party loses right to deficiency
  in consumer-goods transactions (§ 9626(b))

**California specific**: failure to give proper § 9614
notice is an absolute bar to a deficiency judgment against
a consumer in a consumer-goods transaction under California
law. This is a strong defense in repossession + deficiency
cases.

## Chain-of-title analysis using Article 9 concepts

Even though Article 9 does not directly govern the transfer
of unsecured credit-card debt, courts and parties often
describe the transfer using Article 9 language. The defense
can use this:

- **"Assignment of accounts"** language in bulk sale
  agreements may invoke § 9406 notification requirements
- **Failure to notify** the consumer of the assignment
  may allow the consumer to argue that § 9406(a) permits
  payment to the original creditor
- **The assignee's obligation to produce proof of assignment**
  under § 9406(b) supports the discovery demand for
  authenticated chain-of-title documents

## UCC Article 3 (Cal. Comm. Code, §§ 3101–3605) —
Negotiable instruments

If the alleged debt is evidenced by a **promissory note**
or other negotiable instrument, Article 3 applies.

**Holder in due course doctrine** (Cal. Comm. Code, § 3302):
A holder in due course takes a negotiable instrument free
of most defenses the maker (consumer) might have against
the original payee. Elements:

1. Holder took the instrument
2. For value
3. In good faith
4. Without notice of any claim or defense

**Debt-defense implications**: If a debt buyer qualifies as
a holder in due course, it can cut off defenses the consumer
had against the original creditor. However:

- The debt buyer must prove it is a "holder" — i.e., it
  possesses the original instrument or a duly endorsed copy
- Most debt buyers of credit-card accounts are NOT holders
  in due course because credit-card agreements are not
  negotiable instruments — they are open-ended contracts,
  not notes
- For personal loans evidenced by a signed promissory note,
  the holder-in-due-course analysis is more relevant
