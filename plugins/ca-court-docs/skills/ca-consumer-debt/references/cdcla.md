# California Debt Collection Licensing Act (CDCLA)
## Cal. Fin. Code, §§ 100000–100027
## Effective January 1, 2022

The CDCLA created a new licensing requirement administered by
the **California Department of Financial Protection and
Innovation (DFPI)** for debt collectors and debt buyers
operating in California.

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Verify current text and DFPI
> regulations at dfpi.ca.gov.

## Overview and significance

Before the CDCLA, California had no statewide licensing
requirement for debt collectors. The CDCLA changed this:

- **Effective January 1, 2022**: any debt collector or debt
  buyer must obtain a DFPI license before engaging in
  debt-collection activity in California
- **Non-licensure** is an affirmative defense to a collection
  suit, a UCL violation, and grounds for DFPI enforcement

This is a powerful defense tool: check the DFPI database
**at the outset of every California debt-defense matter**.

## DFPI license search

**DFPI licensee search**: https://dfpi.ca.gov/consumers/
check-your-financial-company/

- Search by company name or NMLS number
- Confirm the license is **active** (not expired, suspended,
  or revoked)
- Screenshot the search results — the screenshot is evidence

If the plaintiff (debt buyer or collection agency) lacks a
valid active license:

1. **Affirmative defense**: "As a [Nth] Affirmative Defense,
   Plaintiff lacks a license from the California Department
   of Financial Protection and Innovation as required by
   Cal. Fin. Code, § 100002, and therefore lacks authority
   to collect debts or maintain this action in California."
2. **UCL counterclaim**: the unlicensed activity is the
   "unlawful" predicate
3. **DFPI complaint**: file a complaint with DFPI

## Definitions — § 100001

**"Debt collector"** (§ 100001(b)):

> Any person who engages in the business of collecting or
> attempting to collect, directly or indirectly, consumer
> debts owed or due or asserted to be owed or due another
> person.

Also includes debt buyers who purchase consumer debt and
then attempt to collect it.

**Key expansions over prior law**:
- No minimum number of transactions — one debt-collection
  act for hire requires a license
- Covers in-state AND out-of-state collectors who collect
  from California residents
- "Consumer debt" means money, property, or their equivalent
  due or owing or alleged to be due or owing from a natural
  person

**"Debt buyer"** (§ 100001(c)):

> A person or entity that is regularly engaged in the business
> of purchasing portfolios of defaulted consumer debt for
> collection purposes, whether it collects the debt itself,
> hires other collectors, or sells the debt.

**Exclusions** (§ 100001 — verify current exclusion list):
- Attorneys licensed in California collecting on behalf of
  a client (with conditions)
- Original creditors collecting their own debts (but note:
  some original-creditor servicers may require a license)
- Banks and savings associations (subject to separate
  federal/state oversight)
- Check the current DFPI regulations at 10 Cal. Code Regs.
  § 1800 et seq. for the full exclusion list

## License requirement — § 100002

> No person shall engage in the business of collecting
> consumer debts in this state without first obtaining a
> license from the commissioner pursuant to this division.

"Engage in the business" includes:

- Making collection calls
- Sending collection letters
- Filing collection lawsuits
- Purchasing portfolios of charged-off consumer debt

## License application — §§ 100003–100005

**Application contents** (§ 100003):
- Applicant's name, form of business, and principal place
  of business
- Address of each place of business in California
- Names and addresses of any partners, officers, directors,
  and principals owning 10%+ of the applicant
- NMLS identifier
- Financial statements

**Fees** (§ 100004): application fee set by DFPI regulation
(verify current amounts at dfpi.ca.gov).

**Investigation** (§ 100005): DFPI investigates the applicant
and may require additional information or interviews.

## License issuance and maintenance — §§ 100006–100008

**Grounds for denial, suspension, or revocation** (§ 100006):
- Prior criminal conviction relating to fraud, dishonesty,
  or breach of trust
- Prior disciplinary action by a regulatory authority
- Providing false information in the application
- Failure to maintain adequate financial responsibility
- Failure to pay required fees

**Annual license renewal** (§ 100007): License must be
renewed annually; DFPI may revoke a lapsed license.

**Continuing obligations** (§ 100008): Licensees must notify
DFPI of changes in business form, ownership, or location.

## DFPI supervisory authority — §§ 100009–100015

DFPI may:
- Examine the books and records of licensees
- Conduct investigations
- Issue subpoenas
- Order cessation of unlawful activity
- Seek injunctive relief
- Impose civil money penalties

## Enforcement — §§ 100016–100022

**Administrative enforcement** (§ 100016): DFPI may take
administrative action against violators, including license
revocation and civil penalties.

**Civil enforcement** (§ 100020): DFPI may obtain injunctive
relief and restitution for consumers.

**Criminal penalties** (§ 100022): Willful violations of the
CDCLA are a misdemeanor; second or subsequent violations may
be charged as felonies.

## Consequences of non-licensure in litigation

### Affirmative defense

Cal. Fin. Code, § 100001 et seq. does not expressly create
a private right of action with specific damages. However,
non-licensure has these practical consequences:

1. **UCL "unlawful" prong**: Operating without a required
   license violates § 100002, which is an "unlawful" act
   under Bus. & Prof. Code, § 17200. The consumer can bring
   a UCL claim seeking restitution and injunctive relief.

2. **Affirmative defense to the collection suit**: A court
   may decline to enforce a debt collected in violation of
   the licensing requirement as contrary to public policy.
   While California courts have not uniformly held this, the
   argument is strongest when the plaintiff itself lacks
   a license.

3. **Standing argument**: A debt collector or debt buyer that
   lacks a state license may lack capacity to maintain an
   action in California courts as an unauthorized foreign
   entity engaged in unlicensed business.

4. **DFPI referral**: Filing a complaint with DFPI is
   appropriate; DFPI may investigate and take enforcement
   action.

### Pleading the CDCLA defense

```
[Nth] AFFIRMATIVE DEFENSE
(Unlicensed Debt Collector — Cal. Fin. Code, § 100002)

Plaintiff is a "debt collector" and/or "debt buyer" within the
meaning of Cal. Fin. Code, § 100001, and was required to
obtain a license from the California Department of Financial
Protection and Innovation before engaging in debt-collection
activity in California, including filing this action. Plaintiff
did not hold a valid active DFPI license as of the date this
action was filed. Plaintiff's unlicensed activity violates
Cal. Fin. Code, § 100002 and constitutes an unlawful business
practice under Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code, § 17200.
```

### Pleading the UCL counterclaim based on CDCLA violation

```
[Nth] CAUSE OF ACTION — Unlawful Business Practice
(Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code, § 17200)
[Based on CDCLA violation]

[N]. Plaintiff/Counter-Defendant engaged in an "unlawful"
business practice within the meaning of Bus. & Prof. Code,
§ 17200, by engaging in debt-collection activity in California
without holding a valid license from the DFPI as required by
Cal. Fin. Code, § 100002.

[N+1]. As a direct result of this unlawful practice, Counter-
Claimant suffered harm, including the cost and burden of
defending this unlicensed collection action.

[N+2]. Counter-Claimant seeks injunctive relief ordering
Plaintiff to cease unlicensed debt-collection activity, and
restitution of any amounts collected without a license.
```

## Interaction with other California statutes

| Statute | Relationship to CDCLA |
|---------|----------------------|
| Rosenthal Act (Civ. Code, § 1788) | Parallel requirements; applies to both licensed and unlicensed collectors |
| FDBPA (Civ. Code, § 1788.50) | Applies to debt buyers; CDCLA adds a licensing requirement on top |
| UCL (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 17200) | CDCLA violation = UCL "unlawful" predicate |
| FDCPA (15 U.S.C. § 1692) | Federal; separate; does not incorporate CDCLA |

## Practical checklist

When opening a California debt-defense matter:

1. Identify the plaintiff's full legal name
2. Search the DFPI database
3. Screenshot the results (active / expired / not found)
4. If expired or not found: plead the CDCLA defense and
   UCL counterclaim
5. Serve an RFA asking plaintiff to admit its DFPI license
   status (see `references/rfa-debt-buyer.md`, RFA No. 5–7)
6. Request DFPI license documentation in discovery (see
   `references/rfp-debt-buyer.md`, RFP No. 14)
