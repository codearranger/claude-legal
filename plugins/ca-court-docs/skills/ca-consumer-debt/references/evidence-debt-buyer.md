# Evidence in Debt-Buyer Cases — California
## Cal. Evid. Code § 1271 Business Records Exception

The most important evidentiary issue in California debt-buyer
litigation is the **business-records foundation** under
**Cal. Evid. Code § 1271**, and the related issues of
custodian declarations (§ 1561), the Secondary Evidence Rule
(§ 1521), and authentication (§ 1400 et seq.).

This reference covers the doctrine and gives specific
objection language for use at trial, summary judgment, and
in limine practice.

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Verify each rule and case before
> arguing.

## Cal. Evid. Code § 1271 — Business Records Exception

California's primary hearsay exception for business records.
The exception requires:

1. **Writing**: the record must be a writing (§ 250 —
   broadly defined to include electronic records)
2. **Made at or near the time**: written at or near the time
   of the act, condition, or event recorded
3. **By, or from information transmitted by, a person with
   knowledge**: the person who made the record or supplied
   the information had personal knowledge of the facts
4. **Made and kept in the course of a regularly conducted
   business activity**: the business regularly made and kept
   records of this type
5. **Sources of information, method, and time of preparation
   were such as to indicate its trustworthiness**

**All five elements must be established.** Failure to lay
the foundation for any element leaves the record
inadmissible as hearsay.

The foundation must be laid by **a custodian or other
qualified witness** with personal knowledge of the record-
keeping practices of the business that created the record —
not merely the business that currently possesses the record.

## Cal. Evid. Code § 1561 — Custodian Declarations

California allows the § 1271 foundation to be laid by a
**written declaration** from the custodian (in lieu of live
testimony) in limited circumstances, including in response
to a subpoena duces tecum.

Under § 1561, the declaration must state:

- The affiant is the duly authorized custodian or other
  qualified witness
- The documents are true copies of the original records
- The records were prepared by the personnel of the business
  in the ordinary course of business at or near the time
  of the acts, events, or conditions recorded
- The custodian has personal knowledge of the record-keeping
  practices described

A declaration that meets § 1561 with respect to the
**custodian's own organization's records** does NOT satisfy
§ 1271 for records created by a **different organization**.

## The remote custodian problem

The central authentication challenge in California debt-buyer
cases: the debt buyer's custodian is **not** the original
creditor's custodian.

**The debt buyer's typical evidentiary package**:

- Plaintiff's custodian declaration (under CCP § 2015.5
  penalty of perjury) attesting that Plaintiff's records
  show the account balance and chain of title
- The records themselves: account history, balance summary,
  Bill of Sale, Assignment Schedule

**Why this fails**:

1. The records were created by the **original creditor**
   (e.g., Bank of America). Plaintiff's custodian did not
   work for Bank of America.

2. Plaintiff's custodian cannot testify to Bank of America's
   record-keeping practices — they have no personal knowledge
   of whether those records were made at or near the time
   of the transactions, or whether they were made in Bank of
   America's regular course of business.

3. Under Cal. Evid. Code § 1271, the witness must have
   personal knowledge of the originating entity's record-
   keeping — not just the possessing entity's.

4. The "adopted records" theory — that by acquiring the
   records with the debt, the debt buyer "adopted" them into
   its own regular course of business — has been rejected in
   most contexts. Acquisition of records does not transform
   them into the buyer's records made in its regular course.

## California's Secondary Evidence Rule — § 1521

California eliminated the common-law Best Evidence Rule in
1998 and replaced it with the **Secondary Evidence Rule**
(Cal. Evid. Code § 1520 et seq.):

- **§ 1520**: The original of a writing is not required —
  secondary evidence of the writing's contents is admissible
  unless the court determines that use of secondary evidence
  would be unfair to a party.
- **§ 1521**: Secondary evidence (including copies) is
  admissible unless the court determines it is unfair in
  light of the circumstances.

**Important note**: The Secondary Evidence Rule concerns
the **form** of the evidence (original vs. copy), not the
**foundation** for the hearsay exception. Even if a copy
is admissible under § 1521, the copy must still satisfy
§ 1271's hearsay requirements and § 1400 et seq.'s
authentication requirements.

The debt buyer cannot use § 1521 to escape the § 1271
foundation requirement. The Secondary Evidence Rule is
irrelevant to hearsay.

## Cal. Evid. Code § 1400 — Authentication

Before any writing is admissible, it must be **authenticated**
— shown to be what its proponent claims it to be
(§ 1400).

Methods of authentication include (§ 1410 et seq.):

- Direct testimony by a witness with personal knowledge
  that the writing is what it is claimed to be
- Proof of handwriting
- Comparison with authenticated specimen
- Opinion of a handwriting expert
- Distinctive characteristics (content, substance, internal
  patterns)

For electronically stored records: Cal. Evid. Code § 1552
creates a rebuttable presumption of authenticity for certain
computer-generated records — but this requires proper
foundation.

**The same "remote custodian" problem applies to
authentication as to hearsay**: the person authenticating
must have personal knowledge that the document is what it
is claimed to be. A debt buyer's employee can authenticate
the debt buyer's own records — but not the original
creditor's records.

## Defense objection language

### Hearsay objection (at trial or motion in limine)

```
Objection — hearsay (Cal. Evid. Code § 1200). The records
consist of out-of-court statements by Bank of America
regarding the alleged account, offered for the truth of
the matter asserted — that the account existed, that
the balance was $X, that the defendant made payments.
Plaintiff has not laid the Cal. Evid. Code § 1271 foundation
because: (1) the declarant is Plaintiff's custodian, not
Bank of America's; (2) the declarant lacks personal
knowledge of Bank of America's record-keeping practices;
(3) there is no showing that these records were made at
or near the time of the events recorded in Bank of
America's regular course of business.
```

### Lack of authentication (§ 1400)

```
Objection — failure of authentication (Cal. Evid. Code
§ 1400). Plaintiff claims these are Bank of America's
records of the alleged account, but the only authentication
offered is Plaintiff's custodian's declaration — and
Plaintiff's custodian has no personal knowledge of Bank of
America's records or record-keeping practices.
```

### Secondary Evidence Rule objection (§ 1521)

```
Even if secondary evidence is otherwise admissible under
Cal. Evid. Code § 1521, admission of these unverified
documents would be unfair to Defendant under § 1521(b)
because Plaintiff has made no showing that it is impossible
to produce the originals, or that the originals do not exist.
The only basis offered is that Plaintiff purchased the
account without the originals — which means Plaintiff
cannot authenticate the records at all. Admission would
unfairly prejudice Defendant.
```

### Combined objection

```
Objection — hearsay (Cal. Evid. Code § 1200), failure of
authentication (§ 1400), and failure to lay the business-
records foundation (§ 1271). Plaintiff has produced no
witness with personal knowledge of Bank of America's regular
course of business or record-keeping practices. The
declaration from Plaintiff's custodian describes only
Plaintiff's internal records; it says nothing about how,
when, or by whom Bank of America's records were created.
The § 1271 foundation is entirely absent.
```

## What plaintiff needs to authenticate original-creditor records

**Option A: Live testimony from an original-creditor custodian**

Bank of America's custodian of records testifies (in person
or by deposition) about:

- BofA's regular course of business in maintaining these
  records
- That the records were made at or near the time of the
  transactions by persons with knowledge
- That these specific records are authentic BofA records

Rare in practice — original creditors rarely cooperate with
debt buyers by making custodians available.

**Option B: Cal. Evid. Code § 1561 declaration from BofA**

Bank of America's duly authorized custodian provides a
written declaration under § 1561 with all required elements.

Also rare — original creditors typically will not provide
such declarations.

**Option C: Plaintiff's own custodian (insufficient)**

Plaintiff's custodian attests to Plaintiff's own records.
This authenticates only what Plaintiff created — not what
Bank of America created. Insufficient to lay the § 1271
foundation for original-creditor records.

## Comparison: California vs. Oregon vs. federal

| Jurisdiction | Hearsay exception | Authentication | Remote custodian |
|---|---|---|---|
| California | Evid. Code § 1271 | § 1400–1552 | Defense-favorable; case law developing |
| Oregon | OEC 803(6) | OEC 901 | Defense-favorable; same analysis |
| Federal (9th Cir.) | FRE 803(6) | FRE 901 | Defense-favorable |

The doctrine is similar across jurisdictions. California
courts follow the same analysis: the debt buyer's custodian
cannot authenticate the original creditor's records.

## Motion in limine strategy

File a **Motion in Limine** before trial to exclude
unauthenticated records:

```
DEFENDANT'S MOTION IN LIMINE NO. [N] TO EXCLUDE
PLAINTIFF'S BUSINESS RECORDS
(Cal. Evid. Code §§ 1200, 1271, 1400)

I. RELIEF REQUESTED

   Defendant moves to exclude from evidence at trial all
   records authored by Bank of America, including the
   alleged cardholder agreement, monthly statements, and
   account history, unless Plaintiff produces a qualified
   witness from Bank of America or a compliant declaration
   under Cal. Evid. Code § 1561 from Bank of America's
   authorized custodian.

II. STATEMENT OF FACTS

   [Procedural history; discovery responses]

III. ARGUMENT

   A. The § 1271 foundation requires personal knowledge
      of the originating entity's record-keeping.

      Cal. Evid. Code § 1271 requires that the writing be
      made in the regular course of a business by a person
      with knowledge. The person must have personal
      knowledge of the originating entity's — here, Bank
      of America's — regular practices. Plaintiff's
      custodian has no such personal knowledge.

   B. The § 1561 declaration from Plaintiff's custodian
      is insufficient for records created by a different
      entity.

      Section 1561 allows the foundation to be laid by a
      declaration, but the declarant must be the custodian
      of the records at issue. Plaintiff's custodian is
      Plaintiff's custodian — not Bank of America's
      custodian. The declaration cannot lay the § 1271
      foundation for records Plaintiff did not create.

   C. Admission without proper foundation would be
      prejudicial.

      Defendant cannot cross-examine a witness on Bank of
      America's record-keeping practices when no such
      witness exists. Admission of unverified records would
      deny Defendant a fair opportunity to test the
      evidence.
```

## Summary judgment application

If the court agrees that the original-creditor records are
inadmissible, Plaintiff has no evidence of:

- The existence of the alleged account
- The alleged balance
- The date of last payment (affecting SOL analysis)
- The contractual terms (affecting the claim for fees and
  interest)

File a **Motion for Summary Judgment / Adjudication under
CCP § 437c** — Plaintiff lacks evidence of an essential
element on its complaint.
