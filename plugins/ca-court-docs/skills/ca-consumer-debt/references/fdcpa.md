# Fair Debt Collection Practices Act — Annotated for California Practice

The federal **FDCPA** is codified at **15 U.S.C. §§ 1692–1692p**.
The CFPB's implementing regulation, **Regulation F**, is at
**12 CFR pt 1006**.

This reference summarizes FDCPA sections most invoked in
California debt-defense practice. For the full text, pull from
uscode.house.gov; for Reg F, see `reg-f.md`.

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Verify current text.

## Coverage — who is a "debt collector"?

The FDCPA applies to **"debt collectors"** — third parties
collecting consumer debt on behalf of someone else. Defined at
15 U.S.C. § 1692a(6):

- Person whose principal business is collecting debts of
  another
- Person who regularly collects debts of another
- Includes debt buyers collecting through their own operations
  (whether the "regularly collects" prong applies is the key
  analysis — see *Henson v. Santander Consumer USA, Inc.*,
  582 U.S. 79 (2017))

Excluded:

- Original creditors collecting their own debt (with narrow
  exceptions — but note: the **Rosenthal Act covers original
  creditors**; FDCPA does not)
- Attorneys collecting incidentally to legal practice
- Government agencies
- Officers of creditor companies acting in that capacity

**California practice note**: When the plaintiff is the
original creditor, FDCPA does not apply directly. Use the
Rosenthal Act (Cal. Civ. Code, § 1788.17) and UCL (Bus. &
Prof. Code, § 17200) instead. For the typical **debt-buyer
plaintiff**, FDCPA generally applies if the debt buyer
regularly engages in collection activity.

## Coverage — what is "consumer debt"?

15 U.S.C. § 1692a(5):

- A debt
- Arising from a transaction in which the money, property,
  insurance, or services were primarily for **personal,
  family, or household purposes**

Commercial debt is not covered. Personal credit-card debt
and personal medical bills are covered.

## Key sections

### § 1692c — Communications

- (a)(1): No communication at unusual times (before 8 AM /
  after 9 PM local time at the consumer's location)
- (a)(2): No communication if the collector knows an attorney
  represents the consumer
- (a)(3): No communication at consumer's workplace if
  employer prohibits
- (b): No communication with third parties (other than
  the consumer's attorney, a credit bureau, or the creditor)
- (c): Cease-and-desist — consumer can demand the collector
  stop all contact; collector must then cease except to notify
  that it is terminating contact or taking specified legal action

### § 1692d — Harassment or abuse

Prohibits any conduct whose "natural consequence" is
harassment, oppression, or abuse. Specific examples:

- Use of violence or threat of violence
- Use of obscene or profane language
- Publication of a debtor list (other than to credit bureaus)
- Repeated or continuous telephone ringing with intent to
  annoy, abuse, or harass
- Causing a telephone to ring repeatedly to annoy

### § 1692e — False or misleading representations

The **workhorse provision** for California debt-defense
counterclaims. Prohibits false, deceptive, or misleading
representations. Specific examples:

- (2): False representation of the character, amount, or
  legal status of the debt
- (5): Threat to take any action that cannot legally be taken
  or is not intended to be taken
- (8): Communicating known false credit information
- (10): Using false representations or deceptive means to
  collect a debt or obtain information about the consumer
- (11): Failing to identify communications as from a debt
  collector (mini-Miranda)

In California debt-buyer cases, most-cited subsections:

- **§ 1692e(2)(A)**: false representation of the amount owed
- **§ 1692e(5)**: threat to sue on time-barred debt; threat
  to sue without proof of standing
- **§ 1692e(8)**: false credit reporting
- **§ 1692e(10)**: filing suit without chain-of-title documents;
  using false FDBPA-deficient complaint as a collection tool
- **§ 1692e(11)**: failure to include mini-Miranda in
  collection letters

### § 1692f — Unfair practices

The catch-all for unfair conduct:

- (1): Collection of any amount not expressly authorized by
  the agreement creating the debt or permitted by law —
  the most-cited subsection; post-charge-off interest not in
  the original contract violates § 1692f(1)
- (2): Demanding payment by post-dated check
- (5): Communicating by post card
- (6): Taking or threatening nonjudicial action when there
  is no present right to do so (no security interest, no
  right of repossession)

Most cited in California:

- **§ 1692f(1)**: collecting inflated post-charge-off interest
  or fees not authorized by the cardholder agreement

### § 1692g — Validation notice

The **validation-notice** requirement. Within 5 days of the
debt collector's **initial communication** with the consumer,
the collector must provide a written notice containing:

- The amount of the debt
- The name of the creditor to whom the debt is owed
- A statement that the consumer has 30 days to dispute
- A statement that, if disputed within 30 days, the collector
  will obtain and mail verification of the debt
- A statement that the collector will provide the name and
  address of the original creditor upon request

The consumer has 30 days to dispute. Until verification is
provided, the collector must cease collection activity.

**Regulation F (12 CFR § 1006.34)** updates and expands the
validation requirements. See `reg-f.md`.

**California / Rosenthal layer**: Civ. Code, § 1788.17
incorporates § 1692g directly; failure to provide a compliant
validation notice is both an FDCPA violation and a Rosenthal
Act violation.

### § 1692j — Furnishing deceptive forms

Prohibits furnishing "flat-rating" letters designed to make
a third party appear to be collecting when it is not.

### § 1692k — Civil liability

The remedies provision:

- (a)(1): Actual damages — out-of-pocket losses, emotional
  distress (with supporting evidence), attorney consultation
  fees, lost time
- (a)(2)(A): **$1,000 statutory damages** (individual action) —
  per action (not per violation), subject to bona-fide-error
  defense
- (a)(2)(B): Class actions — actual damages + lesser of $500K
  or 1% of the debt collector's net worth
- (a)(3): Reasonable attorney's fees and costs (one-way —
  only consumer can recover fees)

The $1,000 cap on individual statutory damages is a real
limitation. For high-volume violations, the Rosenthal Act's
$1,000-per-violation structure and the UCL's 4-year SOL often
produce better results.

**Statute of limitations** (§ 1692k(d)):

- **1 year** from the date of the violation
- *Rotkiske v. Klemm*, 589 U.S. 8 (2019): the clock runs from
  the violation, not from discovery; discovery rule generally
  inapplicable unless equitable tolling applies
- In California, the UCL's 4-year SOL (Bus. & Prof. Code,
  § 17208) provides a longer tail — plead UCL alongside FDCPA

## Affirmative defenses available to debt collectors

- **Bona fide error** (§ 1692k(c)): must show (1) the
  violation was not intentional, (2) resulted from a bona
  fide error notwithstanding (3) procedures reasonably
  adapted to avoid the error
- Consent of the consumer to the communication
- Legal advice of counsel (limited; reliance must be reasonable)

## California-specific pairing — Rosenthal Act

Always plead the Rosenthal Act alongside FDCPA in California
practice:

- Rosenthal covers original creditors (FDCPA does not)
- Rosenthal has $100–$1,000 statutory damages **per violation**
  (FDCPA is $1,000 per action)
- Rosenthal has the same 1-year SOL (Civ. Code, § 1788.30(f))
- UCL borrows both as "unlawful" prong violations with a
  4-year SOL

See `rosenthal-act.md` for Rosenthal-specific counterclaim form.

## Sample FDCPA counterclaim — California

```
SECOND CAUSE OF ACTION — Fair Debt Collection Practices Act
(15 U.S.C. § 1692 et seq.)
[Brought as counterclaim against Plaintiff/Counter-Defendant]

[N]. Plaintiff/Counter-Defendant [LVNV Funding, LLC] is a
"debt collector" within the meaning of 15 U.S.C. § 1692a(6).

[N+1]. The alleged debt is a "consumer debt" within the
meaning of 15 U.S.C. § 1692a(5) — namely, an obligation
arising from a personal credit-card account.

[N+2]. Plaintiff/Counter-Defendant violated the FDCPA in the
following ways:

  (a) By falsely representing the character, amount, and
      legal status of the alleged debt, in violation of
      15 U.S.C. § 1692e(2);

  (b) By threatening to take action — filing this lawsuit —
      that cannot legally be taken, because Plaintiff
      cannot prove chain of title, in violation of
      15 U.S.C. § 1692e(5);

  (c) By using deceptive means to collect a debt, in
      violation of 15 U.S.C. § 1692e(10);

  (d) By filing this action after the statute of limitations
      under CCP § 337 had expired, in violation of
      15 U.S.C. § 1692e(5), § 1692f, and 12 CFR § 1006.26(b);

  (e) By attempting to collect amounts not authorized by the
      original contract, in violation of 15 U.S.C. § 1692f(1);

  (f) By failing to provide a validation notice complying
      with 15 U.S.C. § 1692g(a) and 12 CFR § 1006.34.

[N+3]. As a direct and proximate result, Counter-Claimant
suffered actual damages including time spent on this
litigation, lost wages from court appearances, attorney
consultation expenses, and emotional distress, and is
entitled to $1,000 in statutory damages under 15 U.S.C.
§ 1692k(a)(2)(A).

PRAYER (on this cause of action): Actual damages to be
proven at trial; $1,000 statutory damages under 15 U.S.C.
§ 1692k(a)(2)(A); reasonable attorney's fees and costs under
15 U.S.C. § 1692k(a)(3).
```

## Common California debt-defense FDCPA patterns

| Pattern | Subsections |
|---------|-------------|
| Time-barred debt suit | §§ 1692e(2), e(5), f(1); Reg F § 1006.26 |
| Inflated balance (post-charge-off interest) | §§ 1692e(2)(A), f(1) |
| Failure to validate | § 1692g; Reg F § 1006.34 |
| Unauthorized fees | § 1692f(1) |
| False credit reporting | § 1692e(8) |
| Communication after dispute | §§ 1692c, 1692g |
| Suit filed without chain-of-title | §§ 1692e(5), e(10) |
| Communication after attorney representation | § 1692c(a)(2) |
| Failure to include mini-Miranda | § 1692e(11) |

## SOL strategy — FDCPA vs. UCL

| Statute | SOL | Notes |
|---------|-----|-------|
| FDCPA | 1 year from violation | File counterclaim within 1 year |
| Rosenthal Act | 1 year from violation | Civ. Code, § 1788.30(f) |
| UCL | 4 years | Bus. & Prof. Code, § 17208; borrows FDCPA violations |

Practical implication: for FDCPA violations more than 1 year
old, plead the UCL "unlawful" prong instead to capture the
longer limitations window.
