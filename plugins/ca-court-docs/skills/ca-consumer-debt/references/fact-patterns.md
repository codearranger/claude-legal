# Fact-Pattern Triage — California Consumer-Debt Defense

Five common California debt-defense fact patterns with
defenses, counterclaims, discovery approach, and expected
timeline for each.

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Real cases combine elements of
> multiple patterns. This triage is a starting point.

## Pattern 1: Debt-buyer suit on credit-card debt — FDBPA

**Facts**: Defendant receives summons and complaint. The
plaintiff (e.g., LVNV Funding, Midland Funding, Portfolio
Recovery Associates, Cavalry SPV) alleges defendant owes
a charged-off credit-card account originally with Bank of
America, Capital One, or a similar original creditor.
Plaintiff is not the original creditor — it purchased
the account.

**Key questions to ask immediately**:
- When was the last payment? (SOL trigger)
- Does defendant recognize the account? (Identity theft
  possibility)
- Has plaintiff filed in limited civil ($10,000 or less)
  or unlimited civil? (Different procedural rules)
- Is the complaint dated within the past 30 days? (Answer
  deadline clock)
- What does the DFPI database show for plaintiff?

**FDBPA checklist for the complaint**:
- [ ] Name of original creditor alleged? (§ 1788.58(b)(1))
- [ ] Account number or last 4 digits alleged? (§ 1788.58(b)(2))
- [ ] Date of charge-off alleged? (§ 1788.58(b)(3))
- [ ] Balance at charge-off alleged? (§ 1788.58(b)(4))
- [ ] Date of last payment alleged? (§ 1788.58(b)(5))
- [ ] Statement that action is timely? (§ 1788.58(b)(6))
- [ ] Chain of ownership from OC to plaintiff? (§ 1788.58(b)(7))

If any element is missing, plan a demurrer.

**Defenses**:
1. FDBPA noncompliance — demurrer if complaint lacks required
   elements
2. Statute of limitations — CCP § 337 (4 years from last
   payment on written contract)
3. Lack of standing / chain of title — plaintiff cannot
   prove ownership
4. CDCLA non-licensure — check DFPI database
5. Cal. Evid. Code § 1271 foundation — plaintiff's custodian
   cannot authenticate original-creditor records

**Counterclaims**:
1. FDCPA (§§ 1692e, 1692f, 1692g) — if plaintiff is a
   "debt collector" and there are FDCPA violations
2. Rosenthal Act (Civ. Code, § 1788.17) — incorporates
   FDCPA violations; also applies to FDBPA violations
3. UCL (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 17200) — "unlawful" prong via
   FDCPA / Rosenthal / FDBPA / CDCLA violations

**Discovery focus** (first 30 days):
- Serve RFPs 1–9 (cardholder agreement, monthly statements,
  bills of sale, assignment schedules, charge-off itemization,
  pre-suit notice, DFPI license)
- Serve RFAs 1–18 (corporate form, DFPI license, account
  facts, chain of title)
- Serve Interrogatories 1–21 (identity, CDCLA, chain of
  title, balance, FDBPA compliance)

**Timeline**:
- Day 1–30: Answer (or demurrer) due; serve discovery
- Day 30–75: Plaintiff's discovery responses due
- Day 75–120: Assess responses; M&C disputes; potentially
  file motion to compel (45-day deadline from responses)
- Day 120–180: Discovery disputes resolved; assess whether
  plaintiff can authenticate chain-of-title documents
- Day 180+: Motion for summary judgment / adjudication if
  plaintiff lacks evidence

**Likely outcome**: With aggressive discovery, defendant
typically forces dismissal or favorable settlement. Plaintiff's
inability to produce authenticated chain-of-title documents,
combined with FDBPA non-compliance and SOL issues, is fatal
in most cases.

## Pattern 2: Original creditor lawsuit

**Facts**: The original creditor — Bank of America, Synchrony
Bank, Capital One, or a similar bank — is the plaintiff.
Defendant recognizes the account but disputes the amount,
or acknowledges default but disputes the claimed balance.

**Key differences from Pattern 1**:
- FDCPA may not apply (original creditors are not "debt
  collectors" unless they use a third-party collector —
  see *Henson*)
- FDBPA does NOT apply (FDBPA is only for debt buyers)
- CDCLA may not apply if the original creditor is a licensed
  bank or credit union (check exemptions)
- **Rosenthal Act DOES apply** — covers first-party collectors

**Defenses**:
1. Account stated / timely objection — if defendant disputed
   the account in writing, there is no "account stated"
2. Statute of limitations — CCP § 337 (4 years)
3. Unauthorized charges — identity theft or fraud
4. Failure of consideration — goods or services not received
5. Payment in full — partial or full satisfaction not
   properly credited

**Counterclaims**:
1. Rosenthal Act — applies to original-creditor collection
   practices; any harassing or false representations
2. UCL — "unlawful" prong via Rosenthal Act violations
3. FCRA (15 U.S.C. § 1681s-2) — if false credit-reporting
   entries; requires willful or negligent conduct
4. Civ. Code, § 1717 — reciprocal fees if the cardholder
   agreement has a fee clause

**Discovery focus**:
- Account history (all transactions, payments, disputes)
- Authorization for disputed charges
- Communications between defendant and creditor's collection
  department
- Defendant's prior written disputes (account stated defense)
- Credit-reporting records (FCRA)

**Timeline**: Similar to Pattern 1 but typically faster to
resolution because original creditor usually has the records.

## Pattern 3: Out-of-statute lawsuit (zombie debt)

**Facts**: Plaintiff — either original creditor or debt buyer
— files suit on a debt that is past the applicable California
SOL. Common when debt buyers hold old pools of charged-off
accounts that have aged past 4 years since last payment.

**Key SOL analysis**:
- Written contract: CCP § 337 — 4 years from breach
  (usually date of last payment or charge-off date,
  whichever is earlier)
- Open book account: CCP § 337(a) — 4 years from last entry
- Oral contract: CCP § 339 — 2 years

**Defenses**:
1. Statute of limitations — the primary, often dispositive
   defense; plead prominently
2. No revival — plaintiff has no written acknowledgment
   signed by defendant, and no voluntary partial payment
   referrable to this specific debt (CCP § 360)
3. Borrowing statute — if the debt arose in another state
   with a shorter SOL (CCP § 361)

**Counterclaims** (strong):
1. FDCPA § 1692e(5) — threat to take action that cannot
   legally be taken (suit on time-barred debt)
2. FDCPA § 1692e(2) — false representation of legal status
   of the debt (implying it is legally enforceable when
   it is time-barred)
3. Reg F § 1006.26(b) — collector must not bring legal
   action on debt the collector knows or should know is
   time-barred
4. Rosenthal Act (§ 1788.17 incorporates FDCPA violations)
5. UCL — "unlawful" prong; 4-year SOL extends reach for
   older UCL violations

**Discovery focus**:
- Plaintiff's account history showing dates of last payment
  and charge-off
- All communications plaintiff claims restarted the SOL
- Any alleged "written promise to pay" — specifically request
  the writing and where plaintiff obtained it

**Timeline**:
- File motion for summary judgment early if SOL defense is
  clear from the face of the complaint and plaintiff's records

**Likely outcome**: SOL defense is often dispositive without
extensive discovery if the dates are clear. Move for summary
judgment quickly to avoid discovery costs.

## Pattern 4: Identity theft / mistaken identity

**Facts**: Defendant is sued on an account that is not
theirs — either (a) a fraudster opened an account in
defendant's name, or (b) plaintiff sued the wrong "John Doe"
(common name mistake), or (c) the account was opened by a
family member without authorization.

**Key California statute**: Civ. Code, § 1798.93
(Identity Theft Victims Privacy and Protection Act):

- Defendant can seek a judicial declaration that the debt
  is the result of identity theft (§ 1798.93(a))
- Must provide a written claim to the creditor with a police
  report (§ 1798.93(b))
- If creditor continues collection after notice, liable for
  actual damages, minimum $30,000, punitive damages, and
  attorney's fees (§ 1798.93(c)(2))

**Steps to take immediately**:
1. File a police report for identity theft (required by
   § 1798.93)
2. Send written notice to plaintiff per § 1798.93(b)
   including the police report
3. Dispute the account with all three credit bureaus under
   FCRA § 1681c-2
4. Send a dispute letter to plaintiff under FDCPA § 1692g

**Defenses**:
1. Civ. Code, § 1798.93 — identity theft victims protection
2. General denial — "This is not my account"
3. Lack of standing — plaintiff cannot prove the defendant
   (not the fraudster) is the account holder
4. Authentication challenge — plaintiff must prove through
   admissible evidence that defendant opened and used the
   account

**Counterclaims**:
1. Civ. Code, § 1798.93(c)(2) — if creditor continued
   collection after written notice with police report
2. FDCPA — continued collection after dispute is a violation
3. FCRA (§ 1681i / § 1681s-2) — if inaccurate credit
   entries remain after dispute
4. Rosenthal Act — false representations about defendant
   owing the debt
5. UCL — "unlawful" prong via all of the above

**Discovery focus**:
- Documents plaintiff used to identify defendant as the
  account holder (address, SSN, driver's license records)
- Date and method by which the account was opened
- IP address and device information for online account
  opening (to show it was not defendant's device)
- Signature comparison if account was opened by signature

**Timeline**: These cases often resolve quickly once the
police report is produced; if the creditor continues to
pursue, the § 1798.93 damages are substantial enough to
create strong settlement leverage.

## Pattern 5: CDCLA-unlicensed collection

**Facts**: Plaintiff (a debt buyer or third-party collector)
lacks a valid active DFPI license under Cal. Fin. Code,
§ 100002, and filed this action after January 1, 2022.

**Check the DFPI database first**:
1. Go to https://dfpi.ca.gov/consumers/check-your-financial-
   company/
2. Search plaintiff's exact legal name and any variants
3. Screenshot the results with the date
4. If not found or license expired: pursue the CDCLA defense

**Defenses**:
1. CDCLA non-licensure affirmative defense — plaintiff lacks
   authority to collect debts in California
2. UCL "unlawful" prong — the unlicensed activity is an
   unlawful business practice
3. CCP § 430.10(b) demurrer — lack of capacity to sue (if
   the court agrees that licensure is a condition of
   capacity to maintain an action, analogous to corporate
   standing requirements)

**Counterclaims**:
1. UCL (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 17200) — injunction and
   restitution for unlicensed collection activity
2. Rosenthal Act — the unlicensed collection is itself a
   false representation of plaintiff's legal authority
3. FDCPA — false representation of authority to collect
   (§ 1692e(5))

**Discovery focus**:
- RFP No. 13 (DFPI license documentation)
- RFA Nos. 5–7 (admit/deny DFPI license status)
- Interrogatory No. 2 (describe DFPI license status)

**Likely outcome**: This defense is powerful when confirmed
(check the database carefully — some collectors use related
entities or different legal names). If plaintiff lacks a
license, it creates strong pressure for dismissal and
settlement, and supports a UCL claim for restitution.

## Triage decision tree

```
Is the plaintiff the original creditor?
├── Yes → Pattern 2 (Original creditor)
│          FDCPA inapplicable; Rosenthal + UCL
│          Defenses: account stated; identity theft; SOL
│
└── No (debt buyer) → Continue below

Is the debt past the SOL (4 years for written contract)?
├── Yes → Pattern 3 (Time-barred / zombie debt)
│          SOL is likely dispositive
│          FDCPA / Reg F / Rosenthal counterclaims
│
└── No → Continue below

Does defendant recognize the account?
├── No / Not mine → Check Pattern 4 (Identity theft)
│                    § 1798.93; police report; disputes
│
└── Yes → Continue below

Is plaintiff DFPI-licensed?
├── No or expired → Add Pattern 5 (CDCLA)
│                    UCL "unlawful"; affirmative defense
│
└── Yes → Continue below

Does plaintiff's complaint comply with FDBPA § 1788.58?
├── No → Pattern 1 with demurrer
│         File demurrer for missing elements
│
└── Yes → Pattern 1 (Debt-buyer suit)
           Chain of title + Evid. Code § 1271 focus
```

## Combinations are the norm

Most cases combine elements. For example:

- Debt buyer suing on a time-barred debt without DFPI
  license → Patterns 1 + 3 + 5
- Debt buyer suing with FDBPA-deficient complaint →
  Patterns 1 + demurrer
- Original creditor sued the wrong person → Pattern 2 + 4

When in doubt, work through each pattern. The defenses
and counterclaims overlap, but the strongest argument
and the discovery focus vary.

## Strategic priorities in California debt defense

1. **SOL defense** — if available and provable from the face
   of the complaint, this is usually dispositive

2. **CDCLA non-licensure** — check the DFPI database first;
   if confirmed, this is a powerful affirmative defense and
   UCL predicate

3. **FDBPA demurrer** — check the complaint against all
   § 1788.58 requirements; missing elements support a
   demurrer that can force the case to early resolution

4. **Identity theft** — if applicable, the § 1798.93
   mechanism is effective and damages-rich

5. **Chain of title + Evid. Code § 1271** — strong but
   requires discovery to develop; the process takes months
   but the payoff is high

6. **Counterclaims (FDCPA / Rosenthal / UCL)** — for
   leverage, attorney's fee recovery, and damages
