# Chain of Title — Debt-Buyer Cases in California

A California debt-buyer plaintiff must prove every link in
the chain from the original creditor to itself. Since January
1, 2014, the **Fair Debt Buying Practices Act** (FDBPA),
Cal. Civ. Code, §§ 1788.50–1788.64, expressly requires this
showing — both in pre-suit documentation (§ 1788.52) and in
the complaint itself (§ 1788.58). Chain of title is the
principal evidentiary battleground in California debt-defense
litigation.

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Verify each code section before citing.

## The typical California debt-buyer chain

```
Original Creditor (e.g., Bank of America, N.A.)
                ↓
                ↓  Bulk Sale Agreement (master agreement governing
                ↓  the pool sale; contains representations and
                ↓  warranties about the accounts)
                ↓
                ↓  Bill of Sale (transfer document for the pool)
                ↓
                ↓  Assignment Schedule (account-level spreadsheet
                ↓  identifying each account by number and balance)
                ↓
First Debt Buyer (e.g., Sherman Acquisition / Asset Acceptance /
                   CACH LLC)
                ↓
                ↓  Same documents for the resale
                ↓
Second Debt Buyer (e.g., LVNV Funding / Encore Capital Group)
                ↓
                ↓  Possible additional resales
                ↓
Current Plaintiff (e.g., Midland Funding LLC /
                    Portfolio Recovery Associates / Cavalry SPV)
```

For the current plaintiff to prove standing, **every link** must
be documented and authenticated. Defects in any link defeat
the chain.

## FDBPA requirements — what plaintiff must have and allege

### Pre-suit documentation — Civ. Code, § 1788.52

Before a debt buyer can collect, it must have:

- Documentation of chain of ownership from original creditor
  to the current debt buyer
- The original account number
- The name and address of the debtor
- The nature of the debt
- The balance owed, with an itemization of the principal,
  interest, fees, and other charges

**Failure to have this documentation before filing is itself
a violation of the FDBPA.**

### Complaint requirements — Civ. Code, § 1788.58

A debt buyer's complaint must include:

1. The name of the **original creditor**
2. The **account number** at charge-off, or if unavailable,
   the last 4 digits
3. The **date of charge-off** by the original creditor
4. The **balance at charge-off**
5. The **date of last payment** by the debtor on the account
6. A statement that the **action is timely** (i.e., within
   the SOL)
7. The **chain of ownership** — the names of all entities
   that owned the debt from original creditor to plaintiff

A complaint that omits any of these elements is deficient
under § 1788.58. The defendant may demurrer (CCP § 430.10(e))
and, if the demurrer is sustained, the plaintiff may be
required to amend.

### Pre-suit notice — Civ. Code, § 1788.60

Before filing suit, the debt buyer must send written notice
to the debtor containing:

- The name of the original creditor
- The original account number (or last 4 digits)
- The date of charge-off
- The balance owed as of a specified date
- Statement of the debt buyer's right to collect
- Statement of the debtor's right to dispute

**Failure to provide pre-suit notice** is an affirmative
defense to the collection action under § 1788.60(e).

## Required documents per link in the chain

### 1. Bulk Sale Agreement (master)

The master contract between buyer and seller governing the
pool sale. Typically:

- Many pages of boilerplate
- Defines scope (which accounts; which warranties — and which
  are disclaimed)
- Specifies that account-level detail is in the Assignment
  Schedule

The Bulk Sale Agreement alone is insufficient — it shows the
seller's intent to sell some accounts, but not specifically
the defendant's account.

### 2. Bill of Sale

The transfer document. Typically:

- One to two pages
- "Seller transfers all right, title, and interest in the
  accounts listed on Schedule A to Buyer"
- Signed by authorized officers of both seller and buyer

The Bill of Sale alone is insufficient — it incorporates the
Assignment Schedule.

### 3. Assignment Schedule

The account-level spreadsheet. Typically:

- A multi-page spreadsheet identifying each account by:
  - Account number (often partial)
  - Cardholder name
  - Date of last activity / charge-off
  - Balance at charge-off

**Debt buyers frequently produce only redacted assignment
schedules** — defendant's row visible but all others blacked
out. This is inadequate authentication for the same reasons
as in Oregon:

- Redaction conceals the schedule's structure
- Court cannot verify the schedule existed at the time of
  the alleged transfer
- Defendant cannot evaluate the accuracy of their data row

Motion to compel unredacted schedule: California CCP
§ 2031.310 (RFPs); separate statement under CRC rule 3.1345.

### 4. Cardholder Agreement (original)

The contract between defendant and the original creditor.
Establishes:

- The terms of the credit relationship
- Applicable interest rate and fees
- Any arbitration clause
- Governing law

Cardholder agreements are typically **not** in the debt
buyer's possession. Failure to produce the cardholder
agreement undermines:

- Plaintiff's proof of the contract
- Plaintiff's claim for interest at a specific rate
  (§ 1692f(1) bars interest not authorized by the contract)
- Plaintiff's claim for fees and penalties

### 5. Monthly Statements

Showing account history. Establishes:

- Balance accuracy
- Date of last payment (SOL trigger)
- Whether charges were disputed
- Post-charge-off interest and fees (may be unauthorized
  under FDCPA § 1692f(1) and Rosenthal Act § 1788.14(a))

Monthly statements are also typically not in the debt
buyer's possession.

## Common chain-of-title failures

### Failure 1: Missing cardholder agreement

Plaintiff cannot produce the original cardholder agreement;
the original creditor did not include it in the data sale.

**Implications**:
- No proof of the contract terms (interest rate, fees)
- Cannot establish that post-charge-off interest is authorized
- Cannot authenticate original-creditor records under
  Cal. Evid. Code § 1271 without the underlying document

### Failure 2: Redacted assignment schedule

Plaintiff produces a schedule with only defendant's row visible.

**Implications**:
- Authentication fails — court cannot evaluate the integrity
  of the schedule
- Specific objection: Cal. Evid. Code § 1271 requires that
  the record be kept in the regular course of business; a
  record that has been heavily edited post-hoc is not the
  original record

### Failure 3: Bill of Sale without Schedule A

The Bill of Sale references "Schedule A" but no Schedule A
is produced, or it is a generic listing without account-level
detail.

**Implications**:
- No specific transfer of defendant's account documented
- FDBPA § 1788.58 requires the chain to be alleged in the
  complaint — failure to produce the underlying documents
  supports dismissal

### Failure 4: Gap in the chain

Transfer documents show: Original Creditor → Debt Buyer A;
and Debt Buyer B → Current Plaintiff; but no document for
Debt Buyer A → Debt Buyer B.

**Implications**:
- Plaintiff cannot prove ownership traces to original creditor
- FDBPA § 1788.58 requires the complaint to name all entities
  in the chain — a gap is both a pleading deficiency and an
  evidentiary failure

### Failure 5: Defective authentication under Cal. Evid. Code § 1271

Plaintiff offers the documents but cannot authenticate them
through a custodian with personal knowledge of the **original
creditor's** record-keeping practices.

## FDBPA pleading deficiency — demurrer strategy

If the complaint fails to include all § 1788.58 required
elements, file a **Demurrer under CCP § 430.10(e)** —
failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of
action.

```
DEFENDANT'S DEMURRER TO PLAINTIFF'S COMPLAINT
(CCP § 430.10(e); Civ. Code, § 1788.58)

Plaintiff [LVNV Funding, LLC], a debt buyer, has filed this
collection complaint without including the information
required by Cal. Civ. Code, § 1788.58. Specifically, the
Complaint fails to allege:

(a) The date of charge-off [§ 1788.58(b)(3)];
(b) The balance at charge-off [§ 1788.58(b)(4)];
(c) The complete chain of ownership from the original
    creditor to Plaintiff [§ 1788.58(b)(7)].

These are mandatory elements for a debt-buyer complaint.
Plaintiff's failure to include them renders the Complaint
deficient on its face. Defendant respectfully requests the
Court sustain this Demurrer with leave to amend, or without
leave to amend if amendment cannot cure the deficiency.
```

## Discovery approach to chain of title

Defense discovery targets the documents listed above.
In California:

1. **Cardholder agreement** — RFP No. 1
2. **Monthly statements** for the 48-month period before
   charge-off — RFP No. 2
3. **Bill of Sale** for each transfer in the chain — RFP No. 3
4. **Assignment schedule** (unredacted) for each transfer
   — RFP No. 4
5. **Bulk Sale Agreement** for each transfer — RFP No. 5
6. **Account history** (post-charge-off from each holder)
   — RFP No. 6
7. **Pre-suit notice** under § 1788.60 — RFP No. 7

California discovery: 30-day response deadline; **45-day
window to move to compel further responses** from the date
the response is served (CCP § 2031.310(c)).

## UCC Article 9 and assignment of payment rights

Some debt transfers involve security interests in payment
intangibles under Cal. Comm. Code, § 9102(a)(61). For
credit-card debt, the relevant concepts are:

- **Assignment of payment intangibles** (Cal. Comm. Code,
  § 9102(a)(61)): the right to payment not evidenced by an
  instrument, arising from a contract — includes credit-card
  debt
- **Notification of assignment** (Cal. Comm. Code, § 9406):
  the account debtor (consumer) is not obligated to pay the
  assignee until notified
- **Perfection by attachment** for payment intangibles
  (Cal. Comm. Code, § 9309(3)): no filing required; attachment
  is automatic upon sale
