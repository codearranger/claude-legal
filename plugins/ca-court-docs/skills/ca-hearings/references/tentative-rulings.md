# Tentative Rulings — California Superior Court

## What is a tentative ruling?

A tentative ruling is a judge's preliminary written decision on a
pending motion, issued the day before the scheduled hearing. It is
the single most distinctive feature of California motion practice
compared to most other states.

**Governing rule:** California Rules of Court, rule 3.1308.

The tentative ruling:

- Is **not** final — it is the court's preliminary inclination
- May be adopted, modified, or reversed at oral argument
- **Becomes the final ruling automatically** if no party contests
  it by the applicable deadline

---

## Step-by-step: what to do when a tentative posts

### Step 1: Find the tentative

Check the court's website for your department the day before the
hearing. Most courts post by 3:00 p.m. (some by noon or morning):

| Court | Where to find tentative rulings | Typical posting time |
|-------|--------------------------------|---------------------|
| LASC | lacourt.org → "Tentative Rulings" → select department | 3:00 p.m. day before |
| SFSC | sf.courts.ca.gov → Tentative Rulings | By 3:00–4:00 p.m. day before |
| San Diego | sdcourt.ca.gov → Case Search → Tentative | By 3:00 p.m. day before |
| Sacramento | saccourt.ca.gov → Tentative Rulings | By 2:00 p.m. day before |
| Alameda | alameda.courts.ca.gov → Tentative Rulings | By 3:00 p.m. day before |
| Orange | occourts.org → Tentative Rulings by dept | By 1:30 p.m. day before |
| Riverside | riverside.courts.ca.gov | By 3:00 p.m. day before |

If no tentative posts by 4:00 p.m., call the department clerk —
some judges do not post tentatives and expect you to appear.
Others post early on some motions and late on others. Never assume
the absence of a posted tentative means the hearing is canceled.

### Step 2: Read the tentative carefully

Ask:

- Is it in my favor or against me?
- What specific reasons does the judge give?
- If adverse, is there a specific factual gap or legal issue
  identified that I can address at oral argument?
- If favorable, will the other side contest?

### Step 3: Decide whether to contest

**If the tentative is favorable to you:**

- Do NOT automatically contest — you risk triggering oral argument
  that could end with a worse outcome
- Watch whether the other side contests (you should receive
  notification; also check the court's docket)
- If the other side contests, you must appear at the hearing

**If the tentative is adverse to you:**

- You MUST contest to preserve your right to oral argument
- An adverse tentative that is not contested **becomes the final
  order** — no do-overs
- Contest even if you think the judge's mind is made up — oral
  argument gives you a chance to correct factual errors or
  legal misapplications

**If the tentative identifies a specific gap in your papers:**

- Contest and prepare to address that specific issue
- Bring the relevant exhibit or record citation that fills the gap
- Do not attempt to introduce new evidence at oral argument —
  if the record is inadequate, you may need to seek a continuance

### Step 4: Contest the tentative by 4:30 p.m.

You must **both** (a) notify the court and (b) notify opposing
counsel by **4:30 p.m. on the court day before the hearing.**

**Notifying the court (by court):**

- **LASC**: Submit through LACourtConnect at
  appearances.lacourt.org. After logging in, find the hearing and
  select "Request Oral Argument." Alternatively, call the
  department direct line (listed at lacourt.org/division/civil).
- **SFSC**: Call the Civil Department clerk's line or use the
  court's online portal.
- **San Diego**: Call the department or use the court's online
  calendar system.
- **Other courts**: Check the department's standing order;
  most have a posted phone number and instructions.

**Notifying opposing counsel:**
Send an email immediately after contesting with the court:

> "Counsel: I have notified the court that I contest the tentative
> ruling in [Case Name], Case No. [No.], scheduled for hearing on
> [Date] at [Time] in Dept. [No.]. I intend to appear and argue.
> [Name], In Pro Per"

Keep this email. If opposing counsel later claims you did not
notify them, the email is your record.

### Step 5: Appear at the hearing

If you contested the tentative (or the other side did), **you must
appear** — in person or remotely, depending on the court's rules.
If you do not appear after contesting, the court may impose
sanctions or simply adopt the tentative without further notice
to you.

## When no tentative is posted

Some courts and judges do not issue tentative rulings for all
motions. Possible reasons:

- The judge had insufficient time to prepare a tentative
- The court does not use the tentative system for this motion type
  (e.g., ex parte applications, case management conferences)
- The judge chose not to post a tentative for this motion

If no tentative posts by 4:00 p.m.:

1. Call the department clerk: "I have a hearing tomorrow in
   [Case Name], [No.]. Is there a tentative ruling?"
2. If no tentative, confirm whether the hearing is still on
3. Appear as scheduled

---

## Oral argument after a tentative

California law-and-motion oral argument is short — typically
**5 minutes per side** for routine motions (discovery, demurrers)
and **10-15 minutes per side** for summary judgment.

When you appear:

1. State your appearance: "[Name], In Pro Per, appearing for
   the defendant."
2. Address the tentative directly. If adverse:
   > "Your Honor, the tentative denies the motion on the ground
   > that [state the ground]. I would ask the Court to reconsider
   > because [specific reason]. The record shows [specific cite]."
3. Focus on the gap the tentative identifies — not a general
   re-argument of the briefs.
4. If the tentative is based on a legal error, cite the correct
   authority.
5. Ask for the specific order you want.

The judge may:
- Adopt the tentative as written — "The tentative stands. [Relief
  stated in tentative]."
- Modify the tentative — "The tentative is modified to provide
  [X]."
- Reverse the tentative — "The Court has reconsidered the
  tentative. The motion is [granted/denied]."
- Take the matter under advisement — written ruling to follow.

---

## The order after the tentative

If the court adopts the tentative at the hearing:

- **Some courts** (including most LASC departments): the
  prevailing party must prepare and serve a written Order
  reflecting the ruling. See `ca-submit-order` for the procedure.
- **Other courts**: the clerk enters the minute order, which
  constitutes the order of the court.

Check with the specific department whether a formal order is
required. When in doubt, prepare a proposed order and submit it
to the other side for approval; then submit to the court.

## Tentative ruling — common mistakes

| Mistake | Consequence |
|---------|-------------|
| Not checking the tentative until morning of the hearing | 4:30 p.m. deadline already passed; adverse tentative is final |
| Notifying only the court but not opposing counsel | Technically non-compliant with CRC 3.1308; creates disputes |
| Notifying only opposing counsel but not the court | Court may not know to hold a hearing; tentative may go final |
| Failing to contest an adverse tentative | Adverse ruling becomes the final order |
| Contesting and then not appearing | Court may impose sanctions; tentative may stand |
| Re-arguing the entire brief at oral argument | Wastes limited time; judge already read the briefs |
| Attempting to introduce new evidence at argument | Courts do not accept new evidence at oral argument; bring a request for continuance if necessary |
