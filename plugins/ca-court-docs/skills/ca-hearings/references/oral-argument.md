# Oral Argument — California Law-and-Motion

## Purpose of oral argument in California

Oral argument in California law-and-motion is short and focused.
The judge has already read the briefs, reviewed the tentative, and
formed a preliminary view. Oral argument exists to:

1. Clarify factual or legal points the judge is uncertain about
2. Answer specific questions the judge raises at the hearing
3. Correct errors in the tentative ruling
4. Give the losing party one more chance to show why the tentative
   should not be adopted

Oral argument is **not** an opportunity to re-read the brief or
re-present all the arguments in the papers. Doing so wastes time
and frustrates the court.

---

## Time limits

California law-and-motion hearings are typically:

- **5 minutes per side** for routine motions (discovery motions,
  demurrers, motions to strike, motions to compel)
- **10-15 minutes per side** for complex motions (summary
  judgment, anti-SLAPP, motions in limine)
- **Up to 30 minutes per side** for exceptional summary judgment
  hearings by advance arrangement with the department

Some LASC departments are stricter; some are more generous.
Prepare as if you have 5 minutes and you will be ready for any
court. If the judge wants to hear more, they will ask.

---

## Preparation

### Before you appear

1. **Read the tentative ruling.** Know whether it is favorable
   or adverse. If adverse, identify the specific ground(s).
2. **Select 2-3 main points.** For a 5-minute argument, you
   have time for at most 2-3 points. Choose your strongest.
3. **Anticipate judge questions.** What is the hardest question
   the judge could ask? What would you say? Practice the answer.
4. **Prepare a closing line.** The specific order you want
   ("I ask the Court to grant the motion and order further
   responses within 14 days and impose sanctions of $800.").
5. **Know your record.** Know your exhibit numbers and
   declaration paragraph numbers for any key facts. Do not
   say "it's in there somewhere" — say "Doe Declaration,
   paragraph 4" or "Exhibit A, page 2."

### Notes format

Write notes on a single half-page card:

```
OPENING:    Jane Doe, In Pro Per, for Defendant.
            Motion: compel further responses, RFP 1-6.
            Relief: further responses in 14 days; $800 sanctions.

TENTATIVE:  [What it says — one sentence summary]
            [Your counter — one sentence]

POINT 1:    [Strongest argument — 2 sentences max]
            [Record cite]

POINT 2:    [Second argument]
            [Record cite]

Q: [Most likely hard question]  A: [Your answer]

CLOSE:      Grant the motion; further responses within 14 days;
            $800 in sanctions. Thank you, Your Honor.
```

## The argument structure

### 1. State your appearance (mandatory)

When the judge calls the case, stand and state your appearance:

> "Good morning, Your Honor. Jane Doe, In Pro Per, appearing for
> the defendant, Case Number 25STCV12345."

If you are appearing remotely:

> "Good morning, Your Honor. Jane Doe, appearing remotely for
> the defendant, In Pro Per."

The clerk needs your appearance on the record. This is not
optional, even in a two-minute hearing.

### 2. State the motion and the relief you seek (15 seconds)

> "I am here on Defendant's Motion to Compel Further Responses to
> Requests for Production. I am asking the Court to order Plaintiff
> to serve further responses within 14 days and to award $800 in
> sanctions."

This orients the judge and focuses the argument before the 
tentative ruling.

### 3. Address the tentative (30-60 seconds if adverse)

If the tentative is in your favor:
> "Your Honor, I am prepared to rest on the tentative unless the
> Court has questions."

If the tentative is adverse:
> "The Court's tentative denies the motion on the ground that
> the meet-and-confer was insufficient. I would ask the Court to
> reconsider. The meet-and-confer letter — Exhibit C to my
> declaration — identifies each deficient response by number and
> requests supplementation within 14 days. Plaintiff did not
> respond at all. I submit that constitutes the required good
> faith attempt under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310,
> subdivision (b)(2)."

### 4. Your strongest point (2 minutes)

State it cleanly. Lead with the rule, follow with the fact:

> "Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.010, Plaintiff
> must produce all documents in its possession, custody, or
> control that are relevant to the subject matter of this action.
> The cardholder agreement is the document that would either
> prove or disprove Plaintiff's ownership of this account.
> Plaintiff claims to own the account. It cannot simultaneously
> refuse to produce the document evidencing ownership on grounds
> that the request is irrelevant."

### 5. Anticipate the opposing argument (1 minute)

> "Plaintiff will argue that the assignment schedule contains
> proprietary information about other accounts. That is a
> trade-secret objection, not a relevance objection, and the
> response is a protective order — which Defendant has offered
> to stipulate to. Plaintiff has not agreed to a protective order;
> it simply refuses to produce. That is not a valid basis for
> withholding."

### 6. Close (20 seconds)

> "For these reasons, Defendant respectfully requests that the
> Court grant the motion, order Plaintiff to serve further
> responses within 14 days, and award monetary sanctions of $800.
> Thank you, Your Honor."

## Answering judge questions

The judge will interrupt. This is normal and typically a good sign
— judges ask questions when they are engaged with the motion and
want to be persuaded.

**Rules:**

1. **Stop immediately when the judge starts speaking.** Mid-sentence
   if necessary. Never talk over a judge.
2. **Answer the question first.** Yes, no, or "the answer is X."
   Do not pivot to a different topic before answering.
3. **Cite the record.** "Your Honor, the meet-and-confer letter is
   at Exhibit C, page 2, first paragraph."
4. **If you don't know the answer:** "Your Honor, I don't have that
   information immediately available. I believe it appears in the
   declaration at paragraph 7, but I would not want to misstate
   the record."
5. **If the question identifies a gap in your case:** Acknowledge
   it honestly. "Your Honor, that is a fair point. The record does
   not contain that specific document — I would address it this way..."

**Never:**

- Dodge the question and pivot to your talking points
- Say "that's not relevant" to a judge's question
- Argue back with "but what I'm trying to say is..."
- Repeat the same argument you already made if the judge signals
  it hasn't moved them

---

## Listening to opposing party

When the other side argues:

- **Take notes.** Write down specific claims you want to rebut.
- **Do not interrupt.** Wait your turn.
- **Request rebuttal** if it is important: "Your Honor, may I
  briefly respond?" Courts often allow a 1-minute rebuttal.

In rebuttal, respond only to specific new points the other side
raised. Do not repeat your opening argument.

---

## If the judge signals they are not persuaded

Some judges will signal mid-argument that they are skeptical:

> "Counsel, I have read the briefs. I understand your argument.
> I'm not persuaded that [X]. What else do you have?"

When this happens:
- **Acknowledge the signal.** "I understand the Court's concern."
- **Move to your next strongest point.** Don't keep hammering
  the point the judge rejected.
- **Concede if appropriate.** "Your Honor, I recognize the Court
  may rule against me on [Issue A]. I would ask the Court to
  consider, at minimum, [Issue B]."

Tenacity is appropriate; stubbornness is not. Know when to
shift focus.

---

## After the argument

When the judge rules:

- **Write it down.** If the judge rules from the bench, note the
  exact language.
- **Listen for the terms of the ruling.** "The motion is granted"
  — to what extent? For all requests or some? Within how many days?
  With or without sanctions?
- **If unclear, ask.** "Your Honor, may I clarify — is the Court
  ordering further responses to all six requests, or only Nos. 1
  through 4?"

The prevailing party typically prepares the proposed Order After
Hearing. See `ca-submit-order` for procedure.
