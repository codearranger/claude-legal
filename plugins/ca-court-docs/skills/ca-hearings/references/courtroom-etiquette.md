# Courtroom Etiquette — California Superior Court

## Before you enter the courthouse

### What to bring

- A complete copy of your filing packet (motion, opposition, or
  reply; all supporting declarations and exhibits; any working
  copies if required)
- A notepad and pen
- Identification (government-issued photo ID)
- Water (most courthouses allow a sealed water bottle in the
  courtroom; some do not — leave it outside if in doubt)
- No recording devices — recording in a California courtroom
  without the court's express permission is prohibited under
  Penal Code section 146e

### What to wear

California Superior Court hearings are formal proceedings.
Dress to show respect for the court:

- **In-person:** Business or business-casual. For men: slacks,
  collared shirt, and optional jacket. A suit is rarely required
  but always appropriate. For women: equivalent professional
  attire. No jeans, shorts, tank tops, or athletic wear in the
  courtroom.
- **No hats** in the courtroom (except religious head coverings,
  which are always permitted).
- **Remote hearings:** Same standard, top half only. A collared
  shirt or equivalent. Even for a 5-minute motion hearing,
  professional appearance on camera matters.

### Security

Budget extra time for courthouse security — metal detector
screening is standard at all California Superior Court
courthouses. Large courthouses (Stanley Mosk — LASC, Civic
Center — SFSC, Hall of Justice — San Diego) may have 15-20
minute wait times during busy hours (8:00–9:30 a.m.).

**What you may bring through security:**
- Laptops and tablets (typically screened, then returned)
- Cell phones (must be silenced in the courtroom)
- Documents and binders

**What you may NOT bring:**
- Recording devices (audio or video)
- Food (generally not allowed in courtrooms; some courthouses
  ban outside food entirely)

## Entering the courtroom

- **Arrive at least 15-20 minutes before** your scheduled hearing
  time for in-person hearings. Law-and-motion calendars often
  have many matters; the judge calls them in a set order and may
  call yours earlier than expected.
- **Sign the appearance sheet** if the courtroom has one (some
  LASC departments use an appearance sheet by the door; write
  your name, case number, and "In Pro Per / Defendant").
- **Sit in the gallery** until the judge calls your case. Do not
  approach the bench or counsel table until called.
- **Silence your phone.** Turn it off or to airplane mode. A
  ringing phone in the courtroom will annoy the judge and may
  result in a contempt warning.

---

## When the judge enters

- **Stand** when the judge enters the courtroom. The bailiff
  (or clerk) will typically announce "All rise" or "The Honorable
  [Name], presiding." Stand immediately.
- **Remain standing** until the judge sits down and says "Be
  seated" or equivalent.
- If you are already at counsel table when the judge enters,
  rise immediately.

---

## When your case is called

- **Stand.** The judge reads the case name or number; stand as
  soon as you hear it.
- **Approach counsel table** when directed by the judge. There is
  typically a plaintiff's table and a defendant's table; if unclear,
  ask the clerk which side is for the defendant.
- **State your appearance for the record.** Immediately after
  reaching the table:
  > "Good morning, Your Honor. [Full name], In Pro Per, appearing
  > for the defendant."
  Do not begin argument before stating your appearance.

---

## During the hearing

### How to address the judge

- Always: **"Your Honor"**
- Never: Judge [last name] in direct address (use in third person
  only: "The Court has already ruled that...")
- Never: "Ma'am" or "Sir" or the judge's first name

### How to refer to parties

- Refer to the other party as **"Plaintiff"** (or their actual
  name if multiple parties may cause confusion)
- Refer to yourself as **"Defendant"** or "[your name]" — not "I"
  in formal argument (though "I" is acceptable in conversational
  answers to the judge's questions)
- Refer to opposing counsel as **"Counsel"** or "Plaintiff's
  counsel" — not by their first name

### Standing and sitting rules

- **Stand when speaking** to the court — whether at the lectern
  or at counsel table. Some judges allow argument while seated;
  default to standing unless instructed otherwise.
- **Sit when the judge is questioning the other side** — there
  is no need to stand while the other side is speaking.
- **Stand when you have anything to say** — even a brief "Your
  Honor, may I briefly respond?" — stand first, then speak.

### Interruptions

- **Never interrupt the judge.** If the judge starts talking,
  stop immediately. Even mid-sentence.
- **Never interrupt opposing counsel** while they are arguing.
  Note the point you want to rebut; request rebuttal time after
  they finish.
- If the other side interrupts you, simply pause and let the judge
  manage it. Do not argue with opposing counsel directly; address
  the court: "Your Honor, if I may continue..."

### Addressing opposing counsel at counsel table

- Do not speak directly to opposing counsel during the hearing
  unless the judge invites a discussion between counsel.
- Do not make faces, shake your head, or gesture in response
  to opposing counsel's argument — the judge notices.
- Save reactions for your rebuttal argument: address the court,
  not opposing counsel.

---

## Presenting documents at the hearing

- **Do not bring materials to the judge's bench** without
  permission. If you have something to show the court ("Your
  Honor, may I direct the Court's attention to Exhibit A?"),
  ask permission first.
- **Have your documents organized** — tabbed and labeled — so
  you can locate any exhibit immediately when the judge asks.
- **Working copies:** If local rules (e.g., LASC, SFSC) require
  working copies to be delivered before the hearing, ensure they
  were delivered on time. Failure to deliver working copies where
  required may result in the motion not being heard.

---

## After the hearing

### When the judge rules

- **Write down the ruling.** Whether in your favor or against,
  note the exact language.
- **Thank the judge:** "Thank you, Your Honor." Brief, not
  effusive.
- **Remain at counsel table** until the judge moves to the next
  matter. Do not immediately gather your papers and rush out —
  the judge may have a follow-up question.

### If you need clarification

- Ask before leaving the courtroom: "Your Honor, may I ask for
  clarification — does the order require responses to all six
  requests, or only those as to which the Court found objections
  were improper?"
- Keep it brief. The judge has other matters on the calendar.

### Submitting the order after the hearing

In most California Superior Court departments, the **prevailing
party** must prepare, serve, and submit the formal written order.
See `ca-submit-order` for procedure. Do this within the time frame
set by the court or local rule (typically within 15 days of the
hearing).

---

## Special situations

### If you are nervous

Nervousness is normal and visible but manageable:

- Breathe deliberately before starting.
- Slow down — nervous speakers often talk too fast.
- Use your notes — the judge does not think less of a pro se
  litigant for referring to prepared notes.
- Make eye contact with the camera (remote) or with the judge
  (in-person) as naturally as you can.

### If you make a mistake

Stop, correct yourself, and move on:

> "I apologize, Your Honor — I misstated the date. The
> meet-and-confer letter was sent on May 10, not May 1. That
> is at Exhibit C, page 1."

Do not panic. A calm correction is professional. Trying to
hide a mistake or plow through it makes things worse.

### If the judge seems hostile

California judges are generally professional. If the judge asks
sharp questions or seems skeptical, respond directly and factually.
Do not become defensive or emotional. A judge asking hard questions
is often a sign they are engaged, not that they have decided against
you.

If the judge says something that seems genuinely improper (e.g.,
remarks about your status as a pro se litigant that appear biased),
make a calm, clear objection: "Your Honor, I would ask that I be
permitted to present my argument on the merits." Document the
specific language for any later appeal.
