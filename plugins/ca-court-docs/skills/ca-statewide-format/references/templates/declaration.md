# Declaration Template (California)

A declaration with numbered paragraphs, Code Civ. Proc.,
§ 2015.5 verification clause, signature block, and optional
exhibit list + cover pages. Compliant with CRC 2.100-2.119,
CRC 2.111, and Code of Civil Procedure section 2015.5.

## Structure

1. Attorney/party info block (upper-left, within 2.5")
2. Court name (centered)
3. Caption: party block + case number / document title
4. Horizontal rule below caption
5. Document title body heading: "DECLARATION OF [NAME]"
6. Numbered paragraphs with substantive content
7. Verification clause (Code Civ. Proc., § 2015.5)
8. Date and place of execution
9. Signature block
10. Proof of Service (POS-030 or equivalent)
11. Exhibit cover pages + content (if exhibits attached)

## Key California declaration rules

### Verification under Code Civ. Proc., § 2015.5

The verification clause must include all of:
1. "under penalty of perjury"
2. "under the laws of the State of California"
3. Date of execution
4. Place of execution (city, California)

The standard form is:
> "I declare under penalty of perjury under the laws of the
> State of California that the foregoing is true and correct.
> Executed on [date], at [city], California."

**Common errors to avoid**:
- Omitting "under the laws of the State of California" (making
  it look like a federal declaration, which uses 28 U.S.C. § 1746
  language instead)
- Writing "Executed at [city]" rather than "Executed on [date],
  at [city]" — the date must be in the perjury clause, not just
  in the signature block

### Opening paragraph

The first paragraph should always:
1. Identify the declarant by name and role
2. State that the declarant has personal knowledge
3. State that the declarant could and would testify competently

Standard form: "I, [Name], am the [Plaintiff / Defendant /
Movant / [professional role, e.g., "attorney for Defendant"]] in
the above-captioned action. I have personal knowledge of the
matters stated in this Declaration and, if called as a witness,
could and would testify competently to those matters."

### Exhibit authentication

For each exhibit, include a paragraph in the body stating:

> "Attached hereto as **Exhibit [A]** is a true and correct
> copy of [description of document]."

Add the basis for authentication: how and when you obtained the
document, that it is what you say it is.

### Pro se specific notes

For a self-represented litigant:
- Use "Defendant, self-represented" (or "Plaintiff, self-
  represented") in the signature block — not "pro se" (California
  state-court convention)
- You may sign and date the declaration; no notarization required
  for Code Civ. Proc., § 2015.5 declarations
- Your address in the attorney/party info block and in the
  Proof of Service is your home or business address — you do NOT
  need to disclose a home address if using a mailbox or P.O.
  box, but you must have a reliable address for service

### Proof of Service requirement

Every filed document must be served on all other parties. The
Proof of Service (POS-030 Judicial Council form, or an
equivalent declaration of service) is typically:
- Filed as the last page of the document it accompanies, OR
- Filed separately as a stand-alone Proof of Service

For eFiled documents served through Odyssey or File &
ServeXpress: electronic service is complete on transmission
(Code Civ. Proc., § 1010.6(b)(3)); the eFiling portal generates
a service confirmation that can substitute for a separate POS.
Retain the confirmation for your records.

## What NOT to include in a declaration

- **Legal argument**: a declaration states facts, not legal
  conclusions. Legal argument belongs in the Memorandum of
  Points and Authorities. Exception: a brief statement of the
  legal basis for the motion (e.g., "Plaintiff failed to respond
  to Special Interrogatories within the time permitted by Code
  Civ. Proc., § 2030.260") is acceptable in the declaration
  as a contextual statement.
- **Hearsay (without foundation)**: if the declarant doesn't
  have personal knowledge, they cannot state the fact as fact.
  Use "I was told by [person] that..." with the appropriate
  hearsay exception foundation, or omit.
- **Improper lay opinion**: statements like "Plaintiff is
  clearly lying" are argumentative; stick to observable facts.
