# Discovery Objections and Responses — California

## Overview

California courts disfavor boilerplate objections. The Supreme Court
and Court of Appeal have repeatedly held that a responding party
must identify with particularity why each objection applies to each
specific request. A form objection like "overbroad, vague, and
burdensome" without any explanation directed to that particular
request is insufficient.

Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.240, subdivision (b), requires
that any objection state whether the responding party is withholding
documents based on that objection. If so, the party must identify
what categories of documents are being withheld.

The analysis below addresses the most common objections in California
civil practice and how to respond to each from the propounding party's
perspective.

---

## 1. Overbreadth

**The objection:**
> "This request is overbroad in scope, time, and subject matter."

**Why it is often insufficient:**
An overbreadth objection must identify what makes the request broad
and explain the burden. A bare assertion of overbreadth is not
sufficient under Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.240(b).

**How to respond:**
In the meet-and-confer letter:

> "Your overbreadth objection to RFP No. 3 does not identify what
> aspect of the request is too broad or propose a narrowing. The
> request is limited to documents constituting the original cardholder
> agreement for one specific account — it is already narrow. Please
> withdraw the objection and produce the responsive documents, or
> identify what narrowing you propose."

If the request is genuinely broad, consider offering a **narrowed
alternative**:

> "Without conceding the validity of your objection, Defendant is
> willing to narrow RFP No. 8 to documents created between January 1,
> 2019 and December 31, 2022. Please confirm whether Plaintiff will
> produce responsive documents within that time frame."

**At the motion to compel:** Argue that Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.240
requires the responding party to identify what is being withheld;
a bare overbreadth objection is no objection at all. Cite *Korea
Data Systems Co. v. Superior Court* (1997) 51 Cal.App.4th 1513, 1516
for the proposition that discovery responses must be code-compliant
and identifying in nature.

## 2. Vague and ambiguous

**The objection:**
> "This request is vague, ambiguous, and unintelligible."

**Why it is often insufficient:**
A responding party is required to make a **good faith effort** to
respond to the extent the request is clear, and to ask for
clarification in a meet-and-confer rather than simply objecting.
(*Deyo v. Kilbourne* (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 783.)

**How to respond:**
> "Your vagueness objection does not identify what term or phrase
> is ambiguous. Defendant uses 'cardholder agreement' to mean any
> document constituting the terms of the credit card relationship
> between [Original Creditor] and Defendant. If you interpret the
> phrase differently, please contact us and we will provide a
> definition. Standing on a vagueness objection without identifying
> the ambiguity or seeking clarification is not a good faith response."

**Alternative:** Offer a definition. Add a definitions section to
future discovery sets that defines all potentially ambiguous terms.

---

## 3. Burdensome and oppressive

**The objection:**
> "This request is unduly burdensome, oppressive, and harassing."

**Why it is often insufficient:**
Code of Civil Procedure section 2017.020 allows a court to limit
discovery that is "unduly burdensome or expensive." But the burden
must be **quantified** — the responding party cannot simply assert
burden without evidence.

*West Pico Furniture Co. v. Superior Court* (1961) 56 Cal.2d 407:
the party seeking to limit discovery must make a "specific showing"
of burden.

**How to respond:**
> "Your burden objection does not quantify the alleged burden or
> identify any specific documents that would be costly to produce.
> Plaintiff is a commercial debt buyer whose business involves
> maintaining account-level records — the burden of producing
> documents relating to one account is de minimis. Without a
> specific showing, the objection fails. Please supplement or we
> will move to compel."

**At the motion:** If the responding party submits a declaration of
burden in opposition, challenge the declaration's specificity and
proportionality to the needs of the case.

## 4. Attorney-client privilege

**The objection:**
> "This request calls for documents protected by the attorney-client
> privilege."

**When valid:** Communications between the client and its attorney
made **for the purpose of seeking legal advice** are privileged.
(Evid. Code, §§ 950-962.) The privilege belongs to the **client**.

**How to challenge:**
An assertion of privilege without a privilege log is insufficient.
Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.240, subdivision (c)(1)
requires the responding party to provide "sufficient factual
information for other parties to evaluate the merits of that
claim, including, if necessary, a privilege log."

In the meet-and-confer:
> "Your privilege objection to RFP Nos. 4 and 5 does not include
> a privilege log identifying the withheld documents. Under Code
> Civ. Proc., § 2031.240(c)(1), you must identify each withheld
> document with sufficient information (date, author, recipient,
> privilege asserted) for Defendant to evaluate the claim. Please
> provide a privilege log within 14 days or we will move to compel."

**At the motion:** The failure to provide a privilege log can
constitute a **waiver** of the privilege as to those documents.
(*Wellpoint Health Networks, Inc. v. Superior Court* (1997)
59 Cal.App.4th 110, 130.)

**Watch for over-assertion:** Many corporate defendants assert
privilege over ordinary business documents that were reviewed by
in-house counsel. The privilege only attaches to the
**communication for legal advice**, not to the underlying facts
or business documents. A bill of sale reviewed by in-house
counsel is not privileged just because counsel saw it.

## 5. Attorney work product

**The objection:**
> "This request seeks documents protected by the work product
> doctrine."

**Governing statute:** Code of Civil Procedure section 2018.020.
There are two levels:

- **Absolute work product**: writings that reflect an attorney's
  impressions, conclusions, opinions, or legal research and theories.
  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2018.030(a).) **Not discoverable for any
  reason.**
- **Qualified work product**: all other writings prepared by or for
  the attorney for litigation. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2018.030(b).)
  **Discoverable upon a showing of good cause.**

The doctrine extends to work done for **this litigation**. Documents
created in the ordinary course of business — even if later given to
an attorney — are not work product.

**How to challenge:**
> "Your work product objection to RFP No. 6 (account history) does
> not establish that the account history was prepared for litigation.
> Routine business records — account statements, billing records,
> transaction histories — do not become work product because they
> were given to counsel. Please produce the account history or
> identify the specific documents you contend were prepared for
> litigation and include them in your privilege log."

## 6. Third-party privacy

**The objection:**
> "This request seeks confidential information of third parties."

**California is a high-privacy state.** The California Constitution
provides an express right to privacy (Cal. Const., art. I, § 1).
Courts balance the need for discovery against third-party privacy.

**How to challenge:**
Third-party privacy does not automatically preclude disclosure —
the court weighs the **seriousness of the privacy invasion** against
the **relevance** of the information. (*Hill v. National Collegiate
Athletic Assn.* (1994) 7 Cal.4th 1, 35-40.)

**Propose a protective order:** Many third-party privacy objections
can be resolved by agreeing to a protective order limiting use and
disclosure of the produced information to the current litigation.

> "Defendant recognizes that the assignment schedule (RFP No. 3)
> may contain information about other consumers. We are willing to
> stipulate to a protective order designating that document as
> CONFIDENTIAL — ATTORNEYS EYES ONLY / PARTIES ONLY, limiting its
> use to this litigation. Please let us know if Plaintiff will
> agree to a protective order and produce the document."

## 7. Trade secret / proprietary and confidential business information

**The objection:**
> "This request seeks trade secrets and proprietary business
> information."

**Governing statute:** Code of Civil Procedure section 2019.210
(trade secrets in misappropriation cases); Code Civ. Proc.,
§ 2019.060 (protective orders generally). Evid. Code, § 1060
(privilege against disclosure of trade secrets).

**How to challenge:**
The trade secret privilege requires the proponent to **establish**
that the information is a trade secret. (*Jerome's Furniture
Warehouse v. Superior Court* — production of financial information
not automatically a trade secret.) A bulk sale agreement for
receivables portfolios is a commercial document; the mere fact
that a company considers it confidential does not make it a
trade secret.

Propose a protective order:
> "A stipulated protective order designating the assignment schedule
> CONFIDENTIAL would address any legitimate business confidentiality
> concern. Defendant does not intend to publish the document
> publicly. The scheduling and account-identification information in
> it is not competitively sensitive to Plaintiff — it is evidence
> of whether Defendant's account was ever included in a specific
> sale."

## 8. Not reasonably calculated to lead to discovery of admissible evidence / relevance

**The objection (older form):**
> "This request is not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery
> of admissible evidence."

**Note:** California's discovery scope (Code Civ. Proc., § 2017.010)
uses "reasonably calculated to lead to admissible evidence" as an
alternative to direct admissibility — it is an **expansion** of
scope, not a limitation. An objection citing this language as a
reason to refuse production misreads the statute.

Modern California discovery scope is simply "relevant to the subject
matter" — which is broader than relevance to the pleadings.

**How to challenge:**
> "Your relevance objection misreads Code Civ. Proc., § 2017.010.
> That statute defines the scope of discovery broadly as any matter
> relevant to the subject matter of the action. The cardholder
> agreement is directly relevant to whether Plaintiff can prove its
> ownership of the account, which is the central issue of the case.
> The objection is unfounded."

---

## 9. Equally available to requesting party

**The objection:**
> "This information is equally available to Plaintiff / Defendant
> through public records or their own records."

**How to challenge:**
"Equally available" is not a recognized objection under the
California Civil Discovery Act. A responding party cannot refuse
to produce documents simply because the propounding party could
theoretically obtain them elsewhere. The requesting party is
entitled to the responding party's version of the documents,
including any internal annotations, modifications, or the specific
version in the responding party's custody.

> "The equal-availability objection has no basis in the California
> Civil Discovery Act. Defendant is entitled to Plaintiff's own
> records of the account, including Plaintiff's version of the
> assignment documents — not what Defendant might theoretically
> obtain from third parties. Please produce the responsive documents."

---

## Drafting strong responses (responding party)

When *you* are responding to discovery:

1. **Object with particularity.** Every objection must identify
   why it applies to *that* request, not just list generic grounds.

2. **State what you are withholding.** Code Civ. Proc.,
   § 2031.240(b) requires you to identify the categories of
   documents withheld pursuant to each objection.

3. **Produce what is not objectionable.** If a request is
   partially objectionable, respond to the non-objectionable
   portion and object only to the objectionable portion. A blanket
   refusal to produce anything because part of the request is
   overbroad is itself deficient.

4. **Verify the responses.** Unverified responses are a nullity.
   Sign the verification page under penalty of perjury. (Code
   Civ. Proc., § 2031.250.)

5. **Serve a privilege log simultaneously.** If you are withholding
   documents on privilege grounds, serve the log with (or shortly
   after) the responses — not after being pressed.

6. **Meet deadlines.** Late responses — even one day late —
   waive all objections except privilege and work product
   (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300(a)). A missed deadline is
   catastrophic for the responding party.
