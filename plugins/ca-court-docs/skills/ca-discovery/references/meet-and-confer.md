# Meet-and-Confer — California Civil Discovery

## Governing law

The meet-and-confer requirement is codified in the California Civil
Discovery Act:

- **Interrogatories**: Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300(b)(1)
- **Requests for Production**: Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(b)(2)
- **Requests for Admission**: Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.290(b)(1)

All three require the moving party to have made "a reasonable and
good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented
by the motion" before filing a motion to compel further responses.

Additionally, many courts (including LASC) have standing orders or
local rules requiring a telephonic conference and/or a written
letter before accepting the motion.

---

## When meet-and-confer is required

Meet-and-confer is required **before filing** any of the following:

| Motion | Statute |
|--------|---------|
| Motion to compel further interrogatory responses | Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300(b)(1) |
| Motion to compel further RFP responses | Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(b)(2) |
| Motion to compel further RFA responses | Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.290(b)(1) |
| Motion to compel compliance with deposition subpoena | Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.480(b) |
| Motion for protective order (moving party must try to resolve informally) | CRC 3.1020 |
| Motion to quash deposition subpoena | Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.410(c) |

**Note:** A motion to compel initial responses (when the responding
party served nothing at all) does **not** require meet-and-confer —
only motions to compel *further* responses require it.

---

## Timing — the 45-day clock and meet-and-confer

The most critical scheduling fact:

**The motion to compel further responses must be filed within
45 days of service of the verified response** (Code Civ. Proc.,
§§ 2030.300(c), 2031.310(c), 2033.290(c)).

The 45-day deadline is jurisdictional — missing it loses the
right to compel forever (absent a court order).

This means:

- The meet-and-confer letter should go out **within 10-14 days**
  of receiving the deficient response
- Allow 14 days for a response to the letter before escalating
  to a phone call
- If the conference does not resolve the dispute, file within
  the remaining time in the 45-day window

**Sample timeline:**

| Day | Event |
|-----|-------|
| Day 0 | Receive responding party's verified responses |
| Day 7 | Send meet-and-confer letter (detailed) |
| Day 14 | Follow-up phone call if no response |
| Day 21 | Send "impasse" confirmation email |
| Day 28 | File and serve motion to compel + separate statement |
| Day 45 | Jurisdictional deadline (do not miss) |

---

## What the meet-and-confer letter must contain

A sufficient meet-and-confer letter must:

1. **Identify each specific discovery item** at issue by number
   and category (not "your objections were improper")
2. **State your position** on each item — why the objection is
   meritless or the response is incomplete
3. **Request specific supplementation** within a defined deadline
   (10-14 days is typical)
4. **Propose alternatives** if appropriate (e.g., time-limited
   production, redacted production, stipulated protective order
   for confidential documents)
5. **State the consequence**: that if the dispute is not resolved,
   you will file a motion to compel

A one-paragraph letter saying "your objections are improper, please
supplement" is **not** sufficient and will be criticized in the
moving declaration.

## Sample meet-and-confer letter

```
[DATE]

[Opposing Counsel Name]
[Firm Name]
[Address]

Via Email: [email]  /  U.S. Mail

Re:   Meet and Confer — Discovery Responses
      [Party v. Party], Case No. [No.]
      Superior Court of California, County of [County]

Dear [Counsel / Ms. / Mr. ____]:

I write pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310,
subdivision (b)(2), to meet and confer regarding Plaintiff's
responses to Defendant's Requests for Production, Set One
("RFP"), served on [date]. Plaintiff served its responses on
[date]. After review, I believe the following responses are
deficient and require supplementation:

RFP No. 1 — Original Credit Card Agreement

Plaintiff objected on grounds of relevance and overbreadth.
Defendant's position: The original credit card agreement is
directly relevant to whether Plaintiff is the proper assignee
of the debt and whether the terms of the alleged account are
as claimed. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2017.010.) The request is
not overbroad — it is limited to one specific document for
one specific account number. Plaintiff cannot establish its
claim without this document; its refusal to produce it is
not substantially justified.

REQUEST: Please supplement RFP No. 1 by producing all
documents responsive to this request, or provide a verified
statement of inability to comply that identifies what diligent
search was conducted and why no documents were found, no later
than [14 days from date].

RFP No. 3 — Assignment Schedule / Schedule of Accounts

Plaintiff objected on grounds of burden and trade secret.
Defendant's position: Plaintiff's standing to sue depends on
proof that Defendant's account was included in the bulk
assignment from [Original Creditor] to Plaintiff. The
assignment schedule is the core document. The trade-secret
objection, standing alone, is an insufficient basis to refuse
production — Plaintiff must propose a stipulated protective
order if confidentiality is a genuine concern. (Code Civ.
Proc., § 2031.240(c)(1).)

REQUEST: Plaintiff should either (a) produce the assignment
schedule subject to a stipulated protective order, or (b)
explain in writing why it believes a protective order is
insufficient. Please respond no later than [date].

RFP No. 5 — Account History and Statement of Account

Plaintiff provided no response to this request — neither
production, a statement of inability to comply, nor an
objection. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.230.) An omitted
response is a nullity. Defendant will move to compel an
initial response if Plaintiff does not provide one.

REQUEST: Please provide a response to RFP No. 5 no later
than [date].

--------

If we cannot resolve these issues by [date], I will file a
motion to compel further responses under Code of Civil
Procedure section 2031.310. I am available by telephone at
[number] to discuss these issues.

Please confirm receipt of this letter by return email.

Sincerely,

[Name]
In Pro Per / Defendant
[Address]
[Phone]
[Email]
```

## The follow-up call

After sending the letter:

- Wait a **reasonable time** (10-14 days) for a written response
- If no response, call opposing counsel
- Take notes; immediately after the call, send a confirming email:

```
Subject: Follow-up — Meet and Confer, [Case Name], [Case No.]

Dear [Counsel]:

Per our phone call today at [time], I am confirming the
following:

1. You stated that Plaintiff will supplement RFP No. 1 by
   [date].
2. You stated that Plaintiff is unwilling to produce the
   assignment schedule (RFP No. 3) and stands on its
   trade-secret objection. You declined to stipulate to a
   protective order.
3. You agreed to provide a response to RFP No. 5 by [date].

Based on the foregoing, the parties are at impasse as to
RFP No. 3. I will file a motion to compel further responses
as to that item.

Please let me know if I have mischaracterized anything.

[Name]
```

The confirming email is what the meet-and-confer declaration
quotes. Without it, the declaration has no supporting
documentation.

## The meet-and-confer declaration in the motion

The motion to compel must include a **declaration** describing
the meet-and-confer effort (CRC 3.1345(c)):

```
DECLARATION OF [NAME] IN SUPPORT OF MOTION TO COMPEL
FURTHER RESPONSES TO REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION

I, [Name], declare as follows:

1. I am the Defendant in the above-captioned action,
   appearing In Pro Per. I have personal knowledge of the
   following facts.

2. On [date], I served Requests for Production, Set One,
   Nos. 1-6 on Plaintiff. (Exhibit A hereto.)

3. On [date], Plaintiff served its responses. (Exhibit B
   hereto.) Plaintiff objected to all six requests and
   produced no documents.

4. On [date], I sent a detailed meet-and-confer letter
   to Plaintiff's counsel identifying each deficient
   response, explaining my position, and requesting
   supplementation. (Exhibit C hereto.)

5. On [date], I telephoned Plaintiff's counsel and
   confirmed: (a) Plaintiff would supplement RFP No. 1;
   (b) Plaintiff declined to produce documents responsive
   to RFP No. 3. I confirmed the substance of our call
   in writing that same day. (Exhibit D hereto.)

6. Plaintiff has not supplemented any responses as of the
   date of this declaration. The parties are at impasse
   as to RFP No. 3.

7. Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310,
   subdivision (b)(2), the parties have made a reasonable
   and good faith attempt at informal resolution, which
   has been unsuccessful as to RFP No. 3.

I declare under penalty of perjury under the laws of the
State of California that the foregoing is true and correct.

Executed on [date] at [city], California.

______________________________
[Name]
```

## Sanctions request in the motion

Track time beginning with the first meet-and-confer letter:

| Date | Activity | Hours |
|------|----------|-------|
| [Date] | Reviewing deficient responses, researching grounds | 1.5 |
| [Date] | Drafting meet-and-confer letter | 1.0 |
| [Date] | Follow-up telephone call | 0.5 |
| [Date] | Drafting motion, separate statement, declaration | 4.0 |
| [Date] | Hearing preparation | 1.0 |
| **Total** | | **8.0 hrs** |

A California In Pro Per litigant may request sanctions even
without attorney fees — courts may award the **reasonable
value of time** spent. Some courts are more receptive than others;
argue in the alternative that fees at the prevailing California
market rate for a solo practitioner ($200-$350/hour in many
markets) represent the reasonable cost of opposing party's
failure.

## Common meet-and-confer mistakes

| Mistake | Consequence |
|---------|-------------|
| Vague letter ("please supplement your responses") | Fails the "good faith" requirement; motion may be denied |
| No confirming email after telephone call | Declaration lacks documentation; opposing party can dispute what was agreed |
| Sending letter with only 3 days to respond, then filing immediately | Not a "reasonable" meet-and-confer; court may deny and order you to try again |
| Forgetting the 45-day deadline while waiting for meet-and-confer | Jurisdictional; motion cannot be filed after 45 days |
| Meet-and-confer by text only | Courts expect written letters or email; text is insufficient documentation |
