# tn-personal-injury — 2011 tort-reform damages caps (§§ 29-39-102 / 29-39-104) + McClay

## Prompt

I was in a serious car accident in Tennessee — broken back,
spinal cord damage, paralysis in my legs. The other driver
was drunk. What can I actually recover in non-economic and
punitive damages?

## Expected triggers

- `tn-personal-injury`

## Acceptance criteria

### Non-economic damages cap (§ 29-39-102)

- [ ] Identifies the **2011 Tennessee Civil Justice Act** at
      **Tenn. Code Ann. § 29-39-102** as the controlling cap
      on non-economic damages
- [ ] States the standard cap: **$750,000 per injured person**,
      all defendants combined
- [ ] Identifies the **catastrophic-injury elevation to
      $1,000,000** — specifically applicable to **spinal-cord
      injury with paralysis** (one of the enumerated
      catastrophic categories), along with amputation, third-
      degree burns over 40% of body or face, and wrongful
      death of a parent leaving a surviving minor child
- [ ] Catalogs the **§ 29-39-102(c)(2) exemptions** from the
      cap, including (1) intentional infliction of serious
      physical injury; (2) intentional concealment / destruction
      of evidence; (3) **substantial impairment from alcohol /
      drugs**; (4) felony related to the action
- [ ] Recognizes that the **drunk-driver fact pattern likely
      triggers the alcohol-impairment exemption**, removing
      the cap entirely

### Punitive damages cap (§ 29-39-104)

- [ ] Identifies **Tenn. Code Ann. § 29-39-104** as the cap
      on punitive damages
- [ ] States the cap: **the greater of $500,000 or 2x the
      total compensatory damages**
- [ ] Notes the **bifurcated trial** on punitives is automatic
      on request under § 29-39-104(a)(2) — liability /
      compensatory first, then punitives
- [ ] Notes the **same exemption categories** as the non-
      economic cap apply to punitives — alcohol impairment
      removes the punitive cap

### Constitutional challenge

- [ ] Cites ***McClay v. Airport Management Services, LLC*,
      596 S.W.3d 686 (Tenn. 2020)** as upholding the § 29-39-
      102 cap against state-constitutional challenges (jury-
      trial right; equal protection; due process)
- [ ] Notes the caps are firmly in place; constitutional
      attack is foreclosed

### Common-law entitlement to punitives

- [ ] Cites ***Hodges v. S.C. Toof & Co.*, 833 S.W.2d 896
      (Tenn. 1992)** as the controlling standard for
      entitlement to punitives — **clear and convincing
      evidence** that the defendant acted intentionally,
      fraudulently, maliciously, or recklessly
- [ ] Notes that **drunk driving** is well-established as
      satisfying the *Hodges* recklessness standard for
      punitive availability

### Economic damages

- [ ] Confirms economic damages (medicals past + future, wage
      loss, lost earning capacity, future medical needs,
      out-of-pocket costs) are **NOT capped**

## Common failure modes

- States the cap is $250,000 or $500,000 (those are pre-2011
  numbers or other states' caps)
- Misses the catastrophic-injury elevation entirely
- Misses the § 29-39-102(c)(2) alcohol-impairment exemption
  applicable to drunk-driver cases
- Treats the cap as constitutionally vulnerable post-*McClay*
- Confuses GTLA caps ($300k/$700k) with general tort caps
- Asserts economic damages are capped (they're not)
- Says punitives are unavailable on a negligence theory
  without recognizing the *Hodges* recklessness route
