# tn-personal-injury — GTLA $300k/$700k caps + immunity removal + shortened SOL

## Prompt

My elderly mother was hit by a Metro Nashville garbage truck
in a low-speed collision. She's hospitalized with serious
injuries. We want to sue. What's different about suing the
city instead of a private trucking company?

## Expected triggers

- `tn-personal-injury`
- `tn-deadlines`

## Acceptance criteria

### Statutory framework

- [ ] Identifies the governing statute as the **Governmental
      Tort Liability Act (GTLA)** at **Tenn. Code Ann. § 29-
      20-101 et seq.**
- [ ] Frames the GTLA as an **immunity-removal regime**:
      immunity is the default; suit lies only where the GTLA
      removes immunity for the particular conduct

### Immunity-removal category

- [ ] Identifies the negligent-operation-of-motor-vehicles
      immunity removal at **Tenn. Code Ann. § 29-20-202** as
      the controlling category for a city-truck accident
- [ ] Notes the case must be pleaded into a specific removal
      category — bare negligence is insufficient

### Damages caps (§ 29-20-403)

- [ ] States the GTLA caps:
      - **$300,000 per person**
      - **$700,000 per occurrence** (aggregate)
      - **$100,000 per occurrence** for property damage
- [ ] Frames these as **outcome-determinative** in serious-
      injury cases; recommends client-counseling about the cap
- [ ] (Bonus) Notes the cap is independent of the 2011 tort-
      reform non-economic cap; the **lower of the two**
      governs the damages award

### Shortened SOL (§ 29-20-305)

- [ ] Identifies the shortened SOL — **approximately 12
      months** from accrual — as **different from** the
      general 1-year personal-injury clock at § 28-3-104
- [ ] Treats the GTLA SOL as **jurisdictional** and warns
      against relying on the general PI clock

### Bench trial (no jury)

- [ ] Identifies that **GTLA cases are tried to the bench**
      under § 29-20-307 — there is no right to jury trial
      against a governmental defendant
- [ ] Frames this as a strategic consideration affecting case
      valuation, discovery posture, and venue choice

### Discretionary-function immunity overlay

- [ ] Notes that **planning-level / policy decisions** retain
      immunity even within a removal category — *Bowers v.
      City of Chattanooga*, 826 S.W.2d 427 (Tenn. 1992)
- [ ] Distinguishes from **operational-level negligence**
      which is actionable

## Common failure modes

- Treats Metro Nashville as a private defendant with the
  general 1-year SOL and uncapped damages
- Cites $1M or $2M caps (those are the 2011 tort-reform non-
  economic caps, not GTLA)
- Skips the immunity-removal-category framing
- Promises a jury trial against a GTLA defendant
- Misses the discretionary-function immunity overlay
- Mentions "filing a tort claim notice" with the city as if
  it's a separate pre-suit gate (Tennessee's GTLA does not
  have a parallel notice-of-claim statute like some states)
