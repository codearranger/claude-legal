This file gives the **Arkansas-specific framing**: how Regulation F
operates as the CFPB's implementing rule for the FDCPA in an Arkansas
debt-buyer case. Because Arkansas has no comprehensive state
debt-collection statute, Regulation F (with the FDCPA) supplies the
detailed conduct rules.

## What Regulation F is

Regulation F (effective Nov. 30, 2021) is the CFPB rule implementing
and interpreting the FDCPA. It applies to the same "debt collectors"
the FDCPA covers — which captures the **debt buyers** and
**collection agencies** that file Arkansas collection suits. It does
**not** create a separate private right of action; violations are
enforced **through the FDCPA** (a Regulation F violation is generally
actionable as an FDCPA violation), so plead the FDCPA section and cite
the Regulation F rule that fills it in.

## Provisions that do the work in a debt-buyer case

- **Call-frequency presumption (§ 1006.14(b))** — a rebuttable
  presumption that a collector violates the law by calling a consumer
  about a particular debt more than a set number of times within a
  set window (confirm the current counts in the federal text). Useful
  where the consumer has a **call log** (see
  `../../consumer-harm-documentation` framing in the federal skills
  layer) showing excessive calls.
- **Validation notice (§ 1006.34)** — the content and timing of the
  validation notice, including the **itemization date** and
  **itemized amount**. A debt buyer that cannot itemize the balance
  from a date certain has a Regulation-F / § 1692g problem.
- **Limited-content message (§ 1006.2(j))** and **electronic
  communications** (email/text) — opt-out and time/place rules
  (§ 1006.6).
- **Time and place restrictions (§ 1006.6)** — no contact at
  inconvenient times/places; cease-communication on request.

## How it composes in Arkansas

- **With the FDCPA** (`fdcpa.md`) — Regulation F supplies the
  operational detail; the FDCPA supplies the cause of action,
  damages, and fee-shifting.
- **With the validation defense** — a missing or defective validation
  notice supports both a § 1692g claim and a credibility attack on
  the debt buyer's account documentation.
- **With harm documentation** — the call-frequency presumption is
  only as strong as the consumer's contemporaneous **call log**;
  begin one immediately.
