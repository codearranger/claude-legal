# Arkansas Deceptive Trade Practices Act (ADTPA)

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Verify every citation against the current
> Ark. Code Ann. before relying. The 2017 amendments materially
> changed the private right of action — read this section before
> pleading an ADTPA claim.

**Statute:** Ark. Code Ann. **§ 4-88-101 et seq.** (Title 4, Subtitle
7, Chapter 88).

The ADTPA is Arkansas's general consumer-protection / UDAP statute.
Because Arkansas has **no comprehensive mini-FDCPA statute**, the
ADTPA is the closest state-law analog for deceptive conduct — but it
was **substantially narrowed for private plaintiffs by Act 986 of
2017**, so it is **not** a drop-in substitute for the FDCPA in
collection cases.

## What the ADTPA prohibits

The Act declares unlawful a broad list of **deceptive and
unconscionable** trade practices "in business, commerce, or trade":

- § 4-88-107 — the general catalog of deceptive/unconscionable acts
  (false representations, concealment/suppression of material facts,
  unconscionable conduct, etc.).
- § 4-88-108 — concealment, suppression, or omission of a material
  fact in connection with the sale or advertisement of goods or
  services.

The Act is to be construed to protect consumers; its reach to the act
of **collecting** a debt (as opposed to deception in the underlying
transaction) is fact-specific — frame any ADTPA collection theory
carefully and confirm there is no contrary authority (see
`key-cases.md`).

## Act 986 of 2017 — the private-action narrowing (READ FIRST)

**Act 986 of 2017** amended the **private right of action** at
**§ 4-88-113(f)**. The key changes for a private plaintiff:

1. **Actual financial loss required.** A private plaintiff must have
   suffered an **actual financial loss** — not merely a statutory or
   nominal injury. (Volatile element language — verify the current
   text of § 4-88-113(f).)
2. **Reliance required.** The actual financial loss must result from
   the plaintiff's **reliance** on the deceptive practice. This
   reliance element makes the ADTPA materially harder to use than the
   strict-liability FDCPA.
3. **Class-action restriction.** Act 986 **restricts private class
   actions** under the ADTPA. (Verify the precise scope of the
   class-action limitation in the current statute.)

**The Attorney General's enforcement authority is broad and is NOT
subject to these private-action limits.** The AG can seek injunctive
relief, restitution, and civil penalties (see § 4-88-104 / § 4-88-113
enforcement provisions). A consumer who cannot satisfy the private
elements can still **refer** abusive conduct to the AG's Consumer
Protection Division (see `online-sources.md`).

## Remedies on a private claim (verify current text)

Where a private plaintiff **can** satisfy the actual-loss + reliance
elements, § 4-88-113(f) provides for **actual damages** and a
**reasonable attorney's fee** (confirm the current remedy text and
whether any multiplier survives). Punitive damages may be available
on a separate common-law theory but are not an ADTPA-specific
multiplier.

## How to use the ADTPA in a debt case

- **Do not reflexively plead it for ordinary collection conduct.**
  After Act 986, a private ADTPA claim premised on collection conduct
  must clear the reliance/actual-loss bar — the **FDCPA** is usually
  the stronger vehicle for collector misconduct.
- **Reserve the ADTPA** for deception in the **underlying
  transaction** (the original sale, financing, or solicitation) where
  reliance and a concrete financial loss are pleadable.
- **AG referral** is the path for conduct that harms consumers
  broadly but does not produce a pleadable individual reliance loss.
