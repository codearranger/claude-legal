# Arkansas collection-agency licensing — Ark. Code Ann. § 17-24-101 et seq.

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Verify every citation against the current
> Ark. Code Ann. Bond amounts, fee figures, and the exact scope of
> the exemptions are volatile — confirm before relying.

**Statute:** Ark. Code Ann. **§ 17-24-101 et seq.** (Title 17,
Subtitle 2, Chapter 24 — "Collection Agencies").

**Regulator:** the **Arkansas State Board of Collection Agencies**
(the licensing and enforcement body; see `online-sources.md` for the
Board's website and licensee roster).

This is one of Arkansas's most useful **debt-buyer defense hooks**: a
collection agency operating in Arkansas must be **licensed and
bonded**, and an **unlicensed** collector's **capacity / standing to
sue** is challengeable.

## Who must be licensed

The chapter defines "collection agency" broadly to reach businesses
engaged in collecting (or soliciting the collection of) debts owed to
another, and businesses that **buy delinquent debt** and collect it
(debt buyers). A person who operates as a collection agency in
Arkansas without a license is operating unlawfully.

- **License requirement** — a collection agency must hold a current
  license from the Arkansas State Board of Collection Agencies.
- **Bond requirement** — licensees must maintain a surety bond
  (confirm the current amount in the statute / Board regulations).
- **Branch / manager / out-of-state** rules may apply — verify scope
  for an out-of-state debt buyer collecting on Arkansas accounts.

## Exemptions — confirm the plaintiff is actually covered

The chapter contains **exemptions**. Before asserting an "unlicensed
collector" defense, confirm the plaintiff is a covered "collection
agency" and not within an exemption. Typically exempted (verify the
exact current list and scope in § 17-24-103 / the definitional and
exemption sections):

- **Original creditors** collecting their **own** debts in their own
  name (a creditor collecting its own account is generally not a
  "collection agency").
- **Attorneys** acting in the regular practice of law.
- **Banks, trust companies, and certain regulated financial
  institutions.**
- Other categories the statute specifies (e.g., certain real-estate,
  insurance, or governmental collections).

**Practical screen:** if the plaintiff is a **debt buyer** suing in
its own name on a purchased portfolio, it is the paradigm covered
entity and the exemptions usually do **not** apply — but check the
current text, because exemption boundaries shift.

## The capacity-to-sue challenge

The defense theory: a collection agency that is **required** to be
licensed but is **not** licensed lacks the **capacity** to maintain a
collection action in Arkansas, and the suit is subject to challenge.

- Raise it as an **affirmative defense** (lack of capacity / standing)
  in the answer and as a basis for dismissal.
- Frame it under **Ark. R. Civ. P. 9(a)** (capacity is raised by
  specific negative averment) and the licensing chapter.
- **Probe license status in discovery** — interrogatory and RFP
  requests directed to the plaintiff's Arkansas license number,
  status, and bond (see `interrogatories-debt-buyer.md` and
  `rfp-debt-buyer.md`).
- **Verify the roster first** — the Board publishes licensees; confirm
  the plaintiff's (and its servicer's) status before pleading.

**Caution — do not overclaim.** "Unlicensed therefore void" is not a
guaranteed automatic dismissal in every posture; the precise
consequence of non-licensure (capacity bar, defense to the action,
and/or an unfair-practice theory) depends on the current statute and
Arkansas case law. Verify the operative consequence in `key-cases.md`
and the statute before asserting that an unlicensed plaintiff's claim
is categorically barred. Even where it is not a complete bar,
non-licensure is a credibility and pressure point and can feed an
FDCPA § 1692e theory (false implication of a lawful right to collect).
