# Arkansas statutes of limitations — consumer debt

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Verify every period and accrual rule against
> the current Ark. Code Ann. before relying. Limitations analysis is
> fact-specific and frequently dispositive — confirm the dates from
> the account history.

The limitations defense is often the **single most powerful** defense
in an Arkansas debt-buyer case: if the suit is filed after the
applicable period has run, the SOL is a **complete affirmative
defense** to the contract claim, and a suit on time-barred debt can
also ground an **FDCPA** counterclaim (see `fdcpa.md`).

## The two periods that matter for debt

| Obligation | Period | Citation |
|---|---|---|
| **Written contract** | **5 years** | Ark. Code Ann. § 16-56-111 |
| **Oral / unwritten contract & open account** | **3 years** | Ark. Code Ann. § 16-56-105 |

> **Volatile — verify.** The 5-year and 3-year figures above are
> current as researched, but confirm against the live statute before
> relying; limitations periods are amended.

### Why the characterization decides the case

The dispositive question in most credit-card cases is whether the
account is a **written contract** (5 years) or an **open account /
oral contract** (3 years):

- **Written-contract theory (5 years).** Arkansas courts generally
  treat credit-card debt as a **written contract** where a **signed
  cardholder agreement** (or a sufficient writing) exists. The
  plaintiff prefers this characterization — it buys two more years.
- **Open-account theory (3 years).** Where there is **no signed
  agreement** in evidence — common in debt-buyer cases, where the
  buyer cannot produce the original signed agreement — the obligation
  is more naturally an **open account**, governed by the **3-year**
  period of § 16-56-105.

**Defense leverage:** the same evidentiary gap that defeats chain of
title (no original signed agreement) also pushes the account toward
the **shorter 3-year** period. If the plaintiff cannot produce a
signed writing, argue the **3-year** open-account period and pin the
suit as time-barred.

## Accrual — when the clock starts

- The period runs from **breach** (for a written contract) or, for an
  open/revolving account, commonly from the **date of last payment /
  last activity** (fact-dependent — confirm from the account
  statements).
- **Pin down the date of last payment.** Get the account history; the
  date of last payment is usually the cleanest accrual anchor for a
  revolving account.
- **Partial payment / written acknowledgment.** A new promise or
  payment can, in some circumstances, restart or revive a contract
  limitations period — verify the current Arkansas rule before
  conceding that a recent payment reset the clock, and be alert to a
  collector manufacturing a "payment" to re-age the account.

## Judgments — a separate, longer period (revivable)

- A **judgment** is subject to a **longer** limitations period (the
  10-year line, revivable) — Ark. Code Ann. § 16-56-114 and the
  judgment / lien provisions at § 16-65-501 et seq.
- Judgments may be **revived by scire facias** before they lapse —
  relevant where a **default judgment** is already on the books and a
  garnishment surfaces years later (see `ar-post-judgment`). Verify
  the current revival mechanics and period.

## Other periods that can come up

- **Fraud / property damage / general tort** — 3 years (§ 16-56-105),
  with a discovery rule for fraud. Relevant if a counterclaim sounds
  in fraud.
- **ADTPA** — confirm the ADTPA limitations period (it draws on Title
  16, Chapter 56; verify the operative period before pleading; see
  `ar-adtpa.md`).
- **FDCPA** — **1 year** from the violation (15 U.S.C. § 1692k(d)) —
  this is a **federal** period; calendar it separately for any
  counterclaim (see `fdcpa.md`).

## How to plead it

1. **Plead SOL as an affirmative defense** in the answer (Ark. R.
   Civ. P. 8(c)); it is waived if not raised.
2. **Identify the obligation's character** (written vs. open account)
   and state the controlling period.
3. **Establish the accrual date** (last payment / last activity /
   breach) from the account history.
4. Where the suit is time-barred, consider an **FDCPA § 1692e/§ 1692f
   counterclaim** for filing suit on time-barred debt.
