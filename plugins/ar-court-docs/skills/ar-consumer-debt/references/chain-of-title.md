# Chain of title — Arkansas UCC Article 9

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Verify every citation against the current
> Ark. Code Ann. and Arkansas Rules of Evidence before relying.

**Statute:** Arkansas UCC Article 9 — Ark. Code Ann. **§ 4-9-101 et
seq.** (assignment of accounts and chattel paper).

A debt buyer suing on a purchased account must prove it **owns the
specific account** it is suing on. That proof is the **chain of
title**: an unbroken series of assignments from the **original
creditor** through every intermediate buyer to the **plaintiff**,
each link documented and each tied to **this** consumer's account.

## What a complete chain looks like

For each transfer in the chain, the plaintiff should be able to
produce:

1. a **Bill of Sale** (or purchase-and-sale agreement) for the
   transfer; AND
2. an **Assignment** that **specifically identifies this account** —
   by account number or unique identifier, not just "a portfolio of
   accounts"; AND
3. **account-level data** (the electronic field-level record) showing
   **this** consumer's account was within the pool actually sold on
   that date.

The chain must run **end to end**: Original Creditor → Buyer 1 →
Buyer 2 → ... → Plaintiff. A gap at **any** link defeats proof of
ownership.

## The common defects

- **"Portfolio" bills of sale with nothing attached.** A bill of sale
  that recites the sale of "the accounts described in the attached
  Exhibit A" without the exhibit, or that says "all accounts sold on
  [date]," does **not** prove that **this** account was included. The
  plaintiff must connect the bill of sale to this account through the
  account-level data.
- **Redacted or generic assignments.** An assignment that does not
  name the account number, or that is so redacted it cannot be tied
  to this consumer, is insufficient.
- **Missing intermediate links.** Debt is often resold two or three
  times. A plaintiff that can show its own purchase but not the
  upstream purchases has a **broken chain**.
- **Affiant without knowledge of the seller's records.** The
  plaintiff's affiant typically works for the **buyer**, not the
  original creditor, and cannot lay the business-records foundation
  for records the **original creditor** created (see
  `evidence-debt-buyer.md`).

## How chain of title intersects Arkansas fact pleading

Arkansas is a **fact-pleading** jurisdiction (Ark. R. Civ. P. 8(a);
see the SKILL.md body). A complaint that asserts the plaintiff is
"the assignee" or "the current owner" of the account **without
pleading the facts** of the assignment chain pleads a **conclusion**,
not facts — a vulnerability at the **Ark. R. Civ. P. 12(b)(6)** stage.
Where the complaint and its exhibits do not show the chain, raise the
fact-pleading defect.

## How to attack the chain

1. **Read what is attached** to the complaint. Under Ark. R. Civ. P.
   10(c), a written instrument that is an exhibit is part of the
   pleading — so the bill(s) of sale attached (or not attached)
   frame the dispute.
2. **Deny ownership / assignment** in the answer (see
   `examples/answer-debt-buyer.md`).
3. **Demand the full chain in discovery** — RFAs that the plaintiff
   cannot prove each link, interrogatories identifying each seller
   and the account-level data, and RFPs for every bill of sale,
   assignment, and the field-level data file (see
   `rfa-debt-buyer.md`, `interrogatories-debt-buyer.md`,
   `rfp-debt-buyer.md`).
4. **Subpoena the original creditor** if the plaintiff cannot connect
   the dots.
5. **Move for summary judgment / oppose the plaintiff's** under Ark.
   R. Civ. P. 56 where the chain is incomplete — Arkansas requires
   the non-movant to "meet proof with proof," so force the plaintiff
   to put admissible chain evidence in the record (see
   `evidence-debt-buyer.md` and `key-cases.md`).
