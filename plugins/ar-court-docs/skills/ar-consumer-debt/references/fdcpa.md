- `../../../ar-law-references/references/federal-debt-laws/FDCPA.md`

This file gives the **Arkansas-specific framing**: why the FDCPA is
the workhorse of Arkansas debt-defense (because Arkansas has **no
comprehensive mini-FDCPA statute**), how it composes with the ADTPA
and the collection-agency licensing regime, and which provisions do
the work in an Arkansas debt-buyer case.

## Why the FDCPA carries the load in Arkansas

Arkansas does not have a state debt-collection-practices act that
mirrors the FDCPA. Collection-conduct claims therefore run primarily
on the **federal** FDCPA, supplemented by:

- the **ADTPA** (§ 4-88-101 et seq.) — but only where the plaintiff
  can plead reliance-based **actual financial loss** after Act 986 of
  2017 (see `ar-adtpa.md`); and
- the **collection-agency licensing** regime (§ 17-24-101 et seq.) —
  a capacity/standing hook, not a damages statute (see
  `ar-collection-agency-law.md`).

The FDCPA is the one source that supplies **statutory damages, actual
damages, and mandatory fee-shifting** to a prevailing consumer
without a reliance requirement, which is why it is usually the lead
counterclaim.

## Who and what the FDCPA covers

- **Covered: "debt collectors"** — third-party collectors and **debt
  buyers** whose principal business is collecting debts or who
  regularly collect debts owed to another. Most defendants in
  Arkansas debt-buyer suits (Midland, Portfolio Recovery, Cavalry
  SPV, LVNV, Unifin, etc.) are squarely covered.
- **Generally NOT covered: original creditors** collecting their own
  debts in their own name. Identify whether the plaintiff is a
  **debt buyer** (covered) or an **original creditor** (generally
  not) at the outset — it determines whether the FDCPA applies.
- **Covered "debt"** — consumer debt (personal, family, household).
  Business debt is outside the FDCPA.

## Provisions that do the work in a debt-buyer case

| Provision | What it prohibits / requires | Debt-buyer use |
|---|---|---|
| **§ 1692c** | Communication restrictions (time/place, at work, after attorney representation, cease-communication) | Calls after a cease letter; contact at prohibited times |
| **§ 1692d** | Harassment or abuse | Repeated calls, threats, abusive language |
| **§ 1692e** | **False or misleading representations** | Misstating the amount, character, or legal status of the debt; falsely implying the collector can sue on a **time-barred** debt; misrepresenting ownership/assignment |
| **§ 1692f** | **Unfair practices** | Collecting amounts not authorized; **filing suit on a time-barred debt**; adding unauthorized fees/interest |
| **§ 1692g** | **Validation notice + verification rights** | Failure to send the validation notice; suing/continuing collection without verifying after a timely dispute |

**Time-barred debt** is the most important Arkansas-specific overlap:
filing or threatening suit on a debt past the Arkansas limitations
period (see `ar-statutes-of-limitations.md`) can violate **§§ 1692e
and 1692f**. This pairs the SOL defense (a complete defense to the
contract claim) with an affirmative FDCPA counterclaim.

## Remedies (verify current figures in the federal text)

- **Actual damages** — out-of-pocket and, in appropriate cases,
  emotional-distress damages.
- **Statutory damages** — a capped per-action statutory award under
  § 1692k(a)(2) (confirm the current cap in the federal text).
- **Costs and a reasonable attorney's fee** to a prevailing
  consumer — **mandatory** under § 1692k(a)(3) (this is the
  fee-shifting that makes FDCPA counterclaims viable; see
  `fees-consumer-debt.md`).
- **SOL** — an FDCPA claim must be brought within **one year** of the
  violation (§ 1692k(d)). A suit on a time-barred debt is itself a
  "violation," so the one-year clock typically runs from the filing
  or the abusive communication — calendar it.

## Standard of liability

- The FDCPA is a **strict-liability** statute as to most violations
  (no intent requirement), subject to the narrow **bona fide error**
  defense (§ 1692k(c)), which requires the collector to show the
  error was unintentional and that it maintained procedures
  reasonably adapted to avoid it.
- Conduct is judged by the **"least sophisticated consumer"**
  standard in most circuits, including the Eighth Circuit (which
  covers Arkansas) — confirm the current Eighth Circuit formulation.

## Forum

- An FDCPA counterclaim can be raised in the Arkansas state-court
  collection action, or the consumer can file an affirmative FDCPA
  action in **federal court** (federal-question jurisdiction). Weigh
  the forum carefully; see `key-cases.md` for Eighth Circuit
  authority and `ar-first-30-days` for pleading mechanics.
