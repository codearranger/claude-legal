# Evidence foundation — Ark. R. Evid. 803(6) / 902(11)

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Verify every citation against the current
> Arkansas Rules of Evidence before relying.

A debt-buyer plaintiff must get its **account records** and
**assignment documents** into evidence. Those documents are
out-of-court records offered for their truth — **hearsay** — so the
plaintiff must satisfy a hearsay exception and authenticate the
records. The two pillars are **Ark. R. Evid. 803(6)** (business
records) and **Ark. R. Evid. 902(11)** (self-authentication of
certified business records). Arkansas evidence law is modeled on the
Federal Rules of Evidence.

## Ark. R. Evid. 803(6) — the business-records exception

To admit a record under 803(6), the proponent must show the record:

1. was **made at or near the time** of the event by — or from
   information transmitted by — a person with **knowledge**;
2. was **kept in the course of a regularly conducted business
   activity**;
3. was made as a **regular practice** of that activity; and
4. all shown **by the testimony of the custodian or other qualified
   witness**, or by a **certification** that complies with Rule
   902(11).

The exception does **not** apply if the source of information or the
method or circumstances of preparation **indicate a lack of
trustworthiness**.

## Ark. R. Evid. 902(11) — self-authenticating certified records

Rule 902(11) lets the proponent authenticate a domestic record of
regularly conducted activity by a **written certification** of its
custodian (or another qualified person) that the 803(6) elements are
met — **without** a live witness — provided the proponent gives the
adverse party **advance written notice** and makes the record and
certification available for inspection, so the opponent has a fair
opportunity to challenge them. (Confirm the current notice mechanics
in the rule.)

## The debt-buyer foundation problem

This is the heart of the defense. The plaintiff's affiant almost
always works for the **debt buyer**, not the **original creditor**.
That affiant typically **cannot** lay the 803(6) foundation for
records the **original creditor** created, because the affiant lacks
**personal knowledge** of the original creditor's record-keeping
practices.

- **"Incorporated records" / business-records-of-another.** A debt
  buyer sometimes argues it "incorporated" the seller's records into
  its own business records and so can sponsor them. This is
  contested and **fact-specific** — it requires a showing that the
  buyer relied on those records in the regular course and integrated
  them. Probe the affiant's actual basis of knowledge; a conclusory
  "based on records maintained in the ordinary course" recital does
  not establish trustworthiness for **another entity's** records.
- **Trustworthiness challenge.** Account-level data that passed
  through multiple resales, with re-aged dates or mismatched
  identifiers, supports a **lack-of-trustworthiness** objection under
  803(6)'s trustworthiness clause.

## How to use it

1. **Object** to the plaintiff's account records and assignments on
   803(6) / 902(11) / Rule 901 (authentication) grounds where the
   foundation is not laid.
2. **Demand the 902(11) certification** (and the underlying records)
   in discovery, then test whether the certifying person had
   knowledge of the **original creditor's** practices.
3. **Cross-examine the affiant** on personal knowledge of the
   original creditor's record creation and of each assignment.
4. On **summary judgment** (Ark. R. Civ. P. 56), insist that the
   plaintiff's proof be **admissible**; an affidavit that would not
   be admissible at trial (no personal knowledge, no foundation)
   should not carry the plaintiff's burden, and the non-movant's
   obligation to "meet proof with proof" presupposes admissible proof
   from the movant.
