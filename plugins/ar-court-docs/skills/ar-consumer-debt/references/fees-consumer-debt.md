# Fee mechanics — Arkansas consumer-debt cases

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Verify every citation against current law.
> Fee-shifting rules cut **both ways** — a prevailing **plaintiff**
> may also recover fees on a contract claim. Read carefully.

Two fee regimes operate in an Arkansas consumer-debt case: the
**Arkansas attorney's-fee statute** (§ 16-22-308) and the **FDCPA's**
mandatory fee-shifting (15 U.S.C. § 1692k(a)(3)). They point in
opposite directions, so understand both before deciding how to
litigate.

## Ark. Code Ann. § 16-22-308 — the contract fee statute

**Ark. Code Ann. § 16-22-308** authorizes a **reasonable attorney's
fee to the prevailing party** in a civil action to recover on a
**contract** (breach of contract, open account, etc.) — among the
enumerated categories. Key points:

- It is **discretionary** with the court ("the prevailing party **may**
  be allowed a reasonable attorney's fee"), not mandatory.
- It is **bidirectional / reciprocal in effect**: it runs to whichever
  party prevails. A **debt buyer that wins** can ask for fees under
  § 16-22-308; a **consumer who defeats** the contract claim is a
  **prevailing party** and can ask for fees too.
- **Pro se litigants generally cannot recover an "attorney's" fee**
  for their own time (there is no attorney). The statute compensates
  attorney's fees; a self-represented party should not expect a fee
  award for personal time, though **costs** may still be recoverable.

**Strategic consequence:** because § 16-22-308 can shift fees **to the
plaintiff** if the consumer loses the contract claim, weigh the
exposure before, e.g., taking a weak case to trial. Conversely, a
clean win for the consumer can support a fee request **against** the
collector.

## FDCPA fee-shifting — 15 U.S.C. § 1692k(a)(3)

On a successful **FDCPA** claim or counterclaim, the court **must**
award the prevailing consumer **costs and a reasonable attorney's
fee** — this is **one-directional** in the consumer's favor (a
collector does not get fees unless the action was brought in bad faith
and for harassment). See `fdcpa.md`.

- This mandatory fee-shifting is what makes an FDCPA counterclaim
  economically viable and is a major reason to pair the SOL / chain-
  of-title defense with an FDCPA counterclaim where the facts support
  one.
- Again, a **pro se** consumer who is not an attorney generally
  recovers **costs** but not an "attorney's fee" for personal time —
  the fee-shifting matters most when counsel is involved.

## Costs

Ordinary **court costs** are generally recoverable by a prevailing
party under the Arkansas cost rules (verify the current cost
provisions). Filing fees, service costs, and the like are recoverable
even where an attorney's fee is not.

## Practical checklist

- Identify whether the plaintiff's claim is a **contract** claim that
  triggers § 16-22-308 exposure.
- If asserting an **FDCPA** counterclaim, preserve the fee request and
  document time (if counsel is retained).
- For a **pro se** litigant: expect **costs**, not an attorney's fee,
  and weigh § 16-22-308 exposure if the contract claim is contested
  and the consumer might lose.
