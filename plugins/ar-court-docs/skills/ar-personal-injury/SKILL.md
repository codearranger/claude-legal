# Arkansas Personal-Injury Practice

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Arkansas tort practice is
> deadline-sensitive and has hard immunity gates. Statute
> numbers, SOLs, and the immunity rules change — verify every
> SOL, immunity provision, and claims-commission deadline against
> the current Ark. Code Ann. before relying, and consult a
> licensed Arkansas attorney about your specific case.

Use this subject-matter bundle for Arkansas personal-injury and
tort actions — auto accidents, premises (slip-and-fall), product
and general negligence, medical malpractice, wrongful death,
dog-bite, and claims against governmental entities. Arkansas is
distinctive in two directions: it has **no damages caps** (the
constitution forbids them) and it has **near-absolute sovereign
immunity** (the State can essentially never be sued in its own
courts).

## At a glance — the things that move every AR PI case

1. **3-year general tort SOL** at § 16-56-105 — Arkansas's
   personal-injury, negligence, and property-damage clock.
   Calendar at intake.
2. **Modified comparative fault with the "less than" 49% bar**
   at § 16-64-122. A plaintiff whose fault is **equal to or
   greater than** the defendant's recovers **nothing**.
3. **Several liability** under the **Civil Justice Reform Act of
   2003** (§ 16-55-201) — each defendant pays only its allocated
   share — **but the nonparty-fault and punitive-cap provisions
   were struck down**. See `references/tort-reform-and-caps.md`.
4. **NO damages caps.** **Ark. Const. art. 5, § 32** bars any law
   limiting damages for death or personal injury. There is no
   non-economic cap and no punitive cap in force.
5. **Medical malpractice: 2-year SOL** (§ 16-114-203) and **no
   valid affidavit-of-merit prerequisite** — that requirement was
   held unconstitutional in *Summerville v. Thrower*, 2007 Ark.
   366. See `references/medical-malpractice.md`.
6. **Sovereign immunity is near-absolute.** **Ark. Const. art. 5,
   § 20** + *Bd. of Trustees of Univ. of Ark. v. Andrews*, 2018
   Ark. 12 — the State cannot be made a defendant in its own
   courts; claims go to the **Arkansas State Claims Commission**.
   See `references/sovereign-and-municipal-immunity.md`.
7. **Counties and cities are immune except up to insurance
   coverage** under § 21-9-301. Identify any governmental
   defendant at intake.
8. **Wrongful death: 3-year SOL** (§ 16-62-102) with statutory
   beneficiaries.

## Comparative fault — the "less than" 49% bar (§ 16-64-122)

Arkansas follows **modified comparative fault**. The plaintiff
recovers — reduced by the plaintiff's own percentage — **only if
the plaintiff's fault is of *less degree than*** the fault of the
party or parties from whom recovery is sought:

- Plaintiff **less than** the defendant's fault → recovers,
  reduced by the plaintiff's share.
- Plaintiff's fault **equal to or greater than** the defendant's
  (i.e., 50% or more) → recovers **nothing**.

This is the **"less than" / 49% bar**. The exact statutory phrasing
("of less degree than the fault chargeable to the party or parties
from whom the claiming party seeks to recover") matters for
multi-defendant allocation — see `references/comparative-fault.md`.

## Several liability and the struck-down tort-reform provisions

The **Civil Justice Reform Act of 2003 (Act 649)** moved Arkansas
from joint-and-several liability to **several liability** with
fault allocation (§ 16-55-201): each defendant is liable only for
its allocated share. **But the Arkansas Supreme Court struck down
major pieces** of the Act as unconstitutional:

- **Nonparty fault allocation** (the provision letting a defendant
  shift fault to absent nonparties) — struck in **_Johnson v.
  Rockwell Automation, Inc._, 2009 Ark. 241**.
- **The punitive-damages cap** (§ 16-55-208) — struck in **_Bayer
  CropScience LP v. Schafer_, 2011 Ark. 518**.

Both were struck as violating separation of powers / the right to
a jury and **Ark. Const. art. 5, § 32**. The result: several
liability survives, but **fault cannot be allocated to nonparties**
and there is **no punitive cap**. See
`references/tort-reform-and-caps.md`.

## Damages — no caps

**Ark. Const. art. 5, § 32** provides that no law shall limit the
amount of damages recoverable for injuries resulting in death or
for personal injuries. The Arkansas Supreme Court has used this
provision (and separation of powers) to **strike** statutory
damages caps:

- **No non-economic damages cap** in force.
- **No punitive damages cap** (the § 16-55-208 cap was struck in
  *Bayer CropScience v. Schafer*).
- Tort-reform ballot measures (e.g., **Issue 1 of 2018**) aimed at
  capping damages have been **struck from the ballot or failed**.

Economic and non-economic damages are uncapped; punitive damages
require the common-law standard (clear and convincing evidence of
malice or reckless disregard). See `references/tort-reform-and-caps.md`.

## Medical malpractice — Medical Malpractice Act

The **Medical Malpractice Act** (§ 16-114-201 et seq.) governs.
Two Arkansas distinctives:

- **2-year SOL** at § 16-114-203 (shorter than the 3-year general
  tort clock).
- **No valid affidavit-of-merit prerequisite.** The statutory
  expert "reasonable-cause affidavit" requirement (former
  § 16-114-209) was held **unconstitutional** as a separation-of-
  powers violation — the court, not the legislature, controls
  procedure — in **_Summerville v. Thrower_, 2007 Ark. 366**
  (building on *Weidrick v. Arnold*). A plaintiff does **not** have
  to file an affidavit of merit to bring a malpractice suit.

See `references/medical-malpractice.md` for the standard of care,
expert requirements, and the continuous-treatment / discovery
nuance.

## Suing the government — immunity gates

### The State — near-absolute immunity

**Ark. Const. art. 5, § 20**: "The State of Arkansas shall never
be made defendant in any of her courts." The Arkansas Supreme
Court in **_Bd. of Trustees of Univ. of Ark. v. Andrews_, 2018
Ark. 12** held that **even the legislature cannot waive** this
immunity by statute. The State and its agencies (including state
universities) **cannot be sued in circuit court** for damages.

Claims against the State go instead to the **Arkansas State Claims
Commission** (§ 19-10-201 et seq.) — an administrative body, not a
court — with its own filing deadline and procedure. See
`references/sovereign-and-municipal-immunity.md`.

### Counties and municipalities — immune up to insurance

Political subdivisions (counties, cities, towns) have **tort
immunity** under **§ 21-9-301** — immune from tort liability
**except to the extent of any liability-insurance coverage**
they carry. The practical first question is **how much insurance**
the entity has; that ceiling is effectively the recovery cap.

## Wrongful death — § 16-62-102

Arkansas's wrongful-death statute (§ 16-62-102) is the modern
Lord Campbell's Act enactment:

- **3-year SOL** from death.
- The action is brought by the **personal representative** (or,
  if none, the heirs at law).
- **Statutory beneficiaries** recover for pecuniary loss, mental
  anguish, loss of consortium, and the like; the estate may also
  have a **survival** claim under § 16-62-101 for the decedent's
  own pre-death damages.

## Premises and dog bites

- **Premises liability** runs on ordinary negligence with
  invitee / licensee / trespasser status distinctions; the
  3-year SOL applies.
- **Dog bites**: Arkansas has **no strict-liability dog-bite
  statute**. Liability rests on **common-law negligence** and the
  **scienter / "one-bite"** theory — the owner is liable if the
  owner knew or should have known of the dog's dangerous
  propensity.

## High-yield Arkansas PI checks

1. **What's the SOL?** General tort / wrongful death 3 years
   (§ 16-56-105 / § 16-62-102); **medical malpractice 2 years**
   (§ 16-114-203) — the short one.
2. **Comparative fault?** Is the plaintiff's fault **less than**
   the defendant's? At 50%+ the claim is barred (§ 16-64-122).
3. **Governmental defendant?** State → **cannot sue in court;
   Claims Commission** (§ 19-10-201; *Andrews*). City/county →
   **immune except up to insurance** (§ 21-9-301).
4. **Several liability** — each defendant pays its share
   (§ 16-55-201), but **no nonparty-fault allocation**
   (*Johnson v. Rockwell*).
5. **Caps?** **None.** No non-economic cap; **no punitive cap**
   (*Bayer CropScience v. Schafer* struck § 16-55-208);
   Ark. Const. art. 5, § 32.
6. **Med-mal?** 2-year SOL; **no affidavit-of-merit prerequisite**
   (*Summerville v. Thrower*).
7. **Wrongful death?** § 16-62-102 beneficiaries + § 16-62-101
   survival claim.

## Drafting checklist

- [ ] Confirm the **SOL** at intake — flag the **2-year**
      med-mal clock separately from the 3-year general clock.
- [ ] If a **State** defendant is involved, do **not** file in
      circuit court — route to the **Arkansas State Claims
      Commission** and calendar its deadline (*Andrews*).
- [ ] If a **city/county** defendant is involved, identify the
      **liability-insurance coverage** ceiling (§ 21-9-301).
- [ ] Plead comparative fault to keep the plaintiff **below**
      the bar (§ 16-64-122); do **not** allow improper nonparty-
      fault allocation (*Johnson v. Rockwell*).
- [ ] **Do not** cap the damages prayer — Arkansas has no
      statutory cap (Ark. Const. art. 5, § 32); plead punitives
      to the common-law standard.
- [ ] For med-mal, **no affidavit of merit is required**
      (*Summerville v. Thrower*) — do not assume one; line up
      expert proof of the standard of care.
- [ ] For wrongful death, name the **personal representative** and
      the **statutory beneficiaries** with specificity
      (§ 16-62-102).
