# Arkansas Medical Malpractice — Ark. Code Ann. § 16-114-201 et seq.

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Verify the SOL, the expert requirements,
> and the current status of every cited provision against the
> live Ark. Code Ann.

The **Medical Malpractice Act** (Ark. Code Ann. § 16-114-201 et
seq.) governs claims for medical injury in Arkansas. Two
distinctives dominate practice: a **shorter 2-year SOL** and the
**absence of any valid affidavit-of-merit prerequisite**.

## 2-year statute of limitations — § 16-114-203

Medical-malpractice actions run on a **2-year** clock from the
date of the wrongful act or omission — **shorter** than the
3-year general tort SOL. Key nuances (confirm current text):

- **Accrual** is generally at the date of the act/omission, not
  discovery, though a limited **foreign-object / concealment**
  discovery rule applies.
- **Continuing-treatment** considerations may affect accrual in
  some fact patterns — confirm the current case law.
- Calendar the 2-year clock at intake; it is the most common
  malpractice case-killer.

## No affidavit-of-merit prerequisite — *Summerville v. Thrower*

Arkansas once required a plaintiff to file an expert
**"reasonable-cause affidavit"** (affidavit of merit) under
former § 16-114-209 within a set period after filing. The Arkansas
Supreme Court held that requirement **unconstitutional** in
**_Summerville v. Thrower_, 2007 Ark. 366** (following *Weidrick v.
Arnold*, 321 Ark. 583 (1995)): the affidavit requirement was a
**procedural** rule, and **only the Supreme Court** — not the
legislature — may prescribe rules of pleading, practice, and
procedure (separation of powers). 

**There is no valid affidavit-of-merit prerequisite to a medical-
malpractice suit in Arkansas.** A plaintiff may file without one.
(Expert proof of the standard of care is still required to **prove**
the case at trial — see below — but it is not a pleading gate.)

## Burden and standard of proof — § 16-114-206

The plaintiff must prove, by expert testimony from a provider in
the same or a similar specialty (confirm the locality/standard
language in § 16-114-206):

1. The applicable **standard of care**;
2. The defendant's **breach** of that standard;
3. **Proximate causation** of the injury; and
4. **Damages**.

Expert testimony is required unless the negligence is within the
**common knowledge** of laypersons (e.g., a sponge left in the
body).

## Damages — no caps

Consistent with the no-caps rule (Ark. Const. art. 5, § 32; see
`tort-reform-and-caps.md`), there is **no cap** on non-economic or
punitive damages in a malpractice case. Earlier statutory caps in
the malpractice context have been struck.
