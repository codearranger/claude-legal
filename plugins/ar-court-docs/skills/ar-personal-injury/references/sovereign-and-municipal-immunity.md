# Arkansas Sovereign and Municipal Immunity

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Immunity bars are jurisdictional and
> deadline-sensitive. Verify the Claims Commission deadline and
> the insurance-coverage limits against the current Ark. Code Ann.
> before relying.

Arkansas has unusually strong governmental immunity. The first
question in any case with a governmental defendant is **which
immunity regime applies** — State (near-absolute) vs.
political-subdivision (immune except up to insurance).

## The State — near-absolute immunity (Ark. Const. art. 5, § 20)

> "The State of Arkansas shall never be made defendant in any of
> her courts."

This is **near-absolute**. The State, its agencies, and entities
that are arms of the State (including **state universities**)
**cannot be sued for damages in circuit court**.

- **The legislature cannot waive it.** In **_Bd. of Trustees of
  Univ. of Ark. v. Andrews_, 2018 Ark. 12**, the Arkansas Supreme
  Court held that a statute purporting to waive sovereign immunity
  (there, the Arkansas Minimum Wage Act's provision for suing the
  State) was unconstitutional — only the constitution itself, not
  the General Assembly, can create an exception. *Andrews*
  tightened immunity considerably.

- **Where do State claims go?** To the **Arkansas State Claims
  Commission** (Ark. Code Ann. § 19-10-201 et seq.), an
  administrative body that hears and recommends payment of claims
  against the State. It is **not a court**; it has its own filing
  deadline (confirm at § 19-10-204), its own procedure, and its
  awards may require legislative appropriation to be paid.

## Political subdivisions — immune except up to insurance (§ 21-9-301)

Counties, municipalities, and other political subdivisions are
**immune from tort liability** under **Ark. Code Ann. § 21-9-301**
— **except to the extent of any liability-insurance coverage** the
subdivision carries.

- The practical recovery ceiling is the **amount of liability
  insurance** the entity has. With no insurance (or only minimum
  vehicle coverage), recovery may be sharply limited or barred.
- The immunity covers tort claims; intentional torts and certain
  exceptions may differ — confirm the current scope.
- A political-subdivision tort claim is filed in **circuit court**
  (unlike a State claim), subject to the § 21-9-301 insurance
  ceiling and the general 3-year tort SOL.

## Decision tree

1. **Is the defendant the State or an arm of the State** (state
   agency, state university, state board)?
   - Yes → **No circuit-court suit.** File with the **State Claims
     Commission** (§ 19-10-201) and calendar its deadline.
     (*Andrews* — legislature cannot waive.)
2. **Is the defendant a county, city, or town (or its
   employees acting in scope)?**
   - Yes → **Circuit court**, but recovery capped at the entity's
     **liability-insurance coverage** (§ 21-9-301).
3. **Federal claim** (42 U.S.C. § 1983, ADA, etc.)?
   - Runs in parallel under its own framework and is **not** barred
     by state sovereign immunity in federal court (subject to
     Eleventh Amendment / *Ex parte Young* analysis for State
     defendants).
