# Arkansas Comparative Fault — Ark. Code Ann. § 16-64-122

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Verify the statutory text and the current
> several-liability rules against the live Ark. Code Ann.

## The "less than" 49% bar

Arkansas is a **modified comparative fault** state. Under
Ark. Code Ann. § 16-64-122, the claiming party recovers — reduced
by the claiming party's own percentage of fault — **only if** the
claiming party's fault is **"of less degree than"** the fault of
the party or parties from whom recovery is sought:

- Plaintiff's fault **less than** the defendant's → recovery,
  reduced proportionally.
- Plaintiff's fault **equal to or greater than** the defendant's
  → recovery **barred** entirely.

Because "equal to" bars recovery, the practical line is **49%**: a
plaintiff at 49% recovers (reduced); a plaintiff at 50% recovers
nothing.

## The statutory phrasing matters

The statute measures the plaintiff's fault against the fault
"chargeable to the party or parties from whom the claiming party
seeks to recover." In a **multi-defendant** case, this is compared
against the **combined** fault of the defendants from whom recovery
is sought — not against each defendant individually — so a
plaintiff can be more at fault than any single defendant yet still
recover if below the defendants' aggregate. Confirm the current
construction in the case law.

## Fault apportionment by the jury

The jury (or the court in a bench trial) allocates a percentage of
fault to each party. Damages are reduced by the plaintiff's
percentage, and — under several liability (§ 16-55-201) — each
defendant pays only its allocated share.

## Interaction with the struck nonparty-fault provision

The Civil Justice Reform Act of 2003 tried to let defendants
allocate fault to **nonparties** (absent persons not joined in the
suit). That provision was **struck down** in *Johnson v. Rockwell
Automation, Inc.*, 2009 Ark. 241. Fault is allocated **only among
the parties** to the case (and the plaintiff). See
`tort-reform-and-caps.md`.
