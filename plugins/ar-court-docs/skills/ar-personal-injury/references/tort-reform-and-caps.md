## Civil Justice Reform Act of 2003 (Act 649)

The **Civil Justice Reform Act of 2003** restructured Arkansas
tort liability. Its surviving core:

- **Several liability** — Ark. Code Ann. § 16-55-201. Joint-and-
  several liability was abolished for most torts; each defendant
  is liable **only for its own allocated share** of fault. The
  plaintiff bears the risk of an insolvent or absent tortfeasor.

But the Arkansas Supreme Court struck two major pieces as
unconstitutional:

### Nonparty fault — STRUCK (*Johnson v. Rockwell*, 2009)

The Act allowed defendants to **allocate fault to nonparties**
(absent persons not joined in the suit), shifting the plaintiff's
recovery downward toward phantom defendants. In **_Johnson v.
Rockwell Automation, Inc._, 2009 Ark. 241**, the court held the
nonparty-fault provision **unconstitutional** as a violation of
separation of powers (it intruded on the court's rule-making
authority) and the right to a jury. **Fault is allocated only
among the parties** before the court.

### Punitive-damages cap — STRUCK (*Bayer CropScience*, 2011)

The Act capped punitive damages at § 16-55-208. In **_Bayer
CropScience LP v. Schafer_, 2011 Ark. 518**, the court held the
punitive cap **unconstitutional** under **Ark. Const. art. 5,
§ 32**, which forbids laws limiting damages for personal injury or
death. **There is no statutory punitive cap in force.**

## No damages caps — Ark. Const. art. 5, § 32

> "No act of the General Assembly shall limit the amount to be
> recovered for injuries resulting in death or for injuries to
> persons or property..."

This constitutional provision is the engine that strikes damages
caps in Arkansas:

- **No non-economic (pain-and-suffering) cap.**
- **No punitive cap** (§ 16-55-208 struck in *Bayer CropScience*).
- **Medical-malpractice and other statutory caps** have been
  struck under the same provision.
- **Ballot-measure tort reform** — e.g., **Issue 1 of 2018**, a
  proposed constitutional amendment to cap non-economic and
  punitive damages and limit attorney contingency fees — was
  **struck from the ballot** by the Arkansas Supreme Court.
  Arkansas remains a **no-caps** jurisdiction.

## Punitive damages — common-law standard

With no statutory cap, punitive damages turn on the common-law
standard: the plaintiff must show by **clear and convincing
evidence** that the defendant intentionally pursued a course of
conduct for the purpose of causing injury, or acted with malice or
**reckless disregard** of the consequences from which malice may be
inferred. Confirm the current standard and any pattern-instruction
language.
