# Arkansas Arbitration, Fraudulent Transfer, and the ADTPA B2B Element

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Verify the arbitration provisions, the
> UFTA elements, and the ADTPA private-action requirements against
> the current Ark. Code Ann.

## Arbitration — Arkansas Uniform Arbitration Act (§ 16-108-201)

Arkansas adopted the **Revised Uniform Arbitration Act (RUAA)** in
2011, codified at **Ark. Code Ann. § 16-108-201 et seq.** It
governs:

- **Validity** of arbitration agreements (§ 16-108-206).
- **Motion to compel / stay** arbitration (§ 16-108-207).
- **Appointment of arbitrators**, provisional remedies, and
  hearings.
- **Confirmation, vacatur, and modification** of awards
  (§ 16-108-222 to -224).

### FAA preemption

When the contract evidences a transaction involving **interstate
commerce**, the **Federal Arbitration Act (FAA)** applies and
**preempts** conflicting state-law limits on arbitration.

- Arkansas case law historically applied state contract doctrines
  (notably **mutuality of obligation**) to invalidate some
  arbitration clauses. Under *AT&T Mobility LLC v. Concepcion* and
  its progeny, state rules that single out or disfavor arbitration
  are **preempted** by the FAA. A generally applicable contract
  defense (fraud, unconscionability, lack of mutual assent) still
  applies, but it must be one that applies to **any** contract,
  not just arbitration clauses.
- Brief **both** the RUAA and FAA preemption when moving to
  compel or resist arbitration.

## Fraudulent transfer — Arkansas UFTA (§ 4-59-201 et seq.)

Arkansas's **Uniform Fraudulent Transfer Act** lets a creditor
avoid transfers and obligations that defeat collection. **Arkansas
has NOT adopted the UVTA rebrand — it remains UFTA.**

- **Actual-intent fraud** (§ 4-59-204(a)(1)) — a transfer made
  with **actual intent** to hinder, delay, or defraud creditors,
  proven by the **badges of fraud** (insider transfer, retention
  of control, concealment, transfer of substantially all assets,
  insolvency, inadequate consideration, etc.).
- **Constructive fraud** (§ 4-59-204(a)(2) / § 4-59-205) — a
  transfer made **without receiving reasonably equivalent value**
  while the debtor was **insolvent** (or became insolvent), or was
  left with unreasonably small capital.
- **Remedies** (§ 4-59-207) — avoidance of the transfer,
  attachment, injunction, or a money judgment against the
  transferee.
- **SOL / extinguishment** (§ 4-59-209) — claims are extinguished
  after the statutory period (e.g., 4 years, with a 1-year
  discovery extension for actual-intent claims — confirm).

## ADTPA — the Act 986 of 2017 B2B element (§ 4-88-113(f))

The **Arkansas Deceptive Trade Practices Act** (§ 4-88-101 et seq.)
reaches deceptive and unconscionable acts in business and consumer
dealings. **Act 986 of 2017** narrowed the **private** right of
action at **§ 4-88-113(f)**:

- A private plaintiff must prove an **actual financial loss** as a
  result of **reliance** on the deceptive or unconscionable act —
  an **actual-damages + reliance** element. A bare deceptive act
  without proven, reliance-based financial loss does not support a
  private claim.
- **Class actions** by private plaintiffs are **restricted** under
  the amended statute.
- The **Attorney General** retains broad enforcement authority,
  unaffected by the private-action narrowing.

For a **B2B** ADTPA claim, plead the **reliance** and **actual
financial loss** specifically; do not rely on a class theory.
