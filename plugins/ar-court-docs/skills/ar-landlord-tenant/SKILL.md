# Arkansas Landlord-Tenant Practice

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Arkansas is among the most
> landlord-favorable jurisdictions in the country and its
> eviction timeline is fast. Statute numbers, notice periods,
> dollar thresholds, and the unit-count exemption change —
> verify every notice period, cure window, and deposit rule
> against the current Ark. Code Ann. before relying, and
> consult a licensed Arkansas attorney about your specific
> case. If you have received any criminal citation under
> § 18-16-101, treat it as a criminal matter and seek counsel
> immediately.

Use this subject-matter bundle for Arkansas residential
landlord-tenant disputes — evictions, nonpayment, lease
termination, habitability complaints, security-deposit
disputes, and retaliation. Arkansas is structurally the most
landlord-friendly state, with a criminal eviction statute that
exists nowhere else and only a minimal, recently-enacted
warranty of habitability.

## At a glance — the things that move every AR landlord-tenant case

1. **Two separate eviction tracks.** Arkansas has a **criminal**
   failure-to-vacate path (§ 18-16-101) and a **civil**
   unlawful-detainer path (§ 18-60-301 et seq.). They are
   different proceedings with different consequences — identify
   which one you are in first.
2. **Criminal failure to vacate (§ 18-16-101)** is unique to
   Arkansas: a tenant who stays after a nonpayment notice can be
   charged with a **misdemeanor**. Its constitutionality has been
   litigated and the statute was amended in 2021 — see
   `references/criminal-failure-to-vacate.md`.
3. **Civil unlawful detainer (§ 18-60-301 et seq.)** is the
   money-judgment-plus-possession track: written notice to
   vacate, complaint, and a **writ of possession**. See
   `references/unlawful-detainer.md` for the notice period and
   the immediate-possession mechanics.
4. **Implied habitability is brand-new and minimal.** Arkansas
   was the **last** state to adopt any warranty of habitability,
   via **Act 1052 of 2021** (§ 18-17-501 et seq.). It applies
   only to leases entered after its effective date, sets a short
   list of minimum standards, and gives the tenant a **limited
   remedy** — no rent-withholding and no repair-and-deduct. See
   `references/habitability-act-1052.md`.
5. **Security-deposit law has a small-landlord exemption.**
   The § 18-16-301 deposit regime (cap, return-and-itemization
   period, noncompliance remedy) **does not apply** to landlords
   who own at or below the statutory unit count. See
   `references/security-deposits.md`.
6. **District Court is the eviction forum**; an aggrieved party
   appeals to **Circuit Court for de novo review**. See
   `ar-district-courts` for forum mechanics.

## The two eviction tracks — pick the right one

Arkansas is the only state that criminalizes a holdover tenant.
A landlord may proceed on **either** track, and the two have very
different stakes:

| | Criminal failure to vacate | Civil unlawful detainer |
|---|---|---|
| Statute | § 18-16-101 | § 18-60-301 et seq. |
| Nature | Misdemeanor prosecution | Civil suit |
| Notice | Statutory nonpayment notice | Written notice to vacate (3 days) |
| Forum | District Court (criminal) | District or Circuit Court (civil) |
| Outcome | Fine / conviction + vacate | Possession + money judgment + writ |
| Defenses | Payment; defective notice; constitutional | Lease compliance; defective notice; counterclaims |

For a **tenant**, the criminal track is the dangerous one —
a conviction carries a fine and a criminal record. For the
detail of each, see `references/criminal-failure-to-vacate.md`
and `references/unlawful-detainer.md`.

### Civil unlawful detainer mechanics (§ 18-60-301 et seq.)

The civil track runs:

1. **Written notice to vacate** — a **3-day** notice to leave
   the premises after the tenant is in default (confirm the
   exact period and content in `references/unlawful-detainer.md`).
2. **Complaint for unlawful detainer** filed in District (or
   Circuit) Court.
3. **Notice of intention to issue writ of possession** — the
   defendant has a short window to object and post a bond to
   retain possession during the suit. If the tenant does not
   timely object, the court may issue an **immediate writ of
   possession** before final judgment.
4. **Trial** on possession + back rent + damages.
5. **Writ of possession** executed by the sheriff.

The **immediate-possession-with-bond** structure is the engine
that makes Arkansas eviction fast: the burden flips to the
tenant to post a bond to stay. See
`references/unlawful-detainer.md` for the bond and objection
windows.

## Implied warranty of habitability — Act 1052 of 2021

Until **Act 1052 of 2021** (codified at § 18-17-501 et seq.),
Arkansas recognized **no** implied warranty of habitability —
the only state with none. The Act is deliberately narrow:

- **Coverage**: applies to residential leases entered **after**
  the Act's effective date (leases after Nov. 1, 2021). Confirm
  the trigger date in `references/habitability-act-1052.md`.
- **Minimum standards** (§ 18-17-502): a short list — sound
  structure, available potable running water, available
  electricity, working plumbing, and heating/air-conditioning
  available at the start of the lease. This is a floor, not the
  expansive habitability warranty of other states.
- **Tenant remedy is limited.** The tenant must give **written
  notice** of the deficiency, the landlord gets a **cure period**
  (a 30-day window), and if the landlord does not cure the
  tenant's remedy is to **terminate the lease and recover the
  security deposit**. There is **no rent-withholding right and
  no repair-and-deduct right** — both common in other states are
  absent here.
- The standards are a statutory floor, but they are minimal.

Because the remedy is "give notice, wait, then leave," a tenant
who simply stops paying rent over a habitability complaint risks
the eviction (and criminal failure-to-vacate) tracks above. The
notice-and-cure sequence must be followed precisely — see
`references/habitability-act-1052.md` for the written-notice
content and the cure clock.

## Arkansas Residential Landlord-Tenant Act of 2007

The **Arkansas Residential Landlord-Tenant Act of 2007**
(§ 18-17-101 et seq.) is the closest thing Arkansas has to a
comprehensive code, but it is far thinner than the uniform act
adopted elsewhere. It addresses lease terms, the parties'
obligations, prohibited provisions, and remedies, and Act 1052
folded the new habitability floor into its structure. Treat it
as the organizing chapter and read the habitability subchapter
(§ 18-17-501 et seq.) as its key recent addition.

## Security deposits — § 18-16-301 et seq.

The deposit regime sets a cap, a return-and-itemization period,
and the consequences of noncompliance — **but** it carries a
**small-landlord exemption**: a landlord who owns at or below
the statutory unit count (commonly read as five or fewer rental
units, with a management-company caveat) is **exempt** from the
deposit rules entirely. Before asserting a deposit claim,
confirm the landlord is **covered**.

- **Cap** on the deposit amount.
- **Return + itemization** within the statutory period (a 60-day
  window) after the tenant vacates, with an itemized list of
  deductions.
- **Remedy** for a covered landlord's noncompliance.

The exact unit-count threshold, the cap multiple, the return
period, and the noncompliance remedy live in
`references/security-deposits.md`.

## Retaliation and self-help

- **Self-help eviction** (lockouts, utility shutoffs, removing
  the tenant's property) is **not** the lawful route — the
  landlord must use the civil unlawful-detainer process. A tenant
  subjected to a self-help lockout has remedies; note the
  interaction with the criminal failure-to-vacate statute, which
  channels the dispute back to a court process.
- Arkansas's tenant-protection provisions (including any
  anti-retaliation protection) are thin compared to other
  states; do not assume a retaliation defense exists without
  confirming it in the Act. See the Residential Landlord-Tenant
  Act subchapters and `references/habitability-act-1052.md`.

## High-yield Arkansas landlord-tenant checks

1. **Which track?** Criminal § 18-16-101 or civil § 18-60-301?
   A criminal citation is a criminal matter — get counsel.
2. **Notice defect?** Was the notice to vacate in writing, for
   the right period, served correctly? Defective notice is the
   most common eviction defense.
3. **Writ of possession posture?** Did the tenant get the
   notice-of-intent-to-issue-writ and the bond opportunity? Was
   the bond window honored?
4. **Habitability?** Lease after the Act 1052 effective date?
   Did the tenant give written notice and let the cure window
   run? Is the defect a § 18-17-502 minimum standard?
5. **Deposit claim — is the landlord covered?** Confirm the
   landlord is above the small-landlord exemption before
   asserting the § 18-16-301 deposit remedies.
6. **De novo appeal preserved?** A District Court loss appeals to
   Circuit Court for de novo review within the appeal window.

## Drafting checklist

- [ ] Identify **which eviction track** (criminal vs. civil) the
      papers actually invoke.
- [ ] For a civil UD answer, check the **notice-to-vacate**
      period and service, and preserve the **bond/objection**
      right to keep possession during suit.
- [ ] For a habitability claim, **paper the written notice** and
      the cure clock; plead the § 18-17-502 minimum standard at
      issue; do **not** advise rent-withholding or
      repair-and-deduct (not authorized).
- [ ] For a deposit claim, confirm the landlord is **covered**
      (over the unit-count exemption) before pleading the
      § 18-16-301 remedies; attach the itemization (or its
      absence).
- [ ] Calendar the **de novo appeal** window from a District
      Court eviction loss.
- [ ] Never advise self-help eviction or self-help possession.
