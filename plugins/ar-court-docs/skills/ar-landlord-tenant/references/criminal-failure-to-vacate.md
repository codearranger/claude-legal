# Arkansas Criminal Failure to Vacate — Ark. Code Ann. § 18-16-101

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** This is a **criminal** statute. A tenant
> charged under § 18-16-101 is a criminal defendant and should
> seek counsel immediately. Verify the current text, the penalty,
> and the 2021 amendments against the live Ark. Code Ann.

Arkansas is the **only state** that makes a holdover tenant's
failure to vacate a **criminal offense**. Ark. Code Ann.
§ 18-16-101 lets a landlord pursue a **misdemeanor** charge
against a tenant who, after a statutory notice for nonpayment of
rent, fails to surrender possession.

## The structure

- **Trigger**: the tenant is in default for nonpayment of rent
  and fails to vacate after the statutory notice (a 10-day notice;
  confirm the exact period and form in the current statute).
- **Charge**: each day (or the continued holding over) can be
  charged as a misdemeanor. A conviction carries a **fine** and,
  historically, escalating per-day penalties.
- **Forum**: District Court, on the **criminal** docket — distinct
  from the civil unlawful-detainer suit. A landlord can pursue
  the criminal charge **instead of or alongside** the civil
  unlawful-detainer action.

## Constitutional history

The statute has been repeatedly challenged as criminalizing a
civil debt and as denying due process (no opportunity to assert
defenses such as habitability or improper notice before
conviction). Litigation over its constitutionality is a recurring
feature of Arkansas landlord-tenant law.

## The 2021 amendment

The General Assembly **amended § 18-16-101 in 2021** to address
some of the due-process criticism — adjusting the notice
mechanics and the procedure. The statute **survived** as a
criminal remedy. Confirm the precise post-amendment notice period
and procedure against the current text before advising any party.

## Practical posture

- For a **tenant**: a § 18-16-101 citation is a criminal matter.
  Do not treat it as a civil eviction. Preserve defenses
  (payment, defective notice) and get counsel. A conviction is a
  criminal record plus a fine.
- For a **landlord**: the criminal track is fast and coercive but
  constitutionally contested; the civil unlawful-detainer track
  (§ 18-60-301 et seq.) is the route that yields a money judgment
  and a writ of possession. Many landlords use the civil track
  for the judgment and reserve the criminal statute as leverage.
