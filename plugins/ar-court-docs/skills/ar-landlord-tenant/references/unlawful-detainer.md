# Arkansas Civil Unlawful Detainer — Ark. Code Ann. § 18-60-301 et seq.

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Verify every notice period, bond amount,
> and deadline against the current Ark. Code Ann. and the
> applicable District/Circuit Court local rules before relying.

The civil eviction track in Arkansas is **unlawful detainer**,
governed by Ark. Code Ann. § 18-60-301 et seq. It is separate
from the criminal failure-to-vacate statute (§ 18-16-101) and
from the older forcible-entry-and-detainer provisions. It yields
both **possession** and a **money judgment** (back rent +
damages).

## The sequence

1. **Default + written notice to vacate.** The landlord serves a
   **written notice to vacate**. For nonpayment / holdover the
   notice period is **3 days** (confirm the exact period and
   content requirements at § 18-60-304). The notice must be in
   writing and properly served.

2. **Complaint for unlawful detainer.** Filed in District Court
   (the high-volume eviction forum, limited civil jurisdiction
   up to $25,000 and small claims to $5,000) or Circuit Court.
   The complaint pleads the lease/tenancy, the default, the
   notice, and the demand for possession + rent + damages.

3. **Summons + notice of intention to issue writ of
   possession.** This is the distinctive Arkansas mechanic. The
   defendant is served with a **notice that the court will issue
   a writ of possession** unless the defendant **files a written
   objection within five (5) days** (confirm the exact window)
   and, to retain possession during the litigation, **posts a
   bond**. If the tenant does not timely object, the court issues
   an **immediate writ of possession** — before any trial on the
   merits.

4. **Bond to retain possession.** A tenant who timely objects
   must post a bond (set by the court, generally tied to the rent
   accruing during the suit) to remain in possession pending
   trial. The landlord may likewise be required to post a bond to
   take immediate possession. The bond requirement is what makes
   the immediate-possession remedy fast and landlord-favorable.

5. **Trial.** On the merits: possession, back rent, holdover
   damages, and costs.

6. **Writ of possession + execution.** The sheriff executes the
   writ and restores possession to the landlord.

## Common tenant defenses

- **Defective notice to vacate** — not in writing, wrong period,
  improper service, wrong amount demanded. The single most common
  defense.
- **No default / payment made** — rent was tendered or accepted;
  partial-payment waiver.
- **Wrong party / no landlord-tenant relationship** — the
  plaintiff is not the owner or assignee.
- **Bond/objection procedure not honored** — the tenant was not
  given the notice-of-intent-to-issue-writ or the objection
  window.
- **Counterclaims** — security-deposit return (if the landlord is
  covered, § 18-16-301), habitability under Act 1052 (if the
  lease is post-effective-date and the notice-cure sequence was
  followed).

## Forcible entry and detainer

Arkansas also retains older forcible-entry-and-detainer
provisions; the modern statutory unlawful-detainer chapter
(§ 18-60-301 et seq.) is the route used in practice. Confirm
which chapter the plaintiff's complaint actually invokes.

## Appeal

A District Court eviction judgment is appealable to **Circuit
Court for trial de novo** within the appeal window (30 days;
confirm in `ar-deadlines`). De novo means the Circuit Court
rehears the case fresh; the District Court result is not
reviewed for error.
