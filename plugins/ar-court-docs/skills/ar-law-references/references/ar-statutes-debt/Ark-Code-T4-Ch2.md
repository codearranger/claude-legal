## Scope

Title 4, Chapter 2 is Arkansas's enactment of **UCC Article 2 (Sales)** —
the law governing transactions in goods (movable things). It supplies the
formation, warranty, performance, and remedy rules that control most
consumer-goods and merchant sales disputes. It does **not** govern
contracts for services, real property, or pure money/credit transactions
(those sound in common-law contract or other UCC articles). Section numbers
run in the **§ 4-2-NNN** range, tracking the official UCC § 2-NNN numbering.

## Scope and subject matter

- **§ 4-2-102 — Scope.** Article 2 applies to "transactions in goods."
  Mixed goods/services contracts are tested by the predominant-purpose
  inquiry developed in the case law.
- **§ 4-2-105 — Definitions: "goods."** Goods are things movable at the
  time of identification to the contract; the section also defines future
  goods, fungible goods, and lot.
- **§ 4-2-104 — "Merchant."** A party who deals in goods of the kind or
  otherwise by occupation holds itself out as having knowledge or skill
  peculiar to the practice. Merchant status triggers heightened rules
  (e.g., the firm-offer and confirmatory-memo provisions).

## Formation

- **§ 4-2-201 — Statute of frauds.** A contract for the sale of goods for
  the price of **$500 or more** (verify against current text) must be
  evidenced by a signed writing, subject to the merchant confirmatory-memo,
  specially-manufactured-goods, admissions, and part-performance exceptions.
- **§ 4-2-204 — Formation in general.** A contract may be made in any
  manner sufficient to show agreement, including conduct; indefiniteness as
  to one or more terms does not defeat formation if the parties intended a
  contract and there is a reasonably certain basis for a remedy.
- **§ 4-2-205 — Firm offers.** A merchant's signed written offer to hold
  an offer open is irrevocable for the stated time (or a reasonable time,
  not to exceed three months — verify) without consideration.
- **§ 4-2-206 — Offer and acceptance.** Acceptance may be by any reasonable
  manner and medium; an order to ship may be accepted by prompt shipment.
- **§ 4-2-207 — Additional terms (battle of the forms).** Governs the
  effect of an acceptance or confirmation that states terms additional to
  or different from the offer — Arkansas's enactment of the classic
  "battle of the forms" provision.
- **§ 4-2-209 — Modification, rescission, waiver.** An agreement modifying
  a sales contract needs no consideration to be binding; a signed agreement
  may require modifications to be in writing.

## Warranties

- **§ 4-2-313 — Express warranties.** Created by affirmations of fact,
  promises, descriptions, or samples that become part of the basis of the
  bargain.
- **§ 4-2-314 — Implied warranty of merchantability.** Implied in a
  contract for sale if the seller is a **merchant** with respect to goods
  of that kind; goods must be fit for ordinary purposes, adequately
  packaged, and conform to label/promises.
- **§ 4-2-315 — Implied warranty of fitness for a particular purpose.**
  Arises where the seller has reason to know the buyer's particular purpose
  and the buyer relies on the seller's skill or judgment.
- **§ 4-2-316 — Exclusion or modification of warranties.** Disclaimers of
  merchantability must mention "merchantability" and (if written) be
  conspicuous; "as is" / "with all faults" language and course of dealing
  can also exclude implied warranties (verify the precise mechanics).

## Performance, risk, and breach

- **§ 4-2-301 — General obligations.** Seller must transfer and deliver;
  buyer must accept and pay in accordance with the contract.
- **§ 4-2-507 / § 4-2-511 — Tender of delivery / tender of payment.**
  Tender of delivery is a condition to the buyer's duty to accept and pay;
  tender of payment is a condition to the seller's duty to tender and
  complete delivery.
- **§ 4-2-601 — Buyer's rights on improper delivery (perfect-tender rule).**
  If the goods or tender fail in any respect to conform, the buyer may
  reject all, accept all, or accept any commercial units and reject the
  rest.
- **§ 4-2-606 / § 4-2-608 — Acceptance / revocation of acceptance.** Defines
  what constitutes acceptance and when a buyer may revoke an acceptance for
  a substantially impairing nonconformity.
- **§ 4-2-615 — Excuse by failure of presupposed conditions.** Commercial
  impracticability defense.

## Remedies

- **§ 4-2-703 — Seller's remedies in general** (withhold delivery, stop
  delivery, resell under § 4-2-706, recover damages under § 4-2-708, or
  recover the price under § 4-2-709).
- **§ 4-2-708 / § 4-2-709 — Seller's damages / action for the price.**
  Market-contract differential or lost-profit measure; the price action
  is the seller's analog of specific enforcement against a paying buyer.
- **§ 4-2-711 through § 4-2-715 — Buyer's remedies.** Cover (§ 4-2-712),
  market-contract damages (§ 4-2-713), damages for accepted goods
  (§ 4-2-714), and incidental/consequential damages (§ 4-2-715).
- **§ 4-2-718 / § 4-2-719 — Liquidated damages / contractual modification of
  remedy.** Permits agreed remedies and limits on consequential damages,
  subject to unconscionability and failure-of-essential-purpose limits.

## Statute of limitations — the four-year sales clock

- **§ 4-2-725 — Statute of limitations in contracts for sale.** An action
  for breach of any contract for sale must be commenced within **four (4)
  years** after the cause of action accrues (verify against current text).
  The parties may by agreement reduce the period to **not less than one
  year** but may not extend it. A breach-of-warranty cause of action
  generally accrues on **tender of delivery**, regardless of the buyer's
  lack of knowledge, except where a warranty explicitly extends to future
  performance. **Drafting note:** the four-year Article 2 SOL is distinct
  from the general contract limitations periods in Title 16, ch. 56 (the
  five-year written-contract / three-year open-account periods); where a
  dispute is genuinely a sale of goods, § 4-2-725 governs and may be
  shorter than the generic written-contract period.

## Drafting note

In a consumer-goods dispute, frame the warranty theory under § 4-2-314 /
§ 4-2-315 and check § 4-2-316 for an effective disclaimer; in a debt or
price-recovery posture, the seller's claim sounds under § 4-2-709 and is
time-barred under § 4-2-725 if filed more than four years after accrual.
Confirm the predominant-purpose characterization before assuming Article 2
(versus common-law contract) supplies the governing rules.
