## Scope

Title 4, Chapter 3 is Arkansas's enactment of **UCC Article 3 (Negotiable
Instruments)** — the law of notes and drafts (including checks). It governs
when a writing is a negotiable instrument, how rights in it pass by
negotiation, who is a **holder in due course** taking free of most defenses,
and how an instrument is enforced. Article 3 is central to debt litigation:
a promissory note is a negotiable instrument, and a debt buyer suing on a
note (as distinct from an open-account or credit-card receivable) must
satisfy Article 3's enforcement and transfer rules. Section numbers run in
the **§ 4-3-NNN** range, tracking the official UCC § 3-NNN numbering.

## What is a negotiable instrument

- **§ 4-3-104 — Negotiable instrument.** Defines the requirements: a signed,
  unconditional promise or order to pay a fixed amount of money, payable to
  bearer or to order, payable on demand or at a definite time, and without
  other undertaking except as permitted. Distinguishes notes from drafts.
- **§ 4-3-103 — Definitions.** "Maker," "drawer," "drawee," "acceptor,"
  "order," "promise," "good faith," "ordinary care," etc.
- **§ 4-3-106 — Unconditional promise or order.** A reference to another
  writing for the consideration or limiting payment to a particular fund
  generally does not make the promise conditional (verify exact mechanics).
- **§ 4-3-108 — Payable on demand or at a definite time.**
- **§ 4-3-109 — Payable to bearer or to order.**

## Negotiation, indorsement, and holder status

- **§ 4-3-201 — Negotiation.** Transfer of possession (and any necessary
  indorsement) so that the transferee becomes the holder.
- **§ 4-3-203 — Transfer; right to enforce.** Transfer vests in the
  transferee the transferor's right to enforce, including the right to
  become a holder. **Key chain-of-title point:** an assignee who takes by
  transfer but lacks a proper indorsement may still acquire the right to
  enforce, but bears the burden of proving the transfer.
- **§ 4-3-204 / § 4-3-205 — Indorsement; special, blank, and anomalous
  indorsements.** Determine whether an instrument becomes bearer paper
  (negotiable by delivery alone) or order paper (requiring indorsement).
- **§ 4-3-301 — Person entitled to enforce.** The "holder" of the
  instrument, a nonholder in possession with the rights of a holder, or a
  person entitled to enforce a lost/destroyed instrument under § 4-3-309.

## Holder in due course

- **§ 4-3-302 — Holder in due course (HDC).** A holder who takes the
  instrument for value, in good faith, and without notice that it is
  overdue, dishonored, or subject to a defense or claim. An HDC takes free
  of most personal defenses (§ 4-3-305).
- **§ 4-3-305 — Defenses and claims in recoupment.** Distinguishes "real"
  defenses (good even against an HDC — e.g., infancy, duress, illegality,
  fraud in the factum, discharge in insolvency) from "personal" defenses
  (cut off as to an HDC). **Consumer caveat:** the FTC Holder Rule and
  related consumer-protection doctrines may neutralize HDC status in
  consumer-credit transactions — pair this section with the federal
  references corpus before assuming a holder takes free of consumer
  defenses.

## Enforcement, liability, and discharge

- **§ 4-3-308 — Proof of signatures and status as holder in due course.**
  Each signature is admitted unless specifically denied in the pleadings;
  once signatures are established or admitted, the plaintiff is entitled to
  recover unless the defendant proves a defense. **Pleading hook:** a debtor
  who wishes to contest a signature must specifically deny it.
- **§ 4-3-309 — Enforcement of a lost, destroyed, or stolen instrument.**
  A person not in possession may enforce only by proving entitlement to
  enforce when loss occurred, the facts preventing production, and the
  instrument's terms; the court must provide adequate protection against a
  competing claim. **Debt-buyer relevance:** a plaintiff that cannot produce
  the original note must satisfy § 4-3-309.
- **§ 4-3-310 — Effect of instrument on the underlying obligation.**
- **§ 4-3-412 through § 4-3-415 — Liability of maker, drawer, indorser, and
  acceptor.**
- **§ 4-3-601 through § 4-3-605 — Discharge** (payment, cancellation,
  impairment of recourse/collateral).

## Relevance to debt-buyer chain of title

When a debt buyer sues on a **promissory note**, Article 3 controls who may
enforce. The plaintiff must show it is a **person entitled to enforce**
under § 4-3-301 — either the holder (possession + any required
indorsement under § 4-3-201 / § 4-3-204) or a nonholder with the rights of
a holder by transfer under § 4-3-203, or it must satisfy the lost-instrument
requirements of § 4-3-309. A break in the indorsement chain, an absent
original, or an unproven transfer is a defense to enforcement. This is
distinct from open-account / credit-card receivables, which are typically
**not** negotiable instruments and pass as **accounts** under UCC Article 9
(Chapter 9) rather than by negotiation under Article 3 — confirm which
characterization the plaintiff's documents support before framing the
chain-of-title defense.

## Drafting note

For a note-based collection action, scrutinize the indorsement chain
(§§ 4-3-201 to 4-3-205), demand production of the original or a § 4-3-309
showing, and use § 4-3-308's specific-denial mechanism to put the
plaintiff to its proof on signatures. For consumer paper, layer the FTC
Holder Rule analysis (federal references) over the § 4-3-305 HDC
defense-cutoff rules.
