## Scope

Title 4, Chapter 59 contains Arkansas's enactment of the **Uniform
Fraudulent Transfer Act (UFTA)** at § 4-59-201 et seq. **DISTINCTIVE:**
Arkansas has **retained the UFTA framework and has NOT adopted the 2014
"Uniform Voidable Transactions Act (UVTA)" rebrand** — the statute is still
the **Uniform Fraudulent Transfer Act** with the "fraudulent transfer"
terminology. The Act lets a creditor reach assets a debtor has transferred
(or obligations the debtor has incurred) to hinder, delay, or defraud
creditors, or that left the debtor without adequate value while insolvent.
Section numbers run in the **§ 4-59-2NN** range.

## Definitions and reach

- **§ 4-59-201 — Definitions.** Defines "asset," "claim," "creditor,"
  "debtor," "transfer," "insolvent," "relief," and (critically) **"value"**
  given for a transfer. Note the statutory definition of **insolvency**
  (debts exceeding the fair value of assets; plus a presumption of
  insolvency where the debtor is generally not paying debts as they become
  due — verify the precise formulation).
- **§ 4-59-202 — Insolvency** (the balance-sheet and not-paying-debts tests
  and the presumption).
- **§ 4-59-203 — Value.** When value is given for a transfer or obligation;
  reasonably equivalent value as the core of the constructive-fraud analysis.

## The two fraud theories

- **§ 4-59-204 — Transfers fraudulent as to present and future creditors
  (actual + constructive fraud).** A transfer/obligation is fraudulent as to
  a creditor (whether the claim arose before or after) if made:
  - **(a)(1) — with actual intent to hinder, delay, or defraud** any
    creditor (**actual fraud**); or
  - **(a)(2) — without receiving reasonably equivalent value** AND the
    debtor was engaged (or about to engage) in a business/transaction with
    unreasonably small remaining assets, or intended/believed/reasonably
    should have believed it would incur debts beyond its ability to pay
    (**constructive fraud**).
  **The "badges of fraud."** Subsection (b) (verify) lists the
  non-exclusive factors used to infer actual intent — e.g., transfer to an
  insider, retention of possession/control after transfer, concealment,
  threat of suit before the transfer, transfer of substantially all assets,
  absconding, removal/concealment of assets, inadequacy of consideration,
  insolvency at or shortly after the transfer, and the timing relative to
  incurring a substantial debt.
- **§ 4-59-205 — Transfers fraudulent as to present creditors.** Covers
  (1) a transfer for less than reasonably equivalent value while the debtor
  is **insolvent** or becomes insolvent as a result, and (2) transfers to
  insiders for an antecedent debt where the debtor was insolvent and the
  insider had reasonable cause to believe so — both **constructive-fraud**
  theories available to a **present** creditor.

## Remedies and the creditor's burden

- **§ 4-59-207 — Remedies of creditors.** A creditor may obtain:
  - **avoidance** of the transfer/obligation to the extent necessary to
    satisfy the claim;
  - an **attachment** or other provisional remedy against the asset
    transferred;
  - subject to applicable principles and rules of civil procedure, an
    **injunction** against further disposition, **appointment of a
    receiver**, or **any other relief** the circumstances require.
- **§ 4-59-208 — Defenses, liability, and protection of the transferee.**
  A **good-faith transferee for reasonably equivalent value** takes
  protected (the transfer is not voidable against such a transferee); the
  section also addresses the measure of a judgment against a transferee and
  the good-faith-transferee's lien/reduction protections.
- **§ 4-59-209 — Extinguishment / statute of repose.** A claim is
  extinguished unless brought within the prescribed period — generally
  **within four (4) years** after the transfer/obligation (or, for actual-
  fraud claims, within one year after the transfer was or reasonably could
  have been discovered, subject to the outer limit) (verify the exact
  periods against current text). **The creditor's burden:** the claimant
  generally bears the burden of proving the elements of a fraudulent
  transfer, with the badges of fraud supplying circumstantial proof of
  actual intent.
- **§ 4-59-210 — Supplementary general principles** (the Act is supplemented
  by principles of law and equity).
- **§ 4-59-211 — Uniformity of application and construction.**

## Drafting note

To set aside a debtor's transfer, plead the theory precisely: **actual
fraud** under § 4-59-204(a)(1) (marshal the badges of fraud; plead with
Ark. R. Civ. P. 9(b) particularity where intent/fraud is the gravamen), or
**constructive fraud** under § 4-59-204(a)(2) / § 4-59-205 (focus on
reasonably-equivalent-value and insolvency, which do **not** require proof
of intent). Request avoidance plus a provisional remedy (attachment or
injunction) under § 4-59-207, and anticipate the **good-faith-transferee-
for-value** defense under § 4-59-208. Watch the § 4-59-209 limitations /
extinguishment periods, which can bar a stale avoidance claim.
