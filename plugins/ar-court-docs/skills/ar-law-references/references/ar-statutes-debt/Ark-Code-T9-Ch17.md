## Scope

Chapter 17 is Arkansas's enactment of the **Uniform Interstate Family Support
Act (UIFSA)** — the uniform framework that allocates **jurisdiction to
establish, enforce, and modify child-support and spousal-support orders across
state (and international) lines**, and that bars conflicting multi-state orders.
Arkansas's enactment incorporates the **2008 UIFSA amendments** implementing the
**Hague Convention** on international support (verify the enacted-version
amendments against current text). It is administered through Circuit Court and
the state IV-D child-support agency.

## Core UIFSA architecture (by article/section family)

- **§ 9-17-101 — Short title; definitions.** "Uniform Interstate Family Support
  Act"; defines key terms ("tribunal," "support order," "obligor," "obligee,"
  "issuing state/tribunal," "initiating" and "responding" tribunal, etc.).
- **§ 9-17-102 — State tribunal and support-enforcement agency.** Identifies the
  Arkansas tribunal (Circuit Court) and the IV-D agency.
- **Personal jurisdiction over a nonresident (long-arm).** UIFSA's bases for
  exercising **personal jurisdiction over a nonresident individual** to
  establish or modify support (e.g., personal service in-state, consent,
  residence with the child, the child residing in-state as a result of the
  party's acts, intercourse in-state resulting in conception, etc. — verify the
  enumerated bases against current text).
- **The ONE-ORDER / CONTROLLING-ORDER rule.** The defining UIFSA principle: only
  **one support order** may be effective at a time. Where multiple orders exist,
  the tribunal applies the statutory rules to determine the single
  **controlling order**; later tribunals must recognize and not contradict it.
- **Continuing, exclusive jurisdiction (CEJ).** The issuing tribunal retains
  **continuing, exclusive jurisdiction** to **modify** its child-support order
  so long as the obligor, the obligee, or the child resides in the issuing state
  (or the parties consent). Once everyone has left the issuing state, modification
  jurisdiction shifts under the statutory rules.
- **Registration for enforcement.** A support order of another state may be
  **registered in Arkansas for enforcement** by filing the order and the
  required documents; the registered order is enforceable as an Arkansas order
  unless the respondent timely **contests** registration on the limited
  statutory defenses.
- **Registration for modification.** Stricter conditions apply to registering a
  foreign order **for modification** (generally the issuing state must have lost
  CEJ, or the parties must consent); the modifying tribunal applies its own
  procedure but honors nonmodifiable aspects of the original order.
- **Direct income withholding.** An income-withholding order issued in another
  state may be **sent directly to an Arkansas employer** without first
  registering with a tribunal.
- **Two-state (initiating/responding) proceedings, discovery, and choice of
  law.** Provisions govern initiating-state petitions forwarded to a responding
  tribunal, special evidentiary rules (telephonic testimony; admissibility of
  records), and the **choice-of-law** rule that the issuing state's law governs
  the nature, extent, and duration of the support obligation and the
  computation of arrears.

> **Distinctive features:** the **one-order / controlling-order** rule and
> **continuing, exclusive jurisdiction** to modify are the heart of UIFSA; pair
> registration-for-enforcement with the limited contest defenses, and note the
> **issuing-state-law-governs** choice-of-law rule on duration and arrears.
> Interstate **custody** jurisdiction is a separate statute — see the UCCJEA at
> `Ark-Code-T9-Ch19.md`. The substantive child-support **guideline** is
> Administrative Order No. 10 (income shares) — see `Ark-Code-T9-Ch27.md`.
