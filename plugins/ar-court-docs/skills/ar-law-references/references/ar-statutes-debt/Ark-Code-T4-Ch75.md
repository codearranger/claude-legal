## Scope

Title 4, Chapter 75 ("Trade Practices") collects several distinct
commercial subjects. The two of principal litigation interest are
(1) the **Arkansas Trade Secrets Act** (§ 4-75-601 et seq.) — Arkansas's
enactment of the Uniform Trade Secrets Act — and (2) the **covenants-not-
to-compete** statute (§ 4-75-101) enacted by **Act 921 of 2015**, which
codified the enforceability standard for non-compete agreements and
authorized judicial reformation. Section numbers run in the **§ 4-75-NNN**
range.

## Covenants not to compete — § 4-75-101 (Act 921 of 2015)

- **§ 4-75-101 — Covenant not to compete agreement.** Act 921 of 2015
  **codified** the enforceability standard for covenants not to compete,
  changing the prior common-law posture. Key features (verify against
  current text):
  - A covenant not to compete is **enforceable** if the employer has a
    **protectable business interest** and the covenant is **no greater than
    necessary** to protect that interest. The statute identifies recognized
    protectable interests (e.g., trade secrets and confidential information,
    customer/client relationships and goodwill, and specialized/proprietary
    training and information).
  - **A specified geographic limit is NOT required** for enforceability;
    the absence of an express geographic restriction does not by itself
    render a covenant unenforceable so long as it is otherwise reasonable.
  - **Judicial reformation / "blue-penciling" is expressly authorized:** a
    court that finds a covenant **overbroad** as to time, scope, or
    geography **shall reform** the covenant and enforce it as reformed, to
    the extent reasonable, rather than strike it entirely. This is the
    principal change worked by Act 921 — under prior common law an
    overbroad covenant was more likely void in full.
  - A presumption of reasonableness may attach to a covenant whose duration
    is within a statutorily referenced period (**verify the exact duration
    figure against current text**).
  **Drafting hook:** to defend an employee, attack the protectable interest
  and the necessity/reasonableness; to enforce, plead the protectable
  interest with specificity and request reformation in the alternative.

## Arkansas Trade Secrets Act — § 4-75-601 et seq. (UTSA)

- **§ 4-75-601 — Title.** "Trade Secrets Act."
- **§ 4-75-602 — Definitions.** Defines **"trade secret"** (information,
  including a formula, pattern, compilation, program, device, method,
  technique, or process, that derives independent economic value from not
  being generally known or readily ascertainable and is the subject of
  **reasonable efforts to maintain secrecy**) and **"misappropriation"**
  (acquisition by improper means, or disclosure/use without consent by one
  who knew or had reason to know the trade secret was acquired by improper
  means or under a duty of secrecy).
- **§ 4-75-603 — Injunctive relief.** Actual or threatened misappropriation
  may be **enjoined**; an injunction may continue for a reasonable period to
  eliminate any commercial advantage and, in exceptional circumstances, may
  condition future use on payment of a reasonable royalty.
- **§ 4-75-604 — Damages.** A complainant may recover damages for the
  **actual loss** caused by misappropriation **and** the **unjust
  enrichment** not taken into account in computing actual loss; in lieu of
  other measures, damages may be measured by a **reasonable royalty**.
  **Exemplary damages:** for **willful and malicious** misappropriation,
  the court may award **exemplary damages of up to twice (2x)** the
  compensatory award (verify the multiplier against current text).
- **§ 4-75-605 — Attorney's fees.** The court may award **reasonable
  attorney's fees** to the prevailing party where misappropriation was
  willful and malicious, where a claim of misappropriation was made in bad
  faith, or where a motion to terminate an injunction was made or resisted
  in bad faith.
- **§ 4-75-606 — Preservation of secrecy.** The court shall preserve the
  secrecy of an alleged trade secret by reasonable means (protective
  orders, in-camera hearings, sealing).
- **§ 4-75-607 — Statute of limitations.** An action for misappropriation
  must be brought within a prescribed period after the misappropriation is
  discovered or by the exercise of reasonable diligence should have been
  discovered (**verify the period — commonly three years**); a continuing
  misappropriation is treated as a single claim.
- **§ 4-75-608 — Effect on other law / preemption.** The Act **displaces
  conflicting tort, restitutionary, and other law** providing civil
  remedies for trade-secret misappropriation (it does not displace
  contractual remedies, other civil remedies not based on misappropriation,
  or criminal remedies). **Pleading hook:** UTSA preemption can knock out
  duplicative common-law conversion / unjust-enrichment counts predicated on
  the same misappropriation.

## Drafting note

For a trade-secret claim, plead the existence of a protectable trade secret
(§ 4-75-602 — emphasize the **reasonable secrecy efforts**), the
misappropriation, and request both injunctive relief (§ 4-75-603) and
damages (§ 4-75-604), pleading **willful and malicious** conduct to unlock
the up-to-2x exemplary award and § 4-75-605 fees. Anticipate the § 4-75-608
preemption defense to companion common-law counts. For a non-compete
dispute, frame enforceability under § 4-75-101's protectable-interest /
necessity test and request reformation in the alternative; plead any fraud-
based companion theory with Ark. R. Civ. P. 9(b) particularity.
