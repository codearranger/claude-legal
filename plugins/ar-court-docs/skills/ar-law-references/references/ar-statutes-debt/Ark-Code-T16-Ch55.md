## Scope

Title 16, Chapter 55 codifies the **Civil Justice Reform Act of 2003**
(Act 649 of 2003), Arkansas's omnibus tort-reform statute. It replaced
joint-and-several liability with a **several-liability** apportionment
regime, addressed fault allocation, and imposed damages caps. Several of its
key provisions were later **struck down by the Arkansas Supreme Court** on
separation-of-powers and jury-trial grounds — those provisions remain on the
statute books but are **unenforceable**, so the chapter must be read together
with the controlling case law below.

## Key sections

- **§ 16-55-201 — Several liability / apportionment of damages.** Abolishes
  pure joint-and-several liability and provides that, in an action for
  personal injury, property damage, or wrongful death, **each defendant is
  liable only for the share of compensatory damages corresponding to that
  defendant's percentage of fault** (several, not joint). The fact-finder
  assigns a percentage of fault to each party. This several-liability rule is
  the still-operative core of the chapter.
- **§ 16-55-202 — Determination/allocation of fault among "parties."** The
  companion provision directing the finder of fact to determine the fault of
  each "party," including provisions that purported to let fault be allocated
  to **nonparties**. **STRUCK / NARROWED:** the Arkansas Supreme Court held the
  **nonparty-fault allocation** scheme unconstitutional in
  ***Johnson v. Rockwell Automation, Inc.*, 2009 Ark. 241**, on the ground
  that the legislature invaded the judiciary's rule-making authority and the
  right to trial by jury. Fault therefore generally cannot be apportioned to
  a phantom/nonparty under the struck provision — verify the current state of
  the law and any legislative response.
- **§ 16-55-208 — Punitive-damages cap.** Imposed a statutory ceiling on
  **punitive damages** (a fixed-dollar/multiple-of-compensatory cap — verify
  the figures against text). **STRUCK:** held unconstitutional in
  ***Bayer CropScience LP v. Schafer*, 2011 Ark. 518**, as violating
  **Ark. Const. art. 5, § 32** (no law limiting recovery for injuries) and
  separation of powers. The cap is **not enforceable**.
- **§ 16-55-206 / -207 — Punitive-damages standards / proof.** Set the
  substantive standard (clear-and-convincing proof of malice or reckless
  indifence) and procedure for punitive awards. The standard-of-proof
  provisions are generally intact even though the § 16-55-208 *cap* was
  struck — confirm current text.
- **§ 16-55-212 / -220 — Evidence of medical expenses / collateral source
  and related reform provisions.** Address the admissibility and measure of
  medical-expense damages; read together with controlling case law on the
  collateral-source rule.

## ARKANSAS QUIRK — no damages caps survive

Because **Ark. Const. art. 5, § 32** prohibits any law limiting the amount of
damages recoverable for injuries resulting in death or for personal injuries,
the Civil Justice Reform Act's caps did not survive. Combined with *Bayer
CropScience* (punitive cap) and the failure/striking of later ballot
tort-reform measures, **Arkansas currently has no enforceable non-economic or
punitive damages cap.** Do not draft as if a cap applies.

## Drafting note

In a multi-defendant tort case, plead and argue several liability under
§ 16-55-201 (each defendant liable for its own share). Do **not** assume
nonparty fault can be allocated — *Johnson v. Rockwell* struck that — and do
**not** treat punitive damages as capped — *Bayer CropScience* struck
§ 16-55-208. Confirm the current statutory text and the latest case law,
since the legislature periodically revisits this chapter.
