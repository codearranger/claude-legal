## Scope

Title 16, Chapter 114 is the **Arkansas Medical Malpractice Act** — the
statutory framework for actions for **medical injury** against healthcare
providers. It supplies the limitations period, the burden of proof, the
expert-testimony requirement, and the elements of a malpractice claim. A
distinctive feature is that the Act's **pre-suit "affidavit of merit" /
reasonable-cause-affidavit requirement was held unconstitutional**, so there
is **no valid affidavit-of-merit prerequisite** to suit in Arkansas.

## Key sections

- **§ 16-114-201 — Definitions / scope.** Defines "medical care provider,"
  "medical injury," and "action for medical injury," fixing the Act's
  coverage.
- **§ 16-114-203 — Statute of limitations: 2 years.** Actions for medical
  injury must be commenced within **2 years** of the act/omission, with a
  **continuing-course-of-treatment** and **foreign-object** discovery
  refinement (verify the exact accrual and discovery language against current
  text). This is **shorter** than the general 3-year tort period in
  § 16-56-105.
- **§ 16-114-206 — Burden of proof / standard of care.** The plaintiff must
  prove, **through expert testimony** by a provider of the same specialty
  (the "locality" / applicable standard of care), (1) the applicable standard
  of care, (2) breach, and (3) that the breach **proximately caused** the
  injury. Expert testimony is generally **required** to establish the standard
  and causation except in cases of obvious negligence.
- **§ 16-114-207 — Limitations on expert testimony.** Constrains who may
  testify as a standard-of-care expert.
- **§ 16-114-209 — Reasonable-cause / "affidavit of merit" requirement —
  HELD UNCONSTITUTIONAL.** This section purported to require the plaintiff to
  file a **pre-suit affidavit** from a qualified expert attesting to a
  reasonable basis for the claim, on pain of dismissal. The Arkansas Supreme
  Court struck it as an **unconstitutional invasion of the Court's exclusive
  rule-making authority over court procedure** (separation of powers) in
  ***Summerville v. Thrower*, 2007 Ark. 366** (see also *Weidrick v. Arnold*).
  **There is therefore no enforceable affidavit-of-merit prerequisite** to a
  medical-malpractice suit in Arkansas — do not draft a complaint as if one
  is required.

## ARKANSAS QUIRK — no affidavit of merit; no damages cap

- **No affidavit-of-merit prerequisite** (§ 16-114-209 struck — *Summerville*).
- **No damages cap** — caps on recovery for personal injury/death are barred by
  **Ark. Const. art. 5, § 32**, and statutory caps touching malpractice
  recovery have been struck on that ground. Plead damages without assuming a
  ceiling.

## Drafting note

For a medical-malpractice complaint: compute the **2-year** SOL under
§ 16-114-203 (watch the continuing-treatment / foreign-object refinements);
secure a **qualified same-specialty expert** to establish standard of care
and causation under § 16-114-206; and do **not** treat § 16-114-209's
affidavit-of-merit as a live requirement (it was struck in *Summerville v.
Thrower*, 2007 Ark. 366). Label specific figures "(verify against current
text)."
