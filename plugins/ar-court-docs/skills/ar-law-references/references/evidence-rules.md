# Arkansas Rules of Evidence (Ark. R. Evid.) — Summary

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Summary for drafting reference. Read the
> verbatim rule (in `court-rules/` or at arcourts.gov) and confirm the
> current subsection lettering before relying on it.

The Arkansas Rules of Evidence are modeled on the Federal Rules of
Evidence and track them closely. The rules below are the ones that do
the work in a documentary civil case (especially a debt-buyer or
business-records matter).

## Relevance — Ark. R. Evid. 401–403

- **401** — relevant evidence is evidence having any tendency to make a
  fact of consequence more or less probable.
- **402** — relevant evidence is generally admissible; irrelevant
  evidence is not.
- **403** — relevant evidence may be **excluded** if its probative
  value is **substantially outweighed** by the danger of unfair
  prejudice, confusion, or waste of time.

## Authentication — Ark. R. Evid. 901 / 902

- **Rule 901** — **authentication**: the proponent must produce
  evidence sufficient to support a finding that the item is what the
  proponent claims. Live foundation testimony is the default route.
- **Rule 902(11)** — **self-authentication of certified domestic
  records of a regularly conducted activity**. A qualifying **written
  certification** by the custodian (or another qualified person) can
  **substitute for live foundation testimony** for a business record
  that meets the Rule 803(6) conditions. This is the standard pathway
  by which a debt buyer tries to admit account records **without** a
  live witness — scrutinize whether the certification actually satisfies
  the rule and whether the records came from the **original creditor**
  or were merely received by the debt buyer (a "records of another
  entity" / lack-of-personal-knowledge problem).
- Rule 902 also self-authenticates certified copies of **public
  records** and other enumerated categories. [VERIFY the current
  902 subsection lettering against the verbatim rule.]

## Hearsay — Ark. R. Evid. 801–807

- **801** — definitions; a statement offered for the truth of the matter
  asserted is hearsay; **801(d)** carves out admissions / certain prior
  statements as **non-hearsay**.
- **802** — hearsay is inadmissible except as provided by rule.
- **Rule 803(6)** — **the business-records exception** (the load-bearing
  rule for documentary cases). A **record** (memorandum, report, data
  compilation) of acts/events/conditions is admissible if:
  1. it was **made at or near the time** by — or from information
     transmitted by — a person **with knowledge**;
  2. it was **kept in the course of a regularly conducted business
     activity**;
  3. it was the **regular practice** of that business to make the
     record;
  4. shown by the testimony of the **custodian or other qualified
     witness** (or a Rule 902(11) certification);
  - **unless** the source of information or the circumstances indicate a
    **lack of trustworthiness**.
- **803** also contains the other common exceptions (present sense
  impression, then-existing condition, recorded recollection, public
  records, etc.).
- **804** — exceptions when the declarant is **unavailable**.
- **805** — **hearsay within hearsay** must satisfy an exception at
  **each** level (relevant when a debt buyer's file contains records
  generated by the original creditor — each layer needs a foundation).
- **807** — residual exception.

## Other commonly cited rules

- **404(b)** — other crimes / wrongs / acts (not to prove character).
- **408** — compromise and offers to compromise (inadmissible to prove
  liability / amount).
- **602** — a witness must have **personal knowledge** of the matter
  (a recurring problem for an out-of-state custodian testifying about
  the **original creditor's** records).
- **702–703** — testimony by experts / bases of expert opinion.
- **1001–1008** — best-evidence rule (originals / duplicates;
  admissibility of duplicates and of summaries).

## Why this matters in a documentary case

A debt-buyer plaintiff usually needs to get **account records** into
evidence to prove the balance and the chain of title. Those records are
hearsay, so the plaintiff must satisfy **803(6)** — typically via a
**902(11)** certification or a custodian's testimony. The defense
attacks: (a) whether the witness / certification has the required
**personal knowledge** of the **original creditor's** record-keeping
(Rule 602; the "records of another entity" problem), (b) whether the
**805** layered-hearsay problem is solved, and (c) whether the
foundation actually establishes the four 803(6) conditions. See
`ar-discovery` for how discovery develops these challenges and the
`ar-consumer-debt` bundle for the chain-of-title framework.
