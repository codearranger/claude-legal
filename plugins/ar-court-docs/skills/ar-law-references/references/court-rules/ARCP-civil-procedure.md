The Arkansas Rules of Civil Procedure were adopted in 1979 and govern
procedure in the civil divisions of the circuit courts. They are patterned on
the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure but carry Arkansas-specific variations —
most notably the **fact-pleading** standard under Rule 8 and the distinctive
**Rule 60(a) 90-day window** to modify or vacate a judgment. Cite as
`Ark. R. Civ. P. [N]` (e.g., `Ark. R. Civ. P. 12(b)(6)`).

---

## Scope and construction

### Rule 1 — Scope of rules
Governs procedure in all civil proceedings in the circuit courts, with stated
exceptions. Establishes that the rules are to be construed to secure the just,
speedy, and inexpensive determination of every action.

### Rule 2 — One form of action
There is one form of action known as a "civil action." Reflects Amendment 80's
unification of law and equity in a single circuit court.

---

## Commencement, service, time

### Rule 3 — Commencement of action
A civil action is commenced by filing a complaint with the clerk of the court.
The date of filing fixes the commencement point for statute-of-limitations and
related purposes.

### Rule 4 — Summons and service of process
Governs issuance of the summons and the methods of serving process
(personal service, service on an agent, service by mail with return receipt,
warning order/constructive service, service on out-of-state and corporate
defendants). Arkansas imposes a fixed period within which the plaintiff must
obtain service after filing the complaint — historically **120 days** under
Rule 4(i) (verify against current text), extendable for good cause by timely
motion; failure to serve within the period can result in dismissal. Strict
compliance with the service rules is required in Arkansas.

### Rule 5 — Service and filing of pleadings and other papers
Governs service and filing of papers after the original complaint (motions,
discovery, notices, orders). Service may be made on a party's attorney; covers
service by mail, delivery, and electronic means. Electronic filing and
e-service operate under Administrative Order No. 21 (eFlex); registered
e-filers consent to electronic service. The signed certificate of service
documents how and when papers were served.

### Rule 6 — Time
Governs computation and enlargement of time. The day of the triggering event
is excluded and the last day is included; if the last day falls on a Saturday,
Sunday, or legal holiday, the period runs to the next day that is not one of
those. **Rule 6(d)** adds **3 days** to a response period when service is made
by mail (verify the add-on's scope and current day count against the text).
Legal holidays are those listed at Ark. Code Ann. § 1-5-101.

## Pleadings and motions

### Rule 7 — Pleadings allowed; motions
Identifies the permitted pleadings (complaint, answer, reply to a counterclaim,
answer to a cross-claim, third-party complaint and answer) and the form of
motions. A request for a court order is made by motion stating the grounds and
the relief sought.

### Rule 8 — General rules of pleading — ARKANSAS FACT-PLEADING
Requires a pleading that states a claim to set out **a statement in ordinary
and concise language of facts** showing the pleader is entitled to relief, plus
a demand for relief. **ARKANSAS QUIRK:** Arkansas is a **fact-pleading**
jurisdiction — the complaint must plead operative **facts**, not mere legal
conclusions or notice-style generalities. This is stricter than federal notice
pleading and is the analytical basis for many Rule 12(b)(6) challenges.
Rule 8 also governs affirmative defenses, which must be set forth in the
responsive pleading.

### Rule 9 — Pleading special matters; fraud with particularity
Requires that the circumstances constituting **fraud or mistake be stated with
particularity** (Rule 9(b)); other matters of mind (intent, knowledge) may be
averred generally. Also addresses capacity, conditions precedent, and special
damages.

### Rule 10 — Form of pleadings — caption, numbered paragraphs, exhibits
Prescribes the form of pleadings: a caption naming the court, the title/parties,
the docket ("Civil No.") designation, and the designation of the paper
(Rule 10(a)); averments stated in **numbered paragraphs**, each limited as far
as practicable to a single set of circumstances (Rule 10(b)); and the rule that
a copy of a written instrument that is an exhibit to a pleading **is a part of
the pleading for all purposes** (Rule 10(c)). Arkansas publishes no single
statewide page/margin/font rule; typography defers to local rules.

### Rule 11 — Signing of pleadings, motions, and other papers
Every pleading, motion, and paper must be **signed** by at least one attorney of
record or by a pro se party. The signature certifies the Rule 11
representations — that the paper is well grounded in fact and warranted by
existing law (or a good-faith argument to change it) and is not interposed for
an improper purpose. Sanctions are available for violations. Attorneys sign with
their Arkansas Bar Number; pro se filers omit the bar number and designate
"Pro Se" / "Self-Represented."

### Rule 12 — Defenses and objections; motion practice
Governs how defenses are presented. The **answer is generally due within 30
days** after service of the summons and complaint, with a longer period for
out-of-state/nonresident defendants and for service on certain government
parties (verify exact day counts against the text). Certain defenses may be
raised by pre-answer motion, including **Rule 12(b)(6) — failure to state facts
upon which relief can be granted** (the Arkansas analogue to a federal motion to
dismiss, tied to the Rule 8 fact-pleading standard). Other Rule 12 defenses
include lack of jurisdiction, improper venue, insufficiency of process or
service, and failure to join a necessary party. Rule 12 also addresses motions
for more definite statement and to strike, and waiver/consolidation of certain
defenses.

### Rule 13 — Counterclaims and cross-claims
Distinguishes **compulsory** counterclaims (those arising out of the same
transaction or occurrence as the opposing party's claim, which must be pleaded
or are generally barred) from **permissive** counterclaims. Also governs
cross-claims against co-parties.

### Rule 14 — Third-party practice
Permits a defending party to implead a third party who may be liable for all or
part of the plaintiff's claim against the defendant.

### Rule 15 — Amended and supplemental pleadings
Governs amendment of pleadings. Arkansas is generally liberal in allowing
amendments; addresses amendment as of course, by leave or consent, amendments
to conform to the evidence, and the relation-back doctrine for amendments that
arise out of the same conduct/transaction.

## Parties

### Rules 17–25 — Parties
Cover real party in interest and capacity (Rule 17), joinder of claims and
parties (Rules 18, 19, 20), misjoinder (Rule 21), interpleader (Rule 22), class
actions (Rule 23), intervention (Rule 24), and substitution of parties (Rule 25).

---

## Discovery (Rules 26–37)

### Rule 26 — General provisions governing discovery
Sets the general scope of discovery (matter relevant to the claim or defense and
reasonably calculated to lead to admissible evidence), protective orders,
supplementation duties, and the good-faith conferral expectation before discovery
motions.

### Rules 30–31 — Depositions
Govern depositions upon oral examination (Rule 30) and upon written questions
(Rule 31), including notice, conduct, and use.

### Rule 33 — Interrogatories to parties
**Arkansas DOES allow written interrogatories.** Interrogatories are served on a
party and answered under oath. Arkansas imposes a **presumptive numerical cap**
on interrogatories (including discrete subparts) absent leave of court or
stipulation — note **30** as the figure to confirm (verify against current
text). Response time is set by the rule (commonly 30 days, with allowance for
mail service — verify).

### Rule 34 — Production of documents and things; entry on land
Governs requests for production of documents, electronically stored information,
and tangible things, and requests to enter on land for inspection.

### Rule 36 — Requests for admission
Permits a party to serve requests that the other party admit the truth of
matters or the genuineness of documents; matters are deemed admitted if not
timely answered or objected to within the period set by the rule (verify the
day count).

### Rule 37 — Failure to make discovery; sanctions
Governs the **motion to compel** discovery and the sanctions available for
discovery failures, ranging from fee-shifting to issue/evidence preclusion to
dismissal or default. A good-faith effort to confer/resolve the dispute is
expected before moving to compel.

## Trials (Rules 38–53)

### Rules 38–39 — Jury trial
Govern the right to and demand for a jury trial and trial by jury or by the
court.

### Rule 41 — Dismissal of actions
Governs voluntary and involuntary dismissal, including the effect of a
non-suit and the limits on re-filing.

### Rules 47–51 — Jury selection, instructions, verdicts
Cover jurors, instructions to the jury, and the form of verdicts.

## Judgment (Rules 54–63)

### Rule 54 — Judgments; costs
Defines "judgment," addresses the entry of judgment on fewer than all claims or
parties (the Rule 54(b) certification for an otherwise non-final order), demand
for judgment, and costs.

### Rule 55 — Default
Governs **default judgment** when a party fails to plead or otherwise defend:
entry of default, the proof required to obtain judgment, and the grounds and
procedure to **set aside** a default for good cause (e.g., excusable neglect,
unavoidable casualty, or a meritorious defense — verify the rule's grounds).

### Rule 56 — Summary judgment — "MEET PROOF WITH PROOF"
Governs summary judgment. Judgment is rendered where there is no genuine issue
of material fact and the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of
law. **Arkansas standard:** once the movant makes a prima facie showing of
entitlement, the burden shifts to the non-movant to **"meet proof with proof"**
and demonstrate a genuine issue of material fact; the non-movant cannot rest on
the allegations of the pleadings (*Wallace v. Broyles*; *Flentje v. First Nat'l
Bank of Wynne*). The response and supporting materials are due within the period
fixed by the rule — note a **21-day** response posture to confirm (verify
against current text).

### Rule 58 — Entry of judgment
Governs entry of the judgment by the clerk and when a judgment becomes
effective.

### Rule 59 — New trial; amendment of judgments
Governs **motions for a new trial** and to alter or amend a judgment, listing
the grounds (e.g., irregularity, jury misconduct, newly discovered evidence,
error of law, verdict against the evidence). The motion must be filed within the
period set by the rule — note a **10-day** filing window to confirm (verify
against current text); a timely Rule 59 motion can affect the time for appeal.

### Rule 60 — Relief from judgment or order — THE 90-DAY WINDOW
Governs relief from a judgment, decree, or order. **ARKANSAS QUIRK:**
**Rule 60(a)** allows the court, to correct error or prevent the miscarriage of
justice, to **modify or vacate a judgment within 90 days** of its having been
filed of record. After the 90-day window closes, relief is available only on the
narrower grounds in **Rule 60(c)** (e.g., clerical mistakes, misrepresentation,
fraud, void judgment, newly discovered evidence, and the other enumerated
categories — verify the list against the text). The distinct **90-day Rule 60(a)
window** is a defining feature of Arkansas post-judgment practice.

### Rule 62 — Stay of proceedings to enforce a judgment
Governs stays of enforcement and supersedeas.

## Provisional and final remedies

### Rules 64–71 — Remedies and enforcement framework
Cover seizure of person or property, injunctions and restraining orders
(Rule 65), receivers, deposit in court, executions, and related enforcement
mechanics. Specific exemption and garnishment mechanics are statutory (Ark. Code
Ann. Title 16); see the `ar-post-judgment` skill.

### Rules 78–86 — Administrative and miscellaneous
Cover clerks and court administration, rules of the circuit courts, and the
effective-date/transitional provisions.
