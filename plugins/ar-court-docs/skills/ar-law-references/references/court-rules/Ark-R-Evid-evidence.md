# Arkansas Rules of Evidence (Ark. R. Evid.)

> **CURATED SUMMARY — NOT VERBATIM RULE TEXT.** This is a structured,
> citation-rich digest for drafting use. The official rule text is published
> by the Arkansas Judiciary at https://www.arcourts.gov/content/rules-supreme-court-and-court-appeals-state-arkansas. A future run of
> `scripts/pull_arkansas_rules.py` (courtrules.net mirror) will supersede
> this file with verbatim text.
>
> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Verify every rule against the current official text
> before filing.

The Arkansas Rules of Evidence are patterned on the Federal Rules of Evidence
and use the same numbering scheme (Article-based, 100-series through
1100-series). Cite as `Ark. R. Evid. [N]` (e.g., `Ark. R. Evid. 803(6)`). The
business-records exception (803(6)) and self-authentication of certified
business records (902(11)) are the workhorse rules for consumer-debt and
debt-buyer practice, where the foundation for account records and the
assignment chain is contested.

---

## Article I — General provisions (101–106)

### Rule 101 — Scope
The rules govern proceedings in Arkansas courts, subject to the exceptions
stated.

### Rule 103 — Rulings on evidence; offer of proof
Governs preservation of error by timely objection or offer of proof, the
specificity required, and plain error.

### Rule 104 — Preliminary questions
The court decides preliminary questions of admissibility (qualification of
witnesses, existence of privilege, admissibility of evidence).

### Rule 105 — Limited admissibility
When evidence is admissible for one purpose but not another, the court, on
request, restricts the evidence and instructs the jury.

## Article IV — Relevancy and its limits (401–411)

### Rule 401 — Definition of relevant evidence
Evidence is relevant if it has any tendency to make a fact of consequence more
or less probable than it would be without the evidence.

### Rule 402 — Relevant evidence generally admissible
Relevant evidence is admissible unless otherwise excluded; irrelevant evidence
is not admissible.

### Rule 403 — Exclusion for prejudice, confusion, waste of time
Relevant evidence may be excluded if its probative value is substantially
outweighed by the danger of unfair prejudice, confusion of the issues,
misleading the jury, or considerations of undue delay or needless presentation
of cumulative evidence.

### Rules 404–406 — Character, other acts, habit
Govern the use of character evidence and "other crimes, wrongs, or acts"
(Rule 404), methods of proving character (Rule 405), and habit/routine practice
(Rule 406).

### Rules 407–411 — Subsequent remedial measures; compromise; insurance
Cover subsequent remedial measures (407), compromise and offers to compromise
(408), payment of medical expenses (409), and liability insurance (411).

## Article V — Privileges (501–510)

### Rules 501–510 — Privileges
Arkansas codifies its privileges (unlike the open-ended federal approach).
Covered privileges include attorney-client (Rule 502), physician/psychotherapist
patient (Rule 503), husband-wife (Rule 504), and others, together with rules on
waiver and on comment/inference from a claim of privilege. Confirm the specific
privilege and its scope against the rule text.

## Article VI — Witnesses (601–615)

### Rule 601 — General rule of competency
Every person is competent to be a witness except as otherwise provided.

### Rule 602 — Lack of personal knowledge
A witness may testify to a matter only if evidence is introduced sufficient to
support a finding that the witness has personal knowledge of the matter.

### Rule 603 — Oath or affirmation
Before testifying, a witness must declare that the witness will testify
truthfully.

### Rules 607–610 — Impeachment
Cover who may impeach (607), impeachment by character/conduct (608), impeachment
by prior conviction (609), and the bar on inquiry into religious beliefs (610).

### Rule 611 — Mode and order of interrogation
Governs the court's control over the mode and order of examination, leading
questions, and scope of cross-examination.

### Rule 612 — Writing used to refresh memory
Governs a witness's use of a writing to refresh recollection and the adverse
party's right to inspect it.

### Rule 613 — Prior statements of witnesses
Governs examination concerning, and extrinsic proof of, a witness's prior
statements.

### Rule 615 — Exclusion of witnesses
Allows sequestration of witnesses so they cannot hear other testimony.

## Article VII — Opinions and expert testimony (701–706)

### Rule 701 — Opinion testimony by lay witnesses
A lay witness's opinions are limited to those rationally based on the witness's
perception and helpful to a clear understanding of the testimony or a fact in
issue.

### Rule 702 — Testimony by experts
Permits expert testimony by a qualified witness where scientific, technical, or
other specialized knowledge will assist the trier of fact.

### Rule 703 — Bases of opinion testimony by experts
Governs the facts or data on which an expert may base an opinion, including
otherwise-inadmissible material of a type reasonably relied upon by experts in
the field.

### Rule 704 — Opinion on ultimate issue
An opinion is not objectionable merely because it embraces an ultimate issue.

### Rules 705–706 — Disclosure of underlying facts; court-appointed experts
Cover disclosure of the facts/data underlying an expert opinion (705) and
court-appointed experts (706).

## Article VIII — Hearsay (801–806)

### Rule 801 — Definitions; statements that are not hearsay
Defines "statement," "declarant," and "hearsay," and excludes certain
statements from the hearsay definition (including prior statements of a witness
and admissions/statements of a party-opponent).

### Rule 802 — Hearsay rule
Hearsay is not admissible except as provided by the rules.

### Rule 803 — Hearsay exceptions; availability of declarant immaterial
Lists the exceptions that apply regardless of the declarant's availability. The
key exception for civil and consumer-debt practice is **Rule 803(6) — Records of
Regularly Conducted Business Activity** (the business-records exception): a
record of an act, event, condition, opinion, or diagnosis, made at or near the
time by (or from information transmitted by) a person with knowledge, kept in
the course of a regularly conducted business activity, where it was the regular
practice of that business to make the record, as shown by the testimony of the
custodian or other qualified witness **or by a 902(11) certification**, unless
the source/method/circumstances indicate a lack of trustworthiness. Other 803
exceptions include present sense impression, excited utterance, then-existing
condition, records, and public records.

### Rule 804 — Hearsay exceptions; declarant unavailable
Lists the exceptions that apply only when the declarant is unavailable
(e.g., former testimony, statement against interest).

### Rules 805–806 — Hearsay within hearsay; attacking credibility
Govern multi-level hearsay (805 — each layer must independently qualify) and
attacking/supporting the credibility of a hearsay declarant (806).

## Article IX — Authentication and identification (901–903)

### Rule 901 — Requirement of authentication
The requirement of authentication is satisfied by evidence sufficient to support
a finding that the matter in question is what its proponent claims, illustrated
by the rule's examples (testimony of a witness with knowledge, distinctive
characteristics, etc.).

### Rule 902 — Self-authentication
Lists items that are self-authenticating and require no extrinsic foundation. The
key provision for debt-buyer and business-records practice is **Rule 902(11) —
Certified Records of Regularly Conducted Activity**: a business record may be
self-authenticated by a written certification of its custodian or another
qualified person attesting to the 803(6) foundation, subject to the rule's
pre-trial notice and opportunity-to-inspect requirements. This is the mechanism
that lets account records and assignment documents come in without live custodian
testimony when the notice predicates are met.

### Rule 903 — Subscribing witness's testimony unnecessary
The testimony of a subscribing witness is not necessary to authenticate a
writing unless required by the law of the jurisdiction governing its validity.

## Article X — Contents of writings, recordings, photographs (1001–1008)

### Rule 1001 — Definitions
Defines "writings and recordings," "photographs," "original," and "duplicate."

### Rule 1002 — Best evidence rule
To prove the content of a writing, recording, or photograph, the original is
required except as otherwise provided.

### Rule 1003 — Admissibility of duplicates
A duplicate is admissible to the same extent as the original unless a genuine
question is raised about the authenticity of the original or the circumstances
make it unfair to admit the duplicate.

### Rules 1004–1008 — Other proof; functions of court and jury
Cover admissibility of other evidence of contents when originals are lost or
unobtainable (1004), public records (1005), summaries (1006), admissions
(1007), and the respective functions of court and jury (1008).
