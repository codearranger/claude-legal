## Summary judgment — the "meet proof with proof" standard

- **Wallace v. Broyles, 331 Ark. 58, 961 S.W.2d 712 (1998)** — once
  the moving party makes a **prima facie** showing of entitlement to
  summary judgment, the **non-moving party must "meet proof with
  proof"** and demonstrate a **genuine issue of material fact**.
  Conclusory denials are not enough. The court views the proof in the
  light most favorable to the non-movant and resolves doubts against
  the movant. The canonical Arkansas Rule 56 articulation.
- **Flentje v. First Nat'l Bank of Wynne, 340 Ark. 563, 11 S.W.3d 531
  (2000)** — reaffirms the "meet proof with proof" burden-shift; the
  non-movant cannot rest on the allegations of its pleadings once the
  movant has made the prima facie showing. [VERIFY pinpoint.]

> conversion) and wherever a summary-judgment motion or response is in
> play. The practical point in a documentary case: the defendant who
> comes forward with **specific** proof (e.g., a sworn denial of the
> account, gaps in the chain of title) creates a fact question that
> defeats the motion.

## Fact pleading

- Arkansas is a **fact-pleading** jurisdiction: Ark. R. Civ. P. 8(a)
  requires a statement of **facts** showing entitlement to relief, and
  on a Rule 12(b)(6) motion the court **treats the facts as true but
  disregards mere legal conclusions**, looking only at the complaint
  and its attached exhibits. A complaint that pleads only conclusions
  is subject to dismissal. [Cite the current controlling fact-pleading

## Tort reform struck down (separation of powers / jury-trial right)

- **Johnson v. Rockwell Automation, Inc., 2009 Ark. 241** — struck
  down the **nonparty-fault allocation** provision of the Civil Justice
  Reform Act of 2003 (Act 649), holding the provision unconstitutional
  as an infringement on the judiciary's rule-making authority / the
  right to trial by jury. A leading Arkansas tort-reform decision.
- **Bayer CropScience LP v. Schafer, 2011 Ark. 518, 385 S.W.3d 822** —
  struck down the **statutory punitive-damages cap** (Ark. Code Ann.
  § 16-55-208) as violating **Ark. Const. art. 5, § 32** (no laws
  limiting recoverable damages for death / personal injury) and
  separation of powers. Confirms Arkansas has **no punitive-damages
  cap** currently in force. [VERIFY pinpoint.]

> These bear on the personal-injury / tort surface; flag them when a
> party tries to invoke a damages cap or a nonparty-fault apportionment.

## Sovereign / State immunity

- **Bd. of Trustees of Univ. of Ark. v. Andrews, 2018 Ark. 12, 535
  S.W.3d 616** — under **Ark. Const. art. 5, § 20** ("The State of
  Arkansas shall never be made defendant in any of her courts"), the
  State enjoys **near-absolute immunity**, and the **legislature
  cannot waive** it by statute. Claims against the **State** go to the
  **Arkansas State Claims Commission** (Ark. Code Ann. § 19-10-201 et
  seq.), not circuit court. A pivotal (and disruptive) immunity
  decision — flag it whenever the **State** or a state entity is a
  prospective defendant.

## At-will employment / public-policy exception

- **Sterling Drug, Inc. v. Oxford, 294 Ark. 239, 743 S.W.2d 380
  (1988)** — recognized the **public-policy exception** to Arkansas's
  at-will employment doctrine (wrongful discharge for refusing to
  violate, or for reporting a violation of, a well-established public
  policy). Foundational for the employment surface.
