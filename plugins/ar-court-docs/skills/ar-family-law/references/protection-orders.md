# Arkansas Orders of Protection — Domestic Abuse Act, § 9-15-101 et seq.

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Reference notes for drafting. Verify the
> qualifying relationships, the ex parte and final-order timelines,
> the duration, and the firearm provisions against the current
> Ark. Code Ann. § 9-15-101 et seq. and applicable federal law before
> relying on anything here.

Statutory home: the **Arkansas Domestic Abuse Act**, **Ark. Code Ann.
§ 9-15-101 et seq.** Civil **orders of protection** are heard in
**Circuit Court**.

## Qualifying relationships

A petition for an order of protection may be filed by a person who is
in (or was in) a **qualifying domestic relationship** with the
respondent. Qualifying relationships under the Act generally include
(verify the current § 9-15-103 definitions):

- **Spouses** and **former spouses**
- **Family or household members** related by blood, marriage, or
  adoption
- Persons who **live or have lived together**
- Persons who **have a child in common**
- Persons in (or who were in) a **dating relationship**
- A **parent / guardian** filing on behalf of a **minor** household
  member

> If the parties are **not** in a qualifying domestic relationship,
> the proper vehicle may be a different protective remedy (e.g., a
> no-contact order in a criminal case, or a different civil order) —
> verify before filing under § 9-15-101.

## What counts as domestic abuse

"Domestic abuse" under the Act generally includes **physical harm,
bodily injury, assault**, or the **infliction of fear of imminent
physical harm**, and certain **sexual conduct/contact** between
qualifying parties — verify the current statutory definition.

## Procedure — ex parte order then final hearing

1. **Petition.** File a verified petition (often **without a filing
   fee** at the outset under the Act). The Arkansas Judiciary publishes
   a **standard petition form** (see `ar-family-court` for AOC forms).
2. **Ex parte temporary order.** On a showing of **immediate danger**,
   the court may issue an **ex parte temporary order of protection**
   without notice, effective until the final hearing.
3. **Final hearing.** A hearing on a **final order of protection** is
   set within the statutory window (**verify the current number of
   days** after the ex parte order). The respondent is served and may
   appear and contest.
4. **Final order.** After the hearing, the court may enter a **final
   order of protection** for a statutory **duration** — see below.

## Duration — VOLATILE count

| Item | Period (VERIFY against current § 9-15-205) |
|---|---|
| Final order of protection term | not less than **90 days** and not more than **10 years** (range; the court sets the specific term) — **verify** |

The order may be **renewed / extended** on petition before expiration.

## Relief available

A protection order may (verify the current § 9-15-205 menu):

- Order the respondent to **refrain from abuse / contact**
- Exclude the respondent from the **residence** and award **temporary
  possession**
- Award **temporary custody** of and **support** for minor children
- Establish **temporary visitation** terms
- Direct other relief necessary for the protection of the petitioner

## Firearm prohibition / surrender

A **final order of protection** that meets the federal criteria
triggers a **firearm-possession prohibition** under **18 U.S.C.
§ 922(g)(8)** (and state law), and the court may order **surrender** of
firearms. **Verify the current state procedure** for firearm surrender
and the precise federal trigger conditions (notice + opportunity to be
heard; the relationship and finding requirements).

## Coordination with divorce / custody

Orders of protection frequently run **parallel** to a divorce or
paternity case. Coordinate:

- **Custody / visitation** provisions across the protection order and
  the domestic-relations case (the protection order's temporary custody
  and the divorce's custody determination should not conflict).
- **Mediation** is generally **not** appropriate where there is an
  active order of protection — flag that to the court (see
  `ar-family-court`).
- Domestic abuse is a **strong factor** that can rebut the **Act 604
  joint-custody presumption** — see `custody-and-parenting.md`.
