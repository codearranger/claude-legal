# Arkansas Equitable Distribution — § 9-12-315 and the One-Half Presumption

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Reference notes for drafting. Verify the
> factor list, the classification rules, and the citation against the
> current Ark. Code Ann. § 9-12-315 before relying on anything here.

Statutory home: **Ark. Code Ann. § 9-12-315** (Division of property).
Arkansas is an **equitable-distribution** state — **NOT** a
community-property state.

## The two-step framework

### Step 1 — Classify each asset and debt

Property is classified as **marital** or **non-marital (separate)**.

**Non-marital property** (returned to the owner; not divided)
generally includes:

- Property acquired **before the marriage**
- Property acquired by **gift, bequest, devise, or descent** to one
  spouse (inheritances)
- Property acquired in **exchange** for non-marital property
- Property excluded by a **valid agreement** of the parties
- The **increase in value** of non-marital property (subject to
  active-appreciation / contribution rules — verify)

**Marital property** generally includes all other property acquired by
either spouse **during the marriage**. Confirm the statutory
definition and exclusions at the current § 9-12-315(b).

> Non-marital property is **returned to the party who owns it** under
> § 9-12-315(a)(2), though it can inform an **alimony** award (see
> `maintenance.md`).

### Step 2 — Divide marital property: the ONE-HALF presumption

The defining Arkansas feature: marital property is presumptively
divided **one-half (1/2) to each party**.

- The court **may** make an **unequal** division **only** if it finds
  an equal division would be **inequitable**.
- If the court divides unequally, it **must state its reasons and
  basis in writing**, taking into account the statutory factors.

## The § 9-12-315(a)(1)(A) factors (for an unequal division)

When the court considers an unequal division, it weighs (verify the
current enumerated list):

1. The **length of the marriage**
2. **Age, health, and station in life** of the parties
3. **Occupation** of the parties
4. **Amount and sources of income**
5. **Vocational skills**
6. **Employability**
7. **Estate, liabilities, and needs** of each party and the
   opportunity of each for further acquisition of capital assets and
   income
8. **Contribution** of each party in acquisition, preservation, or
   appreciation of marital property, **including the contribution of a
   party as a homemaker**
9. The **federal income-tax consequences** of the court's division of
   property

## Practical drafting notes

- **Plead and prove classification first.** Identify each asset/debt
  and state whether it is marital or non-marital before proposing a
  division.
- **Tracing.** Non-marital character can be lost through
  **commingling**; be ready to **trace** separate funds. Verify the
  current Arkansas tracing / transmutation case law.
- **Debts.** Marital debts are allocated as part of the equitable
  division; identify and assign each.
- **Retirement / pensions.** The **marital portion** of retirement
  benefits accrued during the marriage is typically divided; a
  **Qualified Domestic Relations Order (QDRO)** may be needed for
  ERISA plans.
- **Written findings.** If you seek an unequal split, draft proposed
  **written findings** explaining why equal would be inequitable under
  the factors — the statute requires them.
