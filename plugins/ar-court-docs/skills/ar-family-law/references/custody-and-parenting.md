# Arkansas Custody and Parenting — § 9-13-101 and the Act 604 Joint-Custody Presumption

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Reference notes for drafting. Verify the
> best-interest factors, the joint-custody presumption, and the
> relocation standard against current Ark. Code Ann. § 9-13-101 and
> controlling case law before relying on anything here.

Statutory home: **Ark. Code Ann. § 9-13-101** (Award of custody).
Overarching standard: the **best interest of the child**.

## The Act 604 of 2021 joint-custody presumption (DISTINCTIVE)

**Act 604 of 2021** amended § 9-13-101 to create a **rebuttable
presumption** that **joint custody is in the best interest of the
child** in an **original** custody determination.

- The presumption is **rebutted only by**:
  - **clear and convincing evidence** that joint custody is **not** in
    the child's best interest, **or**
  - the **parties' agreement** to a different arrangement, **or**
  - a circumstance where **one party does not seek custody** (or the
    statute's other enumerated rebuttal conditions — verify current
    text).
- **"Joint custody"** is defined as an **approximate and reasonable
  equal division of time** with the child between the parents.

This is a **strong, distinctive Arkansas feature**: the default
starting point in a contested original custody case is **joint
custody**, and the party opposing it carries the **clear-and-convincing**
burden.

> The presumption applies to **original** determinations. **Modification**
> of an existing custody order remains governed by the
> material-change-of-circumstances + best-interest framework — verify
> the current interaction between Act 604 and the modification
> standard.

## Best-interest factors

Even with the joint-custody presumption, custody is decided in the
**best interest of the child**. Arkansas case law and the statute
weigh, among other things (verify the current enumerated and
case-law factors):

- The **psychological relationship** between the child and each parent
  and the child's need for stability and continuity
- Each parent's **willingness and ability to foster** the child's
  relationship with the other parent
- The **moral fitness, stability, and conduct** of each parent
- **Evidence of domestic abuse / family violence** (a strong factor
  that can rebut joint custody)
- The **preference of the child** if of sufficient age and capacity to
  reason
- The parents' **work schedules** and ability to provide care

## Relocation

Arkansas analyzes **relocation** of a custodial parent under
**controlling Arkansas case law** and the **best-interest** standard.

- The framework distinguishes between a parent with **primary**
  custody seeking to relocate and a **joint-custody** arrangement.
- Verify the **current** controlling Supreme Court authority on
  relocation (the relocation presumption / factors have evolved in
  Arkansas case law, and the rise of the Act 604 joint-custody
  presumption interacts with relocation analysis) before drafting.

## Visitation

The non-custodial parent (in a non-joint arrangement) is generally
entitled to **reasonable visitation** unless visitation would endanger
the child. Courts may set a specific schedule, supervised visitation,
or conditions. Visitation is itself decided in the child's best
interest and may be modified on a material change.

## Practical drafting notes

- **Lead with a joint-custody proposal** in an original determination
  unless the facts (e.g., domestic abuse, distance, parental
  unfitness) support rebutting the presumption — and if rebutting,
  marshal **clear and convincing** evidence and request written
  findings.
- Tie the **parenting-time / overnight** schedule to the
  **child-support** worksheet (overnights drive the income-shares
  adjustment) — see `child-support.md`.
- Where there is family violence, coordinate with any **order of
  protection** (see `protection-orders.md`).
- Include the **UCCJEA** jurisdictional allegations — see
  `uccjea-uifsa.md`.
