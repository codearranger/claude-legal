# Arkansas Non-Compete and Workers' Compensation

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Verify the reformation mechanics, the
> exclusive-remedy exceptions, and any deadlines against the
> current Ark. Code Ann.

## Non-compete — Act 921 of 2015 (§ 4-75-101)

Before 2015, Arkansas courts applied a strict **"all-or-nothing"**
rule: an overbroad covenant not to compete was **void in its
entirety** — no judicial rewriting. **Act 921 of 2015** (codified
at Ark. Code Ann. § 4-75-101) changed the law:

- **Enforceability standard codified.** A covenant not to compete
  is enforceable if:
  1. The employer has a **protectable business interest** (e.g.,
     trade secrets, confidential information, customer
     relationships, specialized training, goodwill); and
  2. The covenant is **reasonable** in **duration** and
     **geographic scope** (and as to the activities restrained).
  - The statute provides that a covenant of a stated duration is
    presumptively reasonable up to the period the statute
    specifies (confirm the presumptive period in § 4-75-101).
- **Judicial reformation ("blue-penciling") authorized.** A court
  **may modify** an overbroad covenant to render it reasonable and
  then enforce it as modified — rather than striking it whole.
  This reverses the prior common-law rule and is the most
  important change Act 921 made.
- The statute does **not** require independent consideration
  beyond employment in many cases — confirm the consideration
  rule.

## Workers' compensation — exclusive remedy (§ 11-9-101 et seq.)

Workplace injuries are governed by the **Arkansas Workers'
Compensation Act** (Ark. Code Ann. § 11-9-101 et seq.):

- **Exclusive remedy** — § 11-9-105 makes workers' compensation
  the **exclusive remedy** against the employer for a covered
  workplace injury. The injured worker **cannot** sue the employer
  in tort for the injury.
- **Forum** — claims are filed with and adjudicated by the
  **Arkansas Workers' Compensation Commission**, an administrative
  agency — **not circuit court**. Appeals run through the
  Commission's full panel and then to the Arkansas Court of
  Appeals.
- **No-fault** — benefits (medical, indemnity, disability) are
  paid without regard to fault; the trade-off is the exclusive-
  remedy bar.
- **Narrow exceptions** — the exclusive-remedy bar may not reach
  **intentional injuries** by the employer, claims against
  **third parties** (a separate tortfeasor can be sued in circuit
  court), or situations where the employer failed to carry
  required coverage. Confirm the current exception case law.

The exclusive-remedy bar is a frequent **defense** to negligence
and intentional-tort claims brought against an employer — screen
for it before pleading any workplace-injury tort.
