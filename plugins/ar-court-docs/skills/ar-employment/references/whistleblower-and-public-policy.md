# Arkansas At-Will, Public-Policy Exception, and Whistleblower

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Verify the protected-activity definition,
> the SOL, and remedies against the current Ark. Code Ann.

Arkansas is a strong **at-will** employment state. The two main
routes to a wrongful-discharge or retaliation claim are the
judicial **public-policy exception** (private and public
employees) and the statutory **Arkansas Whistle-Blower Act**
(public employees only).

## At-will employment

Absent a contract for a definite term, either party may end the
employment **for any reason or no reason**. The doctrine is the
default; the exceptions below are narrow.

## Public-policy exception — *Sterling Drug v. Oxford*

**_Sterling Drug, Inc. v. Oxford_, 294 Ark. 239 (1988)**,
recognized a **public-policy exception** to at-will employment: an
employee discharged in violation of a **well-established public
policy of the state** has a cause of action for wrongful
discharge. Recognized categories include discharge for:

- Refusing to commit an unlawful act;
- Performing a public duty (e.g., jury service);
- Reporting a violation of law or public safety; or
- Exercising a statutory right.

The public policy must be **clear and substantial** — articulated
in a constitution, statute, or regulation, not a generalized
notion of fairness. This tort is the principal route for a
**private-sector** whistleblower (the Whistle-Blower Act does not
cover private employees).

## Implied-contract / handbook exception

Where an employee handbook or policy manual contains express
promises (e.g., progressive discipline, for-cause termination), an
**implied contract** may modify the at-will relationship. Effective
**disclaimers** in the handbook generally preserve at-will status.

## Arkansas Whistle-Blower Act — § 21-1-601 et seq. (public employees)

The **Arkansas Whistle-Blower Act** (Ark. Code Ann. § 21-1-601 et
seq.) protects **public** employees of **public** employers
(state, county, municipal, school) who:

- **Communicate** in good faith to an appropriate authority the
  existence of waste of public funds, property, or manpower, or a
  violation of law/rule; or
- **Participate** in an investigation, hearing, or inquiry; or
- **Refuse** to violate the law.

A covered employee subjected to an **adverse employment action**
for protected activity may sue. Remedies typically include
**reinstatement, back pay, and reasonable attorney's fees**.
Confirm the SOL (a short statutory period) and the exhaustion /
notice requirements against the current statute.

**It does not cover private-sector employees** — they rely on the
*Sterling Drug* public-policy tort.
