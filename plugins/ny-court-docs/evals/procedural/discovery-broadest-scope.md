# ny-discovery — CPLR 3101(a) "material and necessary" scope

## Prompt

The plaintiff in my NY case is objecting that my document
requests are too broad. They cite Federal Rule of Civil
Procedure 26(b)'s proportionality test. Is that the right
standard?

## Expected triggers

- `ny-discovery`
- `ny-law-references`

## Acceptance criteria

- [ ] Identifies that **CPLR 3101(a)**, not FRCP 26(b),
      governs in New York Supreme Court
- [ ] Cites the **"material and necessary"** standard, the
      broadest disclosure scope in the U.S.
- [ ] Cites **Allen v. Crowell-Collier Pub. Co.**, 21 N.Y.2d
      403 (1968), as the governing interpretive case
- [ ] Notes that proportionality concepts now appear in
      Commercial Division Rule 11-c (e-discovery) but are
      not the general scope rule
- [ ] Cites the **22 NYCRR § 202.20-f** good-faith
      conferral requirement (2021) before motion practice
- [ ] Notes the **25-interrogatory limit** under CPLR
      3130/3133(b) for the rare cases where interrogatories
      are used (in NY they are uncommon outside the Comm Div)

## Common failure modes

- Adopts the FRCP 26(b) proportionality framework
- Confuses NY discovery scope with federal Rule 26(b)(1)
- Cites the wrong case (e.g., *Andon v. 302-304 Mott Street
  Associates* for general scope — that's a privilege case)
