# Texas family-law procedure (the TRCP) + Texas Family Code map

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Navigational map only. Read the verbatim rule or
> statute (the canonical txcourts.gov / statutes.capitol.texas.gov page, or
> the corpus once populated) and confirm it is current before relying on it.

> **THERE IS NO SEPARATE TEXAS FAMILY-PROCEDURE RULE SET.** Texas family-law
> cases — divorce, suits affecting the parent-child relationship (SAPCR),
> protective orders — are filed and litigated under the **Texas Rules of
> Civil Procedure (TRCP)**, the same rules that govern general civil cases.
> What is distinct about family law in Texas is the **substantive** law,
> which lives in the **Texas Family Code**, plus a handful of code-based
> procedural and discovery provisions specific to family suits. Use
> **Petitioner / Respondent** for the parties in a family matter (not
> Plaintiff / Defendant). The general civil procedure map is in
> `civil-rules.md`; this file maps the Family Code anchors and the family-
> specific procedural points.

## Forum — the District Court (no separate family trial court)

There is **no separate "family court"** trial court in Texas. Divorce and
SAPCR are heard in the **District Courts** (general jurisdiction). Some
populous counties designate **family district courts** that hear family
matters by local assignment, and many family courts use **associate judges**
(**Tex. Fam. Code Ch. 201**) who hear matters and make recommendations subject
to de novo review by the referring court. A pro se filer drafting a divorce
petition is in **TRCP territory for procedure** and **Family Code territory
for substance**.

## Procedure runs on the TRCP

- A divorce or SAPCR is commenced by an **Original Petition** (e.g., "Original
  Petition for Divorce," "Petition to Establish the Parent-Child
  Relationship"), filed and served under the TRCP. The initiating party is the
  **Petitioner**.
- The responding party files an **answer** (often a general denial under TRCP
  92) or an **Original Answer and Counterpetition**; the responding party is
  the **Respondent**. Citation and the **Rule 99 "Monday rule"** answer
  deadline apply (see `civil-rules.md`).
- **Temporary orders** (temporary use of property, temporary conservatorship
  and possession, temporary child or spousal support, and **standing orders /
  temporary restraining orders** in some counties) are available while the
  case is pending; confirm the county's standing-order practice and the
  temporary-orders hearing procedure.
- **Discovery** in a family case is the TRCP discovery toolkit (see
  `civil-rules.md`), supplemented by Family Code financial-disclosure and
  inventory provisions; confirm the current required disclosures and the
  inventory-and-appraisement practice.

## Divorce — Texas Family Code Chapter 6

- **§ 6.001 — insupportability (no-fault).** The standard no-fault ground;
  Texas also retains **fault grounds** (cruelty, adultery, conviction of a
  felony, abandonment, living apart, confinement for mental illness) in
  **§§ 6.002–6.007**.
- **§ 6.301 — residency.** A suit for divorce generally requires that a party
  has been a **domiciliary of Texas for the preceding 6 months** and a
  resident of the **filing county for the preceding 90 days** (confirm).
- **§ 6.702 — waiting period.** The court may not grant a divorce before the
  **60th day** after the suit was filed (limited exceptions). Treat the 60-day
  waiting period as a definitional framework anchor, but confirm the current
  text and the exceptions.

## Property — community-property division

- **§ 3.003 — community-property presumption.** Property possessed by either
  spouse during or on dissolution of marriage is **presumed community
  property**; the spouse claiming an asset is **separate** bears the burden of
  rebutting the presumption (by the quantum of proof Texas courts apply).
  **Texas is a community-property state.**
- **Ch. 7 — "just and right" division.** On divorce the court divides the
  **community estate** in a manner the court deems **just and right** (not
  necessarily 50/50), having due regard for the rights of each party. Separate
  property is confirmed to the owning spouse, not divided.
- **§ 2.401 — informal (common-law) marriage.** Texas recognizes informal
  marriage on proof of agreement to be married, cohabitation in Texas as
  spouses, and holding out to others as married (or a registered declaration).
  Confirm the current proof standard.

## Conservatorship and possession — Chapter 153

- **§ 153.131 — parental presumption / joint managing conservators.** A
  **rebuttable presumption** that appointing the parents **joint managing
  conservators** is in the child's best interest, subject to the family-
  violence and history-of-abuse exceptions.
- **§ 153.252 — Standard Possession Order (SPO).** A statutory possession
  schedule the court applies (rebuttably presumed in the child's best interest
  for children of a stated age) unless the evidence warrants a different order.
  Confirm the current SPO terms and the expanded-SPO election.
- **Best interest of the child** is the governing standard; courts weigh the
  *Holley* factors (see `key-cases.md`) and the statutory best-interest
  factors. Confirm the controlling formulation.

## Child support — Chapter 154

- **§§ 154.121–154.130 — child-support guidelines.** Texas uses a
  **percentage-of-obligor-net-resources** model: the guideline applies a
  step-up **percentage** to the obligor's monthly **net resources** based on
  the **number of children** before the court (and adjustments for other
  children the obligor supports).
- **Statutory cap on net resources.** The guideline percentages apply only up
  to a **statutory cap on monthly net resources**, which the **Title IV-D
  agency adjusts periodically** (every six years) for inflation. **This cap is
  drift-prone — do not embed the figure; confirm the current cap against
  `tx-statutes-debt/`** and the Attorney General's published amount.
- Medical and dental support, and the net-resources computation (§ 154.062),
  are statutory — confirm.

## Spousal maintenance — Chapter 8

- **Ch. 8 — court-ordered spousal maintenance.** Eligibility is **limited**:
  generally requires (among the statutory paths) a **marriage of at least 10
  years** with the requesting spouse lacking the ability to earn sufficient
  income to meet **minimum reasonable needs**, **or** family violence within a
  defined period, **or** an incapacitating physical/mental disability (of the
  spouse or a child). **Statutory caps on the amount and the duration** of
  maintenance apply and are **drift-prone** — confirm against
  `tx-statutes-debt/`. (This is distinct from contractual alimony, which
  parties may agree to.)

## Interstate and protective-order anchors

- **Ch. 152 — UCCJEA** (Uniform Child Custody Jurisdiction and Enforcement
  Act): home-state and continuing jurisdiction over custody.
- **Ch. 159 — UIFSA** (Uniform Interstate Family Support Act): interstate
  establishment, enforcement, and modification of support; one-controlling-
  order rule.
- **Title 4 (Ch. 81–88) — protective orders / family violence.** Application
  for and issuance of protective orders, ex parte temporary protective orders,
  and the family-violence findings. Confirm the current procedure and the
  required findings.

## Substantive law lives in the Family Code (statutory, not procedural)

The substantive family law is in the **Texas Family Code**; the
**procedure** is the TRCP. Pull the statute text from `tx-statutes-debt/` and
the canonical statutes.capitol.texas.gov pages, and verify current section
text (especially the drift-prone net-resources cap, the maintenance caps, and
the SPO terms) before relying on a number.
