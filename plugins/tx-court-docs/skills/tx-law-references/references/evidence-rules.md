# Texas Rules of Evidence (Tex. R. Evid.) — map

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Navigational map only. Read the verbatim rule (the
> canonical txcourts.gov page, or `court-rules/` once populated) and confirm
> it is current before relying on it.

The Texas Rules of Evidence are modeled on the Federal Rules of Evidence and
share the FRE's three-digit numbering. They apply in civil and criminal
proceedings in Texas courts — **with one major exception for this plugin: the
rules of evidence do NOT apply in Justice (JP) court** except where TRCP Part V
incorporates them (**TRCP 500.3(e)**). Confirm the proceeding-specific scope.

## Relevance — Rules 401-403

- **Rule 401** — definition of relevant evidence (any tendency to make a fact
  of consequence more or less probable).
- **Rule 402** — relevant evidence generally admissible; irrelevant evidence
  inadmissible.
- **Rule 403** — exclusion of relevant evidence when its **probative value is
  substantially outweighed** by unfair prejudice, confusion of the issues,
  misleading the jury, undue delay, or needless presentation of cumulative
  evidence.

## Hearsay — Rules 801-807

- **Rule 801** — definitions; what is and is not hearsay (including
  opposing-party statements, which are **not** hearsay).
- **Rule 802** — the rule against hearsay (hearsay inadmissible unless an
  exception applies).
- **Rule 803 — exceptions where the availability of the declarant is
  immaterial.** The load-bearing exception for documentary civil practice
  (especially debt-buyer and account-records disputes) is:
  - **Rule 803(6) — records of a regularly conducted activity (the "business
    records" exception).** A record is admissible if (a) it was made at or near
    the time by — or from information transmitted by — someone with knowledge;
    (b) it was kept in the course of a regularly conducted business activity;
    (c) making the record was a regular practice of that activity; (d) these
    conditions are shown by the testimony of a **custodian or other qualified
    witness** *or* by a **business-records affidavit under Rule 902(10)**; and
    (e) the opponent does not show that the source of information or the
    circumstances indicate a **lack of trustworthiness**. This is the central
    admissibility hook for account and transaction records.
  - Other commonly used 803 exceptions: present sense impression, excited
    utterance, then-existing state of mind, absence of a business record, and
    public records.
- **Rule 804** — exceptions that apply only when the declarant is unavailable
  (former testimony, statement against interest, etc.).
- **Rule 805** — hearsay within hearsay (each layer must independently qualify
  — important when an account record incorporates information from a prior
  creditor's records).
- **Rule 807** — the residual exception.

## Authentication and identification — Rules 901-902

- **Rule 901** — requirement of authentication: evidence sufficient to support
  a finding that the item is what the proponent claims, including testimony of
  a witness with knowledge, distinctive characteristics, and the like.
- **Rule 902 — self-authenticating evidence** (no extrinsic foundation
  needed). For Texas civil document practice the key provision is:
  - **Rule 902(10) — business records accompanied by affidavit.** A
    Texas-specific provision: a record otherwise admissible under Rule 803(6)
    is **self-authenticated** when accompanied by an **affidavit** that tracks
    the 803(6) elements, **provided** the record and affidavit are **filed with
    the clerk and notice of the filing is served** on the other parties a set
    number of days before trial (so the opponent can inspect and challenge
    them). Rule 902(10) **supplies a sample affidavit form**. This is the
    device debt buyers use **in lieu of a live custodian**.

## Laying a foundation — practical note

The **803(6) / 902(10)** pairing is the standard path for admitting business
and account records without a live custodian. The affidavit must track the
803(6) elements (made at/near the time, by someone with knowledge, kept in the
regular course, regular practice), and the proponent must satisfy the
**902(10) pre-trial filing-and-notice** condition.

For **debt-buyer / assigned-account** matters, the classic foundation attack
is that the affiant — typically an employee of the **current** debt buyer —
**lacks personal knowledge of the original creditor's record-keeping
practices** and is merely repeating records received on assignment (a
**hearsay-within-hearsay** / lack-of-trustworthiness problem under Rules 805 /
803(6)). A defect in either the affidavit content or the 902(10) filing-and-
notice procedure is the usual ground for a foundation objection. Pair this with
the chain-of-title analysis in the consumer-debt bundle.
