# Texas Rules of Civil Procedure (TRCP) — civil-practice map

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** This is a navigational map of the Texas Rules of
> Civil Procedure. Read the verbatim rule (the canonical txcourts.gov page,
> or `court-rules/` once populated) and confirm it is current before relying
> on any number, day count, or threshold.

The TRCP are promulgated by the **Supreme Court of Texas** and govern civil
practice in the **District Courts**, the **County Courts at Law** and
**Constitutional County Courts**, and — through **Part V** — the **Justice
Courts**. **Family-law cases are also governed by the TRCP** (Texas has no
separate family-procedure rule set); the family substantive law is in the
Texas Family Code — see `family-rules.md`.

## Form, pleadings, and signing

- **Rule 45 — form of pleadings.** Pleadings consist of a **plain statement**
  of the cause of action or defense; **no technical forms** are required. Set
  out the petition in numbered paragraphs.
- **Rule 47 — claims for relief.** An original pleading stating a claim must
  contain a short statement of the cause of action and a demand for judgment.
  **Rule 47(c) — statement of the range of relief sought.** The pleading must
  state which **range of monetary relief** the party seeks (e.g., "monetary
  relief of $250,000 or less," or the appropriate higher band — confirm the
  current band language). **Failure to plead the Rule 47(c) range can bar a
  default judgment** — a Texas trap. Rule 47 also keys whether a case falls
  into the **expedited-actions** track (see Rule 169 / Rule 190 Level 1).
- **Rule 57 — signing of pleadings.** Every pleading is signed by the party's
  attorney (or by the party if self-represented). The attorney signature
  block carries the **State Bar of Texas bar number**, mailing address,
  telephone, email, and fax. A self-represented party signs and provides a
  mailing address, phone, and email.
- **Rule 78a — civil case information sheet.** A civil case information sheet
  accompanies the first filing in many courts; confirm the current form.

## Citation, answer, and the "Monday rule"

- **Rule 99 — issuance, form, and the answer deadline.** Citation issues on
  filing; the defendant's answer is due **"by 10:00 a.m. on the Monday next
  after the expiration of twenty days after the date of service."**
  - This is the **Texas "Monday rule"** — **not** a flat 20-day count. Count
    20 days from the date of service, then the answer is due at 10:00 a.m. on
    the **next Monday** after that 20th day. Flag this whenever computing a
    district- or county-court answer deadline.
  - **Justice-court answer timing is different**: in JP court the answer is
    due by the **end of the 14th day** after service of citation (TRCP 502.5
    — confirm) — not the Monday rule.

## Time computation — Rule 4

- **Rule 4** governs computing time: **exclude the first day, include the
  last.** If the last day is a **Saturday, Sunday, or legal holiday**, the
  period runs to the next day that is none of those.
- Legal holidays are enumerated in **Tex. Gov't Code § 662.003** (national
  holidays close the courts; certain state holidays are partial-staffing and
  do not close the courts). See `tx-statutes-debt/`.

## Filing and service — Rules 21 and 21a

- **Rule 21 — filing and serving pleadings and motions.** **Rule 21(f)**
  requires **electronic filing** through the statewide system
  (eFileTexas) for those required to e-file; a notice of hearing or
  submission must be served on all parties.
- **Rule 21a — methods of service** and the **add-on days.** Service may be
  in person, by mail, by commercial delivery, by fax, or by email/e-service.
  When a period runs from service by **mail, commercial delivery, fax, or
  email**, add **3 days** (confirm the current triggers and the e-service
  treatment in the corpus — the add-on rules are drift-prone).

## Challenging the pleadings — no general demurrer

- **Rule 90 — waiver of defects.** Defects in pleadings, including form, are
  waived unless raised by exception or motion.
- **Rule 91 — special exceptions.** The Texas vehicle to challenge **defects,
  vagueness, or omissions** in an opponent's pleading. **There is no general
  demurrer in Texas** (Rule 90); special exceptions are how a party forces the
  opponent to replead a defective claim. A sustained special exception is
  typically followed by an opportunity to amend.
- **Rule 91a — dismissal of a baseless cause of action.** A party may move to
  dismiss a cause of action on the ground that **it has no basis in law or in
  fact** (the Texas analog to a federal 12(b)(6) motion). The motion must be
  filed **within 60 days** after the first pleading asserting the challenged
  cause of action is served. The court decides the motion on the pleadings.
  **Fee-shifting on a Rule 91a motion was changed by 2019 legislation** (once
  mandatory, made discretionary) — confirm the current fee rule in
  `court-rules/` and see `fees-and-costs.md`.

## Verified pleadings — Rules 92 and 93

- **Rule 92 — general denial.** A defendant may answer by a **general denial**,
  which puts the plaintiff to proof of every element of its claim. This is the
  most common Texas answer.
- **Rule 93 — pleas that must be verified.** Certain matters must be raised
  **under oath / by verified pleading**, including (among others) **lack of
  legal capacity**, **defect of parties**, that an account is **not just or
  true** (the **denial of a sworn account under Rule 185 — Rule 93(10)**),
  **failure of consideration**, and that an instrument sued on is **without
  consideration**. If a Rule 93 matter is not verified, it is not properly
  before the court — confirm the full Rule 93 list.

## Suit on a sworn account — Rule 185

- **Rule 185 — suit on a sworn account.** A petition on an open or stated
  account (common in collection and goods-sold-and-delivered suits) supported
  by a proper **affidavit** is **prima facie evidence** of the account.
- To put the account in issue, the defendant **must file a written denial
  under oath** (a **sworn denial**, tied to **Rule 93(10)**). **Without a
  sworn denial, the plaintiff need not formally prove up the account** — flag
  that the verified denial is mandatory to contest a Rule 185 sworn account.

## Summary judgment — Rule 166a

- **Rule 166a(c) — traditional summary judgment.** The movant must show there
  is **no genuine issue of material fact** and it is entitled to judgment as a
  matter of law. The motion and supporting evidence are served at least **21
  days** before the hearing or submission date; the **response is due 7 days**
  before the hearing (confirm current day counts and the leave requirement for
  late responses).
- **Rule 166a(i) — no-evidence summary judgment.** A distinctive Texas device:
  after an adequate time for discovery, a party may move for summary judgment
  on the ground that **there is no evidence** of one or more essential
  elements of a claim or defense **on which the adverse party would have the
  burden of proof at trial**. The non-movant must then produce **more than a
  scintilla** of evidence raising a genuine fact issue, or the motion is
  granted. The no-evidence motion is a signature Texas summary-judgment tool.

## Expedited actions and discovery control plans

- **Rule 169 — expedited actions.** Mandatory for suits seeking monetary
  relief at or below a statutory ceiling (confirm the current ceiling against
  the corpus). Limited discovery, time limits at trial, and a required
  trial-setting. Tied to discovery **Level 1**.
- **Rule 190 — discovery control plans.** Every case is governed by a
  discovery control plan:
  - **Level 1** — expedited / lower-dollar cases (tied to Rule 169); a
    shortened discovery period and tight discovery limits.
  - **Level 2** — the **default** plan; sets the discovery period and the
    presumptive limits that apply unless Level 1 or a Level 3 order governs.
  - **Level 3** — a **court-ordered tailored plan** for cases that need a
    custom schedule and limits.
  - The discovery-period day counts and the per-side limits are in the rule —
    point to `court-rules/` for the current numbers.

## Discovery — Rules 192–215

- **Rule 192 — scope** of discovery and protective orders.
- **Rule 193 — responses, objections, and supplementation.** **Rule 193.7**
  makes a document **produced** by a party **self-authenticated** against that
  party unless it timely objects — useful in debt cases.
- **Rule 194 — required disclosures** (amended effective Jan. 1, 2021 to an
  automatic / initial-disclosure model; the disclosures are due on a schedule
  set by the rule rather than only on request).
- **Rule 196 — requests for production** of documents and tangible things.
- **Rule 197 — interrogatories.** A party may serve a capped number of written
  interrogatories (the cap excludes interrogatories that merely ask the
  responding party to identify persons with knowledge — confirm the current
  cap). Answers are signed and verified.
- **Rule 198 — requests for admission.** A matter is **deemed admitted** if
  not answered or objected to within the response period (generally **30
  days** — confirm).
- **Rule 199 — oral depositions** (and Rules 200/204/205 for related
  deposition and non-party discovery).
- **Rule 215 — abuse of discovery; sanctions.** The vehicle for a **motion to
  compel** and discovery sanctions.
- **Response window.** Written-discovery responses are generally due in **30
  days** after service, with the Rule 21a add-on-days nuance — confirm the
  current windows and the "30 + 3" treatment in the corpus.

## Findings, judgments, and post-judgment

- **Rules 296–299 — findings of fact and conclusions of law** after a bench
  trial (request, due dates, and the consequences of omitted findings).
- **Rules 239–239a — default judgment** and the notice of default; pair with
  the Rule 47(c) range requirement and the Rule 99 answer deadline.
- **Rule 320–329b — new trial and plenary power.** A **motion for new trial**
  is due **30 days after the judgment is signed** (TRCP 329b). The trial
  court's **plenary power** runs **30 days** from the signing, **extended** by
  a timely post-judgment motion (new trial or motion to modify). Confirm the
  current plenary-power extension mechanics — they drive appellate deadlines.

## Justice Court — Part V (Rules 500–510)

Justice (Justice of the Peace) courts run on a **simplified** rule set in
**TRCP Part V**:

- **Rule 500.3 — applicability.** **Rule 500.3(e): the other Texas Rules of
  Civil Procedure and the Texas Rules of Evidence do NOT apply in justice
  court** except where Part V specifically incorporates them. This is the key
  JP-court quirk.
- **Rule 502 — commencement.** A case starts with a petition; small-claims and
  debt-claim petitions have simplified content requirements.
- **Rule 502.5 / 503 — answer and default.** The defendant's answer is due by
  the **end of the 14th day** after service of citation (confirm); default
  procedures follow Rule 503.
- **Rule 506 — appeal de novo to county court.** A party appeals a JP-court
  judgment by filing an **appeal bond, a cash deposit, or a Statement of
  Inability to Afford Payment** within the time set by the rule; the appeal is
  tried **de novo** in the county court. See `fees-and-costs.md`.
- **Rule 508 — debt claim cases** (suits by an assignee/holder of a consumer
  debt or other claim for money). Simplified pleading and disclosure rules.
- **Rule 510 — eviction (forcible entry and detainer).** Expedited trial
  setting and a **short appeal window** for possession; confirm the current
  day counts.
