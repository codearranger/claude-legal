# Texas filing fees and cost / fee-shifting / sanctions framework

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Navigational map only. **Dollar amounts change.**
> Confirm every fee and ceiling with the clerk of the filing court and against
> the statutes in `tx-statutes-debt/` before relying on any figure here.

Texas follows the **American rule**: each party bears its own attorney fees
unless a **statute, court rule, or contract** shifts them. Costs (as distinct
from attorney fees) are more routinely awarded to the prevailing party. The
load-bearing fee-shifting hooks are statutory.

## Attorney fees — CPRC Chapter 38 (contract / sworn account)

**Tex. Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code Ch. 38** authorizes a **prevailing party** to
recover **reasonable attorney fees** on a claim for an **oral or written
contract** (and on rendered services, performed labor, furnished material, and
a **sworn account**), in addition to the amount of a valid claim and costs.

- **Presentment.** Recovery historically requires **presentment** of the claim
  and a failure to tender within the statutory period before suit — confirm
  the current presentment requirement.
- **2021 amendment — who you can recover from.** Ch. 38 was **amended in 2021**
  to expand the entities from which fees may be recovered beyond individuals
  and corporations to include **partnerships, limited liability companies, and
  other organizations**. Confirm the current statutory list and effective date
  in `tx-statutes-debt/`.
- **Two-way exposure.** In contract / debt litigation, Ch. 38 is a **two-way
  risk**: a prevailing **defendant** on a Ch. 38–type claim may recover fees,
  and a losing party may owe the other side's fees. **Never ignore a citation
  — a default judgment on a sworn-account/contract claim can carry fees.**

## Attorney fees — CPRC Chapter 37 (Declaratory Judgments / UDJA)

**Tex. Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code Ch. 37 — the Uniform Declaratory Judgments
Act.** In a declaratory-judgment proceeding the court **may award costs and
reasonable and necessary attorney fees as are equitable and just** (§ 37.009).
The UDJA fee award is **discretionary** and runs to **either** party — confirm
the current text and the case law limiting fee awards where the declaratory
claim merely duplicates another claim.

## Sanctions — TRCP 13 and CPRC Chapters 9 & 10

- **TRCP 13 — groundless and bad-faith pleadings.** The signing of a pleading,
  motion, or other paper certifies that it is not **groundless and brought in
  bad faith** or **for harassment**; a violation exposes the signer to
  sanctions, which may include reasonable expenses and attorney fees.
- **CPRC Ch. 10 — sanctions for frivolous pleadings and motions.** A
  certification standard (legal and factual basis; not for an improper
  purpose) with a sanctions remedy, including a penalty and the movant's
  reasonable expenses and attorney fees.
- **CPRC Ch. 9 — frivolous pleadings** (an older parallel sanctions statute;
  confirm its current interaction with Ch. 10 and Rule 13).

## Rule 91a dismissal fees

**TRCP 91a** (dismissal of a cause of action with **no basis in law or fact**;
see `civil-rules.md`) carries its own **fee-shifting** provision tied to the
prevailing party on the motion. The 91a fee rule was **changed by 2019
legislation** (it was once a mandatory award and was made discretionary) —
**confirm the current fee text** in `court-rules/` before relying on it.

## Limitations / proportionate-responsibility context (for exposure analysis)

When assessing fee and damages exposure, note the surrounding CPRC framework
(confirm current text against `tx-statutes-debt/`):

- **Ch. 16 — limitations** (e.g., the **4-year** limitations period for debt /
  contract under § 16.004; the 2-year period for many torts under § 16.003).
- **Ch. 33 — proportionate responsibility.** Texas applies **modified
  comparative responsibility with a 51% bar** — a claimant whose responsibility
  exceeds 50% recovers nothing. This is a stable doctrinal classification;
  confirm the bar percentage and the apportionment mechanics in the corpus.

## Filing fees and the Statement of Inability to Afford Payment

- **Filing fees** vary by court (District, County Court at Law, Justice Court)
  and by case type, and are revised periodically. **Do not embed amounts
  here** — confirm the current figures with the clerk of the filing court and
  against `tx-statutes-debt/`.
- **Statement of Inability to Afford Payment of Court Costs.** A party unable
  to pay court costs files a **Statement of Inability to Afford Payment of
  Court Costs** (the instrument that replaced the older "affidavit of
  inability" / pauper's affidavit). Filing it lets the party proceed without
  prepaying costs unless the statement is contested and the court orders
  payment after a hearing. The same instrument can substitute for an **appeal
  bond** when appealing a Justice-Court judgment de novo to the county court
  (TRCP 506; see `civil-rules.md`). Confirm the current form and the contest
  procedure.
