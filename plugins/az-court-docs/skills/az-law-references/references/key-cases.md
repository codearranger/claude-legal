## Summary judgment

- ***Orme School v. Reeves***, **166 Ariz. 301, 802 P.2d 1000 (1990)**
  *(citation verified)*. The Arizona Supreme Court set the modern
  **summary-judgment standard** under Ariz. R. Civ. P. 56, aligning
  Arizona practice with the federal *Celotex / Anderson / Matsushita*
  trilogy. The decision frames when a trial court should grant summary
  judgment — generally, when on the record the claim or defense is so
  lacking in probative evidentiary support that a reasonable jury could
  not find for the non-moving party — and cautions against using summary
  judgment as a substitute for trial where genuine factual disputes
  exist. This is the case to cite for the Rule 56 standard.

## Pleading-stage dismissal (Rule 12(b)(6))

- Arizona applies a **notice-pleading** standard and, on a Rule 12(b)(6)
  motion, assumes the truth of the complaint's well-pleaded factual
  allegations and dismisses only where the plaintiff would not be entitled
  to relief under any facts susceptible of proof. *(Doctrine described;
  confirm and cite the controlling Arizona Supreme Court / Court of
  Appeals opinion stating the current 12(b)(6) standard before relying —

## Community property and family law (Title 25)

Arizona is a **community-property** state. The substantive framework is
statutory (A.R.S. §§ 25-211, 25-213, 25-215, 25-318 — see
`family-rules.md` and `az-statutes-debt/`), and the case law construes it:

- **Community-property presumption.** Property acquired by either spouse
  during marriage is presumed to be **community property** under A.R.S.
  § 25-211; the spouse claiming an item is **sole and separate** bears the
  burden of overcoming that presumption by clear and convincing evidence.
  *(Doctrine described; confirm and cite the controlling Arizona opinion
  stating the burden of proof before relying — verify citation.)*

- ***Toth v. Toth***, 190 Ariz. 218, 946 P.2d 900 (1997) *(citation
  verified)*. Holds that joint-tenancy property and community property are
  treated alike under the **equitable-division** statute, A.R.S.
  § 25-318(A), and that an equitable division **need not be equal** where
  the equities (here, separate funds used to buy the home and an
  extremely short marriage) warrant a disproportionate award. Confirm the
  precise holding against the published opinion before relying.

- **Post-petition earnings / business profits.** Income attributable to a
  spouse's personal labor after service of the dissolution petition is
  generally that spouse's separate property, while value attributable to
  the community business above that may remain community and subject to
  equitable division. *(Doctrine described; identify and cite the
  controlling Arizona opinion — verify citation.)*

   Supreme Court `ariz`; Court of Appeals `arizctapp`) or the Arizona

   limited, or superseded by statute.
