# Arizona Rules of Evidence (Ariz. R. Evid.) — map

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Navigational map only. Read the verbatim rule in
> `court-rules/` and confirm it is current before relying on it.

The Arizona Rules of Evidence are modeled on the Federal Rules of
Evidence and share the FRE's three-digit numbering. They apply in civil
and criminal proceedings in Arizona courts (with the usual exceptions for
certain preliminary and limited-jurisdiction proceedings — confirm).

## Relevance — Rules 401-403

- **Rule 401** — definition of relevant evidence (any tendency to make a
  material fact more or less probable).
- **Rule 402** — relevant evidence generally admissible; irrelevant
  evidence inadmissible.
- **Rule 403** — exclusion of relevant evidence when its **probative
  value is substantially outweighed** by unfair prejudice, confusion,
  misleading the jury, undue delay, waste of time, or needless
  cumulative evidence.

## Hearsay — Rules 801-807

- **Rule 801** — definitions; what is and is not hearsay (including
  party-opponent statements treated as non-hearsay).
- **Rule 802** — the hearsay rule (hearsay inadmissible unless an
  exception applies).
- **Rule 803 — exceptions where availability of the declarant is
  immaterial.** The load-bearing exception for documentary civil practice
  is:
  - **Rule 803(6) — records of a regularly conducted activity (the
    "business records" exception).** A record is admissible if (a) it was
    made at or near the time by — or from information transmitted by —
    someone with knowledge; (b) it was kept in the course of a regularly
    conducted activity of a business/organization; (c) making the record
    was a regular practice of that activity; (d) these conditions are
    shown by a **custodian or other qualified witness** *or* by a
    **certification** that complies with Rule 902(11); and (e) the
    opponent does not show that the source of information or the
    circumstances indicate a **lack of trustworthiness**. This is the
    central admissibility hook in debt-buyer and account-records disputes.
  - Other commonly used 803 exceptions: present sense impression, excited
    utterance, then-existing state of mind, records of regularly conducted
    activity, absence of a business record, public records.
- **Rule 804** — exceptions that apply only when the declarant is
  unavailable (former testimony, statement against interest, etc.).
- **Rule 805** — hearsay within hearsay.
- **Rule 807** — the residual exception.

## Authentication and identification — Rules 901-902

- **Rule 901** — requirement of authentication: evidence sufficient to
  support a finding that the item is what the proponent claims, including
  testimony of a witness with knowledge, distinctive characteristics, and
  the like.
- **Rule 902 — self-authenticating evidence** (no extrinsic foundation
  needed). For civil document practice the key provision is:
  - **Rule 902(11) — certified domestic records of a regularly conducted
    activity.** A custodian's (or other qualified person's) written
    certification can satisfy the Rule 803(6) foundation **without a live
    foundation witness**, provided the proponent gives the adverse party
    **advance written notice** and makes the record and certification
    available for inspection. Pair 902(11) with 803(6) when laying a
    paper foundation for account or transaction records.

## Expert and opinion testimony — Rules 701-705

- **Rule 701** — lay opinion testimony.
- **Rule 702 — testimony by expert witnesses.** Arizona **adopted the
  *Daubert* reliability standard by rule effective January 1, 2012.**
  Expert testimony is admissible if the witness is qualified and (a) the
  expert's specialized knowledge will help the trier of fact; (b) the
  testimony is based on sufficient facts or data; (c) it is the product
  of reliable principles and methods; and (d) the expert reliably applied
  those principles and methods to the facts. The trial court is the
  gatekeeper. Confirm current text.
- **Rules 703-705** — bases of expert opinion, opinion on an ultimate
  issue, and disclosure of underlying facts.

## Privileges

Arizona privileges are governed by **Rule 501** in conjunction with
**statutory privileges in the A.R.S.** (e.g., attorney-client,
physician/health-care-provider-patient, marital, clergy, accountant —
codified in Title 12 and elsewhere). Because many Arizona privileges are
statutory rather than purely common-law, check both the rule and the
relevant A.R.S. section in `az-statutes-debt/`. Confirm scope, holder,
and waiver for the specific privilege.
