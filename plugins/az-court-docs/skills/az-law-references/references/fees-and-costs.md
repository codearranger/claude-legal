# Arizona filing fees and cost / fee-shifting framework

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Navigational map only. **Dollar amounts change.**
> Confirm every fee and ceiling with the clerk of the filing court and
> against the verbatim statutes in `az-statutes-debt/` before relying on
> any figure here.

Arizona generally follows the **American rule**: each party bears its own
attorney's fees unless a **statute, court rule, or contract** shifts them.
Costs (as distinct from attorney's fees) are more routinely awarded to the
prevailing party.

## Costs to the prevailing party — A.R.S. § 12-341

**A.R.S. § 12-341** entitles the **successful party** to recover **taxable
costs** as a matter of right. Taxable costs are statutory categories
(filing fees, service fees, certain witness and deposition costs, etc.) —
**not** attorney's fees. The list of recoverable costs is itself
statutory; confirm the current categories.

## Attorney's fees in contract actions — A.R.S. § 12-341.01

**A.R.S. § 12-341.01** is the central fee-shifting statute for
**contested actions arising out of contract** — which makes it the key
provision in **consumer-debt and contract litigation**:

- The award is **discretionary** — the court "may" award reasonable
  attorney's fees to the **successful party**; it is not automatic.
- The award is **capped at the reasonable fees actually paid or owed** by
  the prevailing party (it cannot exceed that amount).
- The statute lists **discretionary factors** the court weighs (e.g., the
  merits of the unsuccessful party's position, whether the litigation
  could have been avoided, whether assessing fees would deter others from
  bringing meritorious claims, and the like).
- It applies to a party who **defends successfully** as well as one who
  prevails affirmatively — relevant when a consumer defeats a collection
  suit.

Because § 12-341.01 reaches **both sides** of a contract dispute, a
consumer defending a debt-collection action should be aware that a losing
defense can expose them to the plaintiff's fees, and a successful defense
can support a fee request. Confirm current text and case law on what
counts as "arising out of contract."

## Offer of judgment — Ariz. R. Civ. P. 68

**Rule 68** allows a party to serve a formal **offer of judgment**. If the
offeree rejects the offer and **does not obtain a more favorable
judgment**, the offeree must pay as a sanction — under Rule 68(g)(1) —
**twenty percent of the difference between the amount of the offer and the
amount of the final judgment** (confirm the current sanction formula and
timing). Confirm whether a family-case offer-of-judgment analog applies
in a Title 25 matter before relying on one.

## Fee deferral and waiver — A.R.S. § 12-302

**A.R.S. § 12-302** lets a party who is **unable to pay** court fees and
costs apply for **deferral or waiver**. The court may:

- **defer** payment (postpone it, often to the end of the case or a
  payment plan),
- **waive** the fees outright, or
- set a **payment schedule**.

Arizona courts provide an **Application for Deferral or Waiver of Court
Fees and Costs** (a fee-waiver / fee-deferral form) — see
`online-sources.md` for the self-service center. Confirm the current
income/eligibility standard and the form name.

## Filing fees — where the amounts live

Superior Court and Justice Court **filing fees, jurisdictional ceilings,
and surcharges** are set by statute and by Supreme Court fee schedules and
are revised periodically. **Do not embed amounts here.** Confirm the
current figures with the clerk of the filing court and against the fee
provisions in `az-statutes-debt/`. The justice-court civil jurisdictional
ceiling and the small-claims ceiling, the compulsory-arbitration dollar
limit (set by county local rule — see `civil-rules.md`), and the Superior
Court filing fee are all subject to change.

## Frivolous-claim / bad-faith sanctions

Beyond contract-action fees, Arizona authorizes fees and sanctions for
**unjustified, frivolous, or bad-faith** conduct (e.g., **A.R.S. §
12-349** for claims brought without substantial justification or for
improper purpose, and **Ariz. R. Civ. P. 11** for improper filings).
Confirm the current standard and the procedure before invoking.
