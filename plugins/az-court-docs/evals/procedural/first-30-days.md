# az-first-30-days — Rule 12(b)(6) + Rule 8(c) affirmative defenses + compulsory arbitration

## Prompt

I just got served with a civil complaint in Arizona Superior Court
for about $30,000. I think the complaint doesn't really state a
valid claim, and I have some defenses. What are my options in the
first month — can I move to dismiss, how do I raise defenses in my
answer, and I heard smaller cases get sent to arbitration?

## Expected triggers

- `az-first-30-days`
- `az-deadlines`
- `az-statewide-format`

## Acceptance criteria

### Pre-answer motion (Rule 12(b)(6))

- [ ] Identifies the **Ariz. R. Civ. P. 12(b)(6)** motion to dismiss
      for failure to state a claim as an option, and explains how it
      tolls/relates to the answer deadline (read current timing from
      the corpus via `az-deadlines`)
- [ ] Notes the Arizona pleading standard the court applies on a
      12(b)(6) motion, citing controlling Arizona authority
      (*Cullen v. Auto-Owners*) read/verified from `key-cases.md`
      rather than asserted from memory

### Answer + affirmative defenses (Rule 8(c))

- [ ] States the answer must respond to each allegation and that
      **affirmative defenses must be pleaded under Ariz. R. Civ. P.
      8(c)** (warning of waiver), including SOL and lack of standing
- [ ] Notes compulsory **counterclaims** must be raised (read the
      rule from the corpus)

### Compulsory arbitration

- [ ] Identifies Arizona's **compulsory (court-annexed) arbitration**
      for cases at or below the jurisdictional dollar threshold set by
      **Ariz. R. Civ. P. 72–77** and the local **superior court**
      threshold — reads the current threshold amount from the
      references corpus rather than asserting a number, and notes the
      **de novo appeal** right from the arbitration award

### Honest sourcing

- [ ] Reads all day counts, the arbitration threshold, and the
      pleading standard from the corpus; cites by rule/case

## Common failure modes

- Asserts the compulsory-arbitration dollar threshold from memory
- Buries affirmative defenses instead of pleading them under
  Rule 8(c)
- Asserts the pleading standard or *Cullen* holding from memory
- Forgets the de novo appeal from an arbitration award
