## Scope — what CEMA reaches

CEMA regulates two things, both defined as messages sent **for a commercial
purpose** (promoting real property, goods, or services for sale or lease):

- **Commercial electronic mail messages** — RCW 19.190.020 / .030.
- **Commercial electronic text messages** — RCW 19.190.060 (the SMS analog;
  same commercial-purpose gating).

CEMA does **not** reach non-commercial messages — the "commercial electronic
mail message" definition cabins the entire chapter to commercial e-mail.
*Brown v. Old Navy*, No. 102592-1 (Wash. Apr. 17, 2025).

## Definitions — RCW 19.190.010

- **(2) "Commercial electronic mail message"** — "an electronic mail message
  sent for the purpose of promoting real property, goods, or services for sale
  or lease." (This definition gates the whole chapter.)
- **"Initiate the transmission"** — refers to the original sender, not an
  intermediary handler.
- **"Assist the transmission"** — providing substantial assistance to a sender
  the assister knows (or consciously avoids knowing) is violating the chapter.
- **"Commercial electronic text message"** — the text-message (SMS) analog of
  the commercial e-mail definition.

## The e-mail prohibition — RCW 19.190.020(1) (verbatim)

> No person may initiate the transmission, conspire with another to initiate the
> transmission, or assist the transmission, of a commercial electronic mail
> message from a computer located in Washington or to an electronic mail address
> that the sender knows, or has reason to know, is held by a Washington resident
> that:
> (a) Uses a third party's internet domain name without permission of the third
> party, or otherwise misrepresents or obscures any information in identifying
> the point of origin or the transmission path of a commercial electronic mail
> message; or
> (b) Contains false or misleading information in the subject line.

**Two truthfulness requirements:** (a) the *transmission-path / origin*
requirement, and (b) the *subject-line* requirement. After *Brown v. Old Navy*,
subsection **(1)(b) reaches ANY false or misleading subject-line information** —
not just deception about the message's commercial nature.

## Per se Consumer Protection Act violation — RCW 19.190.030

A violation of CEMA's e-mail regulations is a **per se** violation of the
Consumer Protection Act (ch. 19.86 RCW). RCW 19.190.030(1), .100. This routes
CEMA claims into the CPA's private right of action while supplying CEMA's own
statutory damages (below) in lieu of the CPA's actual-damages requirement.

## Damages — RCW 19.190.040 (verbatim core)

> (1) Damages to the recipient of a commercial electronic mail message or a
> commercial electronic text message sent in violation of this chapter are five
> hundred dollars, or actual damages, whichever is greater.
> (2) Damages to an interactive computer service resulting from a violation of
> this chapter are one thousand dollars, or actual damages, whichever is greater.

**No actual-damages showing required.** Unlike a generic CPA claim (cf. RCW
19.86.090), CEMA's $500 statutory figure does not require proof of actual
damages — the injury is *receiving* the violating message. *Brown v. Old Navy*,
slip op. at 4. In class litigation this drives the "multi-million / multi-billion
dollar exposure" the defense raised in *Brown* (which the Court declined to treat
as a reason to narrow the statute).

## Preemption — RCW 19.190.110

CEMA is a matter of **statewide concern** and supersedes/preempts local rules,
ordinances, and other local laws on the practices it covers.

## Relationship to other law

- **Washington CPA (RCW 19.86):** CEMA violations are per se CPA violations; see
  `wa-consumer-debt/references/wa-consumer-protection.md` for the *Hangman Ridge*
  CPA framework and CPA remedies (treble damages cap, attorney fees under RCW
  19.86.090) that ride along once liability attaches.
- **Federal CAN-SPAM Act (15 U.S.C. §§ 7701–7713):** CAN-SPAM preempts some
  state spam laws but **expressly saves** state statutes that prohibit
  *falsity or deception* (15 U.S.C. § 7707(b)(1)). CEMA's falsity-based
  prohibitions fall within that savings clause — a defendant's CAN-SPAM
  preemption defense should be evaluated against § 7707(b)(1). Verify current
  authority.
