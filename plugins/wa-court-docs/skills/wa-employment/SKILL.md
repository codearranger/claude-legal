# Washington Employment Law — Statutory Framework

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Employment statutes amended
> regularly — minimum wage adjusts annually (Jan 1); PFML
> rates adjust annually; non-compete threshold adjusts
> annually. Verify current text before relying.

## At a glance — Washington's employment-law architecture

- **Substantive code**: RCW Title 49 (Labor Regulations) +
  RCW Title 50A (Paid Family Medical Leave) + RCW 51
  (Industrial Insurance / Workers' Compensation).
- **Enforcement agencies**: Washington State Department of
  Labor & Industries (L&I) for wage/hour + workers' comp;
  Washington State Human Rights Commission (WSHRC) for
  WLAD; Office of Minority and Women's Business
  Enterprises; EEOC overlay.
- **Plaintiff-friendly orientation**: WLAD has **no
  damages cap**, mandatory attorney's fees, and **broader
  coverage** than federal Title VII (covers employers
  with **8+** employees vs. Title VII's 15+).

## Minimum wage + overtime — RCW 49.46

### Current rate

- **$16.66/hour** statewide minimum (2025; adjusts
  annually for inflation per RCW 49.46.020(3))
- **$20.29/hour** in Seattle (Seattle Municipal Code
  14.19; large employers)
- **$20.29/hour** in SeaTac (port-area workers per
  initiative)
- Tipped employees do NOT get a sub-minimum — WA has no
  tip credit, unlike federal FLSA

### Overtime

- **1.5x** for hours > 40/week (RCW 49.46.130)
- Overtime threshold for salaried-exempt = **2.5x** the
  state minimum wage (2025 threshold ~$1,332.80/week for
  large employers; phased in by 2028)
- Computer professionals exempt at separately scaled
  threshold

### Damages — RCW 49.46.090

Willful violation = **2x unpaid wages** + attorney's fees
(RCW 49.46.090).

## Wage payment + collection — RCW 49.48 + RCW 49.52

### Final paychecks — RCW 49.48.010

- **End-of-pay-period** following termination
- Including **all accrued vacation if employer policy
  treats vacation as wages**
- Willful withholding = up to **2x damages + attorney's
  fees** (RCW 49.48.030)
- Failure to pay timely = lien on employer property (RCW
  49.48.082)

### Wage deductions — RCW 49.52

- Only specific deductions allowed: taxes; benefits with
  written consent; specific authorized deductions for
  cash shortages with high specificity
- Unauthorized deduction = **2x as damages + fees** (RCW
  49.52.070)
- **Criminal penalty** possible for willful violation
  (RCW 49.52.050 — gross misdemeanor)

## Washington Law Against Discrimination (WLAD) — RCW 49.60

### Coverage

- **Employers with 8+ employees** (RCW 49.60.040(11) —
  significantly broader than Title VII's 15+)
- Public accommodation, real estate, credit, insurance
  also covered

### Protected classes

RCW 49.60.180 (employment) prohibits discrimination
because of:

- Race / color / national origin
- Religion / creed
- **Sex / gender / pregnancy**
- **Sexual orientation** (added 2006)
- **Gender identity / expression** (added 2006)
- **Marital status** (broader than federal)
- Age (40+; some claims younger)
- **Disability** (broader definition than ADA)
- Honorable military discharge
- HIV / hepatitis C status
- Use of trained service animal

### Damages

- **No statutory cap** (compare federal Title VII's
  $300k cap for largest employers)
- Recoverable: back pay, front pay, emotional distress,
  punitive damages (where authorized)
- **Mandatory attorney's fees** for prevailing plaintiff
  (RCW 49.60.030(2))

### Administrative remedies

- File complaint with WSHRC within **6 months** of
  discriminatory act
- OR file directly in court (RCW 49.60.030(2)); no
  exhaustion requirement (unlike Title VII's EEOC
  charge)
- SOL on direct court action: **3 years** (RCW
  4.16.080(2) — general tort SOL applied to WLAD per
  *Antonius v. King County*)

### Hostile work environment

WA follows the Meritor / Harris federal framework but
applies it more liberally — Washington supreme court has
emphasized severe-OR-pervasive disjunctive (not
conjunctive). *Loeffelholz v. Univ. of Wash.* (2012).

## Paid Sick Leave — RCW 49.46.200-220

Since **January 1, 2018**:

- Accrual: **1 hour per 40 hours worked**
- Use: as needed for own / family member illness; safe
  leave for domestic violence; school closures
- No cap on accrual; 40-hour carryover minimum
- Retaliation prohibited

## Paid Family and Medical Leave (PFML) — RCW 50A

Since **2020**:

- **12 weeks paid family leave + 12 weeks paid medical
  leave (18 weeks combined cap with one bonding period)**
- Funded by payroll premium (employer 28.57% / employee
  71.43% as of 2024)
- Wage replacement up to **90% of low-wage**, scaling
  down (capped at ~$1,456/week 2025)
- Job-protected if employee has 12+ months service + 1,250+
  hours

## Equal Pay Opportunity Act — RCW 49.58

- **No salary-history inquiries** (RCW 49.58.100 — 2018)
- **Pay scale + benefits disclosure** in job postings for
  employers of 15+ (RCW 49.58.110 — 2023 amendment)
- **No pay differential** between sexes for similar work
  except for legitimate factors (RCW 49.58.020-030)
- Damages: actual damages + **2x as liquidated** + fees

## Non-compete reform — RCW 49.62

Since **January 1, 2020** (the 2019 reform):

- **Non-competes void below salary threshold** —
  $116,594.96/year for 2024 (annual COL adjustment)
- For independent contractors: $291,486.40/year (2024)
- Must be **disclosed at offer** (before employee
  starts) or be supported by independent consideration
- **Maximum duration: 18 months** (presumed
  unreasonable beyond)
- Layoffs trigger automatic invalidation if employee
  receives less than base salary in severance
- **Treble damages or $5,000 — whichever greater** for
  enforcement violation + attorney's fees

## Workers' Compensation — RCW 51 (the L&I exclusive remedy)

### Coverage and exclusivity

- **Exclusive remedy** for workplace injury — employee
  cannot sue employer for negligence (RCW 51.04.010)
- Employer immunity covers co-employees acting in scope
  (RCW 51.24.020)
- L&I provides: medical, time-loss compensation, pension
  for permanent disability, vocational rehabilitation

### Intentional-injury carve-out — RCW 51.24.020

Exclusivity does NOT bar suit against employer for
**deliberate intention** to injure. Standard is high —
employer must have **actual knowledge that injury was
certain to occur** and **deliberately disregarded** that
knowledge. *Walston v. Boeing Co.*, 181 Wn.2d 391 (2014).

### Third-party suits — RCW 51.24

Worker injured by third party (e.g., negligent vendor on
worksite) CAN sue the third party. L&I has subrogation
lien for benefits paid; lien can be apportioned per RCW
51.24.060.

## Retaliation + whistleblower — RCW 49.60.210 + RCW 42.40

### Anti-retaliation for protected activity

RCW 49.60.210 prohibits retaliation for:

- Opposing WLAD-prohibited practice
- Filing complaint
- Participating in investigation

Causation = "substantial factor" (not "but-for" — more
plaintiff-friendly than federal Title VII per *Allison v.
Hous. Auth. of City of Seattle*, 118 Wn.2d 79).

### Private-sector whistleblower

WA has a **common-law wrongful-discharge-in-violation-of-
public-policy** tort. *Thompson v. St. Regis Paper Co.*
(1984); *Cudney v. ALSCO* (2012).

### Public-sector whistleblower — RCW 42.40 / 42.41

State employees protected; comprehensive procedures.
