# Costs and Attorney Fees — RCW 4.84 and Companion Rules

This reference covers how to recover — and how to be exposed to —
costs and attorney fees in Washington civil practice. Subject-matter
skills supply the substantive fee-shifting statutes specific to
their fields (e.g., FDCPA fees, CPA fees, Collection Agency Act
fees live with `wa-consumer-debt`).

> **Key principle (American rule)**: each party bears its own fees
> unless a **contract**, a **statute**, or a recognized **equitable
> exception** authorizes shifting. *Washington follows the American
> rule.* *City of Seattle v. McCready*, 131 Wn.2d 266, 931 P.2d 156
> (1997).

## RCW 4.84 — Costs in civil actions

### RCW 4.84.010 — Prevailing party costs as a matter of right

The **prevailing party** is allowed certain statutory costs, including:

- Filing fees paid to the court
- Service of process fees
- Witness fees (limited)
- Certified copies
- Court reporter fees for depositions used at trial
- Other statutory cost items

These are recoverable **regardless** of whether the case arises from
contract or tort. Costs are different from attorney fees — they are
the out-of-pocket expenses of prosecuting or defending the action.

### RCW 4.84.030 — Prevailing defendant

If the defendant prevails — including by securing dismissal — the
defendant recovers costs as a matter of right: filing fee, service
costs, deposition expenses, and other statutory cost items.

### RCW 4.84.080 — Attorney fees (statutory, as a cost item)

This statute authorizes recovery of a fixed statutory attorney fee
(currently **$200** in superior court and **$125** in district
court, subject to periodic legislative amendment — **always verify
the current amount** at
`https://app.leg.wa.gov/RCW/default.aspx?cite=4.84.080`).

**This is a nominal statutory fee, not a market-rate fee award.**
It is recoverable by any prevailing party in a civil action
regardless of whether they were represented by counsel.

### RCW 4.84.185 — Frivolous actions

If an action is **frivolous and advanced without reasonable cause**,
the court may award reasonable attorney fees to the prevailing
party. This is a rarely-granted remedy, but worth knowing where the
opposing claim lacks any factual or legal basis.

### RCW 4.84.250–.300 — Actions for damages of $10,000 or less

In actions where the amount pleaded is **$10,000 or less**, the
prevailing party may recover reasonable attorney fees if:

1. The party made a proper offer of settlement (4.84.280) at least
   10 days before trial
2. The other party failed to improve on the offer

This is a powerful mechanism in KCDC cases (CRLJ jurisdiction
caps at $100,000; many cases fall below the $10,000 threshold). A
pro se defendant who makes a proper offer of $0 (or a nuisance-
value counteroffer) can potentially recover fees even without an
attorney, because "attorney fees" awarded under this statute are
measured by what a reasonable attorney would have charged — the
pro se does not recover the attorney's fee directly, but it
preserves a ceiling if counsel is later retained.

### RCW 4.84.330 — Reciprocal fee-shifting on one-sided contract clauses

If a contract or lease provides for attorney fees to *one party* in
an action to enforce it, RCW 4.84.330 makes the clause **reciprocal**
— the prevailing party, whoever it is, recovers reasonable attorney
fees.

Many commercial contracts, credit agreements, and leases contain a
one-sided clause favoring the drafter. When the drafter sues and
loses, 4.84.330 makes the clause reciprocal: the **defendant** now
has a statutory basis to recover reasonable attorney fees on the
contract.

Pro se implications:

- **Pro se litigants generally cannot recover attorney fees for
  their own time** under 4.84.330. The statute requires fees *paid
  to an attorney*. *State ex rel. Macri v. Bremerton*, 8 Wn.2d 93,
  111 P.2d 612 (1941) and progeny establish this for pro se in
  most contexts.
- **But costs are recoverable** (4.84.010, .030) — and if the
  party retained counsel for limited-scope representation
  (consultation, brief review, a single appearance), those **paid**
  fees are recoverable.
- **Cross-check with RCW 4.84.350(1)**: in an action to enforce a
  rule, decision, or order of a state administrative agency,
  attorney fees include the cost of representing oneself, but this
  does not reach ordinary civil practice.

## Fees under CR 37(a)(5)

If a motion to compel is granted, the court **must** award "the
movant's reasonable expenses incurred in making the motion, including
attorney's fees," unless:

- The movant filed the motion before attempting in good faith to
  obtain the disclosure without court action
- The opposing party's nondisclosure was substantially justified
- Other circumstances make an award of expenses unjust

**Fee-shifting under CR 37 is mandatory, not discretionary,** absent
one of the exceptions. Pro se movants can recover expenses (filing
fees, postage, service costs, transcription costs) even where fees
for self-representation are not available. Always plead the
37(a)(5) request in every motion to compel. See `wa-discovery`.

## Fees under CR 11 sanctions

CR 11 authorizes sanctions — including reasonable attorney fees —
against a party who signs a pleading that is:

- Not well grounded in fact
- Not warranted by existing law or a good-faith extension
- Filed for improper purpose

Use sparingly; courts disfavor CR 11 sanctions motions. But where
a pleading is filed without factual foundation and the party
refuses to withdraw after notice, CR 11 is available.

## Fee petitions — how to plead

When a party prevails and intends to recover fees, fees must be
affirmatively requested, usually via:

1. **Request in the pleading** (answer, counterclaim, or motion
   prayer for relief)
2. **Post-judgment motion for fees** filed within **10 days after
   entry of judgment** (CR 54(d)(2) superior / CRLJ 54(d)(2)) —
   **verify the rule numbering** at
   `https://www.courts.wa.gov/court_rules/?fa=court_rules.display&group=sup&set=CR&ruleid=supcr54`
3. **Affidavit itemizing** time, rates, tasks (lodestar method)
4. **Proposed order** awarding specific amount

For pro se, replace (3) with an itemization of **costs actually
paid** and time spent (if the court will accept a pro se time
claim on any basis).

## Lodestar — how fees are calculated in Washington

Washington courts use the **lodestar method** for reasonable
attorney fees:

1. Reasonable hours × reasonable hourly rate = lodestar
2. Adjust up or down for factors (novelty, skill, contingency,
   result)

*Bowers v. Transamerica Title Ins. Co.*, 100 Wn.2d 581, 675 P.2d
193 (1983) is the leading Washington case.

## Offer-of-judgment practice — CR 68 / CRLJ 68

A defendant may serve an offer of judgment on the plaintiff at any
time more than 10 days before trial. If the plaintiff refuses and
obtains a judgment that is not more favorable than the offer, the
plaintiff pays **the defendant's costs incurred after the offer**.

Fees are **not** included in CR 68 costs unless the underlying
substantive law provides for fees. Some contract actions do
(via 4.84.330); many subject-matter statutes provide fee-shifting
to a prevailing plaintiff or counterclaimant — subject-matter
skills catalog those provisions.

## Pro se pitfalls

### What a pro se can recover

- **Costs** under RCW 4.84.010 and .030 (filing, service, copies,
  transcripts, witness fees)
- **Statutory attorney fee** under RCW 4.84.080 ($200 / $125)
- **CR 37 expenses** (out-of-pocket motion-to-compel costs)

### What a pro se generally cannot recover

- **Market-rate attorney fees for the pro se's own time** under
  RCW 4.84.330 or most other fee-shifting statutes
- **Fees for unpaid consultations**

### Workarounds

- **Limited-scope counsel** — hire an attorney for specific items
  (review of pleadings, one hearing). *Those paid fees are
  recoverable* as attorney fees.
- **Declaration of time** — document hours carefully from the
  outset; if a court will entertain any fee claim on pro se time,
  the record must exist. Washington's position on pro se fees has
  evolved; check the current case law before foreclosing the
  option.
- **Deliberate offer-of-judgment** — forces cost-shifting even
  without fees.

## Common pleading patterns

### Answer / counterclaim — prayer for relief

```
WHEREFORE, Defendant prays the Court for an Order:

  A. Dismissing Plaintiff's Complaint with prejudice;
  B. Awarding Defendant costs under RCW 4.84.010 and 4.84.030;
  C. Awarding Defendant the statutory attorney fee under
     RCW 4.84.080;
  D. Awarding Defendant reasonable attorney fees under
     RCW 4.84.330 (reciprocal fee-shifting) and under any other
     fee-shifting statute applicable to the claims asserted;
  E. Granting such other and further relief as the Court deems
     just and proper.
```

Subject-matter skills supply the specific subject-matter fee-
shifting statutes for ¶D (e.g., `wa-consumer-debt` adds
RCW 19.16.450, RCW 19.86.090, and 15 U.S.C. § 1692k for
consumer-debt matters).

### Motion to compel — fee request

```
  Defendant further requests, under CR 37(a)(5) / CRLJ 37(a)(4),
  the reasonable expenses incurred in making this motion,
  including but not limited to filing fees, copy and postage
  costs, and the cost of transcripts, as itemized in the
  accompanying declaration. The award of such expenses is
  mandatory unless the Court finds one of the exceptions
  listed in the rule, and none applies here.
```

  P.2d 193 (1983) — lodestar method

## Notes

- **Always plead fees** — courts will not award what was not
  requested
- **Verify the current statutory amount** under 4.84.080 on the
  RCW page before citing a number
- **Document costs contemporaneously** — save receipts, mail
  tracking, printing bills; the affidavit of costs is easier when
  the record exists
- **Ask for fees under every available ground** — the court will
  apply the one that fits
