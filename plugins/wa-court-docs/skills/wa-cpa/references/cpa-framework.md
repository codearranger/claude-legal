# Washington Consumer Protection Act (CPA) — UDAP framework

The matter-neutral framework for Washington's general UDAP statute, the
**Consumer Protection Act, RCW 19.86**. Subject bundles (`wa-consumer-debt`,
`wa-commercial-disputes`, `wa-cema`, landlord-tenant, etc.) compose with this
skill for their per se hooks; this file holds the elements, standards,
remedies, and limits that apply to **any** CPA claim.

## The prohibition

RCW 19.86.020 declares unlawful "**[u]nfair methods of competition and unfair
or deceptive acts or practices in the conduct of any trade or commerce.**"
"Trade" and "commerce" are defined broadly at RCW 19.86.010(2) (includes the
sale of assets or services and any commerce directly or indirectly affecting
the people of Washington).

## The five *Hangman Ridge* elements

To establish a private CPA claim, a plaintiff must prove all five elements of
*Hangman Ridge Training Stables, Inc. v. Safeco Title Ins. Co.*, 105 Wn.2d 778
(1986):

1. **An unfair OR deceptive act or practice** (the two prongs are independent —
   *Klem v. Washington Mutual Bank*, 176 Wn.2d 771 (2013)).
2. **Occurring in trade or commerce.**
3. **Affecting the public interest.**
4. **Injury to the plaintiff in his or her business or property.**
5. **A causal link** between the unfair/deceptive act and the injury.

### Element 1 — unfair OR deceptive

*Klem* recognizes **three routes** to satisfy element 1:

- **Per se (statutory):** the legislature declares that a violation of another
  statute is a per se unfair/deceptive act under the CPA (e.g., CEMA at RCW
  19.190.030; Collection Agency Act at RCW 19.16.440). Establishing the
  predicate violation establishes element 1 — and the public-interest element
  via RCW 19.86.093(1).
- **Deceptive:** an act has the **capacity to deceive a substantial portion of
  the public.** Intent is **not** required, and the plaintiff need not have been
  actually deceived — the standard is objective. *Panag v. Farmers Ins. Co. of
  Wash.*, 166 Wn.2d 27 (2009).
- **Unfair:** *Klem* adopted the FTC-style standard — an act is unfair if it
  **(a) causes or is likely to cause substantial injury to consumers, (b) which
  is not reasonably avoidable by consumers themselves, and (c) is not outweighed
  by countervailing benefits.** "Unfair" is a separate theory from "deceptive";
  conduct can be unfair without being deceptive.

### Element 2 — trade or commerce

Broad (RCW 19.86.010(2)). For the **learned professions**, only the
**entrepreneurial or commercial aspects** of the practice are covered, not
claims going to competence or strategy. *Short v. Demopolis*, 103 Wn.2d 52
(1984).

### Element 3 — public interest (codified at RCW 19.86.093)

The 2009 legislature codified the public-interest element. A claimant may show
the act is injurious to the public interest because it:

> (1) Violates a statute that incorporates [the CPA]; (2) violates a statute
> that contains a specific legislative declaration of public interest impact;
> or (3)(a) injured other persons; (b) had the capacity to injure other
> persons; or (c) has the capacity to injure other persons. — RCW 19.86.093

- Routes (1)–(2) are the **per se** shortcut (element 3 is automatic once the
  predicate statute is violated).
- Route (3) is the **capacity-to-injure-others** showing for non-per-se claims.
  Pre-2009 case-law factors still inform the analysis: the *Hangman Ridge*
  factors for an essentially **private dispute**, and the *Michael v.
  Mosquera-Lacy*, 165 Wn.2d 595 (2009) factors for a **consumer transaction**
  (e.g., whether the act was part of a pattern, whether others are likely to be
  solicited, whether it occurred in the defendant's regular course of business).

### Element 4 — injury to business or property

- The injury must be to **business or property** — **personal injury and mental
  distress alone do not satisfy the injury element.**
- But the injury need not be large or quantified: *Panag* held that costs
  incurred investigating or responding to a deceptive practice (e.g., consulting
  counsel) can be a sufficient **injury**, and that **injury is distinct from
  damages** — a plaintiff can prove injury (unlocking the claim and fee-shifting)
  even with modest provable damages.

### Element 5 — causation

A **proximate ("but for") causal link** between the unfair/deceptive act and
the injury is required. *Indoor Billboard/Washington, Inc. v. Integra Telecom
of Wash., Inc.*, 162 Wn.2d 59 (2007) (rejecting a per se causation presumption
from a statutory violation; the plaintiff must still prove causation).

## Remedies — RCW 19.86.090

A person injured in business or property by a violation may bring a civil
action for:

- **Actual damages.**
- **Injunctive relief** ("to enjoin further violations").
- **Costs of suit, including a reasonable attorney's fee** — **mandatory** to a
  prevailing plaintiff. *Nordstrom, Inc. v. Tampourlos*, 107 Wn.2d 735 (1987).
- **Treble (enhanced) damages** — discretionary, up to three times actual
  damages, but the enhancement **for a § .020 violation may not exceed
  $25,000.** Available to pro se plaintiffs.
- **District-court option:** the action may be brought in district court for
  actual damages up to the RCW 3.66.020 jurisdictional limit, with the same
  treble enhancement capped at $25,000.
- **Damages content:** out-of-pocket loss, loss of use or value, investigation
  costs; emotional-distress damages may be recoverable **where the injury
  element is independently met by business/property injury** (see *Mason v.
  Mortgage America, Inc.*, 114 Wn.2d 842 (1990)) — verify the current contours.

## Statute of limitations — RCW 19.86.120

**Four years.** Tolled during the pendency of an Attorney General action based
on the same conduct.

## Regulated-industry exemption — RCW 19.86.170

Actions or transactions **otherwise permitted, prohibited, or regulated** by a
regulatory body acting under statutory authority are exempt. The exemption is
**narrow** — it applies only where the specific challenged action is actually
required or permitted by the regulator; mere general regulation of an industry
does not immunize all conduct within it.

## Public (AG) enforcement + the service requirement

- The **Attorney General** may sue to restrain violations, recover costs, obtain
  restoration of property, and seek civil penalties (RCW 19.86.080).
- **Service on the AG:** when a private plaintiff requests **injunctive relief**
  under RCW 19.86.090, the initial pleading must be served on the Attorney
  General (RCW 19.86.095). Calendar this — it is easy to miss.

## Per se CPA statutes elsewhere in the marketplace

Many Washington statutes declare their violation a per se CPA violation; the
predicate statute supplies elements 1–3, and the plaintiff proves only injury +
causation:

- **CEMA** — RCW 19.190.030 (see `wa-cema`).
- **Collection Agency Act** — RCW 19.16.440 (see `wa-consumer-debt`).
- Insurance, residential landlord-tenant, and other regulatory statutes contain
  similar declarations — check the predicate statute for an express per se hook
  before pleading the long-form *Hangman Ridge* claim.
