# Washington Personal Injury — Statutory + Doctrinal Framework

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Personal-injury statutes have been
> amended materially since 1986 (Reform Act) and most
> recently in 2024 (med-mal mediation amendments). Verify
> current text. Personal-injury cases involve substantial
> evidentiary + procedural complexity — consult a licensed
> Washington attorney.

## At a glance — Washington's PI doctrinal architecture

- **Comparative fault**: **pure comparative fault** under
  RCW 4.22 — plaintiff recovers reduced by her own fault
  even if 99% at fault. Washington is one of ~12 pure-
  comparative-fault states.
- **Joint vs. several liability**: **several liability is
  the general rule** post-1986 Reform Act (RCW 4.22.070).
  Joint liability applies only in narrow circumstances
  (concert of action; agency; vicarious; non-delegable
  duty; absent-party fault > 50%).
- **SOLs**: 3 years for ordinary tort (RCW 4.16.080(2));
  3 years for medical malpractice with discovery-rule
  extension to 8-year cap (RCW 4.16.350); 3 years for
  wrongful death (RCW 4.16.080(3)); 4 years for product
  liability under WPLA (RCW 7.72.060).
- **Notice of Claim** required before suing the state
  (RCW 4.92) or local governments (RCW 4.96) — 60 days,
  mandatory contents.

## Comparative fault — RCW 4.22

### Pure comparative fault (RCW 4.22.005)

Plaintiff's contributory fault does NOT bar recovery; it
reduces recovery proportionally. Example: jury finds
plaintiff 80% at fault and defendant 20% at fault for
$100,000 in damages — plaintiff recovers $20,000.

This contrasts with **modified comparative fault** states
(50% or 51% bar — most states) and **pure contributory
negligence** states (DC, MD, NC, VA, AL — any fault
totally bars).

### Apportionment among defendants — RCW 4.22.070

Each defendant pays only their proportional share of
damages **EXCEPT** when:

(a) Plaintiff was not at fault AND defendant acted with a
    person whose acts are attributed to defendant
    (agency, vicarious liability)
(b) Defendant was acting in concert
(c) Defendant owed a non-delegable duty
(d) Damages caused intentionally
(e) Plaintiff's claim is for non-economic damages — these
    are several only
(f) Absent or insolvent at-fault party allocated a share

**Practical impact**: under-insured / judgment-proof
defendants leave the plaintiff bearing that share. The
1986 reform shifted significant risk to plaintiffs.

### Empty-chair defense

Defendants commonly attempt to allocate fault to:

- Settling co-defendants (allowed; RCW 4.22.060
  reasonable-settlement reduction)
- Non-parties / absent tortfeasors (allowed under RCW
  4.22.070 if named in answer + supported by evidence)
- The plaintiff herself (comparative-fault reduction)

Plaintiff must defend against all of these allocations or
lose recovery to "empty chairs."

## Wrongful death + survival — RCW 4.20

### Wrongful death — RCW 4.20.010-020

- Beneficiaries: surviving spouse / domestic partner;
  children; parents (only if no surviving spouse / kids);
  no recovery to siblings except in narrow circumstances
- Recoverable: economic loss + loss of consortium /
  companionship + (for *qualified* beneficiaries) general
  damages
- **2020 reform** (SB 6014): expanded beneficiary class +
  parental recovery for adult children

### Survival actions — RCW 4.20.046 + RCW 4.20.060

- The decedent's personal-injury claim survives to the
  estate
- Recovery: pre-death medical expenses, lost earnings,
  pain and suffering
- Statute amended 2019 (HB 1135) to allow general
  damages survival even without dependents

### Wrongful death SOL — RCW 4.16.080(3)

**3 years from date of death.** Sometimes confusable
with the 3-year personal-injury SOL — but it runs from
**death**, not from injury that later caused death.

## Medical malpractice — RCW 7.70

### Cause of action

**RCW 7.70.030** lists 3 grounds:

(1) Negligence — failure to follow accepted standard of
    care
(2) Promise of result not fulfilled
(3) Lack of informed consent (RCW 7.70.050)

### SOL — RCW 4.16.350

- **3 years from act / omission**, OR
- **1 year from discovery** of injury caused by
  negligence
- Whichever is later
- **8-year hard cap** ("statute of repose") with limited
  exceptions (fraud / intentional concealment / foreign
  body)

### Notice of intent — RCW 7.70.100 (the "90-day notice")

Plaintiff must serve a **Notice of Intent to Sue** at
least **90 days before filing**, with:

- Identity of provider
- Description of negligence
- Statement of damages

Filing without 90-day notice = dismissal without
prejudice (subject to refile after notice).

### Mandatory mediation — RCW 7.70.100(2)

For med-mal claims, **mediation is mandatory** before
trial. Either party can demand it; if neither demands,
mediation may be ordered by court. Parties pay equally.

### Certificate of merit — RCW 7.70.150 (held unconstitutional)

**NOTE**: The Washington Supreme Court held RCW 7.70.150
(requiring certificate of merit before filing) **facially
unconstitutional** in *Putman v. Wenatchee Valley Med.
Ctr.*, 166 Wn.2d 974 (2009). DO NOT cite as still
operative.

### Informed consent — RCW 7.70.050

To prove lack of informed consent:

(a) Material risk involved in the procedure
(b) Risk was not disclosed
(c) Material risk materialized in the form of injury
(d) Patient would not have consented if disclosed

## Product liability — RCW 7.72 (Washington Product Liability Act)

### Coverage

WPLA at RCW 7.72 (1981) is the **exclusive** product-
liability remedy for claims of physical harm caused by
manufactured products. WPLA **preempts** common-law
strict-product-liability claims (*Washington State Phys.
Ins. Exch. & Ass'n v. Fisons Corp.*, 122 Wn.2d 299).

### Theories

**RCW 7.72.030(1)**:

(a) **Construction defect** — the product was unreasonably
    safe in construction
(b) **Design defect** — the product was unreasonably safe
    in design (hybrid consumer-expectations + risk-utility
    test per *Soproni v. Polygon Apartment Partners*, 137
    Wn.2d 319)
(c) **Failure to warn** — adequate warning would have
    prevented harm

### SOL — RCW 7.72.060

**3 years from injury** (PI SOL applies) but **product
liability has a 12-year useful safe life presumption**
under RCW 7.72.060(2) — products outside their useful
safe life create a rebuttable presumption against
liability.

### Defenses

- Useful safe life expiration (RCW 7.72.060)
- Alteration after sale
- Misuse
- State-of-the-art (limited; risk-utility analysis)
- Comparative fault

## Notice of Tort Claim — RCW 4.92 (state) and RCW 4.96 (local)

### Against the State of Washington — RCW 4.92.110

Plaintiff MUST file a **Notice of Tort Claim** with the
Office of Risk Management at least **60 days before**
filing suit. Mandatory contents:

- Names and addresses of claimants
- Date / time / place of incident
- Description of incident + cause
- Description of damages
- Amount of damages claimed
- Verification by claimant

Notice substantially compliant if missing immaterial
elements; substantively defective if missing required
elements.

### Against local governments — RCW 4.96.020

Same **60-day notice** requirement for cities, counties,
school districts, etc. Sent to the entity's risk-
management department or governing body.

### Tolling and timing

The 60-day notice **tolls the SOL** (RCW 4.92.110(1)) —
file notice, wait 60 days, then file suit. SOL extended
by exactly 60 days.

## Damages

### Economic vs. non-economic

- **Economic**: medical expenses, lost earnings, future
  earnings, future medical, property damage — joint and
  several among co-defendants per fault allocation
- **Non-economic**: pain and suffering, emotional
  distress, loss of consortium — **several only** per RCW
  4.22.070(1)(e); collateral-source rule applies

### Punitive damages

Washington does **NOT allow punitive damages** at common
law. Allowed only by statute (e.g., RCW 19.86 CPA treble
damages; RCW 49.60 WLAD; some federal claims).

### Caps

- **No general damages cap** in WA — the supreme court
  struck down the med-mal cap in *Sofie v. Fibreboard
  Corp.* (1989) on right-to-jury-trial grounds.
