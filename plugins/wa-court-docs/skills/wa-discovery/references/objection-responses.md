# Discovery Objections — Drafting and Responding

When responding to opposing discovery, objections preserve your rights
but must be specific — boilerplate objections waive.

## CR 26(g) — the signing rule

CR 26(g) requires every discovery request, response, or objection to
be signed by the party or attorney. The signature **certifies** that
the submission is:

- Consistent with the rules
- Not interposed for improper purpose
- Not unduly burdensome

Violation can result in sanctions equivalent to CR 11.

## General objections

Include at the start of every response. Structure:

```
                GENERAL OBJECTIONS

[Responder] incorporates the following objections into each of
[his/her] responses below:

1.  Privilege. [Responder] objects to each request to the extent
    it seeks information protected by the attorney-client privilege,
    the work-product doctrine, or any other applicable privilege.
    [Responder] will assert such privileges individually and will
    produce a privilege log where required.

2.  Relevance. [Responder] objects to each request to the extent
    it seeks information not relevant to any claim or defense and
    not proportional to the needs of the case under CR 26(b)(1).

3.  Overbreadth. [Responder] objects to each request to the extent
    it is overbroad, unduly burdensome, or seeks information beyond
    the scope of CR 26(b)(1).

4.  Vagueness. [Responder] objects to each request to the extent
    it is vague, ambiguous, or uses undefined terms. [Responder]
    responds based on [his/her] reasonable interpretation.

5.  Third-Party Privacy. [Responder] objects to each request to
    the extent it seeks personal information of third parties
    protected by state or federal privacy laws.

6.  Non-Possession. [Responder] objects to each request to the
    extent it seeks documents or information not in [his/her]
    possession, custody, or control.

7.  Continuing Objections. [Responder] does not waive any of these
    objections by responding to any specific request.

Subject to and without waiving the foregoing general objections,
[Responder] responds to each request as follows.
```

## Per-request objections

### Format

For each request:

1. **Restate the request**
2. **Specific objections** (in addition to general objections)
3. **Response** — answer subject to and without waiving objections

### Sample

```
REQUEST NO. 3:
All communications between You and [third party] from 2018 to 2023.

RESPONSE:
[Responder] objects to this request as overly broad and not
proportional to the needs of the case, as it seeks communications
over a five-year period without limitation to subject matter.
[Responder] further objects to the extent the request seeks
information protected by the attorney-client privilege.

Subject to and without waiving the foregoing objections,
[Responder] will produce non-privileged communications concerning
[narrow topic] from [date] to [date]. Responsive non-privileged
documents are produced as Bates [00001 to 00045]. A privilege log
for documents withheld is attached.
```

### Common specific objections

| Objection | When it applies |
|-----------|----------------|
| Privilege | Attorney-client, work-product, clergy, medical, marital |
| Proportionality | Burden substantially outweighs likely benefit |
| Overbreadth | Scope exceeds claims and defenses |
| Vagueness | Request uses undefined or ambiguous terms |
| Not in possession | Request seeks documents not in custody or control |
| Trade secret | Commercial information protected under CR 26(c) |
| Undue burden | Compilation would require disproportionate effort |
| Relevance | No connection to claims or defenses |

## Responding to overbroad requests

### Strategy

1. State the specific objection
2. Offer a narrowed response ("to the extent the request seeks X,
   Responder produces Y")
3. Do not simply refuse — refusal without a narrowed response
   invites a motion to compel

### Narrowing examples

- Time: "Responder limits the response to the period [X] to [Y]
  which is proportional to the claims."
- Scope: "Responder limits the response to documents concerning
  [specific topic] rather than the full category requested."
- Custodians: "Responder searched the records of [named person] and
  is producing responsive documents within that set."

## Preserving objections — common errors

- **Boilerplate** — CR 26(g) requires specificity; "objection —
  overbroad, burdensome, irrelevant" without more is subject to
  sanction and may waive
- **Failure to produce what is not objected to** — if you object in
  part but have non-privileged responsive documents, **produce them**.
  Withholding non-objectionable material is grounds for CR 37
  sanctions
- **No privilege log** — if you withhold on privilege, CR 26(b)(5)
  requires a log identifying what is withheld and why
- **Late objections** — objections not raised timely may be waived,
  especially for RFAs (CR 36(a) — unresponded = admitted)

## Responding to specific types of requests

### Interrogatories

- Answer fully or state no responsive information exists after
  reasonable inquiry
- If the answer requires a compilation from records, CR 33(c) allows
  production of the records instead (but identify them specifically)
- **Verify** the answers under oath — party signs, not counsel

### Requests for Production

- Identify responsive documents by Bates number or description
- Produce electronically stored information in a reasonably usable
  form (CR 34(b))
- If you have documents held by a third party you control (e.g., your
  own accountant), you must produce — "control" includes the
  practical ability to obtain

### Requests for Admission

- Each response must **fairly meet the substance** of the request
  (CR 36(a))
- **Qualified admission** is allowed if necessary: "Responder admits
  [specific part]; denies the remainder."
- "Cannot admit or deny" requires describing the **reasonable inquiry**
  undertaken
- **Deemed admitted** if unanswered in 30 days

## Privilege log

Required when withholding on privilege (CR 26(b)(5)):

```
                PRIVILEGE LOG

| # | Date | Type | Author | Recipients | Subject | Privilege |
|---|------|------|--------|------------|---------|-----------|
| 1 | 2023-01-15 | Email | [Atty] | [Client] | [Topic] | Attorney-client |
| 2 | 2023-02-20 | Memo | [Client] | [Atty] | [Topic] | Work product |
```

Enough detail for opposing party to evaluate the claim, without
disclosing the privileged content.

## Responding to opposing discovery — general best practice

Pro se parties often over-respond. Best practice:

- **Do not volunteer information** beyond the request
- **Object specifically** where appropriate (proportionality,
  privacy, burden)
- **Produce only what is responsive** — organized, labeled
- **Do not admit legal conclusions** in RFA responses
- **Qualify admissions** with specifics ("Defendant admits that a
  document bearing the name '[X]' was signed, but denies the legal
  effect alleged")

## Continuing duty to supplement — CR 26(e)

If after responding you learn that a response is incomplete or
incorrect, you must **supplement** timely. Failure to supplement can
result in exclusion of the evidence at trial.

## Notes

- **Specificity wins** — specific objections hold up, boilerplate
  does not
- **Produce what's not objectionable** — never refuse wholesale
  when part is proper
- **Track every response** — date served, response due, response
  date, deficiencies, meet-and-confer status
- **Read the actual rule text** before relying on these summaries
