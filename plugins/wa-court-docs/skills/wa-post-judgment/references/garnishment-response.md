# Responding to Garnishment — RCW 6.27

Garnishment is the most common collection tool. A writ of
garnishment commands a third party (employer, bank) to hold the
debtor's funds for the creditor. The debtor has a narrow window to
claim exemptions before funds are released.

## Types of garnishment

### Continuing lien on wages (RCW 6.27)

- Employer withholds a portion of each paycheck for **60 days**
- Creditor may renew
- Federal and state exemptions apply

### Non-continuing (one-shot) lien

- Bank or other garnishee holds funds in the account **at the
  time of service**
- Funds deposited after service are not caught (unless a new
  writ issues)

### Writ of execution (RCW 6.17)

- Sheriff-executed; less common in consumer cases
- Levy on personal property, real property, or bank accounts

## Timing — the 21/28 day window

When the writ is served on the garnishee, the creditor must:

1. **Mail** a copy of the writ and the Exemption Claim Form to
   the debtor at the debtor's last known address
2. Include Washington's statutory **notice of exemptions**

The debtor must **return the Exemption Claim Form**:

- **Within 21 days after the date the notice was mailed**, OR
- **Within 28 days after service of the writ** on the garnishee,
- **Whichever is later**

Missing this deadline **waives most exemptions**. Move fast.

## Receiving the writ — what you see

The packet typically contains:

- Writ of Garnishment (issued by court clerk)
- Notice to Judgment Debtor
- Exemption Claim Form
- Instructions
- Sometimes: attorney fees and costs schedule

Read all of it. The Exemption Claim Form is typically pre-printed
with checkboxes for types of exempt property.

## Exemption Claim Form — completing it

### Fields

- **Case name and cause number**
- **Debtor's name, address, phone**
- **Date writ received**
- **Checkboxes** for types of exemption claimed
- **Value / amount** claimed as exempt
- **Signature** under oath

### Common exemptions to claim (bank garnishment)

Check all that apply. Under-claiming loses exemptions you could
have asserted.

| Type | Authority | Notes |
|------|-----------|-------|
| Social Security | 42 U.S.C. § 407; 31 C.F.R. § 212 | 100% exempt |
| SSI | 42 U.S.C. § 1383(d) | 100% exempt |
| Veterans' benefits | 38 U.S.C. § 5301 | 100% exempt |
| Unemployment | RCW 50.40.020 | 100% exempt |
| Workers' comp | RCW 51.32.040 | 100% exempt |
| Child support received | RCW 6.15.020(3)(b) | 100% exempt |
| TANF / public assistance | RCW 74.04.280 | 100% exempt |
| Bank account — statutory allowance | RCW 6.15.010 | $2,500 individual |
| Retirement accounts (401k, IRA) | RCW 6.15.020; 29 U.S.C. § 1056(d) | Generally exempt |

### Common exemptions to claim (wage garnishment)

| Type | Cap |
|------|-----|
| Consumer debt — 80% of disposable earnings exempt | RCW 6.27.150(1)(a)(ii) |
| Non-consumer debt — federal minimum | 75% of disposable earnings exempt or amount above 35 × fed min wage |
| Child support / spousal support — higher garnishment allowed | Up to 50–65% of disposable earnings |

### Supporting documentation

Attach to the form:

- **Bank statements** showing deposit sources (Social Security,
  unemployment, etc.)
- **Pay stubs** for wage-garnishment exemption
- **Award letters** from Social Security Administration or VA
- **Account statement** showing balance and composition

Mark confidential information (SSN, full account numbers)
redacted.

### Where to send

1. **Court clerk** — file original with the court (by mail, in
   person, or e-filing where available)
2. **Creditor's attorney** — address on the writ
3. **Garnishee (bank or employer)** — to ensure they hold the
   money pending the exemption hearing

Keep **dated copies** and **proof of mailing** (certified mail
receipt or email delivery confirmation).

## After you file the claim

### Creditor may object

The creditor has a short window (usually 7 days) to object. If they
object, the court schedules a **hearing**.

### Hearing

- **Debtor carries the initial burden** to show the funds are
  exempt
- Bring bank statements, award letters, pay stubs
- Testify under oath about the source of funds

### If unopposed

If the creditor does not object, the exemption is typically
granted by default order.

## Challenging the underlying judgment

If the garnishment is based on a **default judgment** that was
never properly served, the debtor should **simultaneously file a
CR 60 motion to vacate** — see `motion-to-vacate.md`. Without
vacating the judgment, the exemption claim can only protect the
specific funds at issue; the creditor can re-serve a new writ.

## Emergency — funds wrongly frozen

### Social Security / federal benefits improperly frozen

Under **31 C.F.R. § 212**, banks must:

1. Look back **2 months** of deposits
2. Protect funds equal to the total federal benefits deposited
   in that period
3. Not freeze protected funds

If a bank froze Social Security funds:

1. **Call the bank** immediately — ask for the "regulatory
   compliance" or "legal order processing" department
2. **Send a letter** citing 31 C.F.R. § 212 with documentation
3. **File an Exemption Claim Form** with the court citing 31
   C.F.R. § 212
4. If the bank does not release within a few days, file an
   **ex parte motion** with the court for an emergency order

### Sample emergency language for the motion

> "Defendant moves ex parte for immediate release of funds
> wrongly frozen in Bank Account No. [####]. The funds consist
> entirely of Social Security direct deposits protected from
> garnishment under 42 U.S.C. § 407 and 31 C.F.R. § 212.
> Defendant is elderly [or disabled, etc.] and has no other
> income; continued freeze will prevent payment of rent and
> medication."

## Strategy — when to contest vs. settle

### Contest the garnishment when

- The underlying judgment is void (move to vacate)
- The funds are clearly exempt
- The garnishment is procedurally defective (wrong court, wrong
  name, improper service on garnishee)

### Negotiate when

- The judgment is valid and the funds are partially non-exempt
- Paying a settlement may be cheaper than extended garnishment
- You need to preserve employment (wage garnishment can trigger
  employer discipline despite federal protections under 15
  U.S.C. § 1674)

### Consider bankruptcy when

- Multiple garnishments are active
- The debt is large relative to income
- Bankruptcy discharge will address the judgment
- Consult a bankruptcy attorney — filing stays most
  garnishments automatically (11 U.S.C. § 362)

## Deadlines — calendar

On receipt of the writ, immediately calendar:

- **Day 0**: Writ received / notice mailed
- **Day 7**: Final day to gather records (pay stubs, statements,
  award letters)
- **Day 14**: Draft exemption claim, review, ready to mail
- **Day 21 (or 28 from writ service, whichever is later)**:
  Deadline to file and serve Exemption Claim Form
- **Day ~30**: Likely exemption hearing

If the debtor has also filed CR 60 to vacate, those deadlines run
in parallel.

## Common mistakes

### 1. Ignoring the writ

Money is gone in 21 days. Do not assume you can fix it later.

### 2. Incomplete exemption claim

Check every applicable box. A pro se debtor who claims only
"Social Security" when they also have wage income below the cap
leaves money on the table.

### 3. No documentation

An unsupported "this is all Social Security" claim is weak. Bring
statements.

### 4. Wrong service

Serve all three: court, creditor, garnishee. Omitting the
garnishee means the bank / employer may release the money without
knowing about the exemption.

### 5. No follow-up

Even if you filed the claim, follow up with the bank or employer
to confirm they have stopped the disbursement.

## Sample cover letter to garnishee

```
[Date]

[Bank Name]
Legal Order Processing / Attn: Garnishment
[Address]

    Re:  Garnishment of Account No. [####]
         [Case name], Cause No. [number]
         Debtor: [Defendant Name]

Dear Sir or Madam:

I am the debtor in the above matter. I have today filed an
Exemption Claim Form in the Court claiming exemption for funds in
Account No. [####]. The funds consist of [Social Security
deposits / wages protected under RCW 6.27.150 / etc.].

A copy of the Exemption Claim Form is attached. Please do not
release any funds to [Creditor] until the Court has ruled on the
claim.

Please contact me at [phone / email] if you have questions.

Sincerely,

[Name]
[Address]
```

## Notes

- **Washington is more protective than federal** for consumer wage
  garnishment (80% exempt vs 75% federal)
- **Joint accounts** — exemption claims can still protect the
  portion belonging to an exempt-income spouse; be prepared to
  document contributions
- **Employer retaliation** for a single wage garnishment is
  prohibited by 15 U.S.C. § 1674; multiple garnishments permit
  termination — negotiate with creditors before a second writ
  issues
