# Supplemental Proceedings — RCW 6.32 (Debtor's Exam)

A judgment creditor may apply for an order compelling the debtor
to appear and answer questions under oath about income and assets.
This is called "supplemental proceedings," a "debtor's exam," or
"supp proc."

## Authority

- **RCW 6.32** — supplemental proceedings
- **RCW 6.32.010** — order to appear and answer
- **RCW 6.32.110** — privilege to refuse to answer
- **RCW 6.32.180** — contempt for failure to appear

## What happens

### 1. Creditor files an application

- Served on the debtor personally (usually)
- Sets a date for the debtor to appear
- Usually held in a referee's office or courtroom
- Creditor's attorney asks questions under oath
- A court reporter (or recording) may be present

### 2. Debtor receives the order

- A **summons-like order** to appear on a specific date and
  time
- Often a **subpoena duces tecum** requiring production of
  documents (bank statements, pay stubs, tax returns)

### 3. Appearance is mandatory

- **Failure to appear** is contempt of court (RCW 6.32.180)
- Possible sanctions: bench warrant, fines, jail
- Do not ignore the order

## Preparation — before the exam

### Review the subpoena

- What documents are requested?
- Is the request overbroad or unduly burdensome?
- Are any documents privileged (attorney-client, accountant-
  client in specific contexts)?

### Gather documents

Typical requests:

- **Bank statements** — 6 to 12 months
- **Pay stubs** — most recent year
- **Tax returns** — most recent 1 or 2 years
- **Retirement account statements**
- **Real estate deeds** / title documents
- **Vehicle titles** / registrations
- **Business records** (if self-employed)

### Review your exemptions

Know cold which of your assets are exempt. See `exemptions.md`.
The creditor is hunting for **non-exempt assets**.

### Organize documents

- Bring **only** the documents requested by the subpoena — do
  not volunteer extras
- Redact **irrelevant and sensitive** information (account
  numbers, SSN of family members, medical details)
- Organize in a labeled folder — makes the exam faster and
  avoids revealing other information

## Types of questions

### Standard — income

- What is your current employer?
- What is your current salary / hourly wage?
- Do you have other income — rental, freelance, self-
  employment?
- Are you owed money by anyone?

### Standard — assets

- Do you have bank accounts? At which institutions? Balances?
- Do you own real property? What is the value? Mortgage balance?
- Do you own vehicles? Make, model, year, value?
- Do you own stocks, bonds, cryptocurrency?
- Retirement accounts — where, balance?

### Standard — recent activity

- Have you transferred any property in the last 1-2 years?
- Have you made any large purchases?
- Have you filed for bankruptcy?
- Do you receive government benefits?

### Questions you may refuse

- **Attorney-client privilege**: communications with counsel
- **Fifth Amendment**: if a truthful answer would tend to
  incriminate you in a crime (rare in civil collection —
  consult an attorney before asserting)
- **Spousal privilege**: in some circumstances for communications
  with a spouse
- **Irrelevant and unduly burdensome** topics: e.g., intimate
  details of relationships that have no bearing on collection

Standard refusal: "I assert [privilege] and respectfully decline
to answer." Do not explain further.

## Truthfulness

**Lying at a supp proc is perjury** — a Class B or Class C felony
depending on the context. It is also contempt.

- Answer every question truthfully
- If you don't know the answer, say so — do not guess
- If you can't remember, say "I don't recall" — do not
  fabricate
- If the answer requires checking records, say so: "I would
  need to check my bank statement to give you an accurate
  answer"

## Typical day of the exam

### Arrival

- Arrive 15 minutes early
- Bring photo ID
- Bring subpoenaed documents, organized
- Bring notepad and pen
- Dress professionally (business casual minimum)

### The session

- Typically 30 to 90 minutes
- You will be sworn in
- Creditor's attorney asks questions; you answer
- Take time — do not rush answers
- If you don't understand a question, ask for clarification:
  "Can you rephrase that?"
- It is OK to pause before answering; a pause is not evasive

### Breaks

- Short breaks are allowed, especially for long sessions
- You may step out briefly; tell the attorney you need a break

## What the creditor learns — and what they can do

### With the information

The creditor can:

- **Serve a writ of garnishment** on the bank or employer
  identified
- **File a lien** on real property
- **Execute** (via sheriff) on non-exempt personal property
- **Continue to monitor** — the exam can be repeated annually or
  as permitted

### What the creditor cannot do

- Take **exempt** funds or property (Social Security, homestead,
  etc.)
- **Share** your financial information publicly or with third
  parties beyond those necessary for collection
- **Threaten** you during the exam (any harassment may violate
  FDCPA)

## After the exam

### Review the record

- A transcript is typically prepared
- Request a copy if you need it for defense against the next
  writ
- Errors in the record can be corrected by errata sheet —
  process varies by court

### Expect a writ

If you disclosed non-exempt assets, expect a writ of garnishment
or execution within days or weeks. Be ready to:

- File an exemption claim on any funds captured
- Review whether the writ is legally proper
- Consider payment plans if the debt is valid

## Strategy — if asked to settle at the exam

Creditors' attorneys sometimes use the exam to push a payment
plan.

### Pros of settling

- Stops the collection machinery
- Predictable monthly amount
- Can often negotiate 40–60% of face value on older judgments
- Stops accrual of post-judgment interest (12% per RCW
  4.56.110)

### Cons

- Locks in the debt as valid
- Missed payment may put you in default of the settlement
- Creditor may still garnish if payment plan breaks

### If considering settlement

- **Get the settlement in writing** before paying anything
- **Include a satisfaction of judgment** clause
- **Confirm** that collection activity and credit reporting
  stop
- **Review** with a pro bono attorney before signing

## Refusing to answer — when and how

If a question is improper:

1. **Pause, then state the basis**:
   "I object on grounds of [privilege / relevance / undue
   burden]."
2. **Do not elaborate** — state the basis only
3. **Note it** on your pad
4. The creditor's attorney can **move to compel** if they
   believe the refusal is improper

For Fifth Amendment: "I respectfully decline to answer that
question on Fifth Amendment grounds." Do not explain why.

## Failure to appear — consequences

- **Bench warrant** — judge may issue one
- **Contempt** — possible jail time
- **Costs** — debtor can be ordered to pay the creditor's
  attorney fees for the missed hearing and re-scheduling
- **Reputation** with the court — harder to get discretionary
  relief (like CR 60 vacation) later

If you genuinely cannot appear (hospitalized, emergency):

- **Call the creditor's attorney** immediately
- **File a motion for continuance** before the exam date if
  possible
- **Provide proof** of the emergency (medical records, etc.)

## Sample opening — your posture

"Good morning. My name is [Name]. I am the debtor in this
matter, appearing pro se. I am prepared to answer questions
within the scope permitted by RCW 6.32 and applicable law. If a
question touches on privileged information, I will state the
basis of any objection."

## Notes

- **This is not a trial** — the merits of the underlying
  judgment are not at issue in the exam; the debt has already
  been adjudicated
- **Civility** goes a long way — hostile debtors can prompt the
  creditor to escalate; cooperative debtors sometimes receive
  better settlement offers
- **Pro bono** — the King County Bar Consumer Representation
  Project and the Northwest Justice Project sometimes assist
  with supp proc preparation; call ahead
- **Do not** transfer assets shortly before an exam to hide them
  — fraudulent transfer law (RCW 19.40, the Uniform Voidable
  Transactions Act) allows creditors to undo transfers made to
  hinder collection
