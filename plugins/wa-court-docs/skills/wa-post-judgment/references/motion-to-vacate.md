# Motion to Vacate — CR 60 / CRLJ 60

A defendant who discovers that a default judgment has been entered
may move to vacate. The motion requires a specific ground under CR
60(b), reasonable promptness, and — for most grounds — a
meritorious defense.

## Authority

- **CR 60** (superior court) / **CRLJ 60** (district court)
- **CR 60(b)** — grounds for relief from judgment
- **White v. Holm**, 73 Wn.2d 348, 438 P.2d 581 (1968) — four-
  factor test for excusable neglect
- **Little v. King**, 160 Wn.2d 696, 161 P.3d 345 (2007) —
  standard for vacating default

## The White v. Holm factors

Courts weigh four factors (for excusable neglect or similar
discretionary grounds):

1. **Meritorious defense** — substantial evidence supporting at
   least a prima facie case
2. **Reasonable excuse** — excuse for the failure to timely appear
3. **Diligence** — moved promptly after learning of the default
4. **Prejudice** — whether vacating will harm the non-moving party

Factor 1 is the most important. A movant with no meritorious
defense loses, no matter how compelling the excuse.

## Grounds — specific language and strategy

### CR 60(b)(1) — Mistake, inadvertence, surprise, excusable
neglect

- **Time limit**: 1 year from entry of judgment
- **Standard**: Discretionary; court applies White v. Holm
- **Common scenarios**:
  - Summons received but misplaced
  - Defendant was in hospital / on leave
  - Mail was delivered to wrong address
  - Misunderstood the response deadline

**Sample language:**

> "Defendant's failure to respond timely was the result of
> excusable neglect. [Specific reason — e.g., 'Defendant was
> hospitalized from [date] to [date], as shown by Exhibit A.']
> Defendant moved to vacate within [X] days of learning of the
> default, which is reasonably diligent under *Little v. King*,
> 160 Wn.2d 696. Defendant has a meritorious defense as set
> forth below."

### CR 60(b)(3) — Fraud, misrepresentation, misconduct

- **Time limit**: 1 year from entry of judgment
- **Standard**: Clear and convincing evidence
- **Common scenarios**:
  - Debt buyer falsely claims to own the debt
  - Sewer service — false return of service by process server
  - Misrepresentation about the balance owed

**Sample language:**

> "The judgment was obtained by fraud. Plaintiff represented in
> its Complaint that it owns the Account and attached a bill of
> sale purportedly evidencing the assignment. That bill of sale,
> attached as Exhibit B, does not identify Defendant's Account
> in the assignment schedule. [Additional facts showing
> fraud.]"

### CR 60(b)(4) — Erroneous proceedings (new trial)

Rarely used for default; mostly for procedural errors during
trial.

### CR 60(b)(5) — Void judgment

- **Time limit**: Reasonable time — but diligence still matters
- **Standard**: **No discretion** — if the judgment is void, it
  must be vacated
- **Common scenarios**:
  - No service or defective service (sewer service, wrong
    address, invalid substitute service)
  - Court lacked subject-matter jurisdiction
  - Court lacked personal jurisdiction (non-resident defendant,
    no minimum contacts)

**Why it matters**: Void means there is no White v. Holm balancing.
The court **must** vacate. No meritorious-defense requirement.

**Sample language:**

> "The judgment is void for lack of personal jurisdiction due to
> insufficient service of process. Plaintiff's return of service
> (Sub No. [X]) reflects service at [address] on [date].
> Defendant was not a resident of that address on the date of
> purported service, as shown by Defendant's declaration and
> Exhibit C (lease agreement for a different address effective
> [date]). Because the summons and complaint were not properly
> served on Defendant, the court lacked jurisdiction to enter
> judgment. The judgment is void and must be vacated under CR
> 60(b)(5)."

### CR 60(b)(11) — Any other reason justifying relief

- **Time limit**: Reasonable time
- **Standard**: Extraordinary circumstances
- Catchall for unusual situations not falling under (1)–(10)

## Motion structure

### Caption and form

Use the statewide format (1-inch margins, 3-inch top on page 1,
letter paper, no color — see GR 14).

### Sections

```
[COURT HEADER AND CAPTION]

        DEFENDANT'S MOTION TO VACATE DEFAULT JUDGMENT

                      I. RELIEF REQUESTED

Defendant [Name], pro se, respectfully requests that the Court
vacate the default judgment entered on [date] under CR 60(b)[(1)
/ (3) / (5)].

                     II. STATEMENT OF FACTS

[Facts in numbered paragraphs, with citations to the record and
attached exhibits.]

1.  Plaintiff filed the Complaint on [date].

2.  Plaintiff's Return of Service (Sub No. [X]) states that
    summons and complaint were served on [date] at [address].

3.  Defendant did not reside at [address] on [date].
    [Supporting facts.]

4.  Default judgment was entered on [date] for [$amount].

5.  Defendant first learned of the judgment on [date], when
    [specific event — wage garnishment, credit report, etc.].

6.  [Additional facts establishing the ground.]

                III. STATEMENT OF ISSUES

The issues are:

1.  Whether the default judgment is void under CR 60(b)(5) for
    lack of personal jurisdiction due to insufficient service of
    process.

[or, for other grounds:]

1.  Whether Defendant has shown excusable neglect under CR
    60(b)(1) and a meritorious defense warranting vacation under
    *White v. Holm*, 73 Wn.2d 348 (1968).

                      IV. EVIDENCE RELIED UPON

This motion is supported by:

1.  The Declaration of [Defendant] filed herewith;
2.  Exhibit A to the Declaration — [description];
3.  [Additional exhibits];
4.  The record and files in this action.

                       V. LEGAL AUTHORITY

A.  CR 60(b)(5) / (1) — governing rule.

B.  *White v. Holm*, 73 Wn.2d 348 (1968) — four-factor test
    (if applicable).

C.  *Allstate Ins. Co. v. Khani*, 75 Wn. App. 317 (1994) — void
    judgment must be vacated without balancing.

D.  [Other authorities.]

                        VI. ARGUMENT

A.  The judgment is void [or: should be vacated for excusable
    neglect].

    [One-paragraph opening.]

    1.  The judgment is void because Defendant was not served.

        [Facts + law + conclusion.]

    2.  [Second argument — e.g., meritorious defense, if
        required.]

B.  Defendant has acted with reasonable diligence.

    [One paragraph showing prompt action after learning of the
    judgment.]

C.  Defendant has a meritorious defense.

    [Summary of the defense, with citation to the supporting
    declaration and exhibits. For debt-buyer cases: lack of
    standing, SOL, lack of documentation, etc.]

D.  Plaintiff is not prejudiced by vacation.

    [One paragraph — typically, "Plaintiff retains all of its
    claims and may proceed on the merits." Delay without loss
    of evidence or witnesses is not prejudice.]

                      VII. CONCLUSION

For the reasons stated, Defendant respectfully requests the
Court grant the motion, vacate the default judgment entered on
[date], and grant leave to file an Answer within 14 days of the
Order.

DATED this ___ day of __________, 20__.

____________________________________
[Defendant Name], pro se
[Address, phone, email]
```

## Supporting declaration

Every CR 60 motion needs a **declaration of the defendant** laying
out the facts under oath. Key elements:

- Identity and pro se status
- Residence history relevant to service
- First notice of the lawsuit / judgment
- Actions taken since discovery
- Supporting exhibits (residence records, correspondence, etc.)
- Facts showing the meritorious defense

Use the declaration template from the wa-pro-se skill.

## Meritorious defense — how to show it

For debt-buyer cases, common meritorious defenses include:

### Lack of standing

"Defendant has a meritorious defense that Plaintiff is not the
owner of the Account. The bill of sale attached to the Complaint
as Exhibit [X] does not identify Defendant's Account by number or
name in the assignment schedule. Under *Bank of America v.
Hubert*, 153 Wn.2d 102 (2005), the assignee bears the burden of
proving ownership of the specific account."

### Statute of limitations

"Defendant has a meritorious defense under RCW 4.16.040. The
alleged last payment on the Account was on [date], more than six
years before the date the Complaint was filed. The claim is
time-barred."

### Lack of contract / lack of foundation

"Defendant has a meritorious defense that Plaintiff cannot prove
the terms of the alleged contract. The Complaint attaches no
cardholder agreement, and based on information and belief
following reasonable inquiry, no original signed agreement exists."

### FDCPA / CPA counterclaim

"Defendant has a meritorious counterclaim under 15 U.S.C. § 1692
and RCW 19.86. Plaintiff sent collection communications after
Defendant disputed the debt in writing, without first providing
validation, as shown by Exhibits [X] and [Y]."

## Diligence — show your work

"Reasonable time" means **act quickly after you know**. Include:

- When you learned of the judgment (with source — garnishment,
  credit report, demand letter, etc.)
- What you did immediately after (consulted pro bono clinic,
  researched, drafted motion)
- How much time elapsed between discovery and filing — aim for
  **under 30 days** if at all possible

## Common mistakes

### 1. Moving too late

1 year is the hard cap for CR 60(b)(1)-(3). But **courts weigh
diligence** — a 10-month delay after discovery looks bad.

### 2. No meritorious defense

A bare "Plaintiff is wrong" is not enough. Attach documentation or
sworn specifics of the defense.

### 3. Wrong rule citation

CR 60(b)(5) for void. CR 60(b)(1) for excusable neglect. Do not
mix them — the standards differ.

### 4. Declaration without personal knowledge

Statements must be based on personal knowledge. "I believe I was
not served" is weaker than "I was in Oregon on the date of
purported service, as shown by airline records attached as Exhibit
A."

### 5. Not requesting attached relief

Ask the court to:

- Vacate the default and the judgment
- Quash any writs of garnishment or execution
- Release any funds held
- Grant leave to file an Answer

Without specific requests, the order may be narrower than you
expect.

## Timing

- **Serve** the motion on opposing counsel and file with the
  court
- **Note for Motion Docket** — typical 14 to 21 days out
- **Response** due 11 court days before hearing (CR 6(d) default;
  may be modified by local rule)
- **Reply** due 5 court days before hearing

## If the motion is denied

- **Reconsideration** under CR 59 within 10 days — narrow grounds
- **Appeal** to Court of Appeals within 30 days of entry of the
  denial order
- A denial of CR 60 is generally a **final, appealable order**

## Notes

- **Filing a CR 60 motion does not stay execution**. If a writ
  is active, move immediately for a stay with the vacation
  motion: "Defendant further requests a stay of execution and
  garnishment under CR 62 pending resolution of this motion."
- **Pro bono**: The Northwest Justice Project, King County Bar
  Association, and other clinics frequently help with CR 60
  motions; consult before filing where possible
- **Default rate**: Washington courts grant CR 60 motions
  relatively often where service was defective or the defense is
  substantive; do not assume defeat
