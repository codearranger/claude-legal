# Courtroom Etiquette — Washington Superior and District Court

Small failures in etiquette cost credibility disproportionately for pro
se litigants. Judges form impressions within the first 30 seconds.

## Before you enter

- **Phone off** — not silent, off. A phone ringing during argument
  can draw a warning or a contempt citation
- **No food, no drink** (water usually acceptable at counsel table)
- **Remove hats, sunglasses, earbuds**
- **Check in with the clerk** on arrival so the court knows you
  are present
- **Review the day's docket** — it is posted outside the courtroom.
  Your case position on the calendar tells you how long to wait

## Attire

### Standard

| Item | Expected |
|------|----------|
| Jacket | Required — suit jacket or sport coat |
| Shirt | Collared, tucked in |
| Tie | Expected for men |
| Pants | Dress pants or suit pants |
| Shoes | Closed-toe dress shoes, polished |
| Color | Navy, charcoal, black, dark gray |

### Equivalents

- A conservative dress or pantsuit
- A blazer with dress pants / skirt
- A buttoned shirt with slacks if no jacket is available (still
  acceptable but less polished)

### Avoid

- Jeans, athletic shoes, shorts, t-shirts
- Visible logos or slogans
- Loud patterns or colors
- Hoodies or jackets with the hood visible
- Strong cologne or perfume

### Remote hearings

- Dress the same from the waist up as you would in-person
- Solid colors photograph better than patterns
- Avoid bright white (camera blowout) or pure black (loss of
  detail) — medium tones are best

## Addressing people

### The judge

- **"Your Honor"** — always, first time and every time
- **"The Court"** — when speaking in the third person about the
  court's authority or prior rulings
- Never "Judge," "Ma'am," "Sir," "Mr./Ms. [Name]"
- When handing up a document: "May I approach, Your Honor?"

### Opposing counsel

- **"Ms. / Mr. [Last name]"** — first reference
- **"Counsel"** — subsequent references
- Never by first name
- Even if you know them personally, maintain formality in court

### Opposing party (not counsel)

- **"Mr. / Ms. [Last name]"** if referring to the party
- **"Plaintiff" / "Defendant"** in legal argument

### Court staff

- **"Madam Clerk" / "Mister Clerk"** in court
- By first name outside of court if introduced
- **"Bailiff"** for the uniformed officer — use if asking
  procedural questions in the courtroom

### Witnesses

- **"Mr. / Ms. [Last name]"** — always
- If there is a last-name conflict, clarify first-name only once;
  then revert to last name

## Physical conduct

### Standing

- **Stand** when the judge enters (the bailiff will call "all
  rise")
- **Stand** when addressing the court (making argument, asking a
  question, making a motion)
- **Stand** when the court enters a ruling from the bench
- **Sit** when the judge directs you to, or when returning to
  counsel table after your argument

### Counsel table

- Arrive and set up materials before the court is called
- Keep the table **uncluttered** — binder open to the outline,
  exhibits flagged, water to the side
- Do not rummage in bags during proceedings
- Do not pass notes or whisper to others while the court is in
  session unless necessary — then, briefly

### Eye contact

- **Look at the judge** when addressing the court
- **Look at opposing counsel** when responding to their argument
- **Do not stare** at opposing counsel during their argument —
  look at your notes or the judge

### Interruptions

- **Never interrupt** the judge
- **Never interrupt** opposing counsel unless the judge invites
  response ("Let counsel finish, please, we'll hear you next")
- If opposing counsel misstates the record, note it on your pad
  and raise it when it is your turn

## Speaking

### Cadence

- **Slow down** — 10 to 20 percent slower than conversation feels
  in court; the record needs to capture every word
- **Pause** between key points — not filler ("um," "uh") — deliberate
  silence
- **Don't race** to finish your time; better to stop early than to
  rush

### Volume

- **Speak to the back of the room** without shouting
- In small KCDC courtrooms, speak toward the microphone at counsel
  table
- On Zoom, use a **headset mic** for best clarity

### Vocabulary

- **Plain English** — avoid Latin when English works ("as a
  result" beats "inter alia" for argument delivery)
- Use legal terms of art where precision matters ("standing,"
  "hearsay," "res judicata")
- If you use a term, be prepared to be asked to define it

### "With respect"

- "With respect, Your Honor, the record shows otherwise" is a
  **powerful phrase** — it signals disagreement while maintaining
  decorum
- Overusing it rings hollow — reserve for when you are actually
  correcting the court or opposing counsel
- Never use it sarcastically

## Addressing the record

- **Cite to the record** — "Your Honor, that is at CP 42" (clerk's
  papers), "Exhibit A, page 3," or "Plaintiff's response to RFP
  No. 5 at page 2"
- For depositions: "Smith Dep. 45:12-18"
- For transcripts: "RP [volume] at [page]"
- Pincites are **essential** — the court cannot verify a general
  claim

## Objections (at hearing — rare but possible)

Motion hearings rarely have testimony, but if there is (e.g.,
evidentiary hearing):

- **Stand** to object
- Say only: **"Objection, Your Honor, [ground]"**
- Common grounds: "hearsay," "relevance," "leading," "foundation,"
  "best evidence"
- The judge will say "sustained" or "overruled"
- If sustained, the question is withdrawn; you sit down
- **Do not argue the objection** unless the judge asks "on what
  basis?"

## After the ruling

### If you win

- "Thank you, Your Honor."
- If the judge wants the proposed order on the bench: "May I
  approach with the proposed order?"
- Do not gloat; do not make further argument
- Collect your materials and exit quietly

### If you lose

- "Thank you, Your Honor."
- Do not argue further
- Consider reconsideration (CR 59) within the time allowed — but
  the motion to reconsider must be based on the rule, not
  displeasure
- Collect your materials and exit quietly

### If the ruling is mixed

- Thank the judge
- Confirm the next steps ("Your Honor, so the Court's order is
  that Plaintiff has [X days] to [Y]?")
- Ask who prepares the order if not clear

## Special situations

### Judge takes the bench in chambers

- Same rules as courtroom
- Maintain formality
- Do not sit unless directed

### Off-the-record discussion

- The judge may go "off the record" for administrative matters
  (scheduling, calendaring) — the court reporter stops typing
- Anything said off-the-record is **not part of the record** —
  make sure critical arguments are said on-the-record

### Bench conferences / sidebars

- Rare in pro se motion practice
- If the judge calls you up, approach only when invited
- Speak quietly
- Do not touch papers at the bench without asking

### Recesses

- Stand when the judge leaves
- Do not leave the counsel table until the judge is off the bench
- If you need to use the restroom, ask the bailiff quietly

## Notes

- **Watch a hearing first** — in KCDC, civil motion calendars are
  public (or publicly Zoom-joinable); spend 30 minutes watching
  before your hearing date
- **Every courtroom has its own rhythm** — a small district-court
  calendar moves faster than a superior-court motion calendar
- **When in doubt, be formal** — over-formality is forgiven;
  under-formality is not
