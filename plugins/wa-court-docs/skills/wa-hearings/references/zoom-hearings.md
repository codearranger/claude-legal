# Zoom Hearings — Technical and Format Rules

Most King County District Court civil motion calendars are now
Zoom by default. Superior court uses a mix — check the case's
specific hearing notice and the judge's standing order.

## Before the hearing

### The link

- The link is on the **Notice of Hearing** or **Note for Motion
  Docket**
- Some courts also post links in the **King County Superior
  Court** virtual courtroom portal or the **KCDC** e-notice
  system
- Save the link in your calendar — and copy the **call-in
  phone number** as a backup

### Technical setup — do this the day before

1. **Install the latest Zoom client** — not web version, desktop
   app. Update if prompted
2. **Test your camera and microphone** in Zoom settings — use
   "Test Speaker & Microphone"
3. **Use a wired microphone** — AirPods / Bluetooth are more
   likely to cut out. A USB headset is best
4. **Hard-wired internet** preferred; strong wi-fi if not. Avoid
   cellular hotspot
5. **Close other bandwidth apps** — no streaming, no large
   downloads
6. **Disable notifications** — macOS: Do Not Disturb;
   Windows: Focus Assist; close Slack, email, messenger
7. **Close browser tabs** — especially any playing video

### Environment

- **Quiet room** — no pets, no roommates, no doorbells. Close
  the door; put a sign on it
- **Neutral background** — blank wall or tasteful virtual
  background. Avoid: bedrooms, kitchens, clutter, anything
  that might be distracting
- **Good lighting** — face lit from **in front** (window or
  lamp), not from behind. A backlit silhouette is the number
  one Zoom mistake
- **Camera at eye level** — stack books, use a laptop stand.
  Looking down at a laptop camera is unflattering and reads as
  submissive

### Sign-in name

- Set your Zoom display name to: **"[Last name], [First name] —
  Pro Se Defendant"**
- Example: "Doe, John — Pro Se Defendant"
- The clerk uses the name to call cases; a cute nickname or no
  name delays your case

### Materials

- **One laptop** for Zoom
- **A second device or paper copies** for your outline, brief,
  exhibits — you cannot look at your outline on the same screen
  you are arguing on without breaking eye contact
- **Exhibits ready to screen-share** if you may need to —
  opened to the right page, one click away
- **Phone** on mute, nearby, in case you need the backup
  call-in

## The day of the hearing

### Timing

- **Log in 15 minutes early** — check the microphone and camera
  one more time
- You will be placed in a **waiting room** — the clerk admits
  parties as cases are called
- Do not assume you can join at the exact hearing time — the
  court may call cases slightly early if earlier cases drop
  off

### When admitted

- Your camera will be **on**, your microphone will be **muted**
  by default (clerk controls this)
- The clerk will call the case: "Calling case 22-2-12345, Acme
  v. Doe — Mr. Doe, please state your appearance"
- **Unmute** and state: "Good morning, Your Honor. Jane Doe,
  pro se defendant, appearing from [city], Washington."
- **Re-mute** after your appearance unless the judge is asking
  you questions

### Stating appearance

Standard appearance:

> "Good morning, Your Honor. [Name], pro se defendant,
> appearing from [city], Washington."

Some judges want to confirm location (for court-reporting
purposes); some don't. State your city to be safe.

## During the hearing

### Muting and unmuting

- **Mute when you are not speaking** — accidental noise, dog
  barking, keyboard clicks, are all captured
- **Unmute only when the court calls on you** or when you have
  a proper moment to speak
- Some judges prefer parties unmute and self-regulate; others
  prefer strict mute discipline. Follow the judge's stated
  preference at the start of the calendar

### Speaking

- **Look at the camera**, not at your own image
- **Pause briefly before speaking** — Zoom audio has a 100–300ms
  delay, and the transcriptionist / court reporter needs time
- **Slow down** — 15 to 20 percent slower than in-person
- **Short sentences** — easier to transcribe, easier to parse
  on laggy audio

### Listening

- **Take notes on paper**, not on the laptop — typing is
  audible and distracting
- **Stop looking at other tabs** — the judge can tell. Close
  everything that isn't Zoom and your outline

### Screen sharing

- Rarely needed for motion argument — the court has the briefs
- If you need to show an exhibit:
  - Have it open in a minimal viewer (PDF reader in full screen)
  - Ask: "Your Honor, may I share my screen to display the
    bill of sale?"
  - Share only the specific window, not the full screen
  - **Close other windows first** — don't reveal email, other
    apps, personal messages

### Objections and interruptions

- Same rules as in-person — do not interrupt
- If opposing counsel is talking over you, pause and wait
- If the audio is garbled, politely note it: "Your Honor, I'm
  having trouble hearing. May counsel repeat?"

## King County District Court (KCDC) — specifics

Check the current KCDC General Order on remote hearings and the
judge's standing order — the rules here change frequently.

Typical current practice:

- **Civil motion calendars are Zoom by default**
- **Trials and evidentiary hearings are generally in-person**,
  with Zoom on motion by either party
- **Small claims** is typically hybrid — in-person or Zoom on
  request
- **Check the hearing notice** — occasionally a matter is
  scheduled in-person at one of the KCDC courthouses (Renton,
  Redmond, Shoreline, Burien, Kent)

### KCDC Zoom link format

The Zoom link, meeting ID, dial-in numbers, and phone controls
for a civil motion hearing are printed on the **current Note for
Motion Docket form** for the division (Burien / Redmond /
Seattle). Do **not** rely on values documented in this plugin or
any prior filing — meeting IDs and call-in numbers change
without notice.

### Authoritative source — pull the current form every time

Get the current Note for Motion Docket and the motions calendar
from the King County District Court civil filings page:

- **https://kingcounty.gov/en/court/district-court/courts-jails-legal-system/civil-filings**

That page links to:

- The division-specific Note for Motion Docket form (which
  contains the current Zoom meeting ID, dial-in numbers, and
  phone mute / raise-hand codes for the relevant courtroom)
- The rolling civil motions calendar (the set of dates the
  CivilMGT clerks will schedule off of)
- Filing instructions and document-code reference

**Agent behavior**: when you are drafting the Note, preparing a
hearing appearance, or confirming connection details, fetch the
current form from that page. Do not cache values across sessions
— the form is a living document.

**Technical support**: the current Note form lists the court's
hearing tech-support phone number. Call that number before the
hearing if the link or meeting ID on your form doesn't connect —
walking into a case calendar with a broken link is avoidable.

### KCDC hearing call order

- Uncontested / agreed matters first (default judgments, agreed
  orders)
- Contested matters in docket order
- Judge may call cases out of order if one party is not present

## Technical failures — what to do

### Your audio fails

1. **Immediately switch to the phone call-in** if audio-only is
   workable
2. **Notify the court** via the clerk's phone number (on the
   hearing notice)
3. **Do not leave the meeting** without announcing — the
   court will think you abandoned the hearing
4. **If everything fails** — call the clerk immediately and
   request a continuance. Follow up in writing

### Video fails

- Audio-only is acceptable for most motion hearings — continue
  if possible
- If the judge requires video, request a short recess: "Your
  Honor, my video has failed. May I have 5 minutes to restart?"

### You get disconnected

1. **Rejoin immediately** — the same link is still valid
2. **Mute on re-entry** — wait to be recognized
3. **When called** — "Your Honor, I was briefly disconnected;
   ready to proceed."

### Opposing counsel drops out

- Wait — the judge will notice
- Do not start argument until the court directs
- The judge may continue or may proceed ex parte if opposing
  has been warned

## Recording

- **The court records the hearing** — you do not need to
  record
- **Recording the Zoom yourself is generally prohibited**
  without prior court approval — do not press Record
- If you need a transcript, request one through the court after
  the hearing

## Dressing for Zoom

- **Full professional attire from the waist up** — shirt and
  jacket minimum, tie for men
- **Solid colors photograph better** than patterns or stripes
  (moiré effect)
- **Avoid bright white** (camera blowout) and **pure black**
  (detail loss) — medium tones
- **Comb your hair** — camera exaggerates
- **Minimal jewelry** — nothing that clinks against a headset
  mic

## Unusual situations

### Calling in by phone only

- Identify yourself by first appearance: "Your Honor, I am
  appearing by telephone due to [technical issue]. Jane Doe,
  pro se defendant."
- Speak slightly louder and slower
- You will be called on by name, not by camera

### The judge mutes you by accident

- Wait briefly; the judge will notice
- If you need to signal, use the **Raise Hand** feature (Zoom
  toolbar, Reactions → Raise Hand)
- Do not start waving physically on camera unless the judge
  invites it

### Another person enters your room

- Pause briefly, mute, and ask them to leave
- If you cannot pause (you are mid-argument), continue — the
  judge will forgive a one-time interruption but not a pattern

## Day-of checklist for Zoom

- [ ] Laptop plugged in, full battery
- [ ] Hard-wired internet or strong wi-fi
- [ ] Headset on, tested
- [ ] Zoom app open and updated
- [ ] Hearing link in browser, tested
- [ ] Backup phone number saved
- [ ] Display name set correctly
- [ ] Quiet room, door closed
- [ ] Lighting tested
- [ ] Camera at eye level
- [ ] Outline and brief on a second device or printed
- [ ] Water, quietly
- [ ] Dressed professionally from waist up
- [ ] Notifications off on all devices
- [ ] Logged in 15 minutes early

Zoom adds failure modes. Plan the technical side so your
argument can be the only thing you think about when the judge
calls your case.
