# Oral Argument — Structure and Delivery

The written brief does the work; oral argument answers the judge's
questions. Most motions are **won on the papers** — the hearing is
where doubt is resolved.

## The 5-minute argument

Plan for **5 minutes**. Most motion calendars allow 5 to 10 per side.
If the judge wants more, you will be asked. If the case is strong,
take less.

### Structure

| Phase | Time | Purpose |
|-------|------|---------|
| Opening | 20–30 sec | Identify yourself, state the motion, state the relief |
| Core points | 3–4 min | Two or three points — no more |
| Close | 15–20 sec | One-sentence summary + specific relief |

### Template

```
Good morning, Your Honor. [Name], pro se defendant.

This is Defendant's motion to [dismiss / compel / for summary
judgment].

The central issue is [one sentence].

I have three points. First, [point], and that is supported by
[record citation] and [authority]. Second, [point] — [support].
Third, [point] — [support].

For those reasons, and the reasons in the briefs, Defendant
respectfully requests the Court [specific relief] as set forth
in the proposed order.

Thank you, Your Honor.
```

## The three-point structure

### Why three

- **One point** looks thin — the judge wonders what you're missing
- **Two points** is usable if one is decisive
- **Three points** is the sweet spot — strong but digestible
- **Four or more** blurs — the judge loses track

### Ordering

1. **Strongest point first** — the judge may interrupt with
   questions and you may never reach points two and three
2. **Most factually grounded point second** — tied to the record
3. **Legal / structural point last** — the rule or policy tie-in

### Alternative: decision-tree ordering

If you are moving for summary judgment or dismissal, order by the
test the court must apply:

- Point 1: "If the Court agrees with X, we win"
- Point 2: "If the Court finds X unclear, Y also requires
  judgment"
- Point 3: "And independently, Z"

Each is an independent ground — any one wins.

## Opening — specific examples

### Motion to dismiss (CR 12(b)(6))

> "Good morning, Your Honor. Jane Doe, pro se defendant. This is
> Defendant's CR 12(b)(6) motion to dismiss the Complaint for
> failure to state a claim. The issue is whether Plaintiff has
> pled the elements of assignment and ownership of the Account.
> Defendant respectfully submits that it has not and moves for
> dismissal."

### Motion to compel (CR 37)

> "Good morning, Your Honor. John Doe, pro se defendant. This is
> Defendant's motion to compel responses to Defendant's First
> Requests for Production and Interrogatories. The parties have
> conferred under CR 26(i); the certification is at CP 45.
> Plaintiff has not produced the chain-of-title documents that
> establish its standing. Defendant requests an order compelling
> production within 14 days."

### Summary judgment (CR 56)

> "Good morning, Your Honor. Jane Doe, pro se defendant. This is
> Defendant's motion for summary judgment. The issue is whether
> Plaintiff can prove ownership of the Account. The undisputed
> record — the bill of sale at Exhibit A — does not identify
> Defendant's Account. Plaintiff has admitted non-possession of
> the cardholder agreement, RFA No. 1. There is no genuine
> dispute of material fact, and Defendant is entitled to judgment
> as a matter of law."

## Middle — delivering the core points

### Formula for each point

1. **Headline** — one sentence stating the point
2. **Record** — pin cite to the fact
3. **Law** — one case or rule
4. **Conclusion** — what it means for the motion

### Sample — debt-buyer standing

> "The first point is that Plaintiff cannot identify the Account
> in the bill of sale. The bill of sale is Exhibit A, pages 1–2.
> Under *Bank of America v. Hubert*, 153 Wn.2d 102, the assignee
> must produce evidence identifying the specific account within
> the assigned portfolio. The bill of sale here transfers 'a
> pool of charged-off accounts' without a schedule identifying
> Defendant's account by number or name. That's insufficient
> under *Hubert* and the Court should grant summary judgment on
> standing."

### Delivery

- **One breath per sentence**
- **Look up** from the outline between sentences
- **Flag pincites with your finger** — "that's at page 14" — not
  "let me find that"
- **Do not read** from the brief — the judge has already read it

## Handling interruptions

### Judge interrupts with a question mid-point

1. **Stop immediately**
2. **Answer the question** — "Yes, Your Honor" or "No, Your
   Honor" or the specific fact
3. **Explain briefly** — one or two sentences
4. **Offer to return to the structure**: "Your Honor, shall I
   return to my second point?"

### Judge signals skepticism

- **Probing questions** ("Isn't your position undercut by X?")
  are opportunities — answer them directly on the record
- **Hostile questions** ("Why should I believe this?") are best
  met with calm restatement of the record: "Your Honor, the
  record shows [specific fact], and that directly answers the
  Court's concern."

### Judge tips their hand

- If the judge says "I'm inclined to grant / deny..." — pause.
  They are previewing the ruling
- If you are being told you will win: **stop arguing**. Thank the
  court. Sit down
- If you are being told you will lose: one respectful last try
  on the strongest point, then accept the ruling gracefully

## Rebuttal

The movant typically gets rebuttal after the non-movant's
response.

### Rules

- **Only respond to oral argument** — do not raise new points
- **Shortest possible** — often under one minute
- **Correct factual errors first**
- **One legal clarification**
- **Return to relief**

### Template

> "Briefly, Your Honor, three points in rebuttal. First, counsel
> misstated the record: the response at CP 42 says [X], not
> [Y]. Second, *Smith v. Jones* is distinguishable because
> [one-line distinction]. Third, for the reasons stated and in
> the briefs, Defendant respectfully requests the Court grant
> the motion. Nothing further."

If rebuttal is not needed: **"Nothing further, Your Honor."** — a
powerful signal of confidence.

## Close

The close is your last image in the judge's mind. Craft it
deliberately.

### Templates

**Standard close:**

> "For the reasons in the briefs and argued here, Defendant
> respectfully requests the Court grant the motion and enter the
> proposed order. Thank you, Your Honor."

**Strong close (when the case is yours):**

> "On this record, there is no genuine dispute and judgment is
> required as a matter of law. Defendant respectfully requests
> summary judgment. Thank you."

**Concession-acknowledging close (when you have a weakness):**

> "Your Honor, Defendant acknowledges [weakness], but the [other
> grounds] independently support the relief requested. Defendant
> respectfully asks the Court to grant the motion on those
> grounds. Thank you."

## Common mistakes

### 1. Reading the brief

The judge has read it. Reading it aloud wastes the court's time
and shows you cannot articulate the case.

### 2. Too many points

Every minute you spend on a weak fifth point dilutes the first
four. Pick three. Cut.

### 3. Ignoring the question

If the judge asks about X, do not continue with your prepared Y.
Answer X first.

### 4. Overarguing the win

If the judge signals agreement, stop. Continued argument makes
the judge reconsider.

### 5. Personal attacks on counsel

"Counsel is lying" is almost never appropriate. Say "counsel is
mistaken" or "the record shows otherwise."

### 6. Emotional appeal

"Your Honor, this has been very hard on me" is irrelevant on a
legal motion. Save hardship for sentencing or equitable relief
motions, and then only briefly.

### 7. Overlong pauses to find pincites

Know where everything is. Flag pages. If you need more than 5
seconds, apologize briefly and move on: "Your Honor, I'll
provide that pincite in a supplemental filing if needed."

## Practicing delivery

### Solo

- **Stand up** — do not practice sitting
- **Time yourself** — 5 minutes, with a timer visible
- **Record audio** — listen for filler words ("um," "like,"
  "basically"), pace, clarity
- **Do it three times** — the third is usually usable

### With someone

- Ask a friend to play the judge — not necessarily legally
  trained
- The listener asks any question at any time — simulating real
  judge interruption
- Debrief: was the answer direct? Was the return to structure
  smooth?

### Watching judges

- KCDC Zoom calendars are generally public — join a calendar
  before yours and watch how arguments work in your specific
  judge's courtroom
- Notice: tone, pace, how the judge signals agreement or
  skepticism, whether the judge rules from the bench or takes
  under advisement

## Final checks before the hearing

- Outline is one page, key points bolded
- Anticipated Q&A list reviewed
- Pincites flagged in exhibits
- Proposed Order printed / ready to email
- Clothes laid out
- Route / Zoom link tested
- Confidence: you have read every case you cite, you know the
  record cold, you can answer any question in one sentence

Good oral argument is disciplined simplicity. The judge has the
briefs — give them the two or three things that make this case
easy to decide in your favor.
