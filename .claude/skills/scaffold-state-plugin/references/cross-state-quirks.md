# Cross-State Procedural Quirks

A reference catalog of procedural quirks across the most-
populous U.S. states that should be **prominently flagged**
in the relevant skill descriptions when adding a new state
plugin. A pro se filer porting practice from one state to
another typically gets tripped up by exactly these
differences.

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** Each entry is a starting point.
> Verify the current rule before relying.

## Discovery

### Written interrogatories

| State | Available by default? | Limit |
|-------|----------------------|-------|
| Federal | Yes (FRCP 33) | 25 |
| Washington | Yes (CR 33) | 25 |
| **Oregon** | **NO** — court order required | n/a |
| California | Yes (CCP § 2030) | 35 (form) + 35 (special) |
| Texas | Yes (TRCP 197) | Varies by discovery level |
| Florida | Yes (Fla. R. Civ. P. 1.340) | 30 |
| New York | Yes (CPLR 3130) | 25 |

Oregon's no-interrogatories rule is the most distinctive
discovery quirk in the U.S.

### Discovery control plans (Texas)

Texas TRCP 190 imposes mandatory discovery levels (1, 2, 3)
based on case characteristics — affects discovery scope,
deposition limits, time limits. Unique to Texas.

### Mandatory disclosures

| State | Required? | Authority |
|-------|----------|-----------|
| Federal | Yes | FRCP 26(a) |
| California | NO | n/a (no broad mandatory disclosure) |
| Texas | Yes — TRCP 194 | "initial disclosures" within 30 days |
| Florida | NO | n/a |

### Discovery cutoffs

State practice on discovery cutoffs varies:

- Federal: 30 days before trial typically
- California: 30 days before trial (CCP § 2024.020)
- Texas: 30 days before trial typically
- Florida: pretrial conference sets

## Pleading standards

### Notice vs. fact pleading

| State | Standard |
|-------|----------|
| Federal | Notice pleading (Twombly/Iqbal plausibility) |
| Washington | Notice pleading |
| **Oregon** | **Fact pleading** — must allege ultimate facts (Granewich) |
| California | "Ultimate facts" / fact pleading — but liberalized |
| Texas | Fact pleading |
| Florida | Notice pleading |
| New York | Fact pleading (CPLR 3013) |

When porting a complaint from a notice-pleading state to a
fact-pleading state, expect ORCP 21 A(8)-style failure-to-
state attacks.

### Verified pleadings (New York)

New York has the concept of verified vs. unverified pleadings
with distinct treatment under CPLR 3020. Unique to New York
in modern practice.

## Summary judgment

| State | Rule | Quirks |
|-------|------|--------|
| Federal | FRCP 56 | Standard |
| Washington | CR 56 | 28-day filing window |
| Oregon | ORCP 47 C | 60-day filing window |
| California | **CCP § 437c** | **75-day filing window**; 14-day reply; very procedurally strict |
| Texas | TRCP 166a | "Traditional" + "no-evidence" types |
| Florida | Fla. R. Civ. P. 1.510 | **2021 amendment** aligned standard with federal *Anderson v. Liberty Lobby* |
| New York | CPLR 3212 | No partial summary judgment in some contexts |

California's CCP § 437c timing is the most-aggressive of any
state — the 75-day notice period is a hard floor.

Florida's 2021 SJ standard change is a recent quirk; pre-2021
Florida case law on SJ should be flagged as superseded.

| State | Default rule | Vacation rule |
|-------|--------------|---------------|
| Federal | FRCP 55 | FRCP 60(b) |
| Washington | CR 55 | CR 60 |
| Oregon | ORCP 69 | ORCP 71 |
| California | CCP § 585 | CCP § 473 |
| Texas | TRCP 239 | TRCP 320 (motion for new trial) |
| Florida | Fla. R. Civ. P. 1.500 | Fla. R. Civ. P. 1.540 |
| New York | CPLR 3215 | CPLR 5015 |

### Time limits on default vacation

| State | Outer cap |
|-------|-----------|
| Federal | 1 year (FRCP 60(c)(1)) |
| Oregon | 1 year (ORCP 71 B(1)-(3)) |
| California | 6 months (CCP § 473(b)) |
| Texas | 30 days post-judgment for motion for new trial |
| Florida | 1 year (Rule 1.540) |

California's 6-month cap is shorter than most.

## Service

### ORCP 7 / CCP § 415 / FRCP 4 analogs

Each state has its own service rules. Notable quirks:

- **California (CCP § 415)**: substituted service requires a
  10-day mailing; service complete on 10th day
- **Texas (TRCP 106)**: motion for substituted service
  required; cannot use without court order
- **New York (CPLR 308)**: "nail and mail" service requires
  affidavit detailing prior attempts

### Pre-trial deadlines

The Return of Service (or analog) deadline varies:

| State | Deadline |
|-------|----------|
| Oregon | 63 days (ORCP 7 D(3)) |
| Washington | 90 days (CR 4(d)) |
| California | 60 days (CCP § 583.210) |
| Federal | 90 days (FRCP 4(m)) |

## Statutes of limitations on contract debt

| State | SOL | Authority |
|-------|-----|-----------|
| Washington | 6 yrs (written), 3 yrs (oral) | RCW 4.16.040, .080 |
| Oregon | 6 yrs | ORS 12.080 |
| **California** | **4 yrs (written), 2 yrs (oral)** | **CCP § 337, § 339** |
| Texas | 4 yrs | Tex. Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code § 16.004 |
| Florida | 5 yrs | Fla. Stat. § 95.11 |
| New York | 6 yrs general; **3 yrs for revolving credit (2022)** | NY CPLR § 213; § 214-i |
| Illinois | 10 yrs (written), 5 yrs (oral) | 735 ILCS 5/13-205 |

Notable shorter SOLs:

- California 4-year on contract
- New York's 2022 reform shortening credit-card SOL to 3
  years (CPLR § 214-i)

## Revival of SOL by partial payment

| State | Partial payment revives? | Authority |
|-------|--------------------------|-----------|
| Washington | Yes | RCW 4.16.270 |
| **Oregon** | **NO — written promise only** | **ORS 12.230** |
| California | Yes | CCP § 360 |
| Texas | NO — written promise only | Tex. Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code § 16.065 |
| Florida | Acknowledgment must be written | Fla. Stat. § 95.04 |
| New York | NO — written acknowledgment only (CPLR § 17) | CPLR § 17 |

Many states require a written promise. The Oregon flag in
`or-consumer-debt/references/or-statutes-of-limitations.md` is
applicable to several other states; adapt language when adding
those states' plugins.

## Consumer-protection / unfair-practices statutes

| State | Statute | Key features |
|-------|---------|--------------|
| Washington | RCW 19.86 (CPA) | Hangman Ridge factors; treble damages capped at $25,000 |
| Oregon | ORS 646.605 (UTPA) | $200 statutory minimum; no cap |
| California | Bus. & Prof. Code § 17200 (UCL) + Civ. Code § 1750 (CLRA) + Civ. Code § 1788 (Rosenthal) | Multiple overlapping statutes; private right of action available |
| Texas | Tex. Bus. & Com. Code § 17 (DTPA) | Treble damages on "knowing" violations |
| Florida | Fla. Stat. § 501.201 (FDUTPA) | Actual damages + fees |
| New York | GBL § 349, § 350 | $50 minimum, $1,000 cap on willful; very narrow |

Compared to FDCPA:

- All state statutes typically cover original creditors
  (FDCPA does not)
- State statutes often have no $1,000 cap
- State fee provisions vary; California Rosenthal has
  mandatory fees similar to FDCPA

## Debt-collection licensing

| State | License/Registration? | Effect of non-compliance |
|-------|-----------------------|--------------------------|
| Washington | RCW 19.16 — license | Cannot collect; private right (RCW 19.16.440) |
| Oregon | ORS 697 — registration | **Cannot maintain action (ORS 697.105)** |
| California | Cal. Fin. Code § 100001+ (CDCLA, 2022) — license | License required as of Sept 2022 |
| Texas | Tex. Fin. Code § 392 — bond | Voids communications (§ 392.401) |
| Florida | Fla. Stat. § 559.55+ — license | Various penalties |
| New York | NY Gen. Bus. Law § 600 — license | Affidavit requirements |

The Oregon lack-of-capacity defense (ORS 697.105) is unusually
strong. California's CDCLA (post-2022) has growing analogs.

| Federal | FRCP 6 | 3 days (Rule 6(d)) |
| Washington | CR 6 | 3 days (CR 6(e)) |
| Oregon | ORCP 10 | 3 days (ORCP 10 C) |
| California | CCP § 1013, § 1010.6 | 5 days (CCP § 1013) for mailed papers |

## Citation style

The state's style manual diverges from Bluebook in nearly
every state:

| State | Periods in reporter? |
|-------|---------------------|
| Washington | Yes (Wn.2d, Wn. App., P.3d) |
| **Oregon** | **No (Or, Or App, P3d, USC, CFR)** |
| California | Yes, but specific format (Cal. 3d, Cal. App. 4th) |
| Texas | Yes (Tex., Tex. App.) |
| Florida | Yes (Fla., Fla. App.) |
| New York | Yes (N.Y., N.Y.2d) |

Oregon's no-period convention is the most distinctive among
state style manuals.

## Party-name conventions

| State | Separator | Notes |
|-------|-----------|-------|
| Washington | "vs." | All caps in caption |
| **Oregon** | **"v."** | Lowercase with period |
| California | "v." | Per CRC 2.111 |
| Texas | "v." | |
| Florida | "v." | |
| **New York** | **"-against-"** | NY captions use the literal hyphen-against-hyphen form, not "v." or "vs." (a frequent surprise for out-of-state practitioners) |

|-------|----------------------------------------------------------|

| California | Mixed; varies by court (LA tentative-ruling system) |

## Ethical / pro se rules

| Quirk | State |
|-------|-------|
| OSB# on signature block | Oregon (counsel only) |
| WSBA# on signature block | Washington (counsel only) |
| Cal. Bar # on signature | California (CCP § 396a) |
| OCA # + NY Attorney Registration # on signature | New York (counsel only; pro se omits) |
| "Pro se" vs. "in pro per" | Most states "pro se"; California "in pro per"; New York "Self-Represented" |
| Affirmation under penalty of perjury without notary | **New York (post-2023 CPLR 2106 amendment, L 2023 ch 559)** — now available to any person, not just attorneys |

California's "in pro per" terminology is unique among major
states. **New York's post-2023 universal affirmation under
CPLR 2106** is a major pro-se accessibility win — the rule
used to require attorneys for unsworn affirmations and pro
se litigants to use notarized CPLR 2309 affidavits. The 2023
amendment ended that bottleneck.

## Court system topology

Most states fit the "Superior / Supreme + county-court roll-
up + small-claims" shape that the 21-skill base assumes. A
few states have additional civil-court layers that warrant
**dedicated venue skills** rather than rolling everything
into the county-courts skill.

| State | Distinctive civil-court layers |
|-------|-------------------------------|
| **New York** | Civil Court of the City of New York (Civil Court Act, $50k cap, 5 borough branches); NYC Housing Court (Housing Part of Civil Court under § 208.42); Nassau + Suffolk District Courts (UDCA / 22 NYCRR Part 212, $15k cap; only on Long Island); upstate City Courts (UCCA / 22 NYCRR Part 210, $15k cap); ~1,250 Town and Village Justice Courts (UJCA / 22 NYCRR Part 214, $3k cap); Family Court (FCA / 22 NYCRR Part 205); Court of Claims (22 NYCRR Part 206) |
| New Jersey | Special Civil Part (≤$20k); Small Claims Section (≤$5k); Landlord-Tenant Section (within Special Civil Part) |
| Massachusetts | Housing Court (dedicated trial court for L&T statewide) |
| Texas | Justice Courts (≤$20k since 2020); County Courts at Law; District Courts |

When porting to one of these states, consider peeling out
dedicated skills for the lower-court layers rather than
rolling them into the county-courts roll-up. NY ships 35
skills for this reason.

## NY-specific procedural quirks

These came up during the NY plugin authorship and are good
references for any state with a similarly complex civil
practice:

- **CPLR 2103(b)(2) 5-day mail rule** — when service is by
  mail, add **5 days** to any subsequent response/service
  period (longer than the federal/many-state 3-day rule).
- **Individual Assignment System (22 NYCRR § 202.3)** — one
  Justice owns the case from RJI through judgment. Each
  Justice publishes their own "Part Rules" — pro-se filers
  must look up the assigned Justice's Part Rules before
  every motion.
- **22 NYCRR § 202.48 settle-order procedure** — when a
  court directs an order to be settled, the proposed order
  must be submitted within **60 days** (a jurisdictional
  clock under *Funk v. Barry*). Miss it and the motion is
  deemed abandoned.
- **20-year SOL on money judgments (CPLR 211(b))** — the
  longest in the U.S. CPLR 5014 allows judgment renewal.
- **Verified vs. unverified pleadings (CPLR 3020)** — a
  verified complaint forces a verified answer; debt-buyer
  plaintiffs often verify to make the defendant's life
  harder. Tactical choice for both sides.
- **2022 Consumer Credit Fairness Act (CCFA)** —
  retroactively shortened the SOL on consumer-credit
  actions to **3 years** (CPLR 214-i), added heightened
  pleading at CPLR 3015(e), and added default-judgment
  evidence scrutiny at 22 NYCRR § 202.27-a (Supreme Court)
  / § 208.6-a (NYC Civil Court).
- **RPAPL § 711(2) 14-day rent demand (post-2019 HSTPA)** —
  the predicate for a nonpayment summary proceeding is now
  a 14-day written rent demand (was 3 days pre-HSTPA).
  Defective demand = jurisdictional defense.
- **Local Law 136 of 2017 universal Right to Counsel** —
  NYC Admin Code § 26-1301 provides income-qualified
  tenants with assigned counsel in eviction proceedings.
  Unique to NYC; some upstate cities have piloted similar
  programs.

## Family-law cross-state variations

Family law is sprawlingly state-specific. These tables
catalog the major axes that vary state-to-state. **Use for
research only — do NOT import comparisons into the new
plugin's bodies.**

### Family-court topology

| Pattern | States |
|---|---|
| Separate Family Court (own trial court) | NY, MA, DE, RI, HI |
| Family Division of general-jurisdiction trial court (functionally similar but inside Superior / Circuit Court) | CA, FL, TX, OH, MI, GA, IL, PA, NJ, most other states |
| Specialized Domestic Relations Division | OH (Domestic Relations Court), some CA counties |
| Concurrent jurisdiction (custody + support in two courts) | NY (Family Court + Supreme Court) |

The `<abbr>-family-court` venue skill ships in every state's
plugin regardless — but its body adapts to the topology.

### Divorce grounds — no-fault waiting period

Every state has no-fault divorce as of 2010 (NY was the
last). Waiting period before decree varies:

| State | No-fault waiting period | Fault grounds still recognized? |
|---|---|---|
| New York | 6 months separation (DRL § 170(7)) | Yes (DRL § 170(1)-(6)) |
| California | 6 months from service (Fam. Code § 2339) | No (no-fault only) |
| Colorado | 91 days from service (C.R.S. § 14-10-106) | No |
| Texas | 60 days from filing (Tex. Fam. Code § 6.702) | Yes |
| Washington | 90 days from filing (RCW 26.09.030) | No |
| Florida | None (per Fla. Stat. § 61.052) | No |
| Illinois | 6 months separation (750 ILCS 5/401) | No |
| Pennsylvania | 1 year separation (no-fault); 90 days mutual-consent | Yes |

### Property distribution regime

| Regime | States |
|---|---|
| **Community property** | CA, TX, WA, AZ, NM, ID, LA, NV, WI |
| **Equitable distribution** | All other states + DC |

Community-property states presume 50/50 division of marital
property; equitable-distribution states apply factor-based
discretionary division. The distinction materially affects
case-strategy advice and is worth flagging prominently in
the family-law bundle.

### Child-support guideline model

| Model | States |
|---|---|
| **Income shares** (combined parental income → both pay) | Most states: CO, NY (via CSSA), CA, NJ, OH, MI, GA, FL, IL, MA, PA, VA, MD, plus ~30 others |
| **Percentage of payor's income** (obligor's income → flat %) | WI, ND, NV, TX (under DOM Code Ch. 154), some others |
| **Melson formula** (hybrid — DE, HI, MT) | DE, HI, MT |

Statutory cap on combined parental income (income above
which CSSA percentages may not auto-apply):

| State | Cap (most recent) |
|---|---|
| New York | $183,000 (2024, adjusts every 2 years) |
| California | No statutory cap; high-income deviation discretion |
| Colorado | $30,000/month combined ($360,000/year) — adjusts |
| Texas | $9,200/month obligor net resources (2019) |

### Modification threshold for child support

| State | Threshold to trigger modification review |
|---|---|
| New York | 15% change in either parent's gross income, or **3-year automatic review** under CSSA |
| Colorado | 10% change in support amount under CRS § 14-10-122 |
| California | "Significant change in circumstances" (no fixed %) |
| Texas | 20% or $100/month change |
| Florida | 15% or $50/month change |

### Family-offense / Order of Protection frameworks

Every state has a civil-protective-order regime; the
qualifying-relationship + qualifying-offense + duration
catalogs vary:

- **Qualifying relationship**: spouse / ex-spouse (universal);
  parents-in-common-of-child (universal); cohabitants
  (most); "intimate-partner" relationship (expanding);
  household members (most); blood / marriage family
  members (most).
- **Qualifying offense**: assault + harassment + stalking
  + menacing are nearly universal. NY's FCA § 812 catalog
  is one of the broadest (added coercive control in 2022).
  CA's DV Prevention Act under Fam. Code § 6320 is
  similarly broad. TX's Tex. Fam. Code § 71.004 is more
  restrictive.
- **Duration**: temporary (ex parte) → final. Final order
  duration ranges:
  - NY: up to 2 years; 5 with aggravating circumstances
  - CA: up to 5 years (permanent in narrow cases)
  - CO: 1 year initial; can become permanent on
    showing of continued threat
  - TX: up to 2 years

### Right to assigned counsel in family court

| Proceeding | Assigned counsel? |
|---|---|
| Abuse & neglect (parent respondent) | Yes — universal (federal due-process) |
| Termination of parental rights | Yes — universal |
| Family-offense / OP respondent | Varies (NY yes when OP is sought; most others no) |
| Custody dispute (no abuse allegation) | No (most states) |
| Divorce | No (most states) |
| Child support modification | No |

## How to use this catalog

When adding a new state plugin:

1. Identify the quirks from this catalog that apply to the
   new state
2. Author them prominently in the relevant skill's
   `description:` (so the skill triggers when the user
   stumbles into the quirk)
3. Author them prominently in the skill body (so when the
   skill fires, the agent walks the user through correctly)
4. Add them to the new state's `fact-patterns.md` (the
   common-failure-modes list)

When adding a state not yet on this list (Arizona, Colorado,
Michigan, etc.), research the analogous quirks from primary
sources and update this catalog as well.
